## The Problem with QR Codes Specifically

Before getting into the tools, it is worth understanding what makes QR code scams different from ordinary link scams.

With a phishing email, the link is visible — and if you know to look, warning signs are readable. The URL might be slightly misspelled. The domain might not match the sender. You can hover over the link on a desktop computer and see the destination before you click. A QR code strips all of that away. It is a visual pattern your phone decodes directly into a URL, which your browser then opens. By the time you see the destination, you have already landed on the page.

Scammers take advantage of this by placing [fake QR code](https://snipstack.io/fake-qr-code-scam-how-to-check-2026/)s on top of legitimate ones — in car parks, on restaurant tables, on parcel delivery notices, or on printed posters. Research from the FBI and the UK's National Cyber Security Centre has flagged this technique, known as "quishing," as a growing threat specifically because it bypasses email security filters and prey on physical-world trust.

The habit to build: always check the URL your QR scanner reveals before you tap "open." Every tool in this comparison works at that moment.

## Google Safe Browsing — The Invisible Baseline

Google Safe Browsing runs automatically whenever you use Chrome, Safari, or Firefox. When your phone opens a URL — including one decoded from a QR code — it cross-checks the destination against Google's database of known phishing and malware sites. If there is a match, you see a red warning page before the target site loads.

**What it does well:** It requires nothing from you. Every QR code you scan in Chrome or Safari gets this check automatically. For well-known phishing campaigns that have already been catalogued, the protection is instant and invisible.

**Where it falls short:** Google Safe Browsing relies on a cached blocklist, not a live reputation score. Brand-new scam pages — especially QR code phishing sites, which are often created the same day they are deployed on physical posters — may not yet be in the database. A page that was created last Tuesday to harvest credentials at a specific car park may have no history at all. Safe Browsing gives no details either: you either see a warning or you do not. There is no trust score, no domain age, no context.

**Best for:** Catching widely reported, previously catalogued scam sites as a baseline. Not sufficient on its own for QR code scenarios, where the destination may be brand new.

## VirusTotal — The Most Thorough Manual Check

VirusTotal lets you paste a URL and runs it through more than 70 antivirus and security engines simultaneously. For a QR code, the process is: scan the code with your phone's default camera app, copy the URL it reveals without visiting it, then open VirusTotal and paste the address in. Results take seconds and show you which engines flagged the site and what type of threat they identified.

**What it does well:** Depth and transparency. If 70 independent engines all return clean, that is meaningful. VirusTotal also shows historical scan data, community comments, WHOIS domain registration information, and the categories each engine assigned to the site. For a URL you are genuinely suspicious about, this is the most thorough free check available.

**Where it falls short:** It requires manual steps. You need to remember to use it, have the app or website open, copy the URL before visiting it, and interpret the results. For someone who scanned a QR code quickly in a car park, this workflow requires deliberate effort. VirusTotal also occasionally flags legitimate new domains as suspicious because they have no scan history — a freshly launched business website can look like a new scam domain to automated engines. Reading the results requires some familiarity with what you are looking at.

**Best for:** Technical users or anyone who wants a thorough one-off check on a specific QR code destination before visiting it. Also useful as a second opinion if another tool returns an ambiguous result.

## URLVoid — Aggregated Blacklist Lookup

URLVoid checks a domain against more than 30 security databases simultaneously — including PhishTank, Sucuri, OpenPhish, and SURBL — and returns a pass or fail for each. Like VirusTotal, you use it by pasting the URL revealed by your QR scanner.

**What it does well:** It is fast, free, and pulls from a wide range of established databases. The multi-source view helps you understand the nature of any threat: a flagged result might show that the domain is specifically blacklisted for phishing (via PhishTank) rather than malware distribution (via Sucuri). The domain registration date and hosting details are also shown, which is genuinely useful context — a site registered two days ago and being promoted via a physical QR poster is a significant red flag.

**Where it falls short:** Like VirusTotal, URLVoid requires you to manually copy the URL and paste it in. There is no browser integration, no account system, no alerting, and no history across your checks. A newly created scam domain will return clean results until it has been reported and processed by at least one of the databases URLVoid queries. Given that QR code phishing sites are often used for very short windows before being taken down, this lag matters.

**Best for:** A fast multi-source blacklist check when you have already copied the URL from a QR code and want a second opinion before visiting. Pairs well with VirusTotal — if both return clean, your confidence is reasonably high.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a QR code scam checker?

A QR code scam checker is a tool that evaluates the URL hidden inside a QR code to determine whether it points to a fraudulent, malicious, or phishing website. Since QR codes do not display a readable web address, the check typically happens after your scanner reveals the URL but before you visit the page. Tools range from manual lookups (VirusTotal, URLVoid) to purpose-built apps (Trend Micro QR Scanner) to always-on browser-level protection (Surfshark Scamcheck).

### How do QR code scams actually work?

Scammers create a QR code that points to a fake website — typically one designed to mimic a real organisation (a bank, a delivery company, a car park payment system). They print the code and stick it over legitimate QR codes on physical signs, or distribute it via email and post. When you scan it, your phone opens the fake site, which may ask for your login details, payment information, or personal data. The technique is called "quishing" and is growing because QR codes give no text-based clues that something is wrong before you scan.

### Can I check a QR code without scanning it?

If you have an image of the QR code (for example, in an email or a screenshot), you can decode it without visiting the URL. VirusTotal's file upload feature can decode QR codes from image files and check the embedded URL. Several free online QR decoders (like zxing.org) will show you the URL without opening it, so you can copy it and paste it into URLVoid or VirusTotal manually.

### Is it safe to scan a QR code with my phone's default camera?

Your phone's default camera app will open the URL in your [browser with](https://snipstack.io/browser-with-built-in-ad-blocker-2026/)out a built-in safety check (beyond Google Safe Browsing, which is automatic in Chrome and Safari). For QR codes in trusted environments — a restaurant menu, an official venue — your existing browser protection is generally sufficient. For unexpected QR codes in emails, on posted signs, or on parcels you did not order, pausing to verify the URL before visiting it is worth the extra step.

### Do QR code checkers work for QR codes in emails?

Yes. If you receive an email containing a QR code image, you can screenshot the code, use a QR decoder to reveal the URL, and then run that URL through VirusTotal, URLVoid, or Surfshark Scamcheck. Alternatively, if you have Surfshark active on your device, scanning the QR code will trigger Scamcheck's real-time evaluation before the page loads fully.

### [What should I do if a QR code scam checker flags a destination?](https://snipstack.io/how-to-report-a-phishing-website-2026/)

Close the tab immediately and do not enter any information on the page. If the QR code appeared to come from an organisation you do business with — a bank, a delivery company, a government agency — contact that organisation directly using a number or website address you already know, not any details from the suspicious page. If you received the QR code by post, you can report it to your national cybersecurity authority (in the UK, the NCSC; in the US, report to the FTC or forward suspicious emails to reportphishing@apwg.org).
