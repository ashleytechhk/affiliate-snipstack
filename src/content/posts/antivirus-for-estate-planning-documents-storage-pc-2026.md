## What Makes Estate Planning Documents So Valuable to Criminals

Your will, trust documents, and financial records contain exactly what identity thieves need: full legal names, dates of birth, Social Security numbers, bank account details, property deeds, and beneficiary information. In the wrong hands, this data can be used to:

- Open fraudulent credit accounts in your name or your beneficiaries' names
- Empty bank accounts before your estate is settled
- Forge signatures on legal documents
- Lock your files with ransomware and demand payment to get them back

A scammer who gains access to a scanned will does not need to be a sophisticated hacker. Many use off-the-shelf tools that scan thousands of PCs automatically, looking for files with names like "will," "trust," "deed," or "beneficiary." Your PC could be targeted within minutes of connecting to an unsecured network.

The unfortunate truth is that most seniors are running older Windows versions, may have never installed dedicated [antivirus software](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-2026/) beyond the built-in [Windows Defender](https://snipstack.io/windows-defender-download-2026/), and may not recognize warning signs like a suddenly slow computer or unexpected pop-ups. That combination is exactly what cybercriminals count on.

## The Three Biggest Threats to Your Documents

### 1. Ransomware

Ransomware is a type of malicious software that locks every file on your computer and demands a payment — usually in cryptocurrency — before it will unlock them. For someone storing irreplaceable estate documents, a ransomware attack can be catastrophic. Even if you pay, there is no guarantee you will get your files back.

Ransomware typically arrives through email attachments that look legitimate — a fake bank notice, a fake invoice, or even something that appears to come from a government agency. One click on the wrong attachment is all it takes.

### 2. Spyware and Keyloggers

Spyware runs silently in the background of your PC, recording what you type (including passwords and account numbers), taking screenshots, and sending that data back to criminals. A keylogger installed on your computer means that every time you log in to your bank online or type in a Social Security number, that information is captured and transmitted without your knowledge.

### 3. Phishing and Fake Software

Phishing is when criminals send emails or create websites designed to look exactly like your bank, your insurance company, or a government agency. They trick you into entering your login details, which are then stolen. Some phishing attacks also trick you into downloading fake "security software" that is actually malware itself.

Beyond installing antivirus software, a few simple habits dramatically reduce your risk:

1. **Never open email attachments you were not expecting** — even if the sender appears to be your bank or a government agency, call them directly to verify before opening anything.
2. **Keep Windows updated** — Microsoft releases security patches regularly. Go to Settings > Windows Update and make sure automatic updates are turned on.
3. **Use strong, unique passwords** for your email and any accounts where you store or share documents. A password manager can help you keep track of these.
4. **Back up your estate documents** to an external hard drive that is stored offline, and ideally to a secure cloud storage service as well. If ransomware strikes, you will have copies that were not affected.
5. **Lock your documents folder** with a password if your operating system allows it, so that even if malware gets onto your PC, it faces an additional barrier before accessing your most sensitive files.
6. **Install and run a [reputable antivirus](https://snipstack.io/reputable-antivirus-software-2026/)** — ideally one that is always running in the background rather than one you have to remember to launch.

### Can criminals really find my documents just because I store them on my PC?

If your PC becomes infected with malware, yes. Certain types of malware are specifically designed to search for files named with legal or financial terms and either exfiltrate them (send copies to criminals) or encrypt them for ransom. This is why prevention — stopping the malware from getting in at all — is far more effective than trying to recover afterwards.

### Is it safe to store estate documents digitally at all?

Digital storage is convenient and increasingly common, including among legal professionals. The key is protecting those digital copies properly: antivirus software, strong passwords, regular backups, and keeping your operating system updated. Done right, digital storage is not inherently less safe than paper — and in some ways it is more resilient, because you can keep multiple encrypted backup copies.

### What should I do if I think my computer is already infected?

Do not open any sensitive files or log in to any accounts until the issue is resolved. Run a full scan with a reputable antivirus product immediately. If you suspect ransomware, disconnect from the internet (unplug the ethernet cable or turn off Wi-Fi) to stop the spread. Contact a trusted family member, your local library's tech help desk, or a reputable computer repair service for assistance. Most importantly, install antivirus before you need it — recovery after an infection is far harder than prevention.
