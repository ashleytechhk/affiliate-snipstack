## Why Marketplace Listings Destroy Your Inbox

The exposure isn't always direct. Most users assume that if they keep their email off the public listing, they're safe. They're not — for three reasons:

**1. Reply-from exposure.** When you respond to an inquiry from your real inbox, your email client puts your actual address in the "From" field. Even if the buyer only needed to click "Reply," they now have your address in their sent history — and so does any CRM or device sync tool they use.

**2. Platform data breaches and partner sharing.** Marketplace platforms collect your contact details as part of registration. Several major platforms have faced data-exposure incidents over the past few years. Even absent a breach, many platforms share contact data with "trusted advertising partners" under terms most users never read.

**3. Manual harvesting.** On open platforms like Craigslist, contact details in listing bodies are actively scraped by bots looking for email addresses in plaintext. If you wrote "email me at yourname@gmail.com" in the listing body, that address has already been harvested within minutes of posting.

An email alias for marketplace listings breaks all three exposure paths. You reply from the alias. You put the alias in the listing body if needed. If the platform shares contact data, the alias — not your real address — is what circulates.

### Gmail "+" Addressing — Free But Unreliable

Gmail allows you to append a tag to your address: `yourname+marketplace@gmail.com`. Everything still delivers to your inbox, and you can set up filters based on the tag.

**Why it falls short for listings:**

The `+tag` portion is frequently stripped by platform databases and scraper tools before storage, leaving your real address exposed. Even when the tag survives, you can't "delete" it — you can only attempt to filter each new sender individually. If a scraper harvests the plus-address and strips the tag, you've lost visibility into where the spam came from. There's also no kill switch: your real address is always the underlying target.

**When it's acceptable:** Adding light organization to inquiries from a single trusted marketplace you've used for years, where the buyer base is known and resale is unlikely. Not a substitute for a real alias for any public listing.

### Throwaway Services (Guerrilla Mail, Temp-Mail) — Wrong Tool for the Job

Throwaway inboxes generate a temporary address you check through a separate browser tab. They're designed for one-time actions: click a confirmation link and walk away.

**Why they fail for marketplace sales:**

A listing is a multi-day, often multi-week conversation. A buyer might message on day one, ask a follow-up on day three, offer a final price on day five, and request your address to confirm meetup location on day seven. Throwaway inboxes typically expire within one hour to one day. By the time negotiations are serious, your inbox is gone, the buyer can't reach you, and the sale falls through.

There's also no forwarding. You have to actively open the throwaway tab and check it. Most people forget to do this during a busy week. Messages pile up unseen.

**When they're acceptable:** Accessing a gated "how-to-price your listing" guide behind a form. Any context where you need to receive one email, never reply, and walk away. Not for actual buyer-seller communication.

## FAQ

**Will buyers think the alias address looks fake and refuse to contact me?**

No. Alt-ID aliases are standard-format email addresses with no visible markers identifying them as forwarding aliases. Buyers on Facebook Marketplace, eBay, Craigslist, or any other platform see a normal address and interact with it normally. Platform messaging systems that route through their own interface aren't affected at all.

**What if a buyer needs to contact me after the sale to arrange a return or dispute?**

The alias stays active until you delete it. If the item is still in a potential dispute window, keep the alias alive. Once you're satisfied the transaction is closed, delete it. If a legitimate buyer contacts you after deletion, their message will bounce — at that point, they'd need to use any other contact method they have for you (phone, platform messaging, etc.).

**Can I use one alias for multiple listings?**

You can, but it reduces your ability to identify which listing generated the spam. The recommended approach is one alias per listing. When a compromised alias starts attracting junk, you know exactly which listing was the source — and deleting that one alias cleans up the entire exposure without affecting your other active listings.

**Does an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-to-avoid-spam-2026/) stop platform notification emails too?**

Platform notifications (new message alerts, offer summaries, policy updates) from the marketplace itself go to whatever email you registered with. If you registered the platform account with your real address, those notifications still go to your real inbox. The alias controls what outside parties — buyers, scrapers — can reach. To intercept platform notifications too, you'd need to register the marketplace account itself with the alias.

**[What happens](https://snipstack.io/check-if-email-alias-leaked-in-breach-2026/) if a marketplace platform gets breached and my alias is in the leak?**

The alias gets exposed, not your real address. Delete the alias, create a new one, update your marketplace account registration. Your real inbox is unaffected. This is the core advantage of aliases for any platform that stores your contact details: a breach exposes the disposable address, not the one you use for banking, personal correspondence, and everything else that matters.

Yes. [Alt-ID](https://snipstack.io/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-for-marketplace-listings-2026__faq) is accessible through the Surfshark app on both iOS and Android.
