## What Does "Hacked" Actually Mean?

The word "hacked" can sound dramatic, but it simply means that someone has gained access to your computer — or to your accounts on your computer — without your knowledge or permission.

This can happen in several ways:

- **Malware** — short for "malicious software." A program that sneaks onto your computer, often disguised as something harmless. It can spy on you, slow your machine down, or lock your files.
- **Phishing** — a [fake email](https://snipstack.io/fake-email-sender-with-attachment-2026/) or website that tricks you into typing your password. Once the attacker has it, they log into your accounts as if they were you.
- **Remote access tools** — software that lets someone control your screen from far away. Scammers sometimes talk seniors into installing these by pretending to be tech support.
- **Data breaches** — when a company you use (your bank, your email provider) gets attacked and your login details are stolen in bulk.

You do not have to understand every technical detail. What matters is recognizing the signs that something is wrong.

## Warning [Signs Your](https://snipstack.io/signs-your-phone-has-a-virus-2026/) PC May Be Compromised

### 1. Your Computer Is Suddenly Much Slower

Every program running on your PC uses processing power and memory. If a hidden program — like spyware or a cryptocurrency miner — is running secretly in the background, it consumes those resources. The result: everything else runs slower, including your browser and your email.

A slight slowdown after years of use is normal. A sudden, dramatic slowdown that appeared out of nowhere is worth investigating.

### 2. Programs You Don't Recognize Have Appeared

Open your Start menu and look through your installed programs. If you see software you do not remember installing, that is a red flag. Attackers sometimes install tools that help them maintain access to your machine. Scammers who ran a "tech support" call may have left programs behind as well.

### 3. Your Mouse Cursor Moves on Its Own

This is one of the clearest signs. If you watch your mouse move across the screen without you touching it — clicking on things, opening files — someone is controlling your computer remotely. Stop what you are doing, disconnect from the internet (unplug your network cable or turn off Wi-Fi), and call a trusted person for help immediately.

### 4. Your Webcam or Microphone Light Turns On Unexpectedly

Most laptops have a small indicator light that glows when the camera or microphone is active. If that light turns on when you are not on a video call, something — or someone — may be accessing it without your knowledge. This is called "camfecting" and is more common than most people realize.

### 5. You Receive Password Reset Emails You Did Not Request

If your email inbox has messages from Google, your bank, Facebook, or any other service saying "someone requested a password reset" and you did not request one, someone is trying to get into your accounts. Change your passwords immediately, starting with your email account.

### 6. You Are Locked Out of an Account

If you try to log into your email or a website and your correct password no longer works, an attacker may have already changed it. This is an emergency. Use the "Forgot password" or account recovery option right away, or call the service's support line.

## How to Check Your PC — Step by Step

You do not need to be a computer expert to run these checks. Follow each step at your own pace.

### Step 1: Open Task Manager

Task Manager shows you every program currently running on your computer — including ones you cannot see.

1. Press **Ctrl + Shift + Esc** on your keyboard. A window will appear.
2. Click the **Processes** tab.
3. Look through the list. You will see familiar names like "Chrome" or "Microsoft Word." Look for anything with a strange name you do not recognize, especially if it is using a lot of CPU or Memory (shown in the columns on the right).
4. If you spot something suspicious, right-click on it and choose **Search online** — your browser will open and show you what that program is.

Do not close anything unless you are sure it is safe. When in doubt, write down the name and ask someone you trust.

### Step 2: Run Windows Defender

Windows Defender is a [free antivirus](https://snipstack.io/vs-free-antivirus-2026/) tool built into every [Windows 10](https://snipstack.io/windows-10-antivirus-free-download-2026/) and Windows 11 computer. Running a full scan takes about 30–60 minutes but checks every file on your machine.

1. Click the **Start menu** (the Windows logo in the bottom-left corner).
2. Type "[Windows Security](https://snipstack.io/windows-security-2026/)" and press Enter.
3. Click **Virus and threat protection**.
4. Click **Quick scan** for a fast check, or **Scan options** → **Full scan** for a thorough sweep.
5. Wait for it to finish. If it finds anything, follow the on-screen instructions to remove it.

### Step 3: Check Which Programs Connect to the Internet

Hackers need a way to send information out of your computer. A command called `netstat` can show you every active internet connection your PC is making right now.

1. Click the **Start menu** and type **cmd**.
2. Right-click on **Command Prompt** and choose **Run as administrator**.
3. Type the following and press Enter: `netstat -b`
4. A list will appear. Each line shows a connection and the program making it. Look for programs you do not recognize connecting to addresses you have never seen.

This step is a little more advanced. If the list looks confusing, that is okay — take a photo of the screen and ask a family member or call a local tech shop.

### Step 4: Use Sysinternals Tools (Advanced, Optional)

Microsoft offers a free set of tools called Sysinternals. The most useful for this task is **Autoruns**, which shows every program set to start automatically when your computer turns on — a favorite hiding spot for malware.

You can download Autoruns from Microsoft's official website (microsoft.com/sysinternals). Once open, look for entries highlighted in yellow or red, or any entry with a blank or suspicious publisher name. Again, research anything unfamiliar before removing it.

## What to Do If You Find Something

If any of the steps above turns up something suspicious:

1. **Disconnect from the internet** — unplug your Ethernet cable or turn off Wi-Fi. This cuts off the attacker's access.
2. **Do not enter any passwords** on the affected machine until it is cleaned.
3. **Run a full Windows Defender scan** and let it remove what it finds.
4. **Change your passwords from a different device** — a phone or a family member's computer — starting with your email.
5. **Call a trusted professional** — your local library, a family member, or a reputable computer repair shop — if you are not comfortable proceeding alone.
