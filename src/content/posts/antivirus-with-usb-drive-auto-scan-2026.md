## Why USB Drives Are So Dangerous in 2026

### The threat most seniors never hear about

You probably know not to click suspicious emails. You have heard about fake websites. But USB drive threats rarely make the headlines — and that is part of why they are so effective.

Here is how it happens: Someone gives you a USB stick. Maybe it is labeled "Family Photos 2024" or "Holiday Recipes." You plug it in. Within seconds, before you even open a single file, malware on that drive can start copying itself onto your computer. Some types of malware are designed to run the instant any device connects to a computer — this is called an **autorun attack**.

The danger is not just from strangers. A family member's USB drive can unknowingly carry malware from their own computer. A drive you pick up at a pharmacy counter, a library printer, or a community center can carry malware from a previous user. Even a brand-new drive bought from a discount store has, in documented cases, arrived already infected.

A 2023 report from Honeywell's industrial cybersecurity team found that **52 percent of USB drives tested at industrial facilities contained threats** — and those were facilities with IT departments. At home, with no scanning at all, the risk is higher.

### Why "just be careful" is not enough

Many seniors are told to simply be careful about what they plug in. The problem with this advice is that infected drives look completely normal. There is no warning label. The drive works fine. Your photos open. Your documents copy over. Meanwhile, in the background, something has quietly installed itself on your computer.

The only reliable defense is software that scans the drive automatically — before you open anything — so you never have to make a judgment call about whether a drive is safe.

## What USB Drive Auto Scan Actually Does

### The difference between manual and automatic

Most basic antivirus software will catch a virus if you open an infected file. But that is too late for certain threats. **Auto scan** means the antivirus checks the entire contents of a USB drive the moment it connects to your computer — before you click, before you open, before anything runs.

Think of it like this: a manual scan is a guard who searches you after you have already walked into the building. An auto scan is a guard at the door who checks everyone before they enter.

### What auto scan catches

- **Malware** — software designed to damage, spy on, or control your computer
- **Ransomware** — programs that lock your files and demand payment to unlock them
- **Spyware** — programs that record what you type, including passwords and bank details
- **Autorun viruses** — threats that activate automatically the moment a drive connects
- **Trojans** — programs disguised as harmless files that quietly install other threats

## Does Windows Defender Scan USB Drives?

This is one of the most common questions seniors ask, and the answer is: partially.

**Windows Defender** — the free antivirus built into Windows 10 and Windows 11 — will scan files on a USB drive when you try to open them. It also runs periodic background scans that include connected drives.

What it does not do by default: scan a drive **instantly and completely** the moment it connects, before you touch anything.

For standard everyday files — documents, photos, videos — Defender's approach is usually fine. But for autorun malware and certain types of zero-day threats, the gap between "connecting" and "opening a file" is enough time for damage to occur.

This is why dedicated antivirus tools — specifically those advertised as having real-time USB protection or removable media scanning — provide a meaningful extra layer of protection that Defender alone does not.
