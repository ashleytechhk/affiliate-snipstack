## How the SSA Impersonation Scam Works: The Full Playbook

Understanding the sequence helps you recognise it at each stage.

### Phase 1 — The Robocall

An automated recording claims your Social Security number has been linked to criminal activity, drug trafficking, or a fraud case. The message instructs you to call back a specific number or press a key to speak with an "SSA officer." Some versions go straight to a live scammer.

The caller ID may appear to show a Washington DC area code (202) or even the SSA's real number — a technique called caller ID spoofing that costs scammers almost nothing.

### Phase 2 — The Live Scammer

If you engage, a person comes on the line. They have a professional tone, may use your name (gathered from data breaches or public records), and will ask you to "verify your identity" by reading out your Social Security number, date of birth, or bank account details. They may threaten immediate arrest if you hang up.

Some scammers claim your bank accounts are "frozen for your protection" and instruct you to withdraw cash and load it onto gift cards or transfer it to a "safe account."

### Phase 3 — The Follow-Up Email

After the call, many victims receive an email with an official-looking SSA letterhead, a fake case number, and a link to "complete identity verification" or view their "case file." This email is designed to look like legitimate government correspondence.

Clicking that link typically leads to a phishing page that harvests login credentials, or a malware download.

## What the Real SSA Will and Will Not Do

Knowing the SSA's actual policies is the single most powerful defence against impersonation scams. These rules are absolute.

**The SSA will never:**

- Call you to threaten arrest or legal action if you do not comply
- Ask you to confirm your full Social Security number over the phone to "verify your identity"
- Demand payment by gift card, wire transfer, cryptocurrency, or cash
- Suspend your Social Security number (this is not possible — SSNs cannot be suspended)
- Ask you to keep the call secret from family members
- Send you emails with links asking you to verify your account or log in

**The SSA does routinely:**

- Send notices by postal mail
- Call you if you have an existing claim or application in progress — but even then, they will never demand immediate payment or threaten consequences
- Provide a callback number you can verify independently

If a caller claims to be from the SSA and does anything on the "will never" list, the call is fraudulent. End it.

## Step-by-Step: Verifying a Suspicious SSA Contact

### If You Received a Call

**Step 1 — Hang up or ignore.** You are not required to stay on the line. Scammers train their agents to make hanging up feel dangerous ("If you hang up, we will issue a warrant"). This is false. The real SSA has no authority to arrest anyone over the phone.

**Step 2 — Do not call back the number from the call.** Scammers use callback numbers that route back to them, even if the original caller ID looked official.

**Step 3 — Call the SSA directly using a verified number.** The official SSA contact number is 1-800-772-1213 (TTY: 1-800-325-0778), available Monday through Friday, 8 a.m. to 7 p.m. You can find this number on the back of your Medicare card, on SSA.gov, or in any genuine SSA letter you have received. Ask whether there is any issue with your account. There almost certainly will not be.

**Step 4 — Report the scam call.** File a report with the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov and the SSA's Office of the Inspector General at oig.ssa.gov. Reporting takes five minutes and helps investigators track down scam operations.

## If You Already Responded: [What to Do](https://snipstack.io/what-to-do-after-malware-infection-2026/) Now

If you gave out personal information or sent money, act immediately. The steps below are time-sensitive.

**If you shared your Social Security number:** Contact the Social Security Administration (1-800-772-1213) and the three major credit bureaus — Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion — to place a fraud alert or credit freeze on your file. A freeze prevents new accounts from being opened in your name without your explicit authorisation.

**If you sent money via gift card:** Call the gift card company's customer service number (printed on the back of the card) immediately and explain you were scammed. Some companies can freeze the card balance if you act quickly enough. File a report with the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov.

**If you sent money via wire transfer:** Contact your bank the same day. Ask them to reverse or recall the transfer. Recovery is not guaranteed, but acting on the same day significantly improves the chances.

**If you clicked a link and entered your login credentials:** Change that password immediately from a different, trusted device. If you use the same password elsewhere, change it on every account. Enable two-factor authentication on your email account as a priority.

**If you are unsure what you shared:** Write down everything you remember from the call and email while it is fresh. This record will be useful when reporting to the FTC, the SSA's Office of the Inspector General, and local law enforcement.

## Why Seniors Are Specifically Targeted — and How to Stay a Step Ahead

SSA impersonation scams are disproportionately aimed at people over 60. There are practical reasons for this: Social Security benefits are a primary income source for many older adults, making an SSA threat feel more consequential. Retirement assets represent larger potential payouts. And less familiarity with spoofed caller IDs and phishing email techniques means warning signs are easier to miss.

Scammers also spend real effort personalising their approach. Your name, address, and partial account details may already be in circulation from previous data breaches — making the scammer's pitch feel uncomfortably specific and credible.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does the Social Security Administration ever call people?

The SSA does make outbound calls in limited circumstances — for example, to follow up on an existing claim or application. However, they will never call to report that your SSN has been "suspended," to demand immediate payment, or to threaten arrest. If you receive an unexpected SSA call that involves any of those elements, it is a scam. Call 1-800-772-1213 directly to verify whether there is any genuine activity on your account.

### How can I tell if a Social Security email is real?

Genuine SSA correspondence by email is rare and always comes from an `@ssa.gov` address. The email will not contain urgent threats, demands for immediate payment, or links asking you to "verify your identity" on a third-party site. If you receive an email claiming to be from the SSA with a link or attachment, copy the link and check it with [Surfshark's Email Scam Checker](/blog/scamcheck?sub=social-security-scam-call-verify-2026__faq) before you click anything. If the sender domain is anything other than `ssa.gov`, delete it.

### Can scammers actually suspend my Social Security number?

No. Social Security numbers cannot be suspended. This claim — a core part of most SSA impersonation scripts — is entirely fabricated. The SSA has no mechanism to suspend a Social Security number, and no government agency can freeze your benefits by phone without written notice sent by mail.

### What should I do if I suspect my personal information is already in circulation?

Place a fraud alert or credit freeze with all three major credit bureaus (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion). Review your Social Security statement at SSA.gov to check for any unfamiliar earnings records or benefit changes. ### Is there a way to report SSA scam calls?

Yes. Report to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov and to the SSA's Office of the Inspector General at oig.ssa.gov/report. You can also report by phone to the OIG hotline at 1-800-269-0271. Reporting does not cost anything and helps law enforcement track scam operations. If you lost money, also file a local police report — some banks require this for fraud recovery.
