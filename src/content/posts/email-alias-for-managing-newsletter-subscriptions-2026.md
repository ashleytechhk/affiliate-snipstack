## Why Unsubscribing Doesn't Fix the Root Problem

Clicking "unsubscribe" on a legitimate newsletter usually works — it removes you from that one list. The problem is [what happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) after your address has already been shared, resold, or scraped by a data broker somewhere along the way. There's no unsubscribe link for that. New spam sources keep appearing under names you don't recognize, and each one requires a separate manual action.

Bulk-unsubscribe tools like Unroll.me or Clean Email speed up the manual process — they scan your inbox, group senders, and let you mass-unsubscribe in a few clicks. That's genuinely useful for a one-time cleanup. But it treats the symptom. Your real address is still out there, still discoverable, still available to be sold to the next mailing list. Six months later, you're running the same cleanup again.

The structural fix is to stop handing out your real address for newsletter sign-ups in the first place.

## The Gmail "+" Trick: Free but Ineffective for Spam

Gmail lets you sign up as `yourname+news@gmail.com`, and the email still lands in your normal inbox. You can filter by the "To:" field to sort newsletters automatically as they arrive.

It looks like an alias, but it isn't a privacy tool. Anyone receiving `yourname+news@gmail.com` can strip the `+news` portion and land directly on your real address — most bulk mailing software does this automatically, intentionally or not. There's also no way to "delete" the variation; it will always deliver mail to your inbox. If a newsletter list gets sold, the spam keeps arriving regardless of what you do.

Use the "+" trick for sorting the newsletters you already receive. Don't rely on it to stop new spam from arriving.

## Provider Aliases: Outlook and iCloud

**Outlook** lets you add up to 10 aliases to one Microsoft account, all delivering to the same inbox. You can deactivate an alias, but you can't fully delete it — and you're stuck inside the Outlook ecosystem if you ever want to switch email providers.

**iCloud Hide My Email** (bundled with iCloud+) generates random, non-guessable addresses that forward to your iCloud inbox. This is a solid implementation — you can delete an alias outright, and no one can trace it back to your real address. The tradeoff is the same lock-in: your alias system only works as long as you stay on iCloud.

Both are decent choices if you're already committed to one platform. Neither helps if you want an alias system that survives a future email provider switch.

## Setting Up a Newsletter-Only Alias System

Whichever tool you pick, the workflow that actually works looks like this:

**Step 1 — Create one alias just for newsletters.** Don't reuse it for shopping or account sign-ups; keep the category clean so you can spot leaks fast.

**Step 2 — Use it for every newsletter sign-up going forward.** Blog subscriptions, product updates, "10% off your first order" pop-ups — all of it goes through the alias, never your real address.

**Step 3 — Watch the "To:" field.** If your newsletter alias starts receiving anything that isn't a newsletter you actually signed up for, that's your signal the list got sold or scraped.

**Step 4 — Delete and recreate.** One click, and the spam stops. Create a fresh alias, resubscribe to the newsletters you actually want to keep, and move on.

## FAQ

**Will using an alias stop me from receiving newsletters I actually want?**

No. Aliases forward mail exactly like your real address does — you'll receive every newsletter you subscribe to, just routed through an address that isn't your primary inbox. The only difference is you control whether that address stays active.

**What happens to old emails if I delete a newsletter alias?**

Anything already delivered stays in your inbox. Deleting the alias only stops future mail sent to that address — it doesn't touch your existing inbox history.

**Is a paid alias tool actually worth it over free bulk-unsubscribe apps?**

For a one-time cleanup, a free bulk-unsubscribe tool works fine. For ongoing prevention, less so — those tools don't hide your real address, so new newsletter spam tends to reappear after every fresh cleanup. A dedicated alias tool aims to prevent the problem instead of repeatedly cleaning it up.

**Can I use the same alias for every newsletter, or do I need one per site?**

One category alias (such as "newsletters") works for most people and keeps things simple. If you want to identify exactly which site leaked your address, use one alias per site instead — more setup, more precise forensics.

**Do newsletter senders know I'm using an alias?**

Not typically. A properly masked alias looks like a normal email address to the sender. There's no visible indicator that it forwards elsewhere, and reply-masking (available with Alt-ID and SimpleLogin) keeps it that way even if you reply.

**Will any legitimate newsletters reject an alias address at sign-up?**

Occasionally. A small number of sign-up forms block known alias domains, mostly to prevent trial abuse. If that happens, you'll need to use your real address for that specific newsletter — treat it as an exception, not the rule.
