## Why Retargeting Follows You Home From One Purchase

Retargeting doesn't need cookies alone anymore. Most large retailers upload your email address (hashed, but still matchable) to ad platforms like Meta's Custom Audiences or Google's Customer Match. That hash gets compared against the ad platform's own database of hashed emails tied to your social accounts. If they match, you're now a "custom audience" — and every site running ads on that network can potentially target you, not just the original store.

This is why the shoes follow you to unrelated websites, not just the retailer's own homepage. Your email address is the thread connecting your shopping history to your social media identity.

**The practical fix:** use an email address at checkout that has no relationship to the address tied to your social accounts. If the checkout email and your "real" identity graph never intersect, the matching pipeline has nothing to hash and compare.

That's simple in theory. In practice, most disposable options break down the moment you need a return, a warranty claim, or a shipping update. Here's how the main options actually hold up.

## Option 1: Throwaway Inboxes (10 Minute Mail, Guerrilla Mail, Temp Mail)

These services generate a random address that lives for a short window — sometimes ten minutes, sometimes 24 hours — then disappears forever. They're genuinely useful for one thing: verifying a signup you'll never use again, like downloading a PDF that's gated behind an email form.

For online shopping, they're close to useless. Order confirmations, tracking numbers, and delivery updates arrive over days, not minutes. By the time your package ships, the inbox is gone and so is your proof of purchase. If anything goes wrong with the order, you have no paper trail.

**Verdict:** good for one-off downloads, bad for anything you plan to actually buy.

---

## Option 2: The Gmail Dot and Plus Trick

Gmail ignores dots in the local part of an address and treats anything after a `+` as a tag, so `yourname@gmail.com`, `your.name@gmail.com`, and `yourname+shop1@gmail.com` all land in the same inbox. This lets you generate near-infinite variations without creating new accounts.

It's free and easy, but it has a well-known weakness: plenty of retailers and ad platforms strip everything after the `+` before hashing the address, which reconnects it to your base account. It's a decent inbox-organization trick — not a real retargeting shield.
