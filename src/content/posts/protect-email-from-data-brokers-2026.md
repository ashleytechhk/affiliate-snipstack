## What Is a Data Broker?

A data broker is a company whose primary business is collecting, aggregating, and selling personal information. They don't sell you products or services. *You* are the product.

Data brokers pull from a wide range of sources:

- **Public records** — voter registrations, property records, court filings
- **Commercial transactions** — loyalty card programs, warranty registrations, purchase histories
- **Online behavior** — website tracking pixels, cookies, social media public profiles
- **App data** — mobile apps that share location, device IDs, or contact lists with third-party data platforms
- **Third-party list sales** — other companies that sell the email addresses of their own customers or users

The result is a profile that includes your name, email address, physical address, [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/), estimated income, purchasing habits, and more. That profile gets sold to marketers, advertisers, and anyone else who buys targeted contact lists.

There are hundreds of data brokers operating legally in the US and EU. The largest — Acxiom, Epsilon, LexisNexis, Experian Marketing Services — process billions of records. Smaller brokers buy from the larger ones and resell at a markup. By the time a marketing agency buys a list with your address, it may have passed through four or five different brokers.

## Why Your Inbox Is the Main Victim

Email is the most valuable piece of contact information a data broker can sell. It's the one channel that reaches you directly, doesn't cost the sender per-message at scale, and is almost impossible for you to permanently abandon.

Every time your address changes hands, you get added to another sender's rotation. That sender may comply with unsubscribe requests — or may not. Either way, the copy of your address that was sold to the *next* broker doesn't disappear when you unsubscribe. Opt-outs are per-sender, not per-database.

This is why clicking unsubscribe rarely solves the long-term problem. You remove yourself from one list while your address continues circulating in the data-broker ecosystem, generating new subscriptions faster than you can remove old ones.

## [How Data Brokers Get Your Email Address](https://snipstack.io/how-data-brokers-get-your-email-address-2026/) in the First Place

Understanding the entry points is the first step to closing them. The most common ways data brokers acquire email addresses:

**Sign-up forms with buried data-sharing clauses.** Many free services — weather apps, recipe sites, coupon aggregators — sell their user database as a revenue stream. The data-sharing terms are in the privacy policy, which most users don't read. You sign up for a free recipe app; your email is sold within 30 days.

**Data breaches and dark-web markets.** Breached databases from retailers, forums, and healthcare companies get purchased by data brokers who verify and "clean" the lists before reselling them. A breach you experienced three years ago may still be generating spam today because the data continues circulating.

**Partner networks and co-registration.** When you opt into marketing emails from one company, the fine print sometimes includes consent for "partners" to contact you. Those partners are often data networks with dozens of client brands. One opt-in triggers contact from fifteen senders.

**Social media scraping.** Public-facing email addresses on LinkedIn profiles, Twitter/X bios, Facebook business pages, and personal websites get harvested automatically by scraper bots. If your email is visible anywhere public, it's already been collected.

**Lead-generation forms.** Contest entries, giveaways, free quote requests, and comparison sites are often operated by lead-gen companies whose core business is collecting email addresses to sell, not delivering the stated service.

## Other Ways to Reduce Data Broker Exposure

Alt-ID handles the forward-looking problem — new signups, new forms, new data collection. For existing exposure, a few additional steps help:

**Opt-out from major brokers directly.** Data brokers operating in the US are required to provide opt-out mechanisms under various state privacy laws. Services like DeleteMe or Privacy Bee automate removal requests. This reduces (but rarely eliminates) existing exposure over several months.

**Use a password manager with alias support.** Pair Alt-ID with a password manager (1Password, Bitwarden) and store the alias alongside the password for each service. You'll always know which alias to use when logging back in.

**Audit new signups before submitting.** Before entering your email on any new form, ask: does this service need to reach me again? If not, use a throwaway. If yes, use a labeled Alt-ID alias so you can trace and terminate it later.

**Review app permissions.** Mobile apps with contact list or email permissions can share your address with analytics and ad networks. Audit which apps have contact access and revoke it where unnecessary.

## FAQ

**What exactly is a data broker and why do they have my email?**

A data broker is a company that collects personal information from public records, commercial transactions, online behavior, and other sources, then sells that data to marketers and other buyers. Your email address likely entered their databases through a combination of sign-up forms, data breaches, and partner networks. You don't have a direct relationship with them — your data was acquired through intermediaries. [Alt-ID](/blog/altid?sub=protect-email-from-data-brokers-2026__faq) prevents your real address from entering new data-broker pipelines by providing aliases instead.

**Can I get my email removed from data broker lists?**

You can submit opt-out requests to individual brokers, and many are legally required to honor them. The challenge is volume — there are hundreds of data brokers, and manual opt-outs are time-consuming. Third-party removal services automate this process. Even after removal, your address can be re-added if you continue using it for new signups. That's why forward-looking protection (using aliases) matters more than reactive removal.

**Does unsubscribing from spam stop data brokers from selling my address?**

No. Unsubscribing removes you from one sender's mailing list. The copy of your address held by data brokers is not affected. That address continues to be bought and sold, generating new subscriptions you'll need to unsubscribe from again. Unsubscribing treats symptoms. Aliases treat the cause.

**Will using an alias email address cause problems with account verification or recovery?**

No, as long as you keep the alias active for accounts you care about. Verification emails, password resets, and account notifications all forward to your real inbox. The important habit: don't delete an alias for an active account without first updating that account to a new alias or your real email. For accounts you no longer use, deletion is fine — you won't need to recover them.

**How is Alt-ID different from Gmail's `+` trick or a [disposable email](https://snipstack.io/disposable-email-that-receives-messages-2026/)?**

Gmail's `yourname+tag@gmail.com` format is widely filtered by marketers — many platforms strip the `+tag` before storing the address, so your real address gets recorded anyway. [Disposable email](https://snipstack.io/disposable-email-address-for-trials-2026/)s (Guerrilla Mail, 10 Minute Mail) expire after minutes or hours and are typically on public blocklists, causing signup rejections. Alt-ID provides persistent, real-looking aliases on a non-blocklisted domain, that you control and can delete at any time. It's the middle ground: as functional as your real address, as disposable as a throwaway.

**How many aliases does Alt-ID allow?**

You can go more granular (one alias per service) if you want forensic-level identification of exactly which service leaked your address.
