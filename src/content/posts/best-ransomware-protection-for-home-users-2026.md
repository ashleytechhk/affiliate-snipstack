## What Is Ransomware? (Plain English Explanation)

Ransomware is a type of malicious software — often called "malware" — that sneaks onto your computer and then locks your files so you cannot open them. The criminals who sent it then demand money (a "ransom") to unlock everything. If you do not pay, they may delete your files permanently.

Here is how ransomware typically gets onto a home computer:

- **Clicking a link in a suspicious email** — An email claims to be from your bank, a delivery company, or even a government office. One click on the wrong link and the download begins without you noticing.
- **Opening an attachment** — A file attached to an email (often pretending to be an invoice, a photo, or a document) runs hidden software when you open it.
- **Visiting a compromised website** — Some legitimate-looking websites have been secretly infected and can download ransomware onto your computer just from a visit.
- **Fake software updates** — A pop-up warns you that your software is out of date and asks you to click "Update Now." The update is actually malware.

Once ransomware is on your computer, it works fast — often locking thousands of files in minutes before you even realize something is wrong.

## Why Seniors Are Targeted More Often

Cybercriminals are calculated. They choose their victims based on who is most likely to pay and least likely to recover files on their own.

Seniors are frequently targeted for a few specific reasons:

**Unfamiliarity with online threats.** If you did not grow up with computers, it can be hard to spot a suspicious email from a real one. Scammers design their fake messages to look completely official.

**Valuable, irreplaceable files.** Older adults often store decades of family photos, scanned documents, and personal records on their computers — files with enormous emotional value that they would pay to recover.

**Less likely to have backups.** Younger users may automatically back up files to the cloud, but many seniors store everything only on their main computer.

**Slower to act on warnings.** If a security alert pops up, an unfamiliar user might dismiss it, not knowing what it means. Ransomware exploits exactly these moments.

Understanding why you are a target is the first step to not becoming a victim.

## Simple Habits That Make Ransomware Protection Even Stronger

Even with great antivirus software running, a few simple habits dramatically reduce your risk:

**Think before you click.** If an email arrives from your bank or a delivery company asking you to click a link urgently, stop. Call the company directly using the number on their official website — not any number in the email.

**Never open unexpected attachments.** If you were not expecting a file, do not open it. Even if the email appears to come from someone you know, their account may have been hacked.

**Keep Windows or macOS updated.** Software updates often fix security holes that ransomware exploits. Turn on automatic updates if you have not already.

**Back up important files.** An external hard drive or a simple cloud storage account (like iCloud Photos for your pictures) means that even if ransomware strikes, you have copies. Your antivirus software is your first line of defense; a backup is your safety net.

**Do not pay ransom.** If the worst happens and ransomware locks your files, do not pay. Contact a local computer technician or call your country's cybercrime reporting line first. Paying does not guarantee file recovery and encourages further attacks.
