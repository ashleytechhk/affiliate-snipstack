## What is a VPN?

A VPN — virtual private network — creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a server operated by the VPN provider. Every website you visit, every app that phones home, every search query travels through that tunnel. The Wi-Fi router at the coffee shop, your internet provider, and any surveillance tool sitting on the network between you and your destination sees only encrypted noise, not your actual activity.

A VPN does three things in practice:

**Hides your real IP address.** Websites see the IP address of the VPN server, not your home or office address. This limits the fingerprinting advertisers and data brokers use to track you across sites.

**Encrypts traffic on untrusted networks.** Hotel Wi-Fi, airport lounges, cafe hotspots — these are the environments where unencrypted connections are genuinely risky. A VPN makes interception on those networks pointless because the captured data is unreadable without the decryption key.

**Prevents your ISP from logging your browsing.** Internet providers in many countries can legally record and sell browsing histories. An active VPN connection removes that visibility.

What a VPN does not do: it does not scan your device for malware, and it does not tell you if your password was stolen in a breach at a company you signed up with years ago. That second gap is exactly what dark web monitoring fills.

## What is Dark Web Monitoring?

The dark web is a part of the internet accessible only through specialist software. It is where criminals trade stolen data: email addresses, passwords, credit card numbers, [social security](https://snipstack.io/social-security-scam-call-verify-2026/) numbers, passport scans. When a company suffers a data breach, the stolen records frequently end up listed for sale on dark web marketplaces.

Dark web monitoring is a service that continually scans those marketplaces and breach databases. When your email address or other monitored credentials appear in a newly discovered dataset, the service sends you an alert. That alert is your window to act — change the compromised password, lock the affected account, notify your bank — before someone else does.

![How dark web monitoring works — from breach to alert to action](/og/vpn-plus-dark-web-monitoring-bundle-2026-inline-1.png)

A few important clarifications about what dark web monitoring actually does:

**It monitors, it does not remove.** An alert tells you that your data was found in a breach database. It does not delete the data from the dark web, because no tool can do that reliably. The value is early warning, not erasure.

**It covers known breaches.** Services scan databases that have been indexed or discovered. A fresh breach that has not yet surfaced publicly will not trigger an alert until the data is found and catalogued — typically days or weeks after the initial incident.

**It is credential-focused.** Email addresses, passwords, credit card numbers, and identity document numbers are the primary targets. It does not monitor whether someone has physically stolen your identity or opened a fraudulent account in your name — that is the territory of full [identity theft protection](https://snipstack.io/identity-theft-protection-software-2026/) services, which are a separate and more expensive category.

For most people, those limitations are not dealbreakers. The majority of credential theft begins with an old breach from a service you signed up for years ago and promptly forgot. Dark web monitoring is the service that tells you it happened so you can do something about it.
