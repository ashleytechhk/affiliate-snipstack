## Why Dating Apps Are a Spam Machine

Dating platforms are structurally incentivised to keep you engaged — and to monetise your data while doing it.

**Notification volume by design.** "Someone liked you." "Your profile was viewed 14 times." "New matches nearby." Dating apps turn these triggers into email campaigns because re-engagement drives premium conversions. Even after you've set preferences, the emails keep coming, because the marketing team's incentives don't align with yours.

**Data partnerships and ad targeting.** Most major dating apps operate under privacy policies that permit sharing anonymised user data — including email metadata — with advertising partners. Your address ends up in audience segments for relationship products, self-improvement services, and adjacent subscriptions you never asked for.

**Third-party integrations.** Spotify linking for music taste. Instagram photo imports. Facebook login. Google authentication. Each integration creates another data-sharing surface between your identity and a commercial partner's tracking infrastructure.

**Data breaches.** Dating apps are high-value targets. Platforms holding relationship intent data, location history, and personal photos are attractive to attackers and brokers alike. A breach that exposes email addresses puts those addresses into circulation on spam and phishing lists for years.

**Account deletion doesn't clean up the data.** When you delete a dating profile, most platforms retain your data for an extended period under their retention policies. The email address you signed up with can remain in marketing lists long after you've left the platform.

An alias solves all of this at the source. Your real address never touches the platform's database. If the platform is breached, the leaked address is an alias you can delete in one click.

## Why the Gmail Plus-Trick Fails for Dating

The `+tag` approach is popular because it requires no new tools. The problem is structural: it provides the appearance of separation without the reality.

When you register as `yourname+hinge@gmail.com`, your base address — `yourname@gmail.com` — is technically derivable by anyone who receives the tagged version. Dating apps pass your email to marketing platforms, analytics tools, and in some cases advertising networks. Any of those systems can strip the tag. Your real address gets added to lists, not the alias.

There is also no revocation. If you start receiving spam at `yourname+hinge@gmail.com`, the only way to stop it is to filter messages in Gmail — which is maintenance, not a solution — or change your Gmail address entirely. You cannot delete the alias the way you can delete an Alt-ID alias, because it's not a real separate address. It's a convention your inbox applies to incoming mail.

For a use case where you want to be able to permanently sever an address from an app's marketing infrastructure, `+tags` provide no real capability.
