## How Substack Subscriptions Lead to Spam

Substack positions itself as a clean newsletter platform, but several real-world failure modes expose subscriber addresses:

**1. Writers migrate off Substack.** When a writer exports their subscriber list and moves to Mailchimp, Beehiiv, or a proprietary ESP, they take your email address with them. Their new platform may have looser re-engagement practices, and their new advertisers often receive broad access to the list.

**2. Writers sell or abandon their publication.** Substack publications have been sold to new owners who had no relationship with the original subscribers. The new owner inherits the list — and your address.

**3. Third-party sponsor activations.** A writer's "brand partner" mention in an issue can include pixel tracking or list-sharing arrangements that are buried in fine print.

**4. Your email address becomes a known quantity.** The more places you sign up with the same address, the more likely it lands in a data broker's aggregated profile. Substack subscriptions add up.

Using a dedicated alias per newsletter (or at minimum one alias for all Substack activity) breaks this exposure chain.

### 2. Apple Hide My Email

Apple's iCloud+ feature generates random `@icloud.com` forwarding addresses. If you already pay for iCloud storage (50 GB and above), you have access at no extra cost.

**Strengths for Substack:** Works natively on iPhone and Safari, including inside the iOS Substack app. Apple integrates it into the keyboard — tap your email field and "Hide My Email" appears as a suggestion.

**Limitations:** You cannot use it outside the Apple ecosystem. Managing aliases requires the Settings app or iCloud.com. If you read Substack on a Windows machine or Android phone, the extension is not available. There is also no fake-identity generator — Apple gives you a masked address but not a masked name.

**Verdict:** Excellent if your Substack reading is entirely on Apple devices. Limited if you are cross-platform.

---

### 3. Firefox Relay

Mozilla's alias service offers five permanent forwarding aliases on the free tier, with unlimited aliases on the Premium plan ($USD 1.99/month). It has a Firefox extension that auto-suggests an alias when it detects an email field.

**Strengths:** The free tier is genuinely useful if you only subscribe to a handful of newsletters. Firefox Relay also strips known tracking pixels from forwarded emails — a meaningful privacy bonus that none of the other tools on this list offer.

**Limitations:** The five-alias cap on the free tier runs out fast for active newsletter readers. The browser extension is Firefox-only, so Chrome or Safari users get less friction-free integration. Aliases cannot be reactivated once deleted — you can only create new ones.

**Verdict:** A solid free option for light Substack users with three to five subscriptions. Heavy newsletter readers will hit the free cap quickly.

---

### 4. SimpleLogin

An open-source alias service with a free tier of 15 aliases and an unlimited paid plan at around $4/month. Acquired by Proton in 2022 and well-regarded in the privacy community.

**Strengths:** Works with any email provider, has browser extensions for Chrome and Firefox, and offers two-way alias support — meaning you can reply *from* your alias address, keeping your real address hidden even in outgoing mail. Useful if you ever reply to a Substack writer and do not want your real address in the From field.

**Limitations:** The dashboard is more technical than Alt-ID or Apple's offering. No fake-identity generator. The 15-alias free tier is more generous than Firefox Relay but still finite.

**Verdict:** Strong choice for technically-inclined privacy users. The two-way alias is a genuine differentiator that the others lack.

---

### 5. DuckDuckGo Email Protection

DuckDuckGo's free forwarding service gives you a `@duck.com` address. Unlimited aliases. Every forwarded email has tracking pixels removed before it reaches your inbox.

**Strengths:** Completely free, no account sign-up required beyond a DuckDuckGo email address. The tracker-removal feature is the strongest on this list for privacy-conscious readers.

**Limitations:** You cannot delete a specific alias — you can only pause it. Spam that arrives on a paused alias is bounced, but the address still "exists" and can be unpaused. If a sender is determined, the pathway is not fully closed the way a deleted alias is. No fake-identity generator. Browser extension is available but not as polished as competitors.

**Verdict:** Best for tracker blocking. Weaker for the core Substack use case of permanently killing a compromised address.

---

### 6. Gmail `+` Trick

Gmail allows `youraddress+anything@gmail.com` variants that all deliver to your real inbox. You could use `yourname+substack@gmail.com` for all Substack subscriptions.

**Why it falls short:** Marketers and spam operators are fully aware of this pattern. Many email forms strip the `+tag` suffix before storing the address, meaning the variation is lost and the sender sees your real address. More importantly, anyone who receives `yourname+substack@gmail.com` can trivially derive `yourname@gmail.com`. There is no privacy, no deletion capability, and no barrier once the real address is known.

**Verdict:** Zero effort, zero protection. Not recommended for anything beyond casual experimentation.

## FAQ

**Can I use an email alias on Substack without creating a Substack account?**

Yes. Substack allows newsletter subscriptions via email without a full account sign-up. Enter your alias address in the subscribe form. Confirmation and issues arrive as normal emails in your real inbox. Your alias is what Substack and the writer see — not your personal address.

**[What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) if a Substack writer I like migrates to another platform?**

If they export their list and move to Mailchimp or Beehiiv, your alias migrates with you in their system. You keep receiving their emails. If their new platform starts spamming you with ads or partner content, you delete the alias — cutting the connection without any interaction with the writer or their new provider.

**Will an alias address work with Substack's paid subscriptions?**

Yes. Substack's payment flow is tied to the email address you subscribe with. Your alias forwards confirmation emails and receipts to your real inbox. Payment is handled through Stripe or your card, which is separate from your email address. You can subscribe, pay, and receive paid content entirely through an alias. [Alt-ID](/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-for-substack-subscription-2026__faq) works the same way as a real address from the newsletter platform's perspective.

**Can I switch from my real email address to an alias on an existing Substack subscription?**

Yes. Log into Substack and update your account email to your alias address. All future emails from Substack and that writer will go to the alias and forward to your real inbox. You are not unsubscribing and resubscribing — you are updating the address on the existing subscription.

**Does Alt-ID work on mobile when subscribing through the Substack app?**

The Surfshark browser extension is a desktop tool (Chrome/Firefox). On mobile, you would manually copy your alias address from the Surfshark app and paste it into the Substack subscribe form. It takes an extra step but works on both iOS and Android. Apple Hide My Email is the only tool with native mobile OS integration at the email-field level.

**How many Substack newsletters does the average reader subscribe to?**

According to Substack's own published data, the average paying subscriber follows multiple publications. Active readers often track 10 to 20 newsletters across different writers. That is 10 to 20 sign-ups with your email address — each a potential leak point. One shared Substack alias, or one alias per writer, keeps all of that exposure siloed and revocable.
