## Why Norton Users Are Looking for Alternatives

Norton built its reputation over decades of antivirus work, and the core malware detection engine is still competent. The problem is the product strategy around it.

After Broadcom's acquisition of Symantec's enterprise business and subsequent restructuring, Norton's consumer products leaned harder into subscription lock-in. The first-year promotional rate hooks you. Year two, the renewal hits your card automatically at the standard rate — often three to four times higher.

The VPN situation is frustrating for a different reason. Norton Secure VPN is included in 360 Deluxe, but it has historically lacked features that VPN-first providers treat as table stakes: split tunneling, WireGuard protocol support, and a reliable kill switch across all platforms. For a power user who wants VPN for actual privacy rather than a marketing checkbox, it falls short.

Then there is the interface itself. Norton's app surfaces upsell prompts regularly — upgrade to LifeLock, add dark web monitoring, try the password manager. Each one is a real product, but the net effect is an experience that feels more like a subscription upsell funnel than a security tool.
