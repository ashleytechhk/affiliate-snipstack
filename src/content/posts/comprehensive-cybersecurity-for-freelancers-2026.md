## What Comprehensive Cybersecurity for Freelancers Actually Requires

Before comparing products, it is worth being specific about the threat model. Freelancers face a different set of risks than either a home user or a corporate employee.

**Client data liability.** You hold contracts, NDAs, financial documents, and sometimes login credentials for client systems. A data breach that exposes that material does not just inconvenience you — it damages the client relationship and may carry legal consequences depending on what you signed.

**Public Wi-Fi exposure.** Cafes, co-working spaces, airport lounges, hotel lobbies — freelancers use these constantly. Unencrypted connections on shared networks expose every piece of data your machine transmits.

**Competitive intelligence risk.** When you research a client's competitors, browse job boards, or look into a prospect's business, that search history is associated with your IP. On an unprotected connection, it can also be visible to the network.

**Credential theft.** Freelancers juggle more accounts than most — multiple client portals, project management tools, invoicing platforms, cloud storage. More accounts mean more exposure when any one of those services gets breached.

**Device blurring.** Personal streaming, work email, and client file access often happen on the same machine. There is no IT policy separating the risk zones.

A [comprehensive security](https://snipstack.io/comprehensive-security-for-seniors-online-2026/) setup for a freelancer needs to address all of these — not just malware.

Most freelancers who have [antivirus software](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-for-iphone-2026/) are running Norton 360, McAfee Total Protection, or a similar legacy suite. These products were built for a different era of threats, and their commercial model has drifted significantly from their original value proposition.

Here is what that drift looks like in practice.

**You are paying full renewal price on a product designed around introductory pricing.** Norton and McAfee set headline prices low to attract subscribers, then step up aggressively at renewal. The gap between year-one pricing and year-two renewal is commonly 150 to 300 percent. You noticed this. It is why you are reading this article.

**The VPN that came with your suite is not a real VPN.** Norton 360's base tier includes a VPN capped at 200MB per day. That is enough for roughly one brief email check. As a freelancer connecting from external locations daily, that cap is meaningless. A full VPN without data restrictions is either sold as an add-on or requires a more expensive plan tier.

**Scans slow your machine at the worst moments.** Norton and McAfee scheduled scans routinely spike CPU usage to 50–80 percent for extended periods. On a mid-range laptop during a client call or while running multiple apps, that is a forced performance penalty you cannot opt out of.

**The dashboard exists to sell you things.** Every feature you do not have appears in the interface as a prompt to upgrade. On products like Norton and McAfee, the app itself functions partly as a storefront.

None of this means these products fail at malware detection. It means you are paying a substantial premium for a distribution model that does not serve freelancers particularly well.

### VPN: The Wi-Fi Safety Layer You Actually Need

When you connect from a co-working space or a hotel, the network operator can see which sites your devices are communicating with unless your traffic is encrypted. A VPN creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and the internet, so the coffee shop router sees nothing useful.

For freelancers, the VPN in One matters for three distinct reasons:

**IP privacy from clients and competitors.** When researching a prospect's business, pricing, or competitive landscape, your IP address is visible to the sites you visit. Routing through a VPN server keeps your research private from people paying attention to their server logs.

**Regional access for software pricing.** Design tools, project management platforms, and creative software subscriptions often have regional pricing. A VPN lets you check pricing in different markets — useful when renewing annual tool subscriptions.
