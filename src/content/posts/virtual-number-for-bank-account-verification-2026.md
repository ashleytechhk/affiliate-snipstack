## The Link Between Dating Apps and Your Bank Account

Most people think about phone number privacy in two separate categories: the number they share with dates, and the number their bank uses for verification. In practice, these are almost always the same number.

Here's why that matters.

When you sign up for Tinder, Hinge, or Bumble, the app asks you to verify your phone number via SMS. You enter your real number. The app stores it. From that point forward, your primary contact number — the same one your bank sends one-time passcodes to — exists in that dating app's database.

Dating app databases get breached. In 2021, data from approximately 2.28 million MeetMindful users was published on a hacker forum, including phone numbers. Dozens of smaller apps have experienced similar exposures. Beyond outright breaches, dating apps embed third-party analytics and advertising SDKs that can share device and contact identifiers with ad networks. And millions of people hand their real number directly to matches within days of meeting them.

Every one of those exposure points is also an exposure point for the phone number sitting between a criminal and your bank account.

## How SIM-Swap Turns a Leaked Number Into a Financial Attack

SIM-swap fraud is the attack that closes the gap between "someone has my phone number" and "someone has access to my bank account."

The attack works like this. A fraudster who has your phone number contacts your mobile carrier, claims to be you, and convinces them to transfer your number to a SIM card they control. The carrier sends the confirmation to their device. Every SMS your carrier routes to your number — including your bank's verification codes — now goes to them.

From that point, resetting passwords on your bank account, your email, and any other service that uses SMS authentication is trivial. The average SIM-swap attack, once the number is in hand, takes under two hours to complete.

The phone number they need to start that attack? The one you verified your dating app with three months ago.

## The Data Broker Step Most People Skip Over

You don't need a dramatic breach for your number to circulate. The quieter, more systematic path is through data brokers — and it starts the moment you hand your number to a dating app.

Dating apps monetize through advertising. They integrate third-party SDKs from ad networks and analytics platforms. Those SDKs collect device identifiers and, in many cases, hashed or partial contact data. Ad networks aggregate this across thousands of apps and cross-reference it with public records, loyalty program data, and retail signups to build enriched profiles. Those profiles are sold to data brokers. Data brokers sell them to marketers — and to people-search sites that anyone can query for $1.99.

Enter your phone number into any major people-search service right now. If it's your real primary number, there's a reasonable chance it returns your full name, your approximate home address, your relatives' names, and associated email addresses. Some services add employer information and social profiles.

That's the lookup file anyone motivated to find you can pull together from a number you gave to a dating app.

## Why Standard Workarounds Don't Solve This

### Giving Out Your Number Only to Matches You Trust

The problem is that "matches you trust" is determined after you've already handed over your number. By the time a match has revealed concerning behavior, your number is in their phone. Blocking someone on the app doesn't remove your number from their contacts — and your real number is now permanently in their possession, tied to every platform that uses it.

### Using Google Voice

Google Voice is a workable free option for US residents. It gives you a separate number you can hand out instead of your primary line. The limitations: it's US-only, requires an active Google account (creating another connection between your identity and the alias), and has historically been unreliable for SMS verification on dating apps that run VoIP detection. If you're outside the United States, it's not available at all.

### Just Blocking Spam Calls

Blocking individual numbers is entirely reactive and addresses the symptom rather than the source. The underlying exposure — your real number in databases, linked to your identity and to your bank's 2FA — persists regardless of how many numbers you block.
