## Quick Verdict

The most effective combination is to report in two places: your email client (Gmail or Outlook has a one-click button that improves their filters immediately) and the Anti-Phishing Working Group at reportphishing@apwg.org (which feeds global security databases). If the scam impersonates a specific brand — your bank, a retailer, a delivery company — add a third report directly to that brand's abuse team. For phishing websites that are still live, submitting the URL to Google Safe Browsing pulls the broadest lever, since Chrome, Firefox, and Safari all use that blocklist. ## Channel 1 — Your Email App (Gmail or Outlook)

The fastest report you can make — and the one most people overlook as "doing anything useful."

When you click **Report Phishing** in Gmail or Outlook, the email is forwarded to the email provider's security team and immediately removed from your inbox. More importantly, it contributes to the spam and phishing model that protects everyone using the same platform. If ten thousand people report the same sender, Gmail can block the entire campaign within hours.

**How to do it:**

- **Gmail:** Open the email. Click the three-dot menu (top right of the message). Select "Report phishing." Done.
- **Outlook:** Select the email. Click "Report" in the toolbar. Choose "Report phishing."
- **Apple Mail:** Drag the email to your Junk folder. Then forward the full message to `reportphishing@apple.com`.

**Scope:** Improves filters for all users of that email platform. Does not directly take down the phishing website itself.

**Best for:** Anyone receiving a phishing email — this should always be your first step because it takes under ten seconds.

## Channel 2 — Google Safe Browsing (The Broadest URL Blocklist)

![Submitting a phishing URL to Google Safe Browsing](/og/how-to-report-a-phishing-website-2026-inline-1.png)

Google Safe Browsing is the database that Chrome, Firefox, and Safari all check before loading a website. When a URL is added to this list, any user on those three browsers sees a red warning screen before the page loads. That is approximately three billion browsers worldwide.

Reporting a phishing URL here is the single highest-leverage action you can take to protect other people from the same scam.

**How to do it:**

1. Copy the full URL of the phishing website (the one the scam email pointed to).
2. Go to `safebrowsing.google.com/safebrowsing/report_phish/`
3. Paste the URL into the form.
4. Add a brief comment if you want (optional).
5. Submit.

No account required. Google will review the submission and, if confirmed, add it to the blocklist. There is no notification when this happens, but the page typically gets flagged within 24 to 48 hours for active phishing campaigns.

**Scope:** Global. Any browser using Safe Browsing will warn users about this URL.

**Best for:** Phishing websites — the fake login pages and spoofed brand sites that scam emails link to.

## Channel 3 — Anti-Phishing Working Group (APWG)

The APWG is a non-profit coalition of security companies, banks, and law enforcement agencies. When you forward a phishing email to `reportphishing@apwg.org`, it enters a shared database that over ten major security vendors — including those that power [antivirus](https://snipstack.io/vs-antivirus-2026/) products, browser extensions, and corporate email filters — use to update their threat feeds.

This is the most efficient way to have one report ripple across many different security products simultaneously.

**How to do it:**

1. Do not copy and paste the email text. Forward the original phishing email as an attachment if possible, or forward it normally.
2. The recipient address is: `reportphishing@apwg.org`
3. You do not need to create an account or provide your name.

**Scope:** Feeds into security products and blocklists used globally by antivirus companies, email providers, and browser vendors.

**Best for:** Phishing emails that contain links to active scam websites. Email plus URL together in one submission.

## Channel 4 — FTC and National Reporting Bodies

If the phishing scam targeted you specifically — and especially if money or personal data was involved — a report to a national consumer protection body starts a formal record.

**United States:** Report to the Federal Trade Commission at `reportfraud.ftc.gov`. The FTC does not investigate individual cases, but reports are shared with over 3,000 law enforcement partners. If you lost money, also file a report with the Internet Crime Complaint Center at `ic3.gov`.

**United Kingdom:** Report to Action Fraud at `actionfraud.police.uk`. They are the national reporting centre for fraud and cybercrime and will give you a case reference number.

**Australia:** Report to Scamwatch at `scamwatch.gov.au`, run by the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission.

**Scope:** National. Contributes to law enforcement intelligence. Unlikely to trigger an immediate takedown but essential if financial harm occurred.

**Best for:** Situations where you received a targeted scam, lost money, or believe your personal information was compromised.

## Channel 5 — The Impersonated Brand's Abuse Team

Almost every major bank, retailer, and online service has a dedicated team that handles phishing emails impersonating their brand. Reporting to them directly lets the company pursue legal action, contact the registrar, or issue a takedown notice — steps that can bring a scam site offline faster than any government form.

**Common abuse addresses:**

- PayPal: `phishing@paypal.com`
- Amazon: `stop-spoofing@amazon.com`
- Microsoft: `phish@office365.microsoft.com`
- Apple: `reportphishing@apple.com`
- Your bank: Search for "[bank name] report phishing" — most major banks list a dedicated email address on their security pages.

**How to do it:**

Forward the full phishing email to the brand's abuse address. Include the website URL if the email linked to one. No account required.

**Scope:** Brand-specific. The company can file legal takedown requests and alert customers.

**Best for:** Phishing emails that impersonate a specific, well-known company.

## Channel 6 — Domain Registrar and Hosting Provider

Every phishing website is registered through a domain registrar (the company that sold the web address) and hosted on a server. Both the registrar and the hosting provider have acceptable-use policies that prohibit phishing, and both can suspend the domain or take the server offline.

This channel takes more effort but can permanently kill a phishing site rather than just flagging it.

**How to find the registrar:**

1. Go to `lookup.icann.org` — this is the official tool run by the body that oversees internet domain names.
2. Enter the phishing website's domain.
3. Look for the "Registrar" field — this shows the company responsible for the domain.
4. Most registrars list an abuse contact email on their own website, typically something like `abuse@registrar-name.com`.

**Scope:** Can result in full domain suspension. Slower — usually 24 to 72 hours for a response, and not every registrar acts quickly.

**Best for:** A phishing site that has been live for several days and is still operating despite other reports.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### [What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) after I report a phishing website?

It depends on the channel. Your email app improves its filters immediately. Google Safe Browsing typically reviews and flags confirmed phishing URLs within 24 to 48 hours. The APWG distributes your report to partner security vendors, who update their databases on their own schedules. Domain registrar reports move the slowest — typically 24 to 72 hours before the registrar acts. There is no automatic notification for most channels; you are contributing to a system that protects others, not receiving a personal case outcome. [Verify the link is still live before reporting →](/blog/scamcheck?sub=how-to-report-a-phishing-website-2026__faq)

### Do I need to create any accounts to report phishing?

No. Gmail, Outlook, and Apple Mail have built-in report buttons that require no separate account. The APWG accepts email forwards without registration. The Google Safe Browsing form requires no login. The FTC form asks for basic information but not an account. The only channel in this guide that involves an account is Surfshark Scamcheck — and that is used for verification before you report, not for the report itself.

### I already clicked the link. What should I do?

Close the tab immediately. Do not enter any information on the page. Run a malware scan using your device's built-in security tools. If you entered a password, change it on the real website right away. If you entered financial information, contact your bank. Then report the URL using the channels above — especially Google Safe Browsing and the APWG — so others are protected.

### Is there a single place to report everything?

Not yet. The APWG comes closest — forwarding a phishing email to `reportphishing@apwg.org` covers the most ground with one action. But combining that with a Gmail or Outlook in-app report (for email filtering) and a Google Safe Browsing submission (for browser protection) takes under two minutes and significantly increases the chance of the scam being taken down.

### How is reporting different from blocking?

Reporting sends information to a security team or database, which may eventually lead to the phishing site being taken down or the sender being blocked for everyone. Blocking in your email app only stops future emails from that specific address reaching your inbox — it does not alert anyone else or contribute to removing the scam. Both are worth doing. Report first, then block.

### Can I report a phishing text message as well as an email?

Yes. In the US, forward suspicious SMS messages to 7726 (SPAM) — this works on most carriers and feeds reports to the GSMA, a global mobile industry body. The FTC at `reportfraud.ftc.gov` also accepts text message scam reports. For [phishing link](https://snipstack.io/phishing-link-checker-free-2026/)s inside SMS messages, submit the URL to Google Safe Browsing the same way you would for an email link.

## The Reporting Sequence That Works

If you want to do this right without spending more than five minutes, here is the order that covers the most ground:

1. **Verify first** — paste the link into a scam checker to confirm it is malicious.
2. **Report in your email app** — one click, instant improvement to your own inbox and platform-wide filters.
3. **Forward to APWG** — `reportphishing@apwg.org` — one forward that reaches ten-plus security vendors.
4. **Submit the URL to Google Safe Browsing** — protects three billion browsers.
5. **Report to the impersonated brand** if applicable — they have the fastest path to legal takedown.

That sequence takes under five minutes and covers the email, the website, and the global threat intelligence databases all at once.

Reporting phishing is worth doing. Every submission makes the scam harder to run and gives the next person who receives the same email a better chance of being warned before they click.
