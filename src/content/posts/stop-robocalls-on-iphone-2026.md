## Step 1 — Turn On Silence Unknown Callers

This is Apple's built-in nuclear option. Any call from a number not in your contacts, recent calls, or Siri Suggestions gets silenced automatically and sent to voicemail.

**How to enable it:**

1. Open the **Settings** app on your iPhone.
2. Scroll down and tap **Phone**.
3. Tap **Silence Unknown Callers**.
4. Toggle it **on** (green).

That's it. Unknown numbers will no longer ring through. They'll appear in your Recents list as a missed call, so you won't lose the call entirely — you'll just deal with it on your terms.

**Gotcha:** If you're waiting for a call from a new doctor's office, a delivery courier, or a first date who hasn't saved your number yet, they'll also be silenced. Toggle it off temporarily when you're expecting an important unknown call, then toggle it back on.

## Step 2 — Enable Your Carrier's Built-In Filter

Your carrier runs call-reputation databases that can flag and block known robocall numbers at the network level — before the call even hits your phone. Here's how to enable it for the three major US carriers.

### AT&T — ActiveArmor

1. Download the **AT&T ActiveArmor** app from the App Store (free tier available).
2. Sign in with your AT&T account.
3. Enable **Automatic Fraud Blocking** and **Suspected Spam Calls to Voicemail**.
4. Optionally, turn on **Nuisance Call Alerts** for flagged numbers that still ring through.

AT&T's free tier covers fraud call blocking. The paid tier (ActiveArmor Advanced, ~$3.99/month) adds reverse number lookup and a call history with threat labels.

### Verizon — Call Filter

1. Download the **Verizon Call Filter** app (basic version is free for most postpaid plans).
2. Sign in with your My Verizon credentials.
3. Enable **Spam Filter** — this automatically routes likely spam to voicemail.
4. Set your **Risk Level** threshold (Low / Medium / High) depending on how aggressive you want the filter.

Call Filter Plus ($2.99/month per line) adds caller ID, a spam lookup tool, and a personal block list.

### T-Mobile — Scam Shield

1. Download **T-Mobile Scam Shield** from the App Store (free for T-Mobile customers).
2. Log in with your T-Mobile ID.
3. Enable **Scam Block** — this blocks calls T-Mobile identifies as scam-likely at the network level.
4. Turn on **Caller ID** to see labels on incoming calls before you pick up.

T-Mobile's Scam Shield also lets you dial `#662#` to enable Scam Block directly from your dialer without the app.

## Step 3 — Add a Third-Party Call-Blocking App

Carrier filters are good but not comprehensive. Third-party apps maintain independent spam databases updated in real time, which often catch newer robocall numbers that carrier lists haven't added yet.

### Hiya

Hiya is one of the most widely deployed caller ID and spam-detection services. It powers spam detection for Samsung and several carriers, so its database is large.

**Setup:**
1. Download **Hiya** from the App Store.
2. Open the app and grant it **Phone** permission.
3. Go to iPhone **Settings → Phone → Call Blocking & Identification**.
4. Enable **Hiya** under "Allow These Apps to Block Calls and Provide Caller ID."

The free tier flags suspicious calls. Hiya Premium (~$3.99/month) adds automatic blocking without prompts.

### RoboKiller

RoboKiller takes a more aggressive stance: it answers robocalls with pre-recorded "answer bots" that waste the caller's time. It's entertaining in theory and genuinely reduces call volume in practice because many robocall systems mark numbers as "answered" and deprioritize them.

**Setup:**
1. Download **RoboKiller** (paid, ~$4.99/month after a trial).
2. Follow the onboarding — it walks you through enabling it under **Settings → Phone → Call Blocking & Identification**.
3. Customize which types of calls to block (robocalls, spam, political calls).

### Truecaller

Truecaller relies on a crowdsourced database of flagged numbers. Users report spam in the app, which builds a shared blocklist.

**Setup:**
1. Download **Truecaller** from the App Store.
2. Sign up with your phone number (note: Truecaller adds your number to its directory by default — review their privacy settings if that's a concern).
3. Enable it under **Settings → Phone → Call Blocking & Identification**.

## Step 4 — Block Individual Numbers Manually

For persistent callers that get through the filters:

1. Open the **Phone** app and go to **Recents**.
2. Tap the **info (i)** button next to the number.
3. Scroll down and tap **Block this Caller**.
4. Confirm.

You can also manage your full block list at **Settings → Phone → Blocked Contacts**.

Manual blocking is reactive — you're adding numbers after they've already called — but it's useful for recurring numbers that slipped through automated filters.

## Step 5 — Report Spam Calls

Reporting robocalls contributes to the databases that your carrier and third-party apps use to block future calls for everyone.

- **FTC:** File a report at donotcall.gov
- **FCC:** Report at fcc.gov/consumers/guides/filing-informal-complaint
- **In-app:** Most third-party apps (Hiya, RoboKiller, Truecaller) have a "Report Spam" button in the call log

Registering on the Do Not Call Registry is still worth doing for legitimate telemarketers. Actual robocallers and scammers ignore the list, but compliant businesses do check it.

## Troubleshooting

**Silence Unknown Callers is on but robocalls still ring through.**
The number is probably saved somewhere on your device — in a text thread, an email, or a note. iOS treats Siri Suggestions (numbers it finds in your messages and emails) as "known." Check whether the robocall number appears in any communication thread and delete it.

**My carrier filter blocks calls I actually want.**
Reduce the risk threshold to Low in your carrier app, or add the specific number to your contacts. Contacts are whitelisted across all carrier filters.

**Third-party apps aren't blocking anything.**
Confirm the app is enabled under **Settings → Phone → Call Blocking & Identification**. Only apps enabled here can intercept calls on iOS. Also check that you've granted the app Phone permissions in **Settings → Privacy & Security → Contacts** (some apps need contact access to cross-reference known numbers).

**I keep getting texts from short codes I never signed up for.**
This is a different vector from robocalls — it's SMS spam. iOS lets you filter unknown sender texts: **Settings → Messages → Filter Unknown Senders**. Third-party SMS filtering apps are also available on the App Store.

**My alias number is starting to get spam too.**
This usually means the alias was used in a context where it leaked to a broker list. Discard the alias and generate a new one. That's the advantage of an alias system — rotation is built into the design.

## FAQ

**Does Silence Unknown Callers block all spam?**
It silences all calls from numbers not in your contacts or Siri Suggestions. It doesn't distinguish between spam and legitimate unknown callers, so a delivery driver or new contact will also be silenced. It's the most aggressive built-in option iOS offers.

**Are carrier call filters free?**
Basic filtering is free on AT&T (ActiveArmor), Verizon (Call Filter), and T-Mobile (Scam Shield). Each carrier offers a paid tier with additional features like reverse lookup and detailed spam history. For most users, the free tier is sufficient.

**Does the Do Not Call Registry actually work?**
For compliant telemarketers, yes. For robocall scammers — no. Scammers don't check the registry. The FTC continues to pursue robocallers legally, but the registry alone won't stop junk calls in 2026.

**Is Truecaller safe to use?**
Truecaller adds your number to its public caller ID directory when you sign up. For privacy-focused users, this is a trade-off worth considering. You can opt out of the directory in the app's privacy settings, though the opt-out process has historically been cumbersome. **What's the difference between a VPN and an alias number for privacy?**
A VPN encrypts your internet traffic and masks your IP address — it has no effect on phone calls. An alias number protects your real phone number from being shared or sold to data brokers. They solve different problems. If you're concerned about both call privacy and online tracking, you'd use them together, not interchangeably.
