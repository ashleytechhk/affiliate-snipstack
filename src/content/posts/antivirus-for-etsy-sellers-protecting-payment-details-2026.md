## Why Etsy Sellers Are Prime Targets for Hackers

Most people think hackers only go after big companies. The truth is the opposite. Criminals prefer small sellers because they are less likely to have proper security in place.

When you run an Etsy shop, you have something valuable sitting right on your computer: your Etsy login, your PayPal or bank details, your customers' delivery addresses, and years of sales history. To a criminal, that is a goldmine.

Here are the most common threats Etsy sellers face:

**Phishing emails that look official.** You receive an email that appears to come from Etsy, PayPal, or your bank. It says there is a problem with your account and asks you to click a link and log in. That link takes you to a fake website that captures your username and password the moment you type them.

**Malware that watches your screen.** Some malicious software — called spyware or keyloggers — hides on your computer and records every keystroke. Every time you type your password or enter your banking PIN, the software sends that information to a criminal overseas.

**Fake buyer scams with infected attachments.** A "customer" sends you a message on Etsy asking about a custom order. They attach a file — claiming it is a reference photo or a design specification — but opening that file installs malware on your computer.

**Unsecured public Wi-Fi.** If you ever check your Etsy shop at a coffee shop or library, a criminal on the same network can potentially intercept the information you send and receive.

## Extra Habits That Keep Your Etsy Shop Safe

Antivirus is your most important layer of protection, but a few simple habits make you even safer:

**Use a strong, unique password for Etsy.** Do not reuse the same password you use for email or banking. A password like "Etsy2026SunflowerShop!" is far stronger than "password123."

**Turn on two-step verification.** Etsy offers this option in your account settings. Even if a criminal steals your password, they cannot log in without a code sent to your phone.

**Never click links in unexpected emails.** If you receive an email claiming to be from Etsy or PayPal, open a new browser tab and go directly to the website yourself. Do not click the link in the email.

**Keep your computer updated.** Windows and Mac updates often include security patches. Do not delay or skip them.

**Be cautious with Etsy messages from new buyers.** If someone asks you to open a file or click a link before placing an order, that is a red flag.
