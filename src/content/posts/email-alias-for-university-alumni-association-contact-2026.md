## Why Alumni Association Email Is a Different Kind of Spam Problem

Most spam has a clean origin story: you sign up, one company oversells you, you unsubscribe, it's over. Alumni contact doesn't work that way, for three reasons.

**First, the relationship has no natural end date.** A retailer stops emailing you once you stop buying. A university keeps your record forever, since your class year and degree never expire — annual giving, homecoming, and class notes requests are a permanent fixture on the advancement calendar, not a one-time subscription.

**Second, your contact record gets shared internally and externally.** One alumni database record often feeds advancement, your college, career services, athletics boosters, and any licensed affinity partners — credit card issuers, insurance brokers, travel operators, class ring or directory-publishing companies. Opting out of the main newsletter doesn't touch those other lists.

**Third, giving out your real address is close to unavoidable in the moment.** RSVP-ing for a reunion or requesting a transcript needs a working email that can receive a reply. You can't dodge it with a five-minute throwaway. You just don't need that address to be good for the next 30 years of fundraising appeals.

That gap between "I need this email to work today" and "I don't want this email to work forever" is exactly what an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/how-to-manage-multiple-email-aliases-one-inbox-2026/) is for.

## Option 1: Your Real Email Address (Baseline)

**Pros:** Works instantly. No setup. Never bounces a confirmation.

**Cons:** No way out. Once it's in the advancement database, it stays through every department, every affinity partner, and every future data migration. If the alumni association or one of its vendors has a breach — and university systems have been breached before — your real address is in it, tied to your name and giving history.

**Verdict for alumni contact:** The default almost everyone uses, and the reason alumni email lists are so hard to escape.

---

## Option 2: Gmail `+tag` Trick

Add a `+tag` to your Gmail address — `yourname+alumni@gmail.com` — and mail still lands in your inbox, filterable by label.

**Pros:** Free. Instant. Lets you label and sort alumni mail separately from everything else.

**Cons:** Advancement CRMs frequently strip everything after the `+` when they normalize an email field on import — the record stored is your plain `yourname@gmail.com`. There's also no delete button; a `+tag` address can only be filtered or archived, never turned off.

**Verdict for alumni contact:** Fine for a quick inbox filter. Not a real privacy layer, since the CRM usually just discards the tag.

## Option 3: Secondary Email Account

Create a dedicated Gmail or Outlook account for alumni correspondence, and hand that address to every alumni-related form you fill out.

**Pros:** A real address that clears CRM validation everywhere. Keeps alumni mail entirely separate from your main inbox.

**Cons:** No forwarding — you must remember to check a second inbox for RSVPs and confirmations. Letting the account go stale isn't the same as deleting it; the address still sits there, reachable, on every list it was ever added to. Setup also takes a few minutes and, depending on the provider, a [phone number for](https://snipstack.io/virtual-number-for-paypal-setup-2026/) verification.

**Verdict for alumni contact:** Workable if you're disciplined about checking it, but the missed-confirmation risk is real and it never truly goes away.

## Option 4: 10 Minute Mail or Guerrilla Mail (Throwaway)

Free services that spin up a random inbox for a few minutes to an hour, no account required.

**Pros:** No personal information attached at all.

**Cons:** Event confirmations and receipts rarely arrive within the throwaway's lifespan. Many university web forms also reject known throwaway-mail domains outright, so submission fails before you even test the inbox.

**Verdict for alumni contact:** The wrong tool here. It's built for a one-off download, not for a relationship — however unwanted — that needs to receive real replies.
