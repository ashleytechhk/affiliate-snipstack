## What "No Activity Logs" Actually Means

A VPN provider that keeps "no activity logs" is making a specific technical claim: when you connect to their servers, they do not record which websites you visit, which IP addresses you connect to, or what data flows through the connection.

This is different from keeping no logs at all. Most reputable providers do keep minimal operational data — aggregate bandwidth usage to manage server load, connection timestamps for troubleshooting — but none of this ties back to your specific activity. Activity logs are the sensitive kind: a timestamped record of exactly what you did and when.

### What Gets Logged vs. What Doesn't

To be concrete about this, here is what a genuine no-activity-logs policy covers:

**Not recorded:**
- Which websites or services you connect to
- Your browsing or streaming history
- Your real IP address linked to your session activity
- DNS queries revealing what you looked up
- Volume of data transferred per session when tied to your identity

**May still be retained (operational only):**
- Total server bandwidth usage (aggregate, not per-user)
- Crash reports and diagnostic data submitted by users
- Account authentication events (required to verify your subscription)

The distinction matters practically. Even a provider that genuinely does not log your browsing activity could still log your connection time and match it to your account. That connection metadata alone can be revealing — and it is exactly this kind of data that courts sometimes request.

A full no-activity-logs policy closes both gaps.

## Why "Verified" Changes Everything

Here is the core problem with self-reported privacy policies: they cost nothing to write. A VPN provider can publish a twelve-page no-logs privacy policy, have a lawyer draft it, translate it into eight languages — and then log everything on the backend anyway.

Verification changes the equation. A third-party audit brings in an independent firm with no commercial interest in the outcome to examine:

- **The server infrastructure** — do the servers have the capacity to store logs? Is logging software installed?
- **The network configuration** — are DNS queries, connection metadata, or traffic records being written anywhere in the stack?
- **The operational policies** — do employee access controls and data retention policies align with the published privacy commitment?

An audit is not a permanent pass. It is a point-in-time assessment. But it is evidence, not a promise — and that distinction separates a verified claim from a marketing statement.

## Choosing a VPN With Verified No-Logs: What to Look For

Not every provider that claims "no logs" has the audit to back it. When evaluating a VPN for this standard, these are the questions worth asking:

**1. Has the no-logs policy been audited by an independent firm?**
Look for a named auditor — a recognizable accounting or cybersecurity firm — and a described scope. Self-certification does not count. Surfshark's audit was conducted by Deloitte; that level of attribution is what "verified" means in practice.

**2. What exactly did the audit examine?**
A review of the privacy policy document is not the same as a technical inspection of the server infrastructure. The stronger audits examine actual server configurations, not just legal text.

**3. How recently was the audit conducted?**
A 2019 audit tells you relatively little about 2026 operations. Look for providers that repeat audits on a regular cadence — annually is a meaningful signal.

**4. Has the provider ever been compelled to produce user records?**
Search for cases where the provider received legal demands. A provider that has faced a court order and genuinely had nothing to provide is more trustworthy than one that has never been tested.

**5. Is the pricing sustainable?**
[Free VPNs](https://snipstack.io/vs-free-vpns-2026/) and ultra-cheap providers often monetize user data. A verified no-logs policy requires expensive infrastructure and independent auditing. Pricing that reflects those costs is a signal that the business model does not depend on selling your data.

## FAQ

### What is the difference between "no logs" and "verified no logs"?

"No logs" is a claim any provider can make. "Verified no logs" means an independent firm examined the actual server infrastructure and confirmed that the logging mechanisms to record activity are not present. The verification is evidence; the claim alone is not.

### Does a no-logs VPN mean I am completely anonymous?

No. A no-logs policy means the VPN provider does not record your activity. Your device, your browser, the services you log into, and your payment method for the VPN subscription can all still carry identifying information. A verified no-logs VPN removes one significant layer of exposure — the provider's records — but it is not a complete anonymity solution.
