## What Is a VPN, and Why Does It Matter for Small Businesses?

A VPN, or Virtual Private Network, creates an encrypted tunnel between a device and a secure server run by the VPN provider. All internet traffic is routed through this tunnel before reaching its destination.

Two things happen when a VPN is active:

1. **Traffic is encrypted.** Anyone monitoring the connection — whether on a shared office network, a public hotspot, or an ISP — sees only scrambled data. The content of files transferred, emails sent, or systems accessed is not readable without the encryption key.
2. **The IP address changes.** Websites, platforms, and online services see the VPN server's IP address and location rather than the device's real address. This is what allows access to region-specific pricing on software and streaming services.

For small businesses, both functions are directly valuable. Encryption protects client data, financial records, and internal communications in transit. IP masking opens the door to subscription arbitrage — paying the local price for tools and services your business uses every day.

## The Real Risks Small Businesses Face Without a VPN

Small business owners often assume that data security threats target only large enterprises. This assumption is expensive. Smaller operations are frequently targeted precisely because they lack the IT infrastructure of larger companies.

Here are the practical risks that a VPN addresses:

**Unprotected remote work.** A team member working from a coffee shop, airport, or shared coworking space is on an unencrypted public network. Without a VPN, any traffic they send — including logins to your accounting software, client database, or internal tools — can be intercepted by other users on the same network.

**Data interception on shared networks.** Even in a legitimate office building with shared internet infrastructure, other tenants on the same network can potentially observe unencrypted traffic. This applies to any environment where you do not own and control the full network stack.

**Credential theft via man-in-the-middle attacks.** Attackers can insert themselves between a device and the network to capture login credentials, session tokens, and sensitive transfers. A VPN prevents this by encrypting everything before it leaves the device.

**Competitor and ISP surveillance.** ISPs can legally monitor and log browsing activity in many jurisdictions. Competitors operating in the same industry could theoretically access this data through data brokers. A VPN blocks ISP-level visibility into your business's internet activity.

## How VPN for Small Business Data Protection Works: Step by Step

Understanding the mechanics helps you explain it to your team and set expectations for what a VPN does and does not do.

1. **A team member opens the VPN app and connects to a server.** The app negotiates an encrypted connection to a VPN server in the chosen location. This takes a few seconds.
2. **The device routes all outgoing traffic through the encrypted tunnel.** Every request — browser traffic, email sync, cloud storage transfers, SaaS logins — goes through the encrypted channel.
3. **The VPN server forwards requests to the destination.** The destination server sees only the VPN server's IP address. It cannot identify the device or its actual location.
4. **Responses return through the same encrypted tunnel.** Data coming back to the device is encrypted in transit until it reaches the VPN app.
5. **The VPN app decrypts and delivers the data.** The end user sees a normal browsing or working experience with no visible change.

From the perspective of anyone monitoring the network — a wifi hotspot operator, an ISP, or someone running packet-capture software on a shared network — all they see is an encrypted connection to a VPN server. The actual content of what your business does online is not visible.

The encryption standard that matters here is AES-256, which is the same standard used by financial institutions and government agencies. Breaking AES-256 encryption is computationally infeasible with current technology.

## FAQ

### Does a VPN actually protect small business data?

Yes, within its scope. A VPN encrypts all traffic in transit between your device and the VPN server, blocking interception on public, shared, or monitored networks. It does not protect data stored on servers, prevent phishing attacks, or replace endpoint security software. For small businesses, a VPN handles the network transmission layer — which is where most opportunistic data interception occurs.

### Is a VPN enough for small business security on its own?

A VPN is an important layer but not a complete security solution. Pair it with strong unique passwords and a password manager, two-factor authentication on all business accounts, and regular software updates. Surfshark's CleanWeb feature adds malware and phishing domain blocking on top of the encrypted tunnel, which meaningfully extends the protection scope without additional tools.
