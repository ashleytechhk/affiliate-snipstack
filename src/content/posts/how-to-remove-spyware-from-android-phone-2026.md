## What Is Spyware, and Why Should You Care?

Spyware is a type of malicious software — malware — that hides on your phone and secretly collects your personal information. It can record your text messages, capture your passwords when you type them, take photos using your camera, track your location, and even listen through your microphone.

The people who create spyware are not random hackers in movies. They are often organized criminals who specifically target people who may not notice something is wrong with their device. Older adults are frequently targeted because they may be less familiar with how phones behave when something is wrong.

Spyware does not announce itself. That is the whole point. It works silently in the background while you browse, call family, or check your bank account.

### How Does Spyware Get Onto an Android Phone?

Understanding how spyware arrives helps you avoid it in the future. The most common entry points are:

- **Clicking a link in a suspicious text message or email** — these often look like messages from your bank, a delivery company, or even a government office
- **Downloading apps from outside the official Google Play Store** — unofficial app stores carry far higher risk
- **Installing an app that seemed legitimate but contained hidden malware** — some apps slip through even the Play Store's filters
- **Someone physically installing it on your phone** — unfortunately this can happen in cases of domestic abuse or elder fraud

You do not have to do anything obviously "wrong" to get spyware. Sometimes one curious click on a message is enough.

## Warning Signs Your Android Phone Has Spyware

Your phone will usually tell you something is wrong — you just need to know what to look for. Watch for any of these warning signs:

**Your battery drains much faster than usual.** Spyware runs constantly in the background, consuming power even when you are not using the phone.

**Your phone feels hot even when you are not using it.** Background spy processes generate heat.

**You notice apps you did not install.** Spyware sometimes installs additional apps as part of its operation.

**Your data usage has spiked unexpectedly.** Spyware sends your information back to criminals, which uses your mobile data.

**The phone is slow or freezes more than before.** Malware consumes your phone's processing power.

**Strange text messages appear in your sent folder.** Some spyware uses your phone to send messages to other potential victims.

**Your screen lights up when the phone is idle.** This can indicate an app is activating your phone remotely.

If you recognize two or more of these signs, take the situation seriously. It does not automatically mean spyware is present — some of these can have innocent explanations — but it warrants investigation.

## How to Remove Spyware From Your Android Phone: Step-by-Step

There are two approaches: manual removal and using a security app. We recommend doing both, starting with the manual steps to remove obvious threats, then running a scanner to catch anything hidden.

### Step 1: Put Your Phone in Safe Mode

Safe Mode starts your Android phone with only the original apps that came installed. This stops most spyware from running while you work.

To enter Safe Mode on most Android phones:
1. Press and hold the power button
2. When the power menu appears, press and hold the "Power off" option
3. Tap "OK" when your phone asks if you want to restart in Safe Mode
4. Your phone restarts — you will see "Safe mode" written in small text at the bottom of the screen

### Step 2: Check Your Installed Apps

Once in Safe Mode:
1. Open **Settings**
2. Tap **Apps** or **Application Manager**
3. Scroll through every app listed
4. Look for apps with no name, strange names, or apps you do not remember installing
5. Tap on any suspicious app and select **Uninstall**

If Uninstall is greyed out, the app may have been granted "Device Admin" privileges — a trick spyware uses to make itself harder to remove. Go to **Settings > Security > Device Admin Apps** and revoke its privileges first, then uninstall.

### Step 3: Check and Revoke Suspicious Permissions

Go to **Settings > Privacy > Permission Manager**. Check which apps have access to your camera, microphone, location, and contacts. If an app you do not recognize — or one that has no reason to — has these permissions, revoke them immediately.

### Step 4: Restart Normally and Run a Security Scan

Restart your phone out of Safe Mode. Now run a reputable antivirus app to scan for anything you may have missed. Manual removal is good at finding obvious apps, but professional scanning software detects hidden malware, rootkits, and code fragments that are invisible to the naked eye.

### Step 5: Change Your Passwords

If you suspect spyware was on your phone for any length of time, treat your passwords as compromised. Change them from a different, trusted device — not the infected phone. Start with your most sensitive accounts: banking, email, and social media.

### Step 6: Consider a Factory Reset as a Last Resort

If the above steps do not solve the problem, or if your phone is badly infected, a factory reset wipes everything and returns it to its original state. This removes all spyware completely. Before you reset, back up your photos and contacts to Google Photos and your Google account — do not back up apps, as the infected one could be among them.

To factory reset: **Settings > General Management > Reset > Factory Data Reset**

## Preventing Spyware in the Future: Simple Rules to Follow

Removing spyware is a one-time fix. Avoiding it in the future requires a few permanent habits:

**Only install apps from the Google Play Store.** Avoid downloading apps sent to you by text message or email, even if the sender seems legitimate.

**Never click links in unexpected text messages.** If your bank or a delivery company wants to contact you, call them directly using the number on their official website or your card.

**Keep your Android software updated.** Security patches fix known vulnerabilities that spyware exploits. Turn on automatic updates in **Settings > Software Update**.

**Use a strong PIN or fingerprint lock.** This prevents anyone from physically installing spyware if your phone is lost or borrowed.

**Run regular antivirus scans.** Once a week is sufficient. Most security apps let you schedule these automatically.

**Be skeptical of urgency.** Any message that says you must act immediately — that your account is locked, that you owe money, that you have won a prize — is almost always a scam. Legitimate organizations do not communicate this way.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can Android phones really get spyware?**
Yes. Android is the most widely used mobile operating system in the world, which makes it a major target. Unlike Apple's iPhone, Android allows apps from outside its official store, which increases the risk. However, spyware can also arrive through malicious links, even without installing any app.

**How do I know if spyware is on my phone right now?**
Watch for rapid battery drain, unexpected data usage, phone running hot when idle, apps you do not recognize, or the phone slowing down noticeably. Running a scan with a reputable antivirus app is the most reliable way to check. [Run a free scan with Surfshark Antivirus.](/blog/antivirus?sub=how-to-remove-spyware-from-android-phone-2026__faq)

**Is it safe to do online banking on my Android phone?**
It can be, but only if your phone is free of malware and you are on a secure, private connection. Avoid doing banking on public Wi-Fi. A VPN and an active antivirus scanner significantly reduce the risk of your banking credentials being intercepted.

**Will a factory reset definitely remove spyware?**
In the vast majority of cases, yes. A factory reset wipes the entire operating system back to its original state, removing virtually all malware. Extremely rare and sophisticated attacks can sometimes survive a reset, but for most spyware targeting ordinary users, a factory reset is a definitive solution.

**What should I do if I think I have already been scammed?**
Act quickly. Contact your bank immediately to freeze your accounts if you suspect financial information was stolen. Change all passwords from a clean device. Report the incident to your local police and to the FTC (in the US) at reportfraud.ftc.gov. The sooner you report, the better the chance of limiting the damage.
