## Why Dropbox Is a Bigger Spam Source Than You Think

Dropbox's core feature — file sharing — is also its biggest privacy surface. Every time you collaborate, your address moves.

**Shared folder invitations.** When you share a Dropbox folder, every recipient gets an invite email that includes your registered address. If any of those recipients are careless with data, or if their own systems are compromised, your address travels further than the original share.

**File request links.** Dropbox's file request feature lets anyone submit files to your folder via a public link. Anyone you send that request link to sees your account's email address embedded in the notification flow.

**Third-party app integrations.** Dropbox connects with Slack, Zapier, Adobe Creative Cloud, Canva, Notion, Salesforce, and dozens of other platforms via OAuth. Each integration is a separate company's data handling infrastructure, with its own breach risk and marketing database.

**Dropbox Transfer.** When you send large files via Dropbox Transfer, recipients receive a download notification that includes your sender identity — including your registered email. Every file transfer is a potential exposure point.

**Dropbox Paper and team workspaces.** In collaborative environments, Dropbox exposes your email to everyone in the workspace. Team-level address books in Dropbox Business accounts are particularly wide-exposure: a single admin can distribute your address to dozens of colleagues and contractors.

**The 2012 Dropbox breach.** Dropbox confirmed in 2016 that a 2012 breach exposed 68 million user credentials, including hashed passwords and email addresses. If your current Dropbox account predates 2014 and you haven't changed the email, that address has been on breach lists for over a decade and is almost certainly still circulating.

The pattern is consistent: Dropbox's usefulness depends on sharing, and sharing leaks your address into systems you don't control.

**Is there any risk to my Dropbox account if I delete an alias?**
