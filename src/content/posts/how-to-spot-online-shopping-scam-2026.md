## Red Flag 1: Stolen Product Photos

Scam shops rarely shoot their own product photography. They lift images straight from Amazon, manufacturer sites, or legitimate retailers. The fix is a reverse image search.

On desktop, right-click any product photo and choose "Search image" (Chrome/Edge) or save it and upload it to Google Images or TinEye. If the same photo appears on a dozen other sites selling the same item at a normal price, you have your answer. If it appears on an entirely different product or brand, the listing is almost certainly fake.

![Step-by-step reverse image search guide](/og/how-to-spot-online-shopping-scam-2026-inline-1.png)

**Watch for:** watermarks that have been cropped out, slightly blurry images (downscaled to remove metadata), and studio photos that don't match the price point (a $12 item in a luxury lightbox shoot).

## Red Flag 2: Copy-Paste Product Descriptions

Fake stores copy descriptions wholesale from legitimate retailers — sometimes they don't even bother removing the original brand name. Grab a distinctive sentence from the product description, wrap it in quotes, and paste it into Google. If it appears verbatim on multiple sites, the shop did not write it.

Thin, generic descriptions are also a tell. "High quality material. Fast shipping. Customer satisfaction guaranteed." says nothing about the actual product and is almost always templated filler.

---

## Red Flag 3: Artificial Pressure Tactics

"Only 2 left in stock!" "Sale ends in 00:04:32!" Countdown timers and fake inventory warnings are psychological pressure tools designed to short-circuit your skepticism. Legitimate retailers use them too, but scam sites weaponize them constantly — and the countdown resets every time you reload the page.

Test it: close the tab, come back an hour later. If the "4 hours left" timer now shows "4 hours left" again, the urgency is manufactured. Trust nothing on a site that relies on this heavily.

## Red Flag 4: Payment Methods That Bypass Buyer Protection

A legitimate online store will accept credit cards or PayPal. These payment methods give you chargeback rights — if the goods don't arrive or are counterfeit, your bank or PayPal can reverse the charge.

Scam shops steer you away from that protection. If the **only** payment options are:

- Gift cards (iTunes, Google Play, Amazon)
- Wire / bank transfer
- Cryptocurrency

...leave immediately. These payment methods are irreversible by design. Once the money leaves, it is gone. No legitimate e-commerce operation requires you to pay with a Google Play card.

## Red Flag 5: No SSL on the Checkout Page

SSL (the padlock icon and `https://` in your browser's address bar) encrypts the data you send — including your card number. Any checkout page without it is transmitting your payment details in plain text.

Check the address bar before you enter anything. If you see `http://` instead of `https://`, or if your browser shows a "Not Secure" warning, close the tab. This is non-negotiable: an unencrypted checkout page is not a privacy preference, it is a security failure.

## Red Flag 6: Missing or Vague Return Policy

Legitimate retailers have clear, findable return policies. Scroll to the footer and look for "Returns," "Refunds," or "Shipping Policy." A missing page is a red flag. A page that exists but says something like "All sales final, no returns accepted under any circumstances" is nearly as bad.

Also look for a physical address and a customer service email or [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/). Scam shops often list a fake address or no contact information at all. Copy the address into Google Maps — if it resolves to a vacant lot or a residential house with no business history, take note.

---

## Red Flag 7: A Brand-New Domain

Scam shops spin up and disappear fast — often within weeks of launching, before complaints accumulate. A domain registered last month selling premium electronics at 70% off is almost certainly a hit-and-run operation.

Run a free WHOIS lookup at who.is or lookup.icann.org. Enter the domain and check the "Creation Date." If the site claims to have been in business for years but the domain was registered three weeks ago, something does not add up.

## Red Flag 8: Prices That Don't Add Up

A $280 pair of AirPods Pro for $38. A $900 Dyson vacuum for $89. If the price is so far below market value that it seems impossible, it probably is. Scammers use these prices specifically because they override rational judgment — the deal feels too good to pass up.

Cross-check the price on at least two other established retailers before committing. If no one else is selling at that price point, you're not finding a deal. You're being baited.

## FAQ

### How do I quickly check if an online store is legitimate?

Start with three fast checks: look for HTTPS in the address bar, run a WHOIS lookup to see when the domain was registered, and search the store name plus "scam" or "review" in Google. If any of those raise concerns, stop there. For a more thorough check, [run the site through a scam checker](/blog/scamcheck?sub=how-to-spot-online-shopping-scam-2026__faq) before you enter any payment details.

### Are gift card payment requests always a scam?

Virtually always, yes. No legitimate e-commerce business requires payment by gift card. This is the single most reliable indicator that something is wrong. Gift cards are irreversible, untraceable, and the payment method of choice for fraud operations worldwide. Walk away.

### What should I do if I've already paid a scam site?

Act immediately. If you paid by credit card, call your card issuer and request a chargeback — most banks have a window of 60–120 days. If you paid via PayPal, open a dispute in the Resolution Centre. If you paid by gift card, wire, or crypto, recovery is extremely difficult; report to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov and your local consumer protection agency. Change any passwords associated with the email address you used.

### Does a professional-looking website mean a store is safe?

No. Website templates are cheap and drag-and-drop builders are free. Scam operations invest in appearance precisely because shoppers associate a polished look with legitimacy. Design quality tells you almost nothing about trustworthiness. Focus on the verifiable signals: domain age, SSL, return policy, payment methods, and contact information.
