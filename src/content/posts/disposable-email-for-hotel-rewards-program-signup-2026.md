## Why Hotel Rewards Programs Generate So Much Spam

Hotel loyalty signups aren't like a random newsletter opt-in. A few things make them worse.

**1. Multi-brand partner networks.** Most major hotel groups run dozens of sub-brands plus airline, car rental, and credit card partnerships. Joining one loyalty program often enrolls you into cross-promotion with every partner in that network — each one a new sender.

**2. Deeper signup data.** Loyalty forms typically collect name, date of birth, home address, and [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-privacy-for-online-selling-2026/) alongside email — far more than a typical newsletter signup. That profile is more valuable to data brokers, and more of it leaks the moment your inbox address does.

**3. Co-branded credit card marketing.** Nearly every large hotel loyalty program has a co-branded credit card, and the marketing engine behind it runs constantly. Card offers, points-multiplier pitches, and "you're pre-approved" emails often follow within days of signup.

**4. Breach history.** Large hotel chains have experienced some of the biggest loyalty-program data breaches on record, exposing millions of members' contact and stay details. Once a program email surfaces in a breach dataset, unrelated spam and phishing volume tends to climb for years afterward.

Handing over a real address at loyalty signup ties it to a marketing machine that runs for as long as the account exists — indefinitely, for most travelers.

### Temp-Mail — Wrong Fit for Loyalty Accounts

Temp-Mail and similar disposable inbox services generate a random address instantly with zero signup. They're built for one-off verification clicks — confirm a download, grab a discount code, done.

Hotel rewards accounts need ongoing access: welcome bonus confirmation, later point statements, occasional password resets. Temp-Mail addresses expire within roughly an hour of inactivity, and there's no forwarding to a real inbox — you check the throwaway inbox separately, and once it expires, everything sent to it is gone. Many hotel booking systems also block known temp-mail domains outright, which can stop a signup before it even completes.

**Best for:** a single confirmation click. Not a multi-year loyalty relationship.

### SimpleLogin (Free) — A Reasonable Backup

SimpleLogin's free tier gives you up to 10 active aliases that forward to your real inbox and can be deleted individually. It handles the "ongoing loyalty emails" requirement fine and hides your real address effectively.

The limitation is capacity — 10 aliases run out fast once you're using one per service across shopping, streaming, forums, and loyalty programs. It also doesn't generate the fake identity details that hotel signup forms often request alongside your email.

**Best for:** users already on SimpleLogin who still have alias slots to spare.

### Gmail + Address Trick — Free but Doesn't Hide Anything

Appending a tag — `yourname+hotelrewards@gmail.com` — still delivers to `yourname@gmail.com` and costs nothing. But anyone reading the address can strip everything after the `+` and see your real one underneath. Many marketing platforms do exactly that automatically, which defeats the purpose before it starts. There's also no way to revoke it — it's still your real address with a label attached.

**Best for:** basic inbox filtering. Not privacy, not spam prevention.

**Is it dishonest to sign up with an alias instead of my real email?**

No. The alias is a genuine, working address — mail sent to it reaches you, and replies work normally.
