## What Is a NAS Device? (Plain English)

You may have heard a family member mention a "NAS" or seen a small box sitting near your home router. A NAS — short for Network Attached Storage — is essentially a private hard drive that connects to your home internet. Unlike a USB drive you plug directly into your computer, a NAS sits on your home network and lets every device in the house access the same files.

Think of it like a personal filing cabinet that your computer, your phone, and your grandchildren's tablets can all reach at the same time.

Common NAS brands you might recognise include **Synology** and **QNAP**. They are often set up by a younger family member to back up photos and documents automatically. If someone said "your photos are backed up to the NAS," this is what they meant.

Here is why this matters for your safety: because a NAS holds everything in one place, it is an extremely attractive target for criminals. Locking or stealing data from a NAS causes maximum damage — far more than infecting a single laptop.

## Why Criminals Target Home NAS Devices

In recent years, ransomware groups have developed specific tools designed to attack home NAS devices. Two well-known strains are:

- **Deadbolt** — Targeted QNAP NAS devices in multiple large waves starting in 2022. Victims woke up to find all their stored files encrypted and a ransom demand displayed on screen.
- **eCh0raix** — Hit both QNAP and Synology devices. Families reported losing irreplaceable wedding photos, home videos, and years of documents.

These are not random accidents. Criminals search the internet for NAS devices with weak passwords or outdated software. When they find one, the attack can happen in minutes.

What makes this especially dangerous for seniors is that many families share a single NAS. If one person's laptop has a virus, that virus can spread across the network and reach the NAS — encrypting everything stored there, including files belonging to other family members.

The path of attack almost always looks like this:

1. A connected device (laptop, phone, or tablet) gets infected — often from a suspicious email link or a fake website.
2. The virus spreads across the home network to the NAS.
3. Files are encrypted or stolen.
4. A ransom demand appears.

Protecting the devices in your home is the single most effective step you can take to protect your NAS.

This is the single most important step. Cover every device in the household:

- Your smartphone (iPhone or Android)
- Any tablets used at home

### Step 2 — Change the NAS Administrator Password

If a family member set up the NAS and never changed the default password, it is vulnerable. Ask them to:

1. Log into the NAS management page (usually accessed from a web browser).
2. Go to the user settings for the administrator account.
3. Change the password to something long and unique — at least 12 characters, mixing letters and numbers.

You do not need to do this yourself — ask the person who set it up. Just make sure it gets done.

### Step 3 — Turn Off Remote Access If You Do Not Use It

Many NAS devices come with remote access enabled by default — meaning someone outside your home could potentially connect to it over the internet. If your family does not need to access files from outside the house, ask your family member to disable this feature in the NAS settings. Fewer open doors means fewer ways for criminals to get in.

### Step 4 — Keep the NAS Firmware Updated

NAS manufacturers release software updates (called firmware) to fix security weaknesses. These should be applied as soon as they are available. Ask your family member to check whether automatic updates are enabled on the NAS. This takes one minute to verify and can prevent a major attack.

### Step 5 — Keep a Separate Backup

Even with all protections in place, having a second copy of your most important files is wise. A simple USB hard drive kept in a cupboard works well. If the NAS is ever affected, your backup remains untouched.

## Warning Signs That Your Network May Already Be Compromised

Sometimes people discover a problem only after it has happened. Watch for these warning signs on any computer or phone in your home:

- Files you did not rename are suddenly called something like "photo.jpg.deadbolt" — this almost always means ransomware.
- A screen appears demanding payment in Bitcoin or gift cards to recover your files.
- Your computer becomes very slow or your browser behaves strangely — opening pages you did not ask for.
- You receive emails warning that your account credentials appeared on a leaked database.
- Family members report that shared files on the NAS have disappeared or are inaccessible.

If you see any of these signs, do not pay any ransom. Contact a trusted family member or a local computer repair professional immediately, and run a full scan using your antivirus program.

## A Note on NAS-Native Security Features

Modern NAS devices from Synology and QNAP include some security features built in — such as automatic IP blocking after multiple failed login attempts, and intrusion prevention. These are worth enabling (ask your family member to check), but they are not a replacement for protecting the connected devices.

A NAS security feature can only see what is happening on the NAS itself. It cannot stop a virus on your laptop from spreading across the network before reaching the NAS. Only device-level antivirus can catch that threat at the source.
