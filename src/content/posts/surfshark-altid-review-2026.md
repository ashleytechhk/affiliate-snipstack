## Why Your Inbox Got This Bad

Most people assume their address got scraped from one place. The reality is messier.

Every service you have ever signed up for has stored your email address. Some sold it. Some were acquired and the buyer monetised the list. Some were breached — and the breach data was packaged, sold, and resold until your address existed on dozens of spam lists you will never be able to trace.

Data brokers sit at the centre of this ecosystem. They compile, verify, and sell lists of email addresses at scale. Once your address is in that pipeline, it circulates indefinitely. There is no opt-out that reaches all of them, no unsubscribe link that touches the underlying list.

The only structural solution is to stop exposing your real address. Give each service something else — an alias that forwards to your inbox but that you can delete the moment it turns hostile. That is the shift Alt ID makes possible.

## The Risk Alt ID Addresses (And How It Addresses It)

### Your Real Address Is a Permanent Liability

Once your real email address is in a data broker's database, you cannot remove it. You can report it, you can opt out of individual brokers, but the lists replicate faster than any individual can track.

An alias breaks this dynamic. The address you give to a retailer, a forum, a free tool — that is the alias. If it appears in a breach, you delete it. The breach data, once sold, leads to an address that no longer exists. Your real inbox is untouched.

### Per-Service Aliases Give You Forensic Visibility

If you use one alias for all shopping, you know a shopping site leaked your data when that alias starts getting spam — but not which one. If you use one alias per service, you know exactly which company was responsible. That information matters: it tells you whether to change your password at that service, whether payment data may be at risk, and whether to report the breach.

Alt ID's unlimited alias allowance makes the per-service approach practical. There is no extra cost to creating 30 aliases instead of five.

### The Generated Identity Limits Breach Damage

A leaked alias connected to your real name, birth date, and address is more damaging than a leaked alias on its own. Alt ID's generated identity feature means that even if the breach record is sold and cross-referenced, the personal data in it does not match you. The alias address bounces. The identity it is registered to does not exist. The chain of traceability breaks.

## Honest Limitations

### Alt ID Is Not a Spam Filter

If spam is already arriving in your real inbox — from your real address — Alt ID does not help with that. It cannot retroactively protect an address that is already circulating in spam lists. The strategy only applies from the moment you start using aliases instead of your real address going forward.

For the inbox you already have, conventional spam filters (Gmail, Outlook's built-in filtering, or third-party tools) remain the practical option.

### Alt ID Does Not Block Tracking Pixels

Emails that land in your inbox via forwarding — routed through the alias to your real address — still contain tracking pixels from the original sender. Opening a forwarded email can confirm to the sender that it was opened, just as it would if the email had been sent directly to your real address.

If tracking-pixel blocking is a priority, you need a tool that strips images at the email-fetching level (some mail clients offer this) rather than at the alias level. Alt ID's scope is address masking, not content sanitisation.

### It Requires Discipline to Work Well

The alias system is only as strong as your consistency. Using your real address even once — on a site that later gets breached — creates the exposure the system is designed to prevent. Building the habit of reaching for the alias dashboard before any new sign-up takes time, and it is where most users find the setup friction.

### Replies From Your Inbox Expose Your Real Address (By Default)

When you receive a forwarded email and reply from your mail client, the reply typically comes from your real address unless you have configured send-from-alias. Check whether your email client and Alt ID's forwarding configuration support reply-from-alias before relying on this for two-way communication where privacy matters.
