## Why Dealership Email Contact Is a Special Problem

Most spam comes from one-off sign-ups. You subscribed to a newsletter, the site got acquired, the new owner blasted the list. Annoying, but low-stakes.

Dealership email contact is different for three reasons.

**First, dealer CRMs are built for long campaigns.** A modern dealership CRM like VinSolutions or DealerSocket runs follow-up sequences that can last 12 to 18 months. "Be-back" workflows send automated emails at day 3, day 7, day 14, day 30, day 60, and beyond. Every salesperson you spoke with may add a personal touchpoint on top of the automated ones. You're not getting one email. You're getting a campaign.

**Second, contact data gets resold.** Dealerships frequently rent or sell their customer contact lists to automotive insurance brokers, extended warranty companies, and finance partners. These are separate businesses with separate lists. When you unsubscribe from the dealership's CRM, you are not unsubscribing from the three companies that already bought your address.

**Third, the context forces you to give a real-looking address.** Unlike a freeware download or a giveaway entry, dealership contact often involves real transactions: a test drive waiver, a finance pre-approval, a service appointment. The dealer needs to reach you. You can't just use a five-minute throwaway and walk away. You actually need the emails — just not for the next two years.

This is precisely the gap an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-twitch-account-privacy-2026/) fills.

## Option 1: Your Real Email Address (Baseline)

**Pros:** Works everywhere. No setup. You get every email.

**Cons:** No exit. Once your address is in the dealer's CRM, it stays there. Unsubscribing removes you from the dealer's marketing list, not from the CRM itself, not from any third-party lists. The address is now permanently associated with your car-buying intent and personal information. If there is a data breach at the dealership or their CRM provider, your real address is in the leak.

**Verdict for dealership contact:** The worst option by far. The pain is invisible until it's already severe.

## Option 2: Gmail `+tag` Trick

Gmail lets you add a `+tag` to your address — `yourname+dealershipname@gmail.com` — and emails still arrive in your inbox. You can create a filter to label or archive emails sent to that tag.

**Pros:** No extra account. Takes five seconds to set up. Keeps emails labeled.

**Cons:** This is the most commonly known trick, and CRMs routinely strip the `+tag` suffix before storing the address. When a dealership's CRM processes `yourname+ford@gmail.com`, it often stores `yourname@gmail.com`. Your real address is in the system anyway. You also cannot delete a `+tag` address — it always exists. There is no kill switch.

**Verdict for dealership contact:** Better than nothing at the labeling stage. Functionally useless as a privacy layer because the CRM strips the tag.

## Option 3: Secondary Gmail or Outlook Account

Create a second email account specifically for dealership and automotive contact. Give that address to every dealer. When it becomes too spammy, stop checking it.

**Pros:** Real address that passes all CRM validation. Works across multiple dealerships. Can be abandoned without affecting your primary inbox.

**Cons:** No forwarding — you must actively check the second inbox to catch anything you actually want (test drive time change, service appointment confirmation, financing approval). Inbox abandonment is not a kill switch — the address and its history remain. If you ever gave that address to someone you do want to hear from, you lose those emails too when you stop checking. Setup takes several minutes and requires a [phone number for](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/) account verification.

**Verdict for dealership contact:** Workable but clunky. The forwarding gap is the real problem — you'll miss time-sensitive emails unless you check both inboxes.

## Option 4: 10 Minute Mail or Guerrilla Mail (Throwaway)

Free services that generate a random address with a short inbox window — typically 10 minutes to an hour. No account needed.

**Pros:** Zero personal information. Cannot be traced back to you.

**Cons:** The lifespan is the entire problem. A test drive confirmation email may take longer than 10 minutes to arrive. A dealer's initial follow-up email may arrive hours later. If you need to retrieve your appointment details the next morning, the inbox is gone. Many dealerships' CRM systems also detect and block known throwaway domains at the point of form submission — the address simply fails validation.

**Verdict for dealership contact:** Wrong tool for this job. Useful for a one-time gated PDF download. Not useful for any transaction where you need the confirmation email.
