## What a VPN Actually Does for Remote Workers

A VPN — Virtual Private Network — routes your internet traffic through an encrypted tunnel to a server in a location you choose. Two things happen simultaneously:

1. **Your connection is encrypted** — data traveling between your device and the VPN server is scrambled, making it far harder for anyone monitoring the network to read your traffic.
2. **Your apparent location changes** — from the perspective of any website or subscription service, you appear to be browsing from wherever your VPN server sits, not from your actual location.

For a remote worker sitting in a co-working space in Medellin or on a beach connection in Koh Samui, both properties matter. The encryption protects your work traffic on networks you do not control. The location shift is what unlocks subscription pricing advantages.

## Why Expats Face a Specific Problem

### Your Home Streaming Library Disappears

Content licensing is territorial. If you paid for a US Netflix account and you are now based in Spain, Netflix detects your Spanish IP and serves you the Spanish catalog. You may lose access to series you were mid-season on, films that are only licensed to your home market, or entire content blocks that do not exist in your new region.

Connecting your VPN to a US server before opening Netflix restores your home library. You are accessing content covered by your existing subscription — the VPN simply ensures the platform sees the location where your subscription is registered.

### Public and Co-Working Networks Are Shared Environments

Remote workers cycle through co-working spaces, hotel business centers, airport lounges, and café networks. These are shared environments where, on a poorly secured network, traffic that is not already end-to-end encrypted can be visible to others. A VPN adds an encrypted layer regardless of what the destination service uses.

This matters practically: corporate VPNs protect company systems but not your personal accounts. A personal VPN covers everything — your banking app, personal email, streaming accounts, all of it — on any network you connect to.

### Banking Apps See Unfamiliar Locations

Many banks flag logins from foreign countries as potential security events. This can trigger multi-step verification, temporary account locks, or declined transactions at the worst possible moment — when you are trying to pay rent in your new city. A VPN connected to a home-country server presents your familiar IP address to your bank, reducing the likelihood of these interruptions.

This is not a fix for every bank — some financial institutions also block known VPN IP ranges — but in practice it smooths the majority of location-flagging issues that expat remote workers encounter.

## How to Pick the Right VPN for Expat Remote Work

Not every VPN serves this use case equally. The factors that matter:

**Server count and country coverage.** The wider the server network, the more regional pricing options you can access and the more likely the service maintains working servers in your specific target country. Look for 3,000+ servers across 90+ countries.

**Streaming unblocking capability.** Services like Netflix, Disney+, and BBC iPlayer actively detect and block VPN traffic. A VPN that routes you to a different region needs servers that stay ahead of these detection systems. This requires dedicated streaming infrastructure and active IP maintenance — not something cheap or [free VPNs](https://snipstack.io/vs-free-vpns-2026/) can sustain.

**Unlimited simultaneous connections.** Remote workers carry multiple devices. Laptop for work, phone for personal, tablet for streaming — possibly a travel router. A VPN capped at five or six simultaneous connections creates management friction. Unlimited connections eliminate the decision entirely.

**Speed overhead.** Slow VPNs make video calls choppy and remote desktop sessions unusable. The best providers retain 70–85% of your base connection speed, which means a 50 Mbps connection stays well above the threshold for HD video work calls.

**Price per year.** The arbitrage case only holds if the VPN costs less than what you save on subscriptions. At $2–3/month on a long-term plan, the economics work in your favor on virtually any combination of streaming savings.
