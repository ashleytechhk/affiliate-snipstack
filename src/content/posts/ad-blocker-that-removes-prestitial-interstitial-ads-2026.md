## What Are Prestitial and Interstitial Ads?

The names sound technical, but the concepts are simple. Understanding the difference helps you know exactly what you are fighting.

### Prestitial Ads

A **prestitial ad** ("pre" = before) appears *before* the main content loads. You click a link, and instead of the article or video you wanted, you see an ad — sometimes with a countdown timer before you can skip it. These are extremely common on news sites, free streaming services, and game portals. On a slow mobile connection, a prestitial ad can add five to ten seconds to every page load.

### Interstitial Ads

An **interstitial ad** ("inter" = between) appears *between* content, usually as a full-screen overlay. Common triggers include:

- Tapping from one article to the next
- Moving between levels in a mobile game
- Navigating between pages on a website

Interstitials are designed to demand your full attention because there is literally nothing else visible. Google penalizes websites that use intrusive interstitials on mobile in its search rankings — yet many publishers still deploy them because the revenue per impression is high.

### Why These Ad Types Are Especially Painful on Mobile

On a laptop, a disruptive ad is annoying. On a phone, it is a worse experience for three reasons:

1. **Smaller target for the close button** — making it easier to accidentally tap the ad
2. **Slower connections** — ads load slowly on mobile data and block faster content beneath them
3. **Data cost** — every ad that loads consumes your data allowance, sometimes several megabytes per rich-media interstitial

A study by the Coalition for Better Ads found that full-page interstitial and prestitial formats rank among the most disliked ad types by mobile users globally. That is precisely why browser makers and regulators have introduced standards to limit them — but enforcement is inconsistent, and many sites ignore them.

### In-App Ads Are a Different Problem

Prestitial and interstitial ads appear not just in web browsers but inside apps — news readers, casual games, recipe apps, podcast players. A browser-only ad blocker touches none of these. In-app interstitials require blocking at a level below the app itself.

### Network-Level Blocking Solves Both Problems

A network-level ad blocker operates at the DNS or VPN layer. It intercepts outbound requests from *every app on your phone* before they reach ad servers. If your phone asks "can I load this ad creative?", the blocker says no — and the request never completes. The ad never downloads, never displays, and never slows down your connection.
