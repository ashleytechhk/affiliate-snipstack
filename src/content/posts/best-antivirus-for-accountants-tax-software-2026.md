## Why Tax Software Makes You a Target

Tax software sits at the intersection of everything a cybercriminal wants: your full name, date of birth, Social Security number, income records, bank routing numbers, and often your employer's details too.

When you open your tax program — or log into a browser-based tax service — your computer connects to the internet. If your device has been quietly infected with spyware or a keylogger (a program that records every key you press), the attacker receives a live feed of everything you type. Every password. Every account number. Every Social Security digit.

### The Keylogger Problem

Keyloggers are especially dangerous for seniors because they are invisible. There is no popup warning. No slow computer. No strange icon. The program simply runs in the background and records. By the time you notice something is wrong — perhaps a fraudulent tax return, or a drained bank account — the damage is done.

Most people get keyloggers from three places:

- Clicking a link in a phishing email that looks like it's from the IRS or a bank
- Downloading a file that appeared to be a legitimate document or software update
- Visiting a website that had been compromised to automatically install malware

A good antivirus blocks all three attack paths.

## What Accountants Face Every Day

If you are an accountant or tax preparer, your risk is multiplied. You are not just protecting your own data — you are the guardian of every client's financial life. A single breach on your machine can expose an entire book of business.

![How tax software threats reach accountants' computers](/og/best-antivirus-for-accountants-tax-software-2026-inline-1.png)

Regulators are paying attention. The IRS requires tax preparers to have a written data security plan under the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act. Part of fulfilling that requirement means having active, up-to-date security software on every device that touches client data. Using antivirus is not just smart — for professional tax preparers, it may be legally required.

### Common Threats Targeting Tax Professionals

**Ransomware** — attackers encrypt your entire client database and demand payment to unlock it. Tax season is peak time for ransomware attacks on small accounting firms.

**Business email compromise** — a scammer impersonates a client or a colleague via email, asking you to wire funds or share a client's W-2. It happens more than you'd think.

**Fake software updates** — a popup tells you your tax program needs an urgent update. You click. Instead of an update, you've installed malware.

**Public Wi-Fi interception** — if you ever work from a coffee shop, library, or airport, unencrypted traffic can be intercepted. An antivirus paired with a VPN closes this gap completely.
