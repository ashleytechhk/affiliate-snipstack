## Warning Sign 1: Listing Photos Stolen From Other Sites

Rental scammers almost never own the property they are advertising. They steal photos from legitimate listings on estate agent websites, or from properties that are genuinely for sale but not for rent.

To check, right-click any photo in the listing and choose "Search image" in your browser (Chrome and Edge have this built in). On a phone, save the image and upload it to Google Images. If the same photo appears on a different address, under a different agency name, or alongside a for-sale listing at a completely different price — you are looking at a stolen photo.

![Step-by-step guide to reverse image search on a rental listing photo](/og/rental-listing-scam-how-to-check-2026-inline-1.png)

Look closely at the photos themselves too. Watch for [signs of a](https://snipstack.io/signs-of-a-fake-website-2026/) different season to the current one (outdoor photos showing autumn leaves in spring), watermarks that have been cropped at the edge, or interior photos that do not match the claimed location at all.

## Warning Sign 2: Rent That Seems Too Good to Be True

Scammers attract victims with prices well below what anything similar rents for in the area. This is deliberate — a bargain creates urgency. You feel you need to act quickly before someone else takes it.

Before you send any message at all, open one or two other rental platforms and search for comparable properties nearby. If a two-bedroom flat in the neighbourhood rents for $1,800–$2,200 per month and this listing is showing $900, that gap is a warning sign in itself.

Legitimate private landlords do sometimes price slightly below market to find a tenant quickly. But a discount of 40–60% with a rushed timeline is a different matter entirely.

## Warning Sign 3: The Landlord Is "Out of the Country"

This is one of the most consistent scripts rental scammers use. When you ask to view the property or meet in person, you receive an explanation: the landlord is currently working abroad, caring for a relative overseas, or on a long assignment somewhere. They are very sorry. They will arrange everything remotely.

A genuine landlord who genuinely cannot meet you in person will offer a reliable alternative — a property manager, a letting agent, a trusted neighbour with a key. They will not simply ask you to trust them and send money.

If the landlord claims to be away and cannot arrange any form of verified access to the property, treat it as a strong warning sign.

---

## Warning Sign 4: No In-Person or Video Viewing

Never send any money for a property you have not been inside. A legitimate landlord has no reason to prevent you from viewing the property before you commit. If viewing is consistently refused, delayed, or redirected to photos and virtual tours only — and particularly if this is coupled with any of the other warning signs on this list — you should stop the conversation.

A simple video call over WhatsApp or FaceTime, in which the landlord walks through the actual property, is a reasonable minimum if an in-person visit is genuinely not possible. If even that is refused, walk away.

---

## Warning Sign 5: Payment Demanded by Wire, Zelle, or Gift Card

This is the clearest signal that you are dealing with a scam. Legitimate landlords accept bank transfer (with a proper tenancy agreement already signed) or agency-managed payments. They do not ask for:

- Wire transfers to accounts you cannot verify
- Zelle or other peer-to-peer payment apps
- Gift cards (Google Play, iTunes, Amazon, or any other kind)
- Cryptocurrency

These payment methods have one thing in common: once the money leaves, there is almost no way to get it back. Scammers choose them for exactly that reason. No legitimate landlord requires you to pay a deposit with a Google Play card.

If you receive a payment request like this, stop all contact and report the listing to the platform where you found it.

## Warning Sign 6: Pressure to Pay Before Signing Anything

A genuine tenancy works in a clear sequence: you view the property, you agree terms, you sign a tenancy agreement, and then you pay a holding deposit. That order exists to protect both parties.

Scammers reverse or skip most of those steps. They tell you there are other interested parties ready to take the property. They say the deposit needs to be paid today to hold it. They may even send you a fake tenancy agreement — designed to look official enough to justify the payment — before any viewing has taken place.

Take your time. If being careful about a large sum of money causes a landlord to withdraw the offer, that landlord was never going to let you the property in the first place.

## Warning Sign 7: The Email Address Does Not Match

Once a scammer has your contact details from the listing platform, communication moves to email — and that is where you can catch them with a quick check.

Look at the sender's actual email address, not just the display name. A display name can say anything: "John Davies, Property Owner." But the real address might be something like `jdavies.rentals-london@gmail.com` or a domain registered two weeks ago that has no connection to any real property business.

## Warning Sign 8: Emails With Links to "Complete Your Application" or "Confirm Your Payment"

Rental scammers often follow up their initial contact with emails containing links to forms, payment pages, or fake property management portals. These pages are designed to harvest your personal details or your banking credentials.

Do not click a link in an email from someone you have not verified. Instead, copy the link text (right-click on desktop, press and hold on phone) and run it through the Email Scam Checker before your browser visits it. The checker will tell you whether the destination is a known phishing site or a domain with suspicious signals.

## What to Do If You Think You Have Found a Scam

If any of the warning signs above appear — particularly if you are feeling rushed — take these steps before you do anything else:

1. **Stop all communication** with the person who contacted you.
2. **Report the listing** to the platform where you found it. Most sites (Zillow, Craigslist, Facebook Marketplace, Rightmove, Gumtree) have a "Report this listing" button.
3. **If you have already sent money**, contact your bank immediately. The faster you act, the better your chances of stopping or reversing the transaction.
4. **Report to the relevant consumer protection body** — in the US, file at reportfraud.ftc.gov; in the UK, report to Action Fraud.

If you are not sure whether a listing or email is suspicious, the Email Scam Checker can help you make that call before you commit to anything.

## FAQ

### How do I check if a rental listing is real?

Start with the photos — run a reverse image search on at least two of them. Then compare the listed rent against similar properties in the area. Search the address on Google Maps to confirm the property exists. If you have received an email from the landlord, [check the sender's address and any links in it here](/blog/scamcheck?sub=rental-listing-scam-how-to-check-2026__faq). Never send money before you have signed a verified tenancy agreement.

### What are the biggest red flags in a rental listing?

The three strongest red flags are: a landlord who is always unavailable to show the property in person; a request for payment by wire transfer, Zelle, or gift card before a tenancy agreement is signed; and a listing price significantly below what comparable properties rent for in the same area. Any one of those should slow you down. Two or more together is a strong reason to stop entirely.

### Can I check a landlord's email address to see if it is genuine?

Yes. Copy the email address or the full email content and paste it into Surfshark's Email Scam Checker. The tool checks whether the sending domain is legitimate, whether the technical records match who the sender claims to be, and whether any links in the email lead to known scam sites. It does not require any technical knowledge and takes less than a minute.

### What should I do if I already sent a deposit and suspect a scam?

Contact your bank immediately and explain that you believe you have been the victim of a fraud. If you paid by bank transfer, ask for an urgent recall of the payment. If you paid by card, request a chargeback. If you paid through Zelle or a peer-to-peer app, contact their support teams directly — recovery is harder but not always impossible. Report the incident to your national consumer protection body and to the platform where you found the listing. Change the password for any email account you used in the communication, in case it has been targeted for further fraud.

### Are rental listing scams common on major platforms?

They appear on all major platforms because scammers deliberately post there to appear legitimate. Craigslist, Facebook Marketplace, Zillow, Gumtree, and similar sites all have fraud reporting tools, and they remove listings when flagged — but scammers repost quickly. Seeing a listing on a well-known platform does not verify it is genuine. The eight checks in this guide apply regardless of which platform you are using.
