## Step 1: Enable the Spam Filter in Google Messages

Android's Messages app has a built-in spam filter powered by Google's detection algorithms. It's off by default on some devices.

**How to turn it on:**

1. Open the **Messages** app
2. Tap your profile icon (top right)
3. Select **Messages settings**
4. Tap **Spam protection**
5. Toggle **Enable spam protection** to on

![Step-by-step illustrated guide showing Android Messages spam filter settings screen in dark mode](/og/how-to-stop-spam-texts-on-android-2026-inline-1.png)

**What this does:** Google's system scans incoming messages for known spam patterns — fake delivery notifications, [phishing link](https://snipstack.io/phishing-link-checker-free-2026/)s, [lottery scam](https://snipstack.io/lottery-scam-email-how-to-spot-2026/)s — and moves them to a separate "Spam & blocked" folder instead of your main inbox.

**What this doesn't do:** It won't catch every spam text, especially from new sender numbers that haven't been flagged yet. It also won't block calls, only messages.

**Troubleshooting:** If you don't see the Spam protection option, update the Messages app via the Play Store and check again. Some older Samsung or manufacturer-branded devices use a different default messaging app — the steps will differ, but the setting exists under similar names.

## Step 2: Use Your Carrier's Spam Blocking Tools

Major US carriers offer their own spam filters, separate from Google's. These work at the network level, which means they can catch spam before it even reaches your device.

### AT&T

1. Download the **AT&T ActiveArmor** app (free on Play Store)
2. Sign in with your AT&T account
3. Enable **Automatic Fraud Blocking** and **Nuisance Alert** settings
4. Optionally enable **Call Protect** for calls as well

AT&T's tool also shows you a fraud risk score for unknown numbers, which is useful for deciding whether to answer.

### Verizon

1. Open the **My Verizon** app or go to verizon.com
2. Navigate to **Add-ons & Apps**
3. Enable **Call Filter** (the free tier blocks known spam numbers; the paid tier adds spam risk scores and a spam filter for texts)
4. For text-specific filtering, go to **Account** > **Manage Products & Apps** > **Message+** settings

### T-Mobile

1. Open the **T-Mobile** app
2. Go to **More** > **Scam Shield**
3. Enable **Scam Block** and **Scam ID**
4. T-Mobile's system is largely automatic — scam calls are labeled in real time on your device

All three carriers update their block lists regularly. This layer catches a lot of the high-volume spam campaigns that originate from known bad numbers.

**Troubleshooting:** Some carrier features require you to be the account owner. If you're on a [family plan](https://snipstack.io/family-plan-security-suite-comparison-2026/) and don't have account access, ask the account holder to enable these settings.

## Step 3: Block and Report Individual Numbers

For texts that slip through the filters, manual blocking is your next tool.

**In Google Messages:**

1. Open the spam text thread
2. Tap the **three-dot menu** (top right)
3. Select **Block & report spam**
4. Confirm

This does two things: blocks that number from contacting you, and sends the message to Google to improve their filter for everyone.

**For numbers that text repeatedly from slightly different numbers** (common with robotexter campaigns that rotate through numbers): blocking individual numbers won't fully stop them. You'll need to rely on the spam filter layers above, or consider a third-party app with pattern-matching.

**Troubleshooting:** If you accidentally block someone you know, go to **Messages settings** > **Spam & blocked** > **Blocked contacts** and remove them.

## Step 5: File an FTC Complaint

If you're receiving spam texts at volume — especially ones that are clearly fraudulent — filing a complaint with the FTC is worth doing.

**How to file:**

1. Go to **reportfraud.ftc.gov**
2. Select **Unwanted calls, emails, and texts**
3. Choose **Unwanted text messages**
4. Provide the sender's number, the content of the message, and the date received
5. Submit

You can also forward spam texts to **7726** (SPAM) — this is a universal shortcode accepted by all major US carriers and feeds directly into carrier and FTC spam databases.

**What this accomplishes:** Individual complaints feed into enforcement actions against large spam operations. The FTC uses aggregated complaint data to identify patterns and pursue legal action. One complaint rarely stops anything on its own — but the data is used.

**What it won't do:** Filing a complaint won't immediately stop the texts. It's a long-term contribution to enforcement, not a quick fix.

## FAQ

**Does Google Messages spam filter work without a data connection?**

The filter itself runs locally, but Google's spam detection relies on updated databases that sync when you're connected. If you're offline, recently-identified spam patterns may not be caught until you reconnect.

**My carrier isn't AT&T, Verizon, or T-Mobile — do I have options?**

Most regional carriers and MVNOs offer some form of spam blocking, though the tools vary. Check your carrier's app or website for "spam," "scam," or "call protection" settings. Failing that, Hiya and Truecaller work independently of your carrier and are the better option for MVNO users.

**Is it legal to file an FTC complaint about spam texts?**

Yes. The FTC actively encourages it. Under the Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA), unsolicited spam texts are illegal in most circumstances. Reporting them is the mechanism for enforcement.

**Can I use an alias number for dating app verification without getting rejected?**

[Surfshark Alt-Number](/blog/altnumber?sub=how-to-stop-spam-texts-on-android-2026__faq) uses real mobile numbers rather than VoIP lines, which are the type that apps commonly flag. Verification codes typically go through. That said, individual app behavior varies — test with one app before making it your standard setup.

**Will blocking a number on Google Messages also block them from calling me?**

In most cases, yes — blocking via Messages blocks both texts and calls from that number. If you want to be sure, also check your Phone app's blocked numbers list to confirm the number appears there.

**How often should I rotate an alias number if I'm actively dating?**

There's no fixed rule. Some people rotate after every match that doesn't work out; others keep the same alias for months. The point is that you *can* replace it whenever you want without affecting your real number. Decide based on how much spam activity you're seeing.
