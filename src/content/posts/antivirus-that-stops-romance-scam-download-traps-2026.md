Scammers have learned that older adults are more likely to be online, more likely to be lonely, and — frankly — more likely to trust a friendly voice. The IC3 report notes that people over 60 lost more money to call-center and confidence-style fraud than every other age group combined. It's not that seniors are less careful. ## How the Download Trap Actually Works

Understanding the mechanism makes it much easier to spot — and to know what your antivirus is actually defending against.

![Illustration of a fake photo attachment icon hiding a malware warning symbol](/og/antivirus-that-stops-romance-scam-download-traps-2026-inline-1.png)

### The "Photo" That Isn't a Photo

A new online contact sends what looks like a picture — sometimes labeled as a photo album, a video, or a document. In reality, it can be a disguised program. Opening it may not show anything obviously wrong on screen, which is exactly why it's dangerous: the file can run quietly while you go on with your day, unaware anything happened.

### The App You're Told You "Need" to Install

As the relationship builds, the scammer may say a special app is needed to keep chatting privately, to make a video call, or to access an "exclusive" investment platform. These apps are sometimes copies of real ones, modified to spy on your device or steal information typed into it — passwords, banking logins, and personal details.

### Why These Files Slip Past "Common Sense"

Most seniors are far too smart to click on an obvious scam. That's precisely why these tricks aren't obvious. The file names look normal. The sender seems like a real person who has spent weeks building trust. There's no red flashing warning sign — which is exactly why software that checks files automatically, rather than relying on you noticing something is wrong, matters so much.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can antivirus really stop a romance scam?**
Antivirus software can't stop a scammer from lying to you or manipulating your emotions — that part is about awareness and caution. What it can do is scan the files and links a scammer sends, and block or flag ones that carry malware, which is often the technical half of these schemes.

**What does a "download trap" actually look like?**
It's usually a file disguised as something harmless — a photo, a video, a document, or an app — sent by someone you've been chatting with online. Once opened, it can install software that spies on your device or steals information, without any obvious warning sign.

**Is it enough to just be careful and never click anything?**
Caution helps a lot, but scam files are designed to look completely normal, and even careful people can be fooled after weeks of built-up trust. Real-time antivirus scanning adds a second layer of defense for the moments when a file slips past good judgment.

**Do I need antivirus on my phone, or just my computer?**
Both. A large share of romance scams happen through phone messaging apps and dating apps, so phone protection matters just as much as computer protection. [See which devices Surfshark Antivirus covers](/blog/antivirus?sub=antivirus-that-stops-romance-scam-download-traps-2026__faq).

**What should I do if I think I already downloaded something suspicious?**
Disconnect the device from the internet, run a full antivirus scan, change important passwords from a different, trusted device, and consider reporting the incident to the FBI's IC3 at ic3.gov. If money was sent, contact your bank immediately.

**How can I tell if someone online is a romance scammer?**
Common warning signs include: they avoid video calls or in-person meetings, their story involves work overseas or a sudden emergency, and — most relevant here — they eventually ask you to open a file, install an app, or click a link to "continue" the relationship or an "investment opportunity."

**Are older adults really targeted more than younger people?**
Yes. FBI IC3 data shows people over 60 reported far higher total fraud losses than any other age group in 2023, and call-center and confidence-style scams disproportionately target this age group.

Scammers are patient, and they're counting on the download trap slipping through unnoticed. A little preparation now — simple habits plus antivirus software that actually checks what lands on your device — closes that gap before it costs you anything.
