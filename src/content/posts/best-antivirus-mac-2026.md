## What Is a Virus, and Why Does It Matter?

Before anything else, let us explain the basics — because these words get thrown around a lot.

A **virus** (or malware — short for "malicious software") is a program that gets onto your computer without your permission. It might:
- Steal passwords you type
- Lock your photos and demand money to release them (called ransomware)
- Spy on what you are doing online
- Pretend to be Apple and show you a scary fake warning

A **scam website** is a fake webpage — often a fake bank login page or fake Apple account page — designed to trick you into entering your details.

**[Antivirus software](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-for-iphone-2026/)** is a program that watches for these threats and stops them before they cause harm.

You do not need to understand exactly how any of this works. You just need the right tools turned on — and this guide will walk you through exactly that.

## "But I Thought Macs Were Safe?"

This is the most common question we hear — and the most important one to answer honestly.

Macs do have strong built-in security. Apple includes tools called **XProtect** and **Gatekeeper** that block many known threats automatically. These are real protections, and they do work.

But here is what those tools do not cover:

- **Scam websites.** If you click a link in a [fake email](https://snipstack.io/fake-email-checker-2026/), Apple's tools will not stop the website loading in your browser.
- **Phishing.** A fake Apple login page looks identical to the real one. Apple cannot stop you typing your password into it.
- **New threats.** XProtect updates regularly, but there is always a gap between a new threat appearing and Apple blocking it.
- **Your email and personal data.** If your email address is leaked in a data breach — which happens to millions of people — Apple has no tool to warn you.

The myth that Macs cannot be infected comes from a time when far fewer people used Macs. Today, with hundreds of millions of Mac users worldwide, attackers have strong reasons to target them.

**You still need a layer of protection beyond what Apple provides.** The good news is that it is easy to add.

## What Apple Already Does For You

You do not need to install anything for these protections — they are already running on your Mac.

### XProtect

This is Apple's built-in virus scanner. It automatically checks files you download against a list of known malicious software. It updates silently, so you never have to do anything.

### Gatekeeper

When you try to open an app you downloaded from the internet, Gatekeeper checks whether it comes from a trusted source. If it does not recognise the developer, it will warn you before the app opens.

### Safari Fraudulent Website Warning

Apple's Safari browser has a built-in warning that appears if you navigate to a known scam website. It will show a red warning page and recommend you go back.

These are genuine protections, and they do help. But as the section above explains, they have real limits — especially when it comes to scam emails, phishing pages, and newer threats that have not yet been added to Apple's known-threat database.

## The Threats Most Likely to Affect Mac Users Over 60

Cybercriminals increasingly target older adults, because they know this group is less likely to spot the warning signs. These are the most common threats to be aware of:

### [Tech Support Scam](https://snipstack.io/tech-support-scam-phone-call-identify-2026/)s

A pop-up appears on your screen. It says your Mac has a virus. It shows an "Apple" logo. It gives you a [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/) to call. **This is a scam.** Real Apple warnings never give you a [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-privacy-for-social-media-2026/). [Antivirus software](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-2026/) can block the websites that trigger these pop-ups.

### Phishing Emails

You receive an email that looks like it is from Apple, your bank, Amazon, or another company you trust. The email asks you to click a link and log in. The link goes to a fake website that steals your details.

### Password-Stealing Software

Some malicious programs sit quietly on your Mac and record the keys you press — including passwords. These can arrive disguised as free apps or attachments in emails.

### Public Wi-Fi Risks

If you use your Mac at a library, coffee shop, or airport, the Wi-Fi connection is shared with strangers. Without extra protection, someone on the same network could potentially monitor what you are doing online.
