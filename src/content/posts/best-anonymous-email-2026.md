## 2. SimpleLogin

**The best open-source alias manager.**

SimpleLogin lets you create unlimited [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-sign-2026/)es on its free tier (up to 10 aliases, then paid) and routes incoming mail straight to your real inbox. Replies work through the alias too, so two-way email stays anonymous. The service is open-source, audited, and — since its acquisition by Proton — tightly integrated with ProtonMail accounts.

The browser extension makes alias creation one-click while you are filling out a signup form, which is where most unwanted subscriptions start. Power users appreciate the custom domain support: point your own domain at SimpleLogin and you get branded aliases like `shopping@yourdomain.com` that look intentional rather than disposable.

Where SimpleLogin stops short is identity generation. It creates an alias address, nothing more. If a signup form also asks for a name and [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/), you are on your own. For inbox protection alone, it is outstanding; for full data minimisation, pair it with a separate identity generator.

## 3. AnonAddy (Addy.io)

**Best for high-volume alias creation on a budget.**

Rebranded as Addy.io in 2024, AnonAddy has always appealed to the technically inclined user who wants granular control over alias behaviour. The free tier is generous: unlimited aliases with a bandwidth cap, custom recipients, and per-alias reply controls. Paid plans add custom domains, unlimited bandwidth, and GPG encryption for forwarded email.

One standout feature is the ability to set catch-all aliases on a shared subdomain — every email sent to `anythinghere@yoursubdomain.anonaddy.com` lands in your inbox, so you never need to create an alias in advance. This is useful for detecting which service sold your address, since the alias name tells you exactly where you signed up.

The interface is more technical than Alt-ID or SimpleLogin, and there is no companion identity generator. But for sheer flexibility and a near-zero cost entry point, AnonAddy remains one of the most capable tools available.

## 4. ProtonMail

**Best for a fully private, encrypted inbox.**

ProtonMail is not an alias service — it is a complete private email provider, headquartered in Switzerland, with end-to-end encryption baked in at the server level. When you create a ProtonMail account, you get a `@proton.me` address that is genuinely separate from your existing accounts. Paid plans add alias addresses, custom domains, and the ability to use SimpleLogin natively inside the Proton ecosystem.

For people who want a clean break from a compromised or over-exposed address, ProtonMail is the right tool. Set up a new account, use it as your permanent private address, and let the aliases (via SimpleLogin integration) handle the throwaway signups. The combination covers both use cases.

The free tier is usable but limited to 1 GB of storage and one address. The paid Proton Mail Plus plan is reasonably priced and worth it if you are migrating your main correspondence here.

## 5. Tutanota (Tuta)

**Best encrypted email if you want a Google-free experience.**

Rebranded as Tuta in 2023, Tutanota has been building encrypted email since 2011. Like ProtonMail, it is a full mailbox — not an alias layer — with zero-knowledge encryption meaning even Tuta cannot read your messages. The free tier gives you 1 GB of storage and one custom address. Paid plans unlock multiple addresses, custom domains, and larger storage.

Tuta differentiates itself with a calendar and contacts app that are also end-to-end encrypted, making it a reasonable alternative to the Google productivity suite for privacy-first users. The mobile apps are clean and fast. Custom domain support is available on all paid plans, which is cheaper than Proton's equivalent tier.

The tradeoff: Tuta uses its own encryption standard rather than the widely supported OpenPGP, so encrypted email only works between Tuta users or when you send a password-protected link to external recipients. If you correspond heavily with ProtonMail users who expect PGP compatibility, Proton is the better fit.

## 6. Guerrilla Mail

**Best for instant, no-signup throwaway addresses.**

Guerrilla Mail has been around since 2006 and its model remains simple and unchanged: visit the website, get a temporary inbox, use it, walk away. No account required. No email address of your own needed. The inbox is public (in the sense that anyone who knows the address can read it), and messages expire after an hour.

This is not a service for ongoing privacy — it is a fire-and-forget tool for the single verification email you need to complete a signup you will never use again. Use it whenever a site gates free content behind an email confirmation and you have no interest in ever hearing from them.

The downsides are obvious: no persistence, no two-way email, no control. But for that narrow use case, nothing is faster or easier.

## 7. iCloud Hide My Email

**Best for Apple users who want alias creation built into the OS.**

Apple's Hide My Email feature, included with any iCloud+ subscription (from $0.99/month), generates random `@privaterelay.appleid.com` aliases directly in Safari, the App Store, and iOS settings. When you tap "Sign in with Apple" on a supported app, the alias is created automatically before you even think about it.

The convenience factor is unmatched for anyone already in the Apple ecosystem. Alias management lives in Settings, and you can deactivate individual aliases without affecting others. The limitation is platform lock-in — this only works smoothly within Apple apps and Safari on Apple devices. Using it on Windows or Android browsers is clunky, and there is no browser extension for non-Safari use.

If you are iPhone-first and already paying for iCloud+, Hide My Email deserves to be in your toolkit. If you work across platforms, Alt-ID or SimpleLogin will serve you better.

## FAQ

### Is anonymous email actually untraceable?

No tool can promise complete untraceability — email headers, metadata, and account payment details can all create a trail under certain conditions. These services significantly reduce your exposure by keeping your real address private and limiting data collection, but they should not be treated as tools for evading legal accountability.

### Does Surfshark Alt-ID work for two-way email?

Yes. Emails sent to your Alt-ID alias forward to your real inbox, and you can reply through the alias so the recipient sees only the alias address. Your real email is never exposed in the exchange.

Learn more at the official listing: [Surfshark Alt-ID on Surfshark One](/blog/surfshark?sub=best-anonymous-email-2026__faq)

### Can I use multiple alias services at the same time?

Absolutely. Many privacy-conscious users run Alt-ID for new signups, SimpleLogin for existing accounts they want to migrate, and ProtonMail as their primary private inbox. There is no technical or practical reason to limit yourself to one tool.

### Will these aliases stop all spam?

They dramatically reduce it. If you use a unique alias per service, spam can only reach that alias — and you can switch it off immediately. Your real inbox stays clean because it was never shared. What these tools cannot do is retroactively protect an address that is already circulating.

### [What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) if a service I signed up with suffers a data breach?

If you used an alias, the breached data only reveals that alias — not your real email address. You delete the alias and create a new one for that service. Surfshark One also includes a data breach alert feature that monitors whether your details appear in known breach datasets.
