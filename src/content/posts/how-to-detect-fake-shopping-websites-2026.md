## Quick Verdict

If you only remember one thing: **cross-check at least two sources before buying from an unfamiliar site.** No single tool is foolproof — brand-new scam shops have no review history yet, and some review platforms can be gamed. The strongest combination is a real-time risk-score tool (ScamAdviser or Surfshark Scamcheck) paired with independent review aggregators (Trustpilot or BBB). For email-based shopping scams — those "exclusive deal" links that land in your inbox — a dedicated email scam checker catches what generic site checkers miss.

## ScamAdviser

ScamAdviser pulls from a wide range of signals: domain registration age, server location, whether the site has been blacklisted, Alexa-style traffic estimates, and SSL certificate data. It returns a trust score from 0–100 within seconds.

**Strengths:** Fast. Works on any URL. Catches very new domains — a classic red flag since most scam shops register their domain days before launch. The free tier is genuinely useful.

**Weaknesses:** Score can be skewed by shared hosting environments (a legit site on the same IP as a flagged one may score lower). Does not read review content, so a high-traffic scam site could score surprisingly well.

**Verdict:** Good first-pass filter. Always pair it with at least one review source.

---

## Trustpilot

Trustpilot is a review aggregation platform where verified buyers leave ratings. Most large e-commerce brands have a Trustpilot profile, and the star distribution (look at the 1-star reviews, not the average) often tells the real story.

**Strengths:** Large database. Reviews are tied to verified purchases on some integrations. Pattern-spotting is easy — if 40% of reviews say "never arrived" or "impossible to reach customer service," that is a signal.

**Weaknesses:** Not real-time. Brand-new fake stores have zero reviews. Some merchants have been caught soliciting fake positive reviews. A clean profile does not equal a safe site.

**Verdict:** Excellent for stores that have been around long enough to accumulate reviews. Useless for sites launched last week.

## Google Reviews & Google Shopping

Google's star ratings appear directly in search results and on Google Maps for businesses with a physical or registered presence. Google Shopping, meanwhile, surfaces product listings from verified merchant feeds.

**Strengths:** Zero friction — you see ratings without visiting a separate site. Merchants must meet Google's policies to run Shopping ads, which filters out some scam operations.

**Weaknesses:** Star ratings are easy to inflate with fake accounts. A business can have glowing Google reviews and still be fraudulent. Coverage is patchy for smaller or newer shops.

**Verdict:** Use it as a quick sanity check, not a trust signal on its own.

---

## Better Business Bureau (BBB)

The BBB tracks complaint history, resolution rates, and grants accreditation to businesses that meet its standards. It is particularly useful for US-registered businesses.

**Strengths:** Complaint history is searchable and specific — you can see *what* customers complained about and whether the business responded. Accreditation (A+ rating) carries some weight because it requires active participation.

**Weaknesses:** BBB is US-centric, so international scam shops operating without a US registration often have no BBB profile at all. A missing profile is not itself a red flag, but it removes a data point.

**Verdict:** Valuable for US-based e-commerce. Less useful for cross-border shopping sites.

## 5-Step Quick Check Before Any Purchase

Run these five steps in under two minutes before entering your payment details on any unfamiliar store.

**Step 1 — Check domain age.** Paste the URL into ScamAdviser or any WHOIS lookup. If the domain is less than six months old, treat it as unverified until you complete the rest of the steps.

**Step 2 — Scan the link.** Use Surfshark Scamcheck to run the URL against real-time threat databases. A clean result does not clear all doubt, but a flagged result is a hard stop.

**Step 3 — Search reviews off-site.** Google "[store name] reviews reddit" or "[store name] scam" — not "[store name] reviews" which can surface planted testimonials. Reddit threads and consumer forums are much harder to fake.

**Step 4 — Inspect the contact page.** Legitimate stores have a real physical address, a working support email, and ideally a phone number. A contact form with no other details is a warning sign.

**Step 5 — Verify the payment method.** Scam shops often push bank transfer, cryptocurrency, or gift cards because those payments are irreversible. A site that only accepts these methods and not credit cards or PayPal should be avoided.

## FAQ

### How do I know if a shopping website is legitimate?

Cross-reference at least two signals: a real-time trust score (ScamAdviser or Scamcheck) and independent reviews that you find through search — not reviews hosted on the site itself. A legitimate store will have a verifiable presence that predates your search.

### Can scam websites have SSL certificates (the padlock icon)?

Yes. SSL certificates are free and take minutes to obtain. The padlock means the connection between your browser and the server is encrypted — it says nothing about whether the person running the server is honest. Never rely on the padlock alone.

### What if I already paid a fake website?

Contact your bank or card issuer immediately and request a chargeback. If you paid by credit card, consumer protection laws in most countries give you strong recourse. Report the site to your national cybercrime agency and to Google's Safe Browsing report tool so it gets flagged for others.

### Are email-based shopping scams different from website scams?

They often overlap. Scam sites frequently drive traffic through phishing emails that impersonate well-known brands ("Your Amazon order is on hold — click here"). The email is the delivery mechanism; the fake site is the trap. Tools like Surfshark Scamcheck are built to handle both — you can check the link from the email before you ever open a browser tab.

### What are the most common red flags on fake shopping sites?

Prices dramatically below market value, no clear return policy, no verifiable business address, recently registered domains, payment methods that exclude credit cards, and product images that reverse-image-search back to other retailers. Any two of these together should put you on high alert.
