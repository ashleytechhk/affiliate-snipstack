## Why Your Real Number on Dating Apps Is a Liability

### The Verification Trap

Every major dating app — Tinder, Hinge, Bumble, OkCupid — requires phone number verification at signup. That verification number gets stored in their database, attached to your profile, and tied to your account history.

When you hand your personal number to a dating app at signup, you are handing it to everyone who will ever breach, buy from, or partner with that app. Dating platforms have had significant data incidents. In 2021, a scraping operation exposed the personal data of over 700 million LinkedIn users, including phone numbers — demonstrating that even data not intended for public display ends up in bulk data sets. Dating platforms, which store explicitly personal information including photos, are even more attractive targets.

### The Match Who Becomes a Problem

The more immediate risk is not a corporate breach. It is the match who seemed fine until they were not.

When you share your real number with a date — during a match, at the end of a first conversation, in a "let's text instead" moment — you hand them a key to multiple parts of your digital life. Your real number is linked to your:

- WhatsApp and iMessage identity
- Google account (via phone verification)
- Apple ID and iCloud
- Banking and financial apps that use SMS two-factor authentication
- Any social platform where you registered with that number

Blocking someone on a dating app does not block them from your phone number. If they already have it, they still have it. Every text, every call, every SIM-swap attempt becomes possible from that point forward.

### SIM-Swap: The Risk Most Daters Ignore

SIM-swapping — where an attacker convinces your carrier to transfer your number to a device they control — is now documented as a threat vector emerging from dating app interactions. The attack requires knowing your phone number plus collecting a few pieces of identifying information through conversation. An attacker who knows your number, your carrier, and a few personal details (your birthday, your employer, your mother's name) can often social-engineer a SIM transfer.

Once they control your number, every SMS-based two-factor code you receive — for your bank, your email, your trading app — goes to them instead of you.

The match asking about your job, your bank, or which phone plan you use may not be making small talk.
