## What Is Hotel Wifi, and Why Is It Risky?

Hotel wifi is a shared public network. Unlike your home router — which you control and which only your devices connect to — a hotel network might have hundreds of guests connected simultaneously across every floor. The network infrastructure is often managed by a third-party provider, and security standards vary widely.

The specific risks fall into a few categories:

**Unencrypted connections.** Many hotel networks use no WPA2/WPA3 encryption at the router level, meaning data sent over the network travels in a form that can be read by anyone with packet-capture software. Even networks that require a room code often provide weak or shared credentials.

**Man-in-the-middle attacks.** A bad actor on the same network can intercept traffic between your device and the websites you visit — particularly dangerous if any of those sites still use unencrypted HTTP connections (not HTTPS).

**Rogue hotspots.** A common trick is setting up a fake wifi network with a name similar to the hotel's official one — "Hotel_Guest_Free" instead of "Hotel Guest WiFi." Devices that auto-connect to familiar-sounding networks are especially vulnerable.

None of this is hypothetical. Security researchers regularly demonstrate these attack techniques at conferences, and business travellers are considered a high-value target because they're likely to handle sensitive emails, corporate logins, and financial accounts while away from the office.

## What Is a VPN and How Does It Help on Hotel Wifi?

A VPN — Virtual Private Network — works by creating an encrypted tunnel between your device and a server operated by the VPN provider. All your internet traffic is routed through that tunnel before reaching its destination.

Here's why that matters for hotel wifi specifically:

1. **Your traffic is encrypted at the device level.** Even if someone on the hotel network intercepts your data, they see encrypted noise rather than readable information.
2. **Your real IP address is hidden.** Websites and services see the VPN server's IP instead of your device's, which also helps with regional content access.
3. **DNS requests are protected.** Without a VPN, DNS queries (the requests your device makes to resolve website addresses) can be logged or spoofed. A good VPN routes these through its own encrypted DNS servers.

What a VPN does *not* do: it doesn't make your device immune to malware you download, it doesn't fix vulnerabilities in the apps you use, and it doesn't make you completely invisible online. It's a privacy and encryption tool — a meaningful one for hotel wifi specifically, but not a catch-all security solution.

## Which Features Matter Most for Hotel Wifi Use

Not all VPNs are equally suited to the hotel wifi scenario. When comparing options, prioritise these:

### Kill Switch

A kill switch automatically cuts your internet connection if the VPN drops unexpectedly. On hotel wifi — where connection quality can be inconsistent — this matters. Without it, your device might briefly revert to unprotected traffic while the VPN reconnects.

### Strong Encryption Protocol

Look for WireGuard or OpenVPN support. Both are well-audited protocols, and WireGuard in particular performs well on variable-quality connections like hotel wifi.

### No-logs Policy (Independently Audited)

A VPN that logs your activity undermines the point. Look for providers with no-logs claims verified by third-party auditors, not just self-reported.

### Multi-device Support

Travellers carry multiple devices. VPNs that cap connections at 5 or 6 can be a friction point; Surfshark's unlimited device policy removes that limit entirely.

### Server Coverage in Your Home Region

For streaming access while abroad, you need servers in the country where your accounts are registered. Any reputable paid VPN covers the US, UK, and most of Europe.

## How to Set Up a VPN on Hotel Wifi: Step by Step

Getting a VPN running takes about five minutes, even if you've never used one:

1. **Sign up and download the app** before you travel. All three providers above have apps for iOS, Android, Windows, and macOS.
2. **Enable the kill switch** in settings. This is usually under Privacy or Connection settings.
3. **Connect to your home-country server** before opening any other apps or browsers. Don't connect to hotel wifi first and then launch the VPN — the brief unprotected window matters.
4. **Verify the connection.** Most VPN apps show a green indicator when active. You can also visit a site that shows your IP address to confirm the location has changed.
5. **Browse or stream as normal.** Platforms will see your VPN server's location rather than the hotel's.

Tip: set the VPN to auto-connect on public networks. Most quality apps support this — it removes the risk of forgetting.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does using a VPN on hotel wifi actually make a difference?

Yes, for a specific threat: it encrypts your traffic so that other people on the same network cannot read what you're sending and receiving. Without a VPN, anyone on the hotel network with basic packet-capture tools can potentially see unencrypted data. With a VPN, they see encrypted traffic that isn't practically useful to them. It doesn't protect against threats unrelated to network interception — like malware or phishing — but for hotel wifi specifically, it's the most effective tool available.

### Is it legal to use a VPN while travelling?

VPNs are legal in most countries. Laws vary, so if you're travelling to regions with specific internet regulations, check local rules. For most business or leisure travellers in Europe, North America, Australia, and similar destinations, using a VPN is entirely lawful. ### Can I use a VPN to access my home streaming subscriptions abroad?

Yes. By connecting to a server in your home country, streaming platforms see a local IP address and grant access to your home library. This is one of the most common reasons travellers keep a VPN subscription active year-round. Review the terms of service for individual platforms if you have questions about their policies.

### Will a VPN slow down hotel wifi?

A VPN adds a small amount of overhead to your connection. On hotel wifi, which is often already slower than home broadband, this can occasionally be noticeable. WireGuard-based VPNs (which Surfshark and NordVPN both support) minimise this overhead. For HD streaming and general browsing, the impact is typically small enough that most users don't notice it in practice.

### How many devices can I protect on one VPN subscription?

It depends on the provider. ExpressVPN allows 8 simultaneous connections, NordVPN allows 10. Surfshark allows unlimited simultaneous connections, which makes it particularly practical for travellers carrying a phone, laptop, and tablet — or for covering a partner's devices on the same trip.
