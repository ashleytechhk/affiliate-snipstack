## The Problem With Your Current Setup

### Norton and McAfee's Dirty Renewal Trick

Both Norton and McAfee use the same playbook: a low introductory price to get you in, followed by a significantly higher auto-renewal rate when year one expires. It is not unusual to see introductory pricing sit around $40–50, then auto-renew at $100–150 per year at full sticker. If you missed the renewal email — which arrives during finals week, naturally — you have already been charged.

### Bloatware That Eats Your Laptop Alive

Student laptops are often mid-range machines: 8 GB of RAM, a mid-tier CPU, already taxed by browser tabs, Zoom calls, and whatever project you have open in the background. Norton's background processes are notorious for consuming RAM and CPU headroom. McAfee is not much lighter. The result: a laptop that stutters during presentations and takes 90 seconds to wake from sleep.

### What You Are Actually Getting (And Not Getting)

Standard [Norton 360](https://snipstack.io/norton-360-antivirus-2026/) and McAfee Total Protection plans include antivirus. A VPN is locked behind higher-tier plans — and even then, those VPNs are limited-data or feature-stripped compared to a dedicated service. You end up paying for a [security suite](https://snipstack.io/security-suite-for-unlimited-devices-2026/) to get a half-baked VPN you probably do not use.

On campus, a VPN is not optional — it is practical. Dorm WiFi and university networks are shared, monitored, and sometimes throttled. A working VPN protects your traffic on shared networks and lets you connect to services as if you were home.

### Campus WiFi Is Not Safe WiFi

University networks are shared infrastructure. Every student on the same dorm subnet can potentially intercept unencrypted traffic from other devices — and campus IT departments often log traffic for compliance reasons. A VPN encrypts your connection at the device level, so what you do online stays between you and your destination, not your university's network logs.

This matters for ordinary things: logging into banking apps on shared WiFi, using library databases from a coffee shop, or just not wanting your browsing habits archived by your ISP.
