## Quick Verdict

The legal distinction between a pyramid scheme and a legitimate MLM comes down to one question: is real money being made from selling real products to real customers who are not in the business? If the primary income source is recruiting new participants rather than product sales, it is a pyramid scheme regardless of what the company calls itself. In practice, identifying which category an offer falls into requires looking at several signals at once. The table below gives you a side-by-side reference, and the sections that follow explain each factor in detail.

## Why the Confusion Exists

Both pyramid schemes and MLMs use a tiered structure where people above you in the hierarchy earn money when you make sales or recruit others. Both reward recruitment. Both require some form of payment to join.

The legal difference, according to the US Federal Trade Commission, is whether the compensation plan is primarily driven by recruitment fees or by genuine product sales to end consumers. A business that pays primarily on recruitment — regardless of whether it has nominal products attached — is a pyramid scheme under US law.

The problem is that many pyramid schemes attach real products to the business model specifically to create legal ambiguity. This is sometimes called a "product-based pyramid scheme" or, informally, a "pseudo-MLM." The products exist, but they are priced far above market value, overstock purchases are required to maintain commission eligibility, and the vast majority of participants lose money.

## The 7 Red Flags That Identify a Pyramid Scheme

### 1. Income Is Driven by Recruitment, Not Sales

This is the defining test. Ask the person pitching you: what percentage of their income comes from selling products to people who are not in the business, versus from recruiting new participants?

If they cannot answer clearly, or if the answer reveals that virtually all income flows from signing up new members, you are looking at a pyramid scheme. In a legitimate MLM, retail sales to customers outside the distributor network should represent a meaningful share of revenue.

The FTC's 2023 guidance on MLMs specifically states that a company is operating unlawfully if it requires participants to recruit others in order to recoup their initial investment.

---

### 2. The Products Cannot Be Sold at a Profit Without Recruiting

Try a simple thought experiment: if you joined, bought the starter kit, and attempted to sell the products to friends, neighbours, or online — with no recruiting — could you turn a profit?

In a genuine MLM, the products need to be competitive enough that retail sales alone are viable. In a pyramid scheme, the products are either too expensive to move at retail price, or there is effectively no retail market for them outside the distributor network. The only realistic way to make money is to bring others in who then buy the products themselves.

---

### 3. Pressure to Buy Large Amounts of Inventory Upfront

A significant red flag is any requirement to purchase substantial quantities of stock in order to qualify for commissions, maintain your distributor status, or achieve the next rank. This is called "inventory loading" and is one of the most common mechanisms by which pyramid schemes extract money from participants.

Reputable MLMs have addressed this specifically: companies that have faced FTC scrutiny now offer 90-day buyback programs allowing distributors to return unsold inventory at 90% of purchase price. If the company you are looking at has no such policy, or if the business opportunity contact is discouraging you from thinking about buybacks, that is a warning sign.

---

### 4. Income Disclosure Statements Show Most Participants Lose Money

Any company operating as a legitimate MLM in the United States is encouraged — and in some states required — to publish an income disclosure statement (IDS). These documents show the actual median and average earnings across distributor ranks.

When these exist, the numbers are frequently sobering: median annual earnings for active distributors are often below a few hundred dollars, with the majority of participants earning less than they spent to participate. This is not necessarily proof of a pyramid scheme — it may simply reflect that most people join part-time with modest goals — but it is essential context when evaluating whether the "business opportunity" pitch reflects reality.

A company that refuses to provide an IDS or cannot produce one on request is a serious red flag.

---

### 5. The Pitch Arrives Unsolicited by Email

Pyramid scheme recruiters often rely on email, social media, and text messages to reach new targets. The recruiting pitch typically comes from someone you know tangentially — a former colleague, a childhood friend, a distant acquaintance — who frames the approach as doing you a personal favour.

The unsolicited nature of the contact, combined with vague descriptions of the "opportunity" designed to create curiosity before revealing the specifics, is a well-documented pattern. Scammers use curiosity as a door-opener because people who might reject a direct sales pitch will often click through to learn more before they have formed a sceptical judgement.

If the initial contact arrived by email, the email address and any links in it can be checked before you engage. Pyramid schemes and the organisations that run them frequently show up in scam databases and phishing blocklists — especially once they have accumulated enough victims.

---

### 6. The "Product" Is the Opportunity Itself

Some schemes have moved away from physical products entirely. The "product" is a course, a training programme, a membership, or access to a business system. You pay to join, and then earn money by selling others access to the same thing.

This structure, sometimes called a "pay-to-play" or "gifting circle" model, is straightforwardly illegal in most jurisdictions. There is no genuine retail value being created — the money flows from new participants to earlier ones until the supply of new recruits runs out.

---

### 7. Emphasis on "Unlimited Earning Potential" and "Financial Freedom"

Language like "unlimited income," "financial freedom," "be your own boss," and "passive income" is not exclusive to pyramid schemes — but it is present in nearly every pyramid scheme pitch. The framing consistently focuses on the income that top earners make while glossing over the statistical reality for average participants.

Compare this to how a legitimate business opportunity is typically described: with specific product ranges, realistic commission structures, named geographic markets, and documented customer demand. The more abstract and aspirational the pitch, the more closely you should look at the underlying mechanics.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is MLM legal?**

Multi-level marketing itself is legal in the United States and most developed countries, provided the primary income source is retail product sales to genuine end consumers rather than recruitment fees. The FTC has pursued enforcement actions against companies operating MLM structures that were functionally pyramid schemes. Legal status depends on how the compensation plan is structured and executed, not on what the company calls itself. [Surfshark Scamcheck](/blog/scamcheck?sub=pyramid-scheme-vs-mlm-how-to-identify-2026__faq) cannot determine legal status, but it can flag whether the company's digital infrastructure matches known scam patterns.

**Q: How do I know if an MLM is legitimate before joining?**

Request the income disclosure statement before making any financial commitment. Legitimate companies publish these and should provide them readily. Check whether the products have a retail market outside the distributor network — look for independent reviews and retail availability online. Search the company name alongside "FTC," "lawsuit," and "complaint" to find any regulatory history. If the company discourages these checks, that is itself a red flag.

**Q: Can I lose money in a legitimate MLM?**

Yes. Income disclosure statements consistently show that the majority of MLM participants earn modest amounts or break even, and a subset incurs a net loss — particularly if they purchased significant inventory that went unsold. Joining an MLM carries financial risk even when the company is operating legally. The risk increases substantially with any requirement to hold large amounts of inventory.

**Q: What should I do if I think I have been targeted by a pyramid scheme via email?**

Do not click any links in the email. Do not reply, as replies confirm your address is active. You can report the email to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov, and in the UK to Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk. If you want to investigate the email without engaging, paste the sender domain and any embedded links into a scam checker tool to see whether they are associated with known fraud infrastructure.

**Q: How can a scam checker help identify pyramid scheme emails?**

A scam checker analyses the technical signals associated with an email or its linked websites — domain age, blacklist status, phishing database matches, and hosting patterns commonly associated with fraudulent operations. Pyramid scheme operators who run email campaigns often use recently registered domains and shared hosting infrastructure that shows up in threat databases before the scheme is widely reported. Running the email through a checker like Surfshark's catches these technical indicators even when the pitch itself sounds plausible.

**Q: What is the "Amway rule" or "70% rule"?**

The "70% rule" is a sales requirement associated with Amway's settlement with the FTC, requiring that distributors sell at least 70% of purchased inventory before reordering. It became an informal benchmark for distinguishing inventory-loading schemes from legitimate MLMs. In practice, regulatory standards have evolved and vary by jurisdiction, but the underlying principle — that distributors should be generating real retail sales rather than sitting on unsold stock — remains the core test.

## The One Question That Cuts Through the Pitch

After everything above, the single most useful question to ask anyone presenting you with a "business opportunity" is this: show me three people in your downline who earn more from retail sales to non-members than from recruiting. Real names, real numbers, verifiable income disclosure statement lines.

If they cannot answer that question — if the answer pivots back to the income potential, the testimonials, the lifestyle content, or the product quality — you have your answer about what the business actually is.

Pyramid schemes are built to be persuasive. The financial pitch is designed to create hope, not scrutiny. The most reliable protection is a clear analytical framework combined with a simple technical check on any email-based approach before you engage.
