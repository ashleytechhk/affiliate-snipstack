## What Is a Privacy Score for Installed Apps?

The phrase sounds technical, but the idea is simple.

When you install any app on your phone, that app asks for "permissions." These are requests to access things on your device, including:

- Your **contacts** — the names and phone numbers you have saved
- Your **location** — where you are right now, and where you have been
- Your **camera and microphone** — what you see and what you say
- Your **photos** — every picture stored on your device

Most people tap "Allow" without reading these requests carefully. And even when you do read them, it is hard to know whether the access is reasonable or excessive.

A **privacy score** takes all of those permission requests and turns them into a simple rating — usually a number or a color — so you can see at a glance whether an app is behaving appropriately or asking for far more than it needs.

Surfshark Antivirus scans every app installed on your device and generates this score automatically. You do not need to read lengthy terms and conditions or understand what a "data broker" is. The tool does the analysis and presents the results in plain language.

### What Counts as a "Bad" Privacy Score?

An app gets a poor privacy score when it asks for permissions that have nothing to do with what the app is supposed to do. A puzzle game that requests access to your microphone, a recipe app that wants your precise GPS location, or a free calculator that reads your contact list — these are all red flags. ## The Hidden Risks Already on Your Phone

Here is something that surprises most people: the real danger rarely comes from downloading an obvious virus. It almost always comes from apps that look completely normal and useful.

Consider these scenarios:

**Free keyboard apps** — Many popular free keyboards record every word you type. That includes passwords, bank account details, and personal messages. They send this data to advertising companies in the background, silently.

**Weather apps** — Some weather apps sell your precise location data to marketing firms. This information can be used to build a profile of your habits and, in the wrong hands, to target you with convincing scams.

**Free games and puzzles** — Casual mobile games often request access to your contacts and photo library. There is almost never a good reason for a word game to know who is in your address book.

**Utility apps** — Battery savers, phone cleaners, and file organizers are among the worst offenders for requesting far more permissions than their function requires.

None of these apps may technically contain a virus. But they are quietly collecting information that could put your finances, your identity, and the people you care about at risk.

Without a tool that checks these apps for you, there is simply no easy way to know which ones are safe and which ones are working against you.
