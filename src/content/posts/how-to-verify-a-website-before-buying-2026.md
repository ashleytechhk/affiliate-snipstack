## Method 1 — Inspect the URL Carefully

Before you do anything else, look at the web address in your browser bar.

**What to check:**
- The domain should match the real brand exactly. `amazon.com` is real. `amazon-deals-center.net` is not.
- Look for extra words added before or after the brand name — `paypal-secure-login.com` is a scam, not PayPal.
- Check for letter swaps: the letter `l` replaced by the number `1`, or `rn` that looks like `m` at a glance.
- The site should start with `https://` — the padlock icon shows the connection is encrypted. A missing padlock is a red flag. A padlock alone does not prove the site is safe — scam sites can and do use HTTPS — but no padlock is an immediate stop sign.

**Who this works for:** Anyone. No tools needed, just your eyes and 60 seconds.

**Its limit:** Well-constructed scam sites buy real SSL certificates and pick convincing domain names. URL inspection is a first filter, not a final verdict.

## Method 2 — Check the Domain Age with a WHOIS Lookup

Scam online shops appear overnight to ride a viral product trend or a season. A site claiming to be an established furniture retailer with a domain registered last Tuesday is a lie.

**How to do it:**
1. Go to `whois.domaintools.com` or type `whois [domain name]` into any search engine.
2. Look for the "Created" date.
3. A legitimate shop that has been trading for years will have a domain that is at least several months old. The older, the better.
4. Also check the registrant country — if the brand claims to be a local business but the domain is registered to a privacy proxy in a distant country, that is worth noting.

**Who this works for:** Anyone who has a couple of minutes and wants a fast factual data point.

**Its limit:** Scammers sometimes buy aged domains specifically to fool WHOIS checks. Combine this with at least one other method.

## Method 3 — Search Trustpilot, BBB, and Review Sites

Legitimate shops have a review trail. Scam shops disappear before enough negative reviews accumulate — but many still have early complaints if they have been running for even a few weeks.

**How to do it:**
1. Copy the shop's name or domain.
2. Search it on `trustpilot.com` — look for the overall score and read the one-star reviews specifically.
3. For US-based shops, check `bbb.org` (Better Business Bureau).
4. Also search Google for `"shopname" reviews` and `"shopname" scam` and read the first page of results.

**What to look for:**
- A pattern of reviews saying "never received item," "card charged, no confirmation email," or "impossible to contact."
- Reviews that all arrived on the same day (bought reviews look like a cluster with no variation).
- No reviews at all — absence of reviews for a shop pushing high-value items is suspicious.

**Who this works for:** Most people. This method takes slightly longer but gives you real social proof — or the absence of it.

## Method 4 — Google the Shop Name Plus "Scam"

Simple, fast, surprisingly effective.

**How to do it:**
1. Open Google or any search engine.
2. Type the shop's name in quotes, then add the word `scam` — for example: `"TrendyGadgetsHub" scam`
3. Also try: `"TrendyGadgetsHub" not received` or `"TrendyGadgetsHub" fraud`
4. Scan the first two pages of results for forum posts, Reddit threads, or consumer complaint sites.

If a scam shop has been operating for even a few days and has already taken money from people, someone will usually have posted about it. Consumer forums, Reddit's r/Scams, and complaint aggregators like ScamAdviser or ComplaintsBoard often surface these quickly.

**Who this works for:** Everyone. Requires no tools — just a search engine.

**Its limit:** Very new scam sites that have not yet produced enough victims to appear in public discussion will return no results. Use a database check alongside this.

## Method 5 — Reverse Image Search the Product Photos

Scam shops often steal product photographs from real retailers and use them to fill out a convincing-looking storefront. If you reverse-search a product image and it turns up on ten other websites, including Amazon, AliExpress, and the brand's real site, you know the shop did not source it legitimately.

**How to do it:**
1. Right-click a product photo on the suspicious site.
2. Select "Search image" (Chrome) or "Search Google for this image."
3. Look at the results. If the exact photo appears on well-known retailers without attribution to the shop you are checking, that is a strong warning sign.

**Who this works for:** Anyone shopping for physical products — clothing, electronics, toys, furniture.

**Its limit:** Some scam shops do produce original photography. And some real dropship retailers legitimately reuse supplier photos. Take the result as a data point, not a final ruling.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does HTTPS mean a website is safe to buy from?**

No. HTTPS means the connection between your browser and the site is encrypted — it does not say anything about whether the site's owner is honest. Scam shops routinely obtain SSL certificates. Always check the domain name and review history alongside the padlock.

**Q: I got an email with a link to a deal. Is it safe to click?**

Hover over the link first to preview where it actually goes — on a computer, hover without clicking and look at the URL that appears in the bottom-left of your browser. If it does not match the claimed brand's real domain, do not click. You can also paste the URL into [Surfshark's Scam Detector](/blog/scamcheck?sub=how-to-verify-a-website-before-buying-2026__faq) to check it before opening.

**Q: What if the shop has no reviews anywhere?**

That absence is itself a signal. A shop asking you to spend real money should have some review trail. If it has none — on Google, Trustpilot, or anywhere else — combined with a recently created domain, treat it as high risk until you find evidence of legitimacy.

**Q: I already paid and the item never arrived. What should I do?**

Contact your bank or card issuer immediately and explain that you believe the transaction was fraudulent. Most banks have a chargeback process for goods not received. Act quickly — most issuers have a time window for disputes. Also report the site to your national consumer protection agency.

**Q: Can a scam website look exactly like a real one?**

Yes. Modern scam shops copy legitimate retailers almost perfectly — same logo, similar domain, same product descriptions. That is why URL inspection alone is not enough. A domain-age check and a quick scam database lookup add the layers that visual inspection misses.
