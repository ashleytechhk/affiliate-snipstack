## Why "Not Being Tracked" Means More Than Hiding Your Address

There are actually three separate things a promo-code hunter might mean by "not being tracked," and most masking tools only solve one of them.

**Address-level tracking** is the obvious one: does the retailer know your real inbox? An [email alias](https://snipstack.io/how-to-use-email-alias-to-filter-spam-2026/) solves this — the store sees a masked address, not your actual one.

**Pixel-level tracking** is subtler. Most promo emails embed a tiny invisible image. When it loads, the sender learns whether you opened the email, when, and sometimes your rough location. Masking your address does nothing about this on its own — the pixel still fires the moment the forwarded message renders. Only a handful of tools, like DuckDuckGo Email Protection and Firefox Relay, strip these pixels before the email ever reaches your inbox.

**Identity-level tracking** is the one that compounds over time: data brokers linking your name, address, and shopping habits across dozens of retailer lists. This is where an alias with a fabricated identity attached — like Alt-ID's generated name and date of birth — earns its keep, even without pixel stripping. A leaked alias doesn't trace back to you.

Hide My Email generates random forwarding addresses inside Mail settings or Safari's autofill. Paired with Mail Privacy Protection (a separate iCloud+ toggle), Apple preloads remote content — including tracking pixels — through its own proxy, so senders can't tell if, when, or where you opened the message. Typing `you+dealsite@gmail.com` at checkout is free and technically creates a filterable variant — but it does not hide your real domain. Anyone who sees the address knows your actual Gmail is `you@gmail.com`. No pixel stripping, no identity layer, no way to kill it separately from your main account. For a one-off filter rule, fine. For actually avoiding tracking, it does close to nothing.

### 6. Disposable Email Sites — Fine for One-Time Codes Only

Sites like Temp-Mail and Guerrilla Mail hand you a throwaway inbox that anyone with the address can read — no forwarding, no persistence. That's a problem for promo codes specifically, because plenty of "welcome discount" codes and coupon reminders arrive hours or days after signup, by which point the [temporary inbox](https://snipstack.io/temporary-email-and-password-free-2026/) has often already expired or been recycled to another stranger. ### Do promo code emails really contain tracking pixels?

Frequently, yes. Retail marketing platforms commonly embed open-tracking pixels in promotional emails to measure engagement. This is standard email marketing practice, not something unique to any one brand — and masking your address alone doesn't stop a pixel from loading once the message is forwarded to your inbox.

### Will an email mask stop the promo code from arriving?

No — a masked address still forwards mail to your real inbox exactly like your normal address would. The retailer just never learns what that real inbox is. Codes, receipts, and confirmations all arrive as expected.

### Can a retailer reject my masked email at checkout?

Some do block recognizable alias domains at signup. If that happens, use a secondary personal address you don't mind exposing rather than your primary one, and reserve your main alias tool for services that accept it.

### Is it worth using a mask for a single small discount code?

If it takes under a minute to generate one, yes. The point isn't the eight dollars off — it's that every signup form is a permanent record somewhere. A masked address means that record doesn't lead back to your real inbox or identity.

### Does deleting an alias cancel a promo code I already redeemed?

No. Deleting the alias only stops future email to that address — it has no effect on a code you've already applied at checkout or a discount already processed on an order.
