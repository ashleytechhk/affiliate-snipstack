## What Is Adware and Why Should You Care?

Adware is a type of software that sneaks onto your phone and then displays advertisements — often non-stop. Some adware is just annoying. But the more dangerous kind does something worse: it tracks everything you do on your phone and sends that information to unknown third parties.

Here is what adware can do once it is on your Android device:

- Show pop-up ads that cover your screen, even when you are not using an app
- Slow your phone down significantly
- Drain your battery faster than normal
- Collect your location, contacts, and browsing history without asking
- Lead you to fake websites designed to steal passwords or payment details

Seniors are targeted more heavily because adware often hides inside free apps marketed toward older users — word puzzle games, health trackers, Bible apps, recipe organizers. These apps appear harmless but quietly run adware code in the background.

The hardest part? **You may never know it is there.** Adware does not announce itself. Your phone just feels slower, ads seem to appear everywhere, and occasionally you notice apps you did not install.

## How Adware Gets Onto Android Phones

![How adware spreads on Android phones](/og/antivirus-that-detects-adware-on-android-2026-inline-1.png)

Android phones are more open than Apple iPhones. That openness is useful — but it also means apps from outside the official Google Play Store can be installed, and even some apps inside the Play Store have slipped through Google's review process carrying adware.

The most common ways adware lands on a senior's Android phone:

### 1. Free App Downloads

Many free apps earn money by bundling advertising software inside them. You download a free clock widget or a free photo editor, and adware comes along for the ride. The permissions screen during installation often lists access to your contacts, camera, or microphone — most people tap "Accept" without reading it.

### 2. Clicking Links in Text Messages

Scam text messages — sometimes called "smishing" — are designed to look like they come from your bank, a delivery company, or even a family member. Tapping the link can trigger a download that installs adware without you realizing.

### 3. Fake App Updates

A pop-up appears saying your phone needs a software update or that a specific app must be updated immediately. These fake prompts install malicious software disguised as a legitimate update.

### 4. Third-Party App Stores

Outside of Google Play, countless app stores exist that offer popular apps for free — apps that normally cost money. These versions are often modified to include adware or worse.
