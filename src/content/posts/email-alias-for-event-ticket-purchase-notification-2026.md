## Why Event Ticket Platforms Are Spam Magnets

Ticketing platforms generate revenue from more than ticket sales. Your email address is part of that revenue model. When you purchase a ticket, your data is typically shared with:

- The venue independently of the ticketing platform
- The artist's or event's promotional mailing list
- "Selected partners" — a catch-all for advertising networks
- Secondary resellers if you transfer or forward your ticket

This happens by default. Opt-outs exist but are buried in account settings or hidden beneath pre-checked boxes during checkout. By the time you find the unsubscribe link, your address has already been distributed.

The problem compounds across platforms. A concert on Ticketmaster, a sports event on AXS, a comedy night on Eventbrite, a festival on Dice — each purchase multiplies the number of mailing lists your address sits on. A single busy year of live events can take a clean inbox and turn it into a daily spam pile.

![How ticketing platforms distribute your email to third parties](/og/email-alias-for-event-ticket-purchase-notification-2026-inline-1.png)

An alias breaks this pattern entirely. Your real address never touches the ticketing system. The alias receives the confirmation and e-ticket, forwards them to your real inbox, and can be silenced the moment it starts generating noise. The ticketing platform loses the ability to track you across other services using your email as an identifier.

Not every alias tool handles the ticket notification use case equally well. Here is what to look for:

### Reliable Attachment Forwarding

Your e-ticket arrives as a PDF, a QR code image, or a mobile wallet pass link. If the alias service strips attachments or delays delivery, you risk missing venue entry requirements — or discovering the problem at the gate. Alt-ID and SimpleLogin both forward attachments without modification. Firefox Relay's free tier has documented limitations with large attachments and some HTML email formats.

### Instant Disable Without Losing History

The goal is to stop spam immediately when it starts without accidentally blocking a last-minute venue update about gate changes or set times. The best tools let you pause forwarding on an alias — muting it — rather than deleting it outright. You preserve the alias history and can reactivate it if a legitimate message needs to come through.

### Custom Naming

Being able to name an alias — for example, `ticketmaster-june@youralias.altid.com` — makes managing dozens of aliases practical. You know at a glance which alias belongs to which platform or event. Apple Hide My Email and Firefox Relay both generate random character strings, which becomes difficult to manage once you have more than a handful active.

### Reply Capability

Some venues send follow-up emails requiring a response: seat upgrade requests, accessibility arrangements, or order corrections. Alt-ID and Apple Hide My Email allow replies that appear to come from the alias address, keeping your real email masked throughout the exchange. Firefox Relay's free tier does not support outbound replies, which creates a gap in this specific use case.

Yes. The alias forwards all incoming emails to your real inbox, including last-minute venue changes, time adjustments, cancellations, and entry instructions. The sender has no way to tell they are contacting an alias address — it functions like any normal email from their side.

Disabling (pausing) an alias stops all forwarding immediately. If you still expect communications from the venue — particularly for multi-day events or events with dynamic QR codes that regenerate closer to the date — keep the alias active until after you have attended.
