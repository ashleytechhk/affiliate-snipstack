## Method 1: Etsy's Built-In Review System

Etsy's review system is the obvious starting point, but it has well-documented weaknesses that scammers exploit.

**What it catches:** Genuine patterns of bad experience — late delivery, items not as described, no response from the seller. If a shop has dozens of complaints about items never arriving, that is a meaningful signal.

**What scammers do to game it:** They seed fresh shops with a burst of five-star reviews from accounts created the same week. On Etsy, you cannot verify whether a reviewer actually purchased from the shop — anyone can leave a review. Look at the reviewer profiles: no purchase history, no avatar, created recently. That pattern is a tell.

**[How to use](https://snipstack.io/how-to-use-email-aliases-for-security-2026/) it properly:** Sort reviews by "Most Recent" rather than "Top Reviews." Check the date spread — did 40 reviews arrive in a two-week window three months ago, and then nothing since? Look for reviews that mention the exact item you are considering, not just generic "great seller!" text.

**Limitations:** Review manipulation is cheap and fast. A scam shop can buy 50 five-star reviews for very little. The review system filters out obvious fakes but does not catch a coordinated seeding effort with plausible-looking accounts.

## Method 2: Reverse Image Search

Many Etsy scam shops steal product photos directly from legitimate sellers or manufacturer sites. The items in the photos either do not exist, do not match what will be shipped, or are counterfeit versions of a real product.

**How to run it:** On desktop, right-click the main product photo and select "Search image" in Chrome or Edge. On mobile, press and hold the image, then use Google Lens. If the same image appears across dozens of unrelated shops, or if it appears on a manufacturer's site with a completely different price point, the listing is not genuine.

**What it catches:** Lifted stock photography, screenshots from legitimate sellers, counterfeit product imagery.

**Limitations:** Scammers increasingly use AI-generated product images that do not appear in any reverse image database. A clean reverse image result does not mean the shop is legitimate — it may just mean they generated new photos. Use this check as supporting evidence, not as a definitive pass.

## Method 3: WHOIS Lookup for External Shops

Some scams involve a fake Etsy shop that redirects you to an external website — often for "wholesale pricing" or "direct checkout." Any time an Etsy seller asks you to leave the platform to complete a purchase, that is a red flag by itself. But if you want to verify whether the external site is legitimate, a WHOIS lookup is your fastest free option.

**How to run it:** Go to who.is or lookup.icann.org. Enter the domain name of the external site. Check two things: the creation date (a domain registered in the last 30–60 days selling premium goods is almost always a scam operation) and the registrant details (legitimate businesses typically have verifiable contact information; scam domains often list privacy proxy services or fake addresses).

**What it catches:** Brand-new domains masquerading as established businesses, sites with no verifiable operator identity.

**Limitations:** WHOIS only tells you about the domain itself, not the site's current behavior. A domain that is six months old can still host a scam shop. WHOIS lookups also give you no real-time protection — it is a one-time check that tells you nothing about whether the link in that email you just received is safe to click.

## FAQ

### How do I quickly check if an Etsy shop is a scam?

Start with three fast checks: sort the shop's reviews by Most Recent and look for a suspicious cluster of reviews with no purchase history shown, run a reverse image search on the main product photo, and check how recently the shop was created (visible on the shop's "About" page). If any of those raise concerns, do not proceed. For links you received by email claiming to be from Etsy, [run the link through a scam checker](/blog/scamcheck?sub=etsy-shop-scam-check-before-buying-2026__faq) before you click it.

### Are fake Etsy shops common?

Yes, and the problem has grown as Etsy has scaled. Scam shops typically fall into two categories: shops that take payment and ship nothing, and shops that ship counterfeit or vastly inferior versions of what was advertised. A separate, growing threat is phishing emails that impersonate Etsy — these do not involve an actual shop at all, but lure you to a fake login page to steal your credentials.

### What should I do if I think I bought from a scam Etsy seller?

Open a case through Etsy's Purchase Protection program immediately. Etsy offers buyer protection for items that do not arrive or do not match the listing — but you must open a case within 100 days of the estimated delivery date. If you paid by credit card, also contact your card issuer about a chargeback. Change your Etsy password and the password on any other account where you use the same credentials.

### Can scammers fake Etsy reviews?

Yes. Review manipulation on Etsy is well-documented. Tactics include seeding a new shop with reviews from accounts created specifically for that purpose, and using fulfillment of low-value items to accumulate verified purchase badges before pivoting to higher-value scams. The most reliable signals to look for are: review accounts with no other activity, a large burst of reviews in a short window, and generic review text that does not reference the specific item.

### Is it safe to click links in Etsy emails?

Not without checking first. Legitimate emails from Etsy will come from an @etsy.com address — check the full sender address, not just the display name. Hover over any link before clicking to see the actual destination URL. If the URL domain is not etsy.com (for example, "etsy-delivery-update.com" or "etsy.support-verification.net"), do not click it. When in doubt, go directly to etsy.com by typing it into your browser and check your account from there.
