## Charity Scam Verification: What You Actually Need to Check

When a charity solicitation arrives in your inbox, there are really two separate questions to answer:

**Question 1: Is this charity a real, registered organisation?**
A scammer can invent a name like "National Children's Emergency Relief Fund" and make it sound completely legitimate. A registered-organisation check tells you whether that name actually appears in official charitable databases.

**Question 2: Did this email actually come from that charity — and is the donation link safe?**
Even if the charity is real, scammers sometimes impersonate legitimate organisations. They register a domain name that looks almost identical — one letter different, or a `.org` swapped for `.com` — and send emails that appear to be from the genuine charity. The donation link collects your card number and routes the money to the scammer.

Most people only think about Question 1. Answering both is what complete charity scam verification looks like.

## Step 1: Check the Register First

Before you do anything else with a charity solicitation email, spend two minutes on a register lookup. Here are the three most reliable free sources:

**Charity Navigator (charitynavigator.org):** Rates US charities on financial health, accountability, and transparency. Search by name. A charity that does not appear at all, or that has a low accountability score, warrants extra caution.

**IRS Tax Exempt Organisation Search (apps.irs.gov/app/eos):** Every legitimate US charitable organisation must be registered with the IRS to accept tax-deductible donations. Search the exact name. If it does not appear, donations to it will not be tax-deductible — and it may not be legitimate at all.

**BBB Wise Giving Alliance (give.org):** The Better Business Bureau's charity arm evaluates organisations against 20 standards covering governance, finances, and fundraising. A failing report or a missing listing is a yellow flag.

If the charity passes a register check, that tells you the organisation exists and is legitimate. It does not tell you whether the email you received actually came from that organisation. That is where Step 2 comes in.

## Other Warning Signs in Charity Emails

Even before you run any check, these patterns are reliable signals that something is wrong:

**The charity name is slightly off.** "American Red Crosss" (double S), "United Way Relief Foundation" (extra words), or a famous name with one letter changed. Scammers count on you reading quickly.

**The sender's email domain does not match the charity's website.** If the email claims to be from the Red Cross but came from `redcross-donations.net` rather than `redcross.org`, that is a major red flag.

**They ask for unusual payment methods.** Gift cards, wire transfers, cryptocurrency, or Zelle are all payment methods that cannot be reversed. Legitimate charities accept credit cards and will give you a receipt.

**There is pressure to donate immediately.** "This offer expires tonight." "Funds must be transferred by midnight." Genuine charities do not impose artificial deadlines on donations.

**The email is vague about how donations will be used.** "100% goes to those in need" without any breakdown of programs, overhead, or governance is a warning sign. Legitimate charities publish financial statements.

### How do I check if a charity is legitimate before donating?

Start with a register lookup. Search the charity's exact name on Charity Navigator, the IRS Tax Exempt Organisation Search, or BBB Wise Giving Alliance. If it appears with good standing, that confirms the organisation is real. Then check the email sender and donation link using a tool like Surfshark's Email Scam Checker to confirm the email actually came from that charity and the donation link is safe. ### Can a charity be registered but the email still be a scam?

Yes — this is one of the most important things to understand. Scammers frequently impersonate real, registered charities. They create a similar domain name, copy the branding, and send emails that look identical to the genuine article. A register check only confirms the organisation exists. A separate email and link check is always necessary.

### What payment methods are safe to use when donating?

Credit cards and PayPal give you chargeback rights — if the donation turns out to be fraudulent, your bank or PayPal can reverse the charge in many cases. Gift cards, wire transfers, cryptocurrency, and apps like Zelle are effectively irreversible. If a charity's only payment option is one of these, do not donate via that email.

### What should I do if I already donated to what might be a scam?

If you paid by credit card, call your card issuer immediately and explain the situation — many banks will open a dispute and may be able to reverse the charge. If you paid by gift card or wire transfer, recovery is much harder, but you should still report it to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov. Change any passwords associated with the email address you used, and watch your accounts for unusual activity. Going forward, [run every charity email through a scam check](/blog/scamcheck?sub=charity-scam-verification-before-donating-2026__faq) before clicking any link or entering any payment details.

### Why do charity scams increase after disasters?

Scammers monitor the news and launch campaigns within hours of major events — earthquakes, floods, conflicts — because they know donation intent is high and people are acting on emotion. They also know that real relief organisations are being overwhelmed, which makes the fake appeals harder to distinguish. This is precisely when a two-step verification routine matters most.
