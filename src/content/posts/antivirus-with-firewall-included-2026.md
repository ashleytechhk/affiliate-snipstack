## Why Seniors Are the Number One Target

Cybercriminals do not pick victims at random. They go where the money is — and research consistently shows that older adults hold more savings and are less likely to recognize a digital attack before it is too late.

Here are the tactics they use most often against seniors right now:

**Phishing emails** — You receive a message that looks like it is from your bank, Medicare, or a delivery company. It asks you to click a link and "confirm your details." The link goes to a [fake website](https://snipstack.io/fake-website-detector-tool-2026/) that steals your password or credit card number.

**Fake virus warnings** — A pop-up fills your screen claiming your computer is infected. It tells you to call a [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/). Real security software never works this way. People who call that number are connected to scammers who try to charge hundreds of dollars for fake "repairs" or to gain remote access to the computer.

**Malware and ransomware** — A virus quietly installs itself when you download something that looked safe — a free game, a PDF from an unknown sender, a video file. Once inside, it can spy on you, steal passwords, or lock your entire computer and demand payment to unlock it.

**Unsecured Wi-Fi attacks** — When you connect to Wi-Fi at a library, doctor's office, or coffee shop, someone on the same network can intercept what you are doing — including any passwords or bank details you type.

The thing all of these attacks have in common is that neither an antivirus alone nor a firewall alone would fully stop them. You need both.

## The Difference Between Antivirus and a Firewall (Plain English)

A lot of people mix these up, and that confusion leaves them unprotected. Think of your computer as a house.

**Antivirus** is like an alarm system inside the house. It watches everything going on indoors, sounds the alarm if it spots a burglar (virus) hiding inside, and calls for help to remove them.

**A firewall** is like the lock on your front door. It controls who and what is allowed to enter in the first place. Before any data comes into your computer from the internet, the firewall checks whether it is legitimate. If something suspicious tries to sneak in, the firewall blocks it before it ever gets inside.

Here is why you need both: a burglar who gets past your front door before the alarm is set will not trigger the alarm. And an alarm that goes off after a burglar is already inside is better than nothing — but you would rather have stopped them at the door.
