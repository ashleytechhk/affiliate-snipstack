## Why Gyms Are Particularly Bad for Spam

Not all sign-up forms are created equal. Gyms sit in a uniquely aggressive category for a few reasons.

**1. High data monetisation incentive.** Gym memberships are a recurring revenue business with thin margins. Fitness brands supplement income by sharing member data with supplement companies, athletic apparel retailers, and health-tech app providers. When you sign up for a membership, the contract's fine print often includes a clause about sharing data with "trusted partners."

**2. Partner ecosystem depth.** A mid-size gym chain typically has a dozen active commercial partnerships — fitness tracking apps, sports nutrition brands, physiotherapy clinics, personal training platforms. Each of those partners becomes a new sender once they have your address.

**3. Low unsubscribe compliance.** Big retailers tend to honour unsubscribe requests within 10 business days because they have legal teams monitoring CAN-SPAM compliance. Many gym operators and their partners are smaller businesses with less rigorous list management. Requests get ignored or processed slowly.

**4. Breach exposure.** Gym membership databases are frequent targets in data breaches. Health and fitness data is commercially valuable, and many gym operators run older, less secure membership management software. A breach means your email — and everything else you submitted at sign-up — ends up on dark-web marketplaces.

The combination of all four means: **give a gym your real email, and that address is compromised, probably within weeks.**

### SimpleLogin (Free Tier) — Decent, With a Ceiling

SimpleLogin is an open-source alias service with a free tier that gives you up to 10 active aliases. It forwards to your real inbox and lets you delete aliases on demand. For gym signups it works adequately — the forwarding is reliable and the alias domain is reasonably well-regarded.

The ceiling hits fast. Ten aliases disappear quickly once you start applying the one-alias-per-service discipline across your whole digital life (shopping, newsletters, forums, trials, gyms). If the gym is literally the only new thing you're signing up for and you have alias budget left, SimpleLogin free is a workable option.

It doesn't generate fake identity details alongside the alias, so you'd be protecting your email but still submitting your real name and date of birth.

**Best for:** Users who are already on SimpleLogin and have alias slots available, and only need basic forwarding.

### Guerrilla Mail — Not Right for Gym Signups

Guerrilla Mail is a zero-sign-up throwaway inbox that gives you an address instantly. It's excellent for one-time sign-ups where you need a confirmation click and nothing else.

Gym memberships are the wrong use case. You need to receive:
- Booking confirmations and class schedule updates
- Membership renewal notices
- Password reset emails when you forget your login
- Potentially direct messages from your PT

Guerrilla Mail addresses expire within roughly an hour of inactivity. There's no forwarding — you have to check a separate tab. The inbox is publicly accessible to anyone who knows the address. And the domain is on most spam-detection blocklists, so many gym sign-up systems will reject it outright.

**Best for:** One-time verification clicks with no ongoing relationship. Not gyms.

---

### Gmail + Addressing — Free but Transparent

Gmail lets you append tags to your address: `yourname+gym@gmail.com` still delivers to `yourname@gmail.com`. It costs nothing and works with Gmail's built-in filtering.

The problem is that it doesn't hide your real address. Anyone who sees `yourname+gym@gmail.com` can trivially deduce your real address is `yourname@gmail.com`. Many marketers and data brokers strip everything after the `+` sign before storing the address, which means spam bypasses your tag-based filters anyway.

It also gives you no kill-switch. You can filter emails from senders that have your tagged address, but you can't revoke the address — it's still `yourname@gmail.com` underneath.

**Best for:** Mild inbox organisation where privacy isn't a concern. Not spam prevention.

Yes. All email sent to the alias forwards to your real inbox automatically. Class confirmations, booking reminders, membership updates — everything arrives as normal. You just never see your real address in the "To" field.

**Can I use the same alias for multiple gyms?**

You can, but it's better practice to create one alias per gym. If you have two memberships and one gym's partner spam starts hitting, you can kill just that gym's alias without affecting the other. Alt ID's unlimited alias approach makes per-gym aliases practical — create as many as you need.

Deleting the alias means the gym can no longer email you at that address. You'd need to update your email address in the gym's member portal before deleting — or accept that you'll lose email contact. If you want to pause emails without losing the alias permanently, use Alt ID's toggle-off feature instead of full deletion.

**Is it dishonest to give a gym a [fake email](https://snipstack.io/fake-email-sender-with-attachment-2026/) address?**

You're giving them a real, functional forwarding address — not a fake one. Emails reach you; replies work. You're simply choosing which address is exposed, the same way you might use a work number for a business contact rather than your personal mobile. Using an alias is a privacy choice, not deception.
