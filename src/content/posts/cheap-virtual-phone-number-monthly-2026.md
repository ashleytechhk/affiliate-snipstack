## Google Voice — Best Free Option, With a Catch

**Google Voice** is the most widely used free [virtual number](https://snipstack.io/virtual-number-for-google-account-verification-2026/) in the US. Sign in with a Google account, pick an area code, and you have a working number for calls, texts, and voicemail — no monthly cost.

For casual dating use, it covers a lot: you can text from the app or the web, forward calls to your real phone, and block specific numbers when needed. The number looks like a real US line to most recipients.

### Where it breaks down

Google Voice numbers are VoIP. That means they sit in a number range that third-party SMS verification systems — the kind dating apps use — increasingly flag. Tinder, Hinge, and Bumble have all gotten more aggressive about rejecting VoIP lines for new account verification. If your main goal is setting up a new dating profile with a private number, Google Voice is a gamble: it might work, or you might hit a rejection screen at the exact moment you need it most.

It's also US-only. If you're outside the US or want a number from a specific country for any reason, Google Voice doesn't apply.

**Best for:** US-based daters who want a free secondary number for day-to-day texting after they're already past verification.

## TextNow — Free, Ad-Supported, Light Use Only

**TextNow** hands you a free US number with unlimited calling and texting over WiFi. The catch is ads throughout the app — the free tier is genuinely ad-heavy. A paid plan removes them and adds a few features, but the underlying number is still a VoIP line.

For the early "moving off the app" stage — sharing a number so a match can text you without you handing over your real digits — TextNow is functional. The number looks normal on the receiving end.

### Where it breaks down

Same VoIP flagging issue as Google Voice, arguably worse because TextNow's number ranges are so widely associated with free services that some platforms block them outright. If your match uses an iMessage-heavy social circle, they may notice the number doesn't activate iMessage — a tell that it's a VoIP line.

WiFi dependency also matters in practice. If you're out on a date and your match texts the number while you're on spotty data, you might miss it entirely.

**Best for:** Budget-conscious daters who want free texting with a match they've already started chatting with, and don't need the number to pass new account verification.

**Can I use one [virtual number](https://snipstack.io/virtual-number-for-bank-account-verification-2026/) across multiple dating apps?**

Yes, in most cases. One number can receive verification codes for multiple accounts on different platforms. The practical limit is that some platforms won't let you use the same number for more than one account on their own service — but across Tinder, Hinge, Bumble, and OkCupid simultaneously, one number is fine.

No. Once you disable or replace the number, calls and texts to the old number either bounce or go to whoever takes the number next (on recycled pool services) or nowhere (on services that retire numbers). Your real phone number is never exposed unless you specifically shared it.

Platforms use third-party number verification services that maintain databases of known VoIP ranges. Depends on how you define "casually." If you're on one app and texting one match, a free VoIP number from Google Voice or TextNow is probably fine for day-to-day texts. If you're actively trying to set up new accounts, dealing with multiple matches, or have already had a VoIP number rejected, the paid option solves real friction.
