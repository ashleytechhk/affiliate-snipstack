Think about what a typical accountant's computer holds that a personal home computer does not: full names, Social Security numbers, bank routing numbers, income history, and signatures — not for one person, but for every client on the books. A home computer breach might expose one household. An accountant's computer can expose an entire client list at once.

## How These Attacks Actually Happen

Understanding the real methods criminals use makes the danger easier to spot before it happens to you.

### The "New Client" Email Trick

An email arrives that looks like a message from a prospective client, asking you to review an attached document — a tax form, a spreadsheet, a scanned ID. The attachment carries hidden software that, once opened, can quietly copy files or record what you type, including passwords to your client files and e-filing accounts.

### The Fake Software Update

A pop-up claims your tax software, antivirus, or operating system urgently needs an update. Clicking it installs malware instead of a real update. Because it looks routine, it rarely raises suspicion until damage is already done.

![How a single phishing email can lead to a full client data breach](/og/antivirus-for-accountant-with-sensitive-client-data-2026-inline-1.png)

### Ransomware That Locks Every File You Have

This is the most damaging version. Malicious software encrypts every client file on your computer at once and demands payment to unlock them. Even without paying, you may lose access to years of client records — and you are still obligated to tell every affected client that their information may have been exposed.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I really need antivirus if I only work with a handful of clients?

Yes. The number of clients does not change how attractive your data is to a criminal — even a small handful of full financial profiles is valuable. Attackers target tax preparers and accountants as a category, because the data is consistently valuable regardless of practice size.

### Is my tax software's built-in security enough on its own?

Tax software typically protects the data inside the program itself, but it does not protect the device the software runs on. If your computer is infected with malware or you enter your login on a fake page, tax software security does not help. A separate antivirus layer covering your whole device closes that gap.
