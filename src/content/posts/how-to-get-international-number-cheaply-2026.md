## Why Your Real Number Is the Biggest Risk You're Taking on Dating Apps

Most people think about dating safety in terms of who they meet in person. The phone number risk is less visible but just as real.

Here's what actually happens when you share your number with a Tinder or Hinge match:

**Reverse phone lookup.** Any number can be entered into a reverse lookup service — many are free. From your number alone, a stranger can often find your full name, current address, employer, and social media profiles. You gave them a number. They now have your life.

**Data broker exposure.** Your phone number has been sold, re-sold, and aggregated by data brokers dozens of times. Someone with your number and a few minutes on Spokeo or Whitepages can build a profile on you without any hacking involved. This is entirely legal and happens constantly.

**Persistent contact after you've ended things.** Blocking works once. Anyone with a [second phone](https://snipstack.io/second-phone-number-for-side-hustle-2026/) or a new number can continue reaching you. If they have your real number, the problem doesn't go away just because you hit block.

**SIM swap fraud.** Less common, but documented: bad actors use personal information gleaned from a target's phone number to convince carriers to transfer the number to a new SIM. Once they have your number, they can intercept two-factor authentication codes and access financial accounts. Dating apps are a documented vector for initial social engineering.

The common thread: once someone has your real number, you cannot un-give it to them.

**Google Voice** is the most commonly recommended free option. It gives you a US number that forwards calls and texts to your real number. The problems: it's US-only (no use if you're abroad or want a number from another country), requires a Google account (so it's still tied to your identity), and many dating apps and SMS verification services have learned to detect and reject Google Voice numbers.

**TextNow and similar apps** offer free numbers with ads. They work for some verifications but are widely flagged by dating platforms as VoIP numbers and frequently rejected. **Using a friend's number** solves one problem and creates three others. They'll receive your verification codes, your date's texts, and an awkward conversation about why you needed this favor.

Free options work until they don't. For something as consistent as dating privacy, they create more friction than they solve.
