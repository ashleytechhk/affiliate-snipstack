## How the Fake Tech Support Alert Scam Actually Works

Understanding the mechanics makes it much easier to recognize an attack in progress. Here is the typical sequence:

1. **You visit a normal website** — reading news, checking email, or clicking a link from a search result.
2. **A pop-up suddenly fills your screen.** It often mimics a Microsoft or Apple warning, sometimes with a loud siren sound, claiming your device has a virus, has been hacked, or is "locked."
3. **The message tells you to call a phone number immediately** — often with a countdown timer to create panic.
4. **A "technician" answers and asks for remote access** to your computer using free software like AnyDesk or TeamViewer.
5. **Once connected, they claim to find "problems"** and pressure you to pay for fake repairs, a "protection plan," or gift cards — or they quietly install real malware to steal your banking details.

The entire scheme depends on one thing: getting you to act fast, before you have time to think it through or ask someone else.

## If You Already Called the Number

If you already called a number from a pop-up, or let someone remotely access your computer, stay calm and take these steps right away:

1. **Disconnect from the internet** immediately — unplug the cable or turn off Wi-Fi.
2. **Uninstall any remote access software** the caller asked you to install (AnyDesk, TeamViewer, AnyViewer, or similar).
3. **Change your passwords** from a different, uninfected device — starting with email and banking.
4. **Call your bank directly** using the number on the back of your card, not any number given by the caller, and ask them to watch for suspicious activity.
5. **Run a full antivirus scan** before reconnecting to the internet on that device.
6. **Report it** to the FBI's IC3 at ic3.gov or call your local police non-emergency line. Reporting helps, and there is no shame in being targeted — these scams are designed by professionals to fool anyone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is a fake tech support alert a virus?

No, in most cases. It is usually a browser pop-up generated by a malicious ad or compromised website — not software installed on your computer. Closing the browser or restarting the device typically removes it. The real danger comes from calling the number and giving a scammer remote access, not from the pop-up itself.

### Why do scammers specifically target seniors?

FBI data shows older adults reported 58% of the total losses from these "call center" scams while making up only 40% of complainants — meaning each successful scam against a senior tends to be far more costly. Scammers assume seniors are more likely to have savings, be less familiar with remote-access software, and be less likely to hang up on an urgent-sounding caller.

### What should I do if the pop-up won't close?

Do not click anything inside the pop-up window, including buttons that say "Close" or "Cancel" — they can trigger a download. Instead, close the entire browser (on Windows, press Alt+F4; on a Mac, press Command+Q). If that fails, restart the computer completely.

### Should I ever give a caller remote access to my computer?

Only if you contacted a company yourself, using a phone number you looked up independently — never a number shown in a pop-up or provided by someone who called you first. Legitimate companies never cold-call you about a virus on your PC.
