## Why Seniors Are Targeted More Than Anyone Else

Cybercriminals are not random. They go where the money is — and they have learned that older adults tend to have more savings, are more likely to answer their phones, and are less likely to recognize a scam in progress.

The tactics have grown far more sophisticated than they used to be. Phishing emails once looked obviously fake — poor grammar, strange logos, urgent ALL-CAPS warnings. Today, a scam email can look identical to a real message from your bank. The sender address looks right. The logo looks right. The link even shows a familiar web address before it quietly redirects you somewhere dangerous.

Common tricks used against older adults include:

- **Fake [package delivery](https://snipstack.io/package-delivery-scam-text-checker-2026/) notices** — "Your Amazon order is on hold. Click here to confirm your address."
- **Government impersonation** — "Your benefits have been suspended. Act immediately to restore access."
- **Grandparent scams** — An urgent message pretending to be a grandchild who needs bail money or emergency funds.
- **Fake [tech support](https://snipstack.io/tech-support-scam-phone-call-identify-2026/) pop-ups** — A scary warning on screen telling you to call a number or click a link to fix your "infected" computer.

Each one works the same way: panic you into clicking before you have time to think. And once you click, the damage can begin within seconds.

## [What Happens When](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) You Click a Suspicious Link

This is the part most people do not realize. A single click on a bad link can trigger a chain of events — most of which are completely invisible to you.

### The First 30 Seconds After a Bad Click

Within moments of clicking a malicious link, your device may silently download what is called **malware** — short for malicious software. This can include several different types:

- **Keyloggers**: Software that records every key you type, including passwords, email addresses, and bank account numbers.
- **Ransomware**: A program that locks your entire computer and demands a payment before you can access your own photos, documents, and files again.
- **Spyware**: Software that watches everything you do on your computer and sends your personal information to criminals without you knowing.
- **Trojans**: Programs disguised as something harmless — a free game, a document, a software update — that secretly open a back door into your device.

You will not see any of this happening. Your screen may look completely normal. The computer may run fine for days or even weeks. By the time you notice something is wrong, the damage is long done.
