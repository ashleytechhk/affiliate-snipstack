### Shared-device safety: what CleanWeb actually does (and doesn't)

Here's where we need to be precise instead of generous. Surfshark's CleanWeb feature blocks ads, trackers, and known malware/phishing domains at the network level — which matters a lot on a shared home network because it stops malicious pop-ups and drive-by malware sites before they ever load, on any device connected, including your kid's tablet or a hand-me-down laptop. That's genuinely useful shared-device hygiene.

What it is **not** is a content filter. It won't block age-inappropriate sites by category, set screen-time limits, or give you a dashboard of what your kids browsed. If you're picturing "parental controls," CleanWeb isn't that product.

### Breach alerts for the accounts your kids sign up for without asking

Kids create accounts. Game logins, school platforms, random apps — and those emails and passwords end up in breach dumps just like anyone else's. Surfshark's Alert feature monitors for your email addresses turning up in known data breaches, which is a quieter but genuinely useful layer for a household where more than one person is creating logins on shared devices. Norton and McAfee offer similar dark-web/identity monitoring, so this category is roughly a wash between the three — the real differentiator is everything else on this list.

### The honest gap: no dedicated parental controls

We're not going to dress this up. Surfshark One does not include a parental-control suite — no content filtering by age category, no app time limits, no location sharing for kids' devices. What Surfshark One is built for is the layer underneath that: keeping the network and the devices themselves clean, so that whatever content-filtering tool you pair it with (Apple Screen Time, Google Family Link, or your router's built-in controls) isn't fighting malware, trackers, and an unsecured VPN connection at the same time.
