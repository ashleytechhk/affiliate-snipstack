## Google Safe Browsing — The Invisible Baseline

Google Safe Browsing is already running whenever you use Chrome, Firefox, or Safari. It cross-checks every URL you visit against Google's database of known phishing sites and malware hosts. When a match is found, your browser throws up a red warning page before you land anywhere dangerous.

**What it does well:** It is completely transparent — no setup, no extra steps. Billions of URLs are checked every day through this system, and it has a low false-positive rate on mainstream sites.

**Where it falls short:** The database relies on Google flagging a site as dangerous first, which takes time. Brand-new scam domains — the kind that appear in a targeted phishing email sent to you this week — may not yet be flagged. Safe Browsing works on a cached blocklist, not a live reputation score, so anything fresh slips through. It also gives you no details: you either see a warning or you do not.

**Best for:** Catching well-known, previously reported scam sites as a safety net — not for checking an unfamiliar URL before clicking it.

## VirusTotal — The Most Thorough Manual Check

VirusTotal (owned by Google) lets you paste a URL or upload a file and runs it through more than 70 antivirus and web security engines simultaneously. The results show you exactly which engines flagged the site and why, including the category they assigned (phishing, malware, spam, etc.).

**What it does well:** Depth. If a URL shows up clean on 70 independent engines, that is a meaningful signal. It also shows community comments, historical scan results, and WHOIS data — useful context when you are unsure whether a site is legitimate.

**Where it falls short:** VirusTotal requires you to manually copy the URL and paste it into the site. For someone who got a link in an email and wants a quick answer, this adds friction. There is no background protection — it is a lookup tool, not a live shield. The browser extension exists but has limited functionality compared to the main web interface.

**Best for:** Tech-comfortable users who want to do a thorough one-off check on a specific URL before visiting it, or IT professionals investigating a suspicious site.

## ScamAdviser — Trust Score for Non-Technical Users

ScamAdviser scores websites on a trust scale from 0 to 100, pulling signals from domain registration age, hosting location, contact information, user reviews, and whether the site appears on known blacklists. A score below 40 is a red flag; above 80 is generally considered trustworthy.

**What it does well:** The trust score format is intuitive for people who do not want to interpret technical output. ScamAdviser also shows *why* a site scored low — for example, "domain registered 3 days ago" or "no return policy found." That context is genuinely useful.

**Where it falls short:** ScamAdviser is slower to flag new scam sites than engine-based tools. The trust score can also be gamed by sites that mimic legitimate signals (professional design, fake reviews). There is no browser extension, so every check requires opening a separate tab and pasting in the URL. Real-time scanning is partial — some data is cached from previous lookups.

**Best for:** Non-technical users who want a human-readable verdict on an unfamiliar shopping or service site.

## URLVoid — Aggregated Blacklist Lookup

URLVoid checks a URL against more than 30 security databases simultaneously — including Sucuri, PhishTank, OpenPhish, and SURBL — and returns a pass/fail for each. It also shows basic WHOIS information (registration date, registrar, hosting country).

**What it does well:** It is fast, free, and pulls from a wide set of established blacklists. The multi-source view means you can see if a site is flagged specifically for phishing (via PhishTank) versus malware distribution (via Sucuri) — useful for understanding the nature of the threat.

**Where it falls short:** Like ScamAdviser, URLVoid has no browser integration. You must copy and paste a URL to check it. The tool reports only what is already in the databases it queries — a new scam site will return clean results until it has been reported and processed. URLVoid also provides no subscription or account feature, so there is no history or alerting system.

**Best for:** A second opinion when another tool flags a site, or a quick lookup for a domain you stumbled across outside a browser session.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a website scam checker tool?

A website scam checker tool assesses whether a URL is likely to be fraudulent, malicious, or associated with phishing. It cross-references the domain against security databases, reputation feeds, blacklists, or runs it through multiple antivirus engines. Results range from simple pass/fail flags to detailed trust scores and engine-by-engine breakdowns.

### Is Google Safe Browsing enough on its own?

For most everyday browsing it provides a useful safety net, but it relies on a cached blocklist. Scam sites that are new — particularly those created specifically for a targeted phishing campaign — may not appear in the database yet. Combining Safe Browsing with a tool that checks real-time reputation (like Surfshark Scamcheck or VirusTotal) gives you better coverage.

### Can I use these tools on my phone?

VirusTotal and ScamAdviser both have mobile-accessible websites. URLVoid works on any browser. Google Safe Browsing protection is built into Chrome and Safari on iOS and Android. Surfshark Alert is available as a mobile app on both platforms, with Scamcheck running in the background the same way it does on desktop.

### Do free website scam checkers work as well as paid ones?

Free tools like VirusTotal and URLVoid do a solid job for manual, on-demand checks. Where paid options like Surfshark Scamcheck differ is in automation and coverage: always-on background scanning, faster flagging of new threats via proprietary feeds, and integration with broader security features like breach alerts. For users who are likely to skip a manual check under pressure, the always-on approach closes a real gap.

### Is it safe to paste URLs into a third-party checker?

Yes — the URL itself is not sensitive data. Entering a suspicious URL into VirusTotal, ScamAdviser, or URLVoid does not put your device at risk. You are submitting text to a web form, not visiting the site. Some services (VirusTotal in particular) may share submitted URLs with security researchers as part of their threat intelligence network, which is worth knowing if you are checking an internal or private URL.

### What should I do if a website scam checker flags a site?

Do not visit it. If the link arrived by email, do not reply to the email either — the sender address may also be spoofed. If the link claimed to be from a company you do business with (a bank, a retailer, a delivery service), go directly to that company's website by typing the address manually or using a saved bookmark, then check your account from there.
