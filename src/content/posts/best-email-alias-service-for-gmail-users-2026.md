## Why Gmail's Built-In Options Are Not Enough

Before comparing dedicated services, it is worth understanding what Gmail already offers — and where it hits a wall.

### The "+" Trick: Clever But Broken

Gmail lets you append a plus sign and any tag to your username: `yourname+shopping@gmail.com`, `yourname+newsletters@gmail.com`, and so on. All variants deliver to the same inbox. On paper, this looks like a free alias system.

In practice, it has two fatal flaws:

**First, it reveals your real address.** Anyone who receives `yourname+shopping@gmail.com` can trivially infer that `yourname@gmail.com` is the underlying account. The tag is transparent — it strips off with two keystrokes.

**Second, most spam-generating services strip the tag entirely.** Marketers learned this trick long ago. Many sign-up forms automatically remove everything from the `+` onward before storing your address. The tag you added for tracking purposes never gets saved — the service records your plain `yourname@gmail.com`, the same address you were trying to protect. You get the spam with none of the attribution.

### Multiple Gmail Accounts: A Maintenance Nightmare

Some users create separate Gmail accounts for different purposes — one for shopping, one for newsletters, one for work. This works until you have to check four inboxes, remember four passwords, and manually forward anything that needs to cross between them. It scales badly and does nothing to protect your primary address, which you will inevitably use somewhere it ends up on a list.

## Option 1: Gmail's "+" Trick

**Best for:** People who want zero setup and do not mind exposing their base address.

As covered above, the plus-tag system is baked into Gmail with no configuration required. You just type `yourname+anything@gmail.com` and Gmail delivers it.

**Strengths:**
- Zero cost, zero setup
- Unlimited variants
- No third-party service required

**Weaknesses:**
- Real address visible to any recipient
- Tags stripped by many sign-up forms before storage
- Cannot revoke a specific tag — the base address is always live
- Provides tracking in theory, not in practice

If the only goal is testing which service sold your data, the plus trick works when sites actually preserve the tag. For most people, it does not.

## Option 2: Apple Hide My Email

**Best for:** iPhone and Mac users already in the Apple ecosystem.

Apple's Hide My Email, included with iCloud+ or Apple One, generates random `@privaterelay.appleid.com` addresses that forward to your real inbox. It integrates natively into Safari and iPhone autofill — when a site asks for your email, Safari offers to generate a private relay address automatically.

**Strengths:**
- Seamless on Apple devices
- Real address never shared with the site
- Reply masking supported
- Unlimited aliases

**Weaknesses:**
- Forward destination must be an iCloud-associated address — you cannot set it to a non-Apple inbox directly
- Managed entirely inside Apple's ecosystem; switching away is friction
- No fake name generation
- Not available on Android or Windows outside a browser extension

For Gmail users on Android, or Gmail users who simply want their aliases to forward directly to Gmail, Hide My Email is a poor fit. The iCloud destination requirement is a hard wall for this use case.

**Does Alt-ID work with Gmail specifically, or only with certain email providers?**

**Why does Gmail's plus sign trick stop working on many sites?**

Many sign-up forms sanitize the email field before saving it to their database. A common sanitization step strips everything from the "+" character onward, leaving only `yourname@gmail.com`. This happens server-side, before you ever receive a confirmation email, so you have no visibility into it. The result is that your real address gets stored even though you used a tag. Dedicated alias services use a completely separate address and domain, which cannot be stripped back to your real address by any sanitization rule.

**Can I reply to emails through my alias without revealing my real Gmail?**

Yes, with a dedicated alias service that supports reply masking — which includes Alt-ID and SimpleLogin. When you reply to a forwarded email from your Gmail inbox, the outgoing message routes through the alias service so the recipient sees the alias address in the "From" field, not your real Gmail. The Gmail "+" trick has no equivalent feature: replies always show your real address.

**[What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) to existing emails if I disable an alias in Alt-ID?**

Emails already delivered to your Gmail inbox before you disabled the alias remain in your inbox untouched. Disabling the alias stops future forwarding — any new messages sent to that alias address are dropped or bounced depending on configuration. Emails already received are never affected.

**Is using an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-investment-account-notifications-2026/) service legal, and does it violate Gmail's terms?**

Using an alias service is completely legal and does not violate Gmail's terms of service. You are simply choosing which address to share publicly — the alias service handles forwarding to your real inbox. This is no different from having a work email address separate from a personal one. Privacy tools including [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-service-that-works-with-apple-mail-2026/)ing are standard recommendations from security professionals.

**How is Alt-ID different from just creating a second Gmail account?**

A second Gmail account is still a Google-hosted address linked to a real identity at sign-up. It can be scraped, breached, and linked back to your identity over time. Rotating it means creating yet another account and manually migrating everything. An Alt-ID alias is issued by a privacy-focused service, is easier to link back to your real address, and can be disabled and replaced in seconds without creating a new account or logging into a new inbox.
