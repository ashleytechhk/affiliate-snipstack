## Why Grandparents and Seniors Should Be Concerned

Many grandparents buy smartphones or tablets for their grandchildren as gifts. It is a kind and generous thing to do. But handing a child a connected device without protection is a little like giving them a bicycle without a helmet — the risk is invisible until something goes wrong.

Children are trusting by nature. They download free games, click on colorful pop-up ads, and accept app permissions without reading them. This behavior is completely normal for their age, but it creates an opening for bad actors who design apps specifically to exploit that trust.

Here is what spyware actually does once it gets onto a device:

- It reads private text messages and contact lists
- It records audio using the phone's microphone
- It tracks the child's physical location in real time
- It collects photos stored on the device
- It can gather passwords if the child logs into school or family accounts

None of this shows any obvious warning signs. The device continues to work normally. The apps look harmless. There is no flashing alarm.

The information collected can be used for targeted scams, identity theft, or worse. And because children often share devices with family members — using a grandparent's Wi-Fi, logging into a shared streaming account, or borrowing a tablet — the risk extends beyond just the child.

## What Is Spyware, Explained Simply

Spyware is a type of malicious software (often called malware) that hides inside another program. A child downloads what appears to be a fun game or a popular app. Hidden inside that download is a second, invisible program that immediately begins collecting information.

Unlike a virus — which typically causes obvious damage like crashing the device or deleting files — spyware is designed to stay hidden for as long as possible. The longer it goes undetected, the more data it gathers.

This is why ordinary virus removal is not enough. You need software specifically designed to scan for spyware behavior: programs that communicate secretly, request unusual permissions, or hide their activity from normal app lists.

## The Real Danger: How Spyware Reaches Your Family

**[Scan your family's devices now with Surfshark Antivirus](/blog/antivirus?sub=antivirus-that-scans-kids-devices-for-spyware-2026__mid)**

Spyware arrives through several common routes, most of which children encounter every day:

### Free Game Downloads

App stores are monitored, but malicious apps slip through regularly. Games that offer free "coins" or "upgrades" in exchange for installing a companion app are a particularly common delivery method.

### Clicking Links in Messages

Children receive messages from friends, classmates, and people they meet in online games. A single click on a bad link can trigger a background download without any visible prompt.

### School and Homework Apps

Some educational tools request broad permissions — access to the camera, microphone, contacts, and location — that go far beyond what any learning app actually needs. Most are legitimate, but not all.

### Public Wi-Fi Without Protection

When a child connects to the internet at a coffee shop, library, or shopping mall, their device is visible to others on the same network. An attacker on that network can attempt to inject software onto an unprotected device.

### Unofficial App Stores

Some children, especially teenagers, learn to download apps from outside the official app stores to get games or features that are restricted in their region. These unofficial sources have no safety checks at all.
