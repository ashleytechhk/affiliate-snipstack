## Why Grocery Loyalty Programs Are a Reliable Inbox Hazard

Not every mailing list is equally aggressive. Grocery loyalty programs sit near the top of the inbox-damage ranking for a specific set of structural reasons.

**1. Data is the product, not the perk.** Grocery chains collect purchase-level data on every loyalty member. What you buy, when you buy it, how your basket changes seasonally — this is high-quality behavioural data that third parties pay for. Your email address is the key that makes your profile addressable. The discount you got on your first visit is the acquisition cost for a data asset.

**2. Partnership networks are wide.** A single grocery chain typically has commercial relationships with insurance providers, pharmacy brands, fuel companies, financial services, meal-kit operators, and digital coupon platforms. Each partner relationship is a potential new email sender once they receive access to the member list.

**3. "Partner communications" clauses are standard.** Read the fine print on a loyalty program sign-up and you'll find a clause about sharing data with "selected commercial partners" or "affiliated brands." Opt-out checkboxes are sometimes pre-ticked. Even if you uncheck them, the definition of what counts as a "partner" can be interpreted broadly.

**4. Breach exposure is real.** Retail databases are a common target in data breaches because of the volume and richness of the data they hold. Member email lists from grocery chains have appeared in breach disclosures multiple times in recent years. Once your address is in a breach, the downstream spam is difficult to trace back to the source.

The pattern is predictable: sign up for the loyalty card, save money on groceries, spend the next six months managing a growing volume of promotional email from senders you've never heard of.

### Guerrilla Mail — Wrong Tool for Loyalty Cards

Guerrilla Mail provides a zero-signup, no-account throwaway inbox that you can use for immediate verification clicks. For a grocery loyalty card sign-up, it fails on multiple fronts.

Loyalty card accounts send ongoing email you need to receive:

- Points balance updates and expiry notices

- Password reset emails when you log into the loyalty app

Guerrilla Mail addresses expire within roughly an hour of inactivity. There is no forwarding — you would need to check a separate tab every time. The inbox is publicly accessible to anyone who guesses or knows the address. And Guerrilla Mail's domain is widely blocklisted, meaning many in-store tablet and online sign-up systems reject it at the email validation step.

**Best for:** Pure one-time confirmation clicks with no ongoing account relationship. Grocery loyalty programs are the wrong use case.

### Gmail + Addressing — Free but Privacy-Transparent

Gmail supports address tags: `yourname+kroger@gmail.com` delivers to `yourname@gmail.com`. It's free, requires no setup, and works with Gmail filters.

The problem is that it doesn't hide your real address. Anyone who receives `yourname+kroger@gmail.com` can trivially see that `yourname@gmail.com` is your real address. Many retailers and data brokers automatically strip everything after the `+` before storing the email — which means the tag gets removed, your real address gets filed, and spam bypasses your filtering rules.

You also have no kill-switch. You can set up filters to route email from senders who have your tagged address, but you can't revoke the address itself. Your real email is still circulating.

**Best for:** Mild inbox organisation where privacy is not the concern. Does not prevent spam or protect your real address.

Yes. In-store tablet sign-up forms and cashier-assisted sign-ups both accept any valid email address. Have your alias ready to paste from your phone's notes or password manager. **Will I still receive my points balance emails and digital receipts?**

Yes. Forwarding is continuous. Every email sent to the alias arrives in your real inbox, exactly as if the alias were your actual address. Points statements, tier upgrade notices, digital receipts — all of it lands normally. You only lose incoming mail if you manually disable or delete the alias.

**What if I need to log into the loyalty chain's app after signing up?**

You log in with the alias address, because that is the email the chain has on file for your account. The alias is permanent until you choose to delete it, so using it to authenticate weeks or months later works exactly as expected. If you delete the alias, you would need to update your email address in the loyalty account before deleting — or create a new account with a new alias.

**Can I use one alias across multiple grocery chains?**

Technically yes, but it defeats the purpose. If you use a single alias for five different chains and one chain's partner spam becomes unbearable, you cannot delete just that chain's channel without closing all five. Best practice is one alias per sign-up: one for each grocery chain, one for each loyalty program. Alt ID's unlimited alias model makes this practical at no extra cost.

**Is it possible the grocery store will reject an alias address?**

Some retailers run validation against known disposable-address domains. Guerrilla Mail and similar services are frequently blocked for this reason. Alt ID uses its own domain infrastructure, which is not blocklisted in the same way, and passes most commercial email validation systems. If a sign-up form ever rejects an address, you can try a freshly generated alias — the domain varies across accounts.

**Does using an alias mean the loyalty points are tied to a fake account?**

No. The loyalty account is real — the alias is just the email address associated with it. Your name on the account (whether real or the generated alternative from Alt ID), your purchase history, and your points balance all function normally. You are simply choosing which email address the chain holds for you, the same way you might use a work address for professional correspondence rather than your personal one.
