## What Is Ransomware, and Why Should Seniors Be Worried?

Ransomware is a type of malicious software — malware — that secretly installs itself on your computer, then locks your files or your entire screen. It then demands money (a "ransom") in exchange for unlocking them. The payment is usually demanded in cryptocurrency, making it nearly impossible to trace or recover.

Here is why seniors are disproportionately targeted:

- **Scammers know older adults often have savings.** Retirement accounts, home equity, and decades of saving make seniors financially attractive targets.
- **Ransomware often arrives disguised as something familiar.** A fake "Microsoft Security Alert," a phishing email that looks like it is from your bank, or a free software download — these are everyday situations that anyone can fall for.
- **The consequences are severe.** Family photos, tax documents, medical records — ransomware can lock everything. Some victims never get their files back even after paying.

According to the FBI, Americans over 60 reported losing more than **$3.4 billion** to cybercrime in 2023 — the highest of any age group. Ransomware is a major part of that figure.

## Signs That Ransomware Is on Your PC Right Now

Before going through removal steps, confirm what you are dealing with. Ransomware usually makes itself obvious, but here are the clearest signs:

| Warning Sign | What It Means |
|---|---|
| Screen covered with a red/black demand message | Active screen-locker ransomware |
| Files have changed names (e.g., "photo.jpg.locked") | File-encrypting ransomware is running |
| You cannot open documents, photos, or spreadsheets | Files already encrypted |
| Your PC is running very slowly for no reason | Ransomware may still be encrypting files |
| A timer is counting down on screen | Attacker is adding pressure — ignore the deadline |
| Desktop wallpaper changed to a ransom note | Ransomware has finished encrypting and is announcing itself |

If you see two or more of these signs, stop using your computer immediately and follow the steps below.

## Step 1 — Disconnect From the Internet Right Now

The very first thing to do is cut your computer's internet connection. Ransomware needs the internet to communicate with the attacker's server, to encrypt files to send back, and sometimes to spread to other devices on your home network (like a shared printer or a second PC).

**For a wired connection (ethernet cable):**
Unplug the network cable from the back of your computer. It looks like a slightly wider phone plug.

**For Wi-Fi:**
1. Look at the bottom-right corner of your screen, near the clock.
2. Click the Wi-Fi icon (it looks like a fan shape or a small signal tower).
3. Click **Disconnect** or toggle Wi-Fi to **Off**.

Once disconnected, do not reconnect until the removal process is complete.

This needs to be said clearly: **paying the ransom does not work most of the time.**

The only time paying ever made any sense was when no other option existed and the locked files were irreplaceable. Even then, it is a gamble. Follow the steps in this guide first.

## Step 3 — Restart Your PC in Safe Mode

Safe Mode starts Windows with the absolute minimum — just the essential parts of the operating system, without most programs loading automatically. Ransomware often cannot run properly in Safe Mode, which makes it much easier to remove.

**For [Windows 10](https://snipstack.io/windows-10-antivirus-free-download-2026/) or Windows 11:**

1. Click the **Start** button (the Windows logo at the bottom of your screen).
2. Click the **Power** button (it looks like a circle with a vertical line).
3. Hold the **Shift key** on your keyboard and click **Restart** at the same time.
4. Keep holding Shift until a blue screen appears with options.
5. Click **Troubleshoot**.
6. Click **Advanced options**.
7. Click **Startup Settings**.
8. Click **Restart**.
9. When your PC restarts and shows a numbered list, press the **4** key on your keyboard to choose **Safe Mode**, or press **5** to choose **Safe Mode with Networking** (needed if you want to download a removal tool).

Your screen may look slightly different in Safe Mode — lower resolution, black desktop background. That is normal.

## Step 5 — Recover Your Files From a Backup

Removing the ransomware program itself does not automatically unlock your files. Files that were encrypted before you disconnected the internet may remain locked. Here is [how to get](https://snipstack.io/how-to-get-us-phone-number-abroad-2026/) them back:

**Option A — Restore from a Windows Backup**

If you had Windows backup or File History turned on before the attack:

1. Click **Start** and type **Restore your files**.
2. Click **Restore your files with File History**.
3. Browse to the folder you need and select the files.
4. Click the green **Restore** button.

**Option B — Restore from an External Drive or USB**

If you regularly back up your photos and documents to an external hard drive or USB stick, connect it now and copy your files back. Make sure to scan that drive too before trusting it.

**Option C — Use a Free Decryption Tool**

A project called **No More Ransom** (nomoreransom.org) — run by law enforcement agencies including Europol and the FBI — offers free decryption tools for many known ransomware strains. Visit the site, upload one of your locked files, and it will tell you if a free decryptor exists for that particular ransomware.

## Step 6 — Change Your Passwords and Check Your Accounts

After removing the ransomware, some strains steal passwords and personal data before locking files. As a precaution:

1. Change your email password first — email is often used to reset everything else.
2. Change passwords for your bank and any financial accounts.
3. Change passwords for social media accounts.
4. Enable two-step verification (also called two-factor authentication) on your email and banking accounts if you have not already. This means even if a criminal has your password, they cannot log in without a second code sent to your phone.

If you use the same password on multiple sites (very common, and very risky), change all of them. A password like your street address, your birthdate, or "password123" can be cracked in seconds.

Cleaning up after ransomware is stressful and often involves permanent file loss. Preventing it in the first place is far simpler. Here is what actually works:

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I remove ransomware myself, or do I need a professional?

Most common ransomware strains can be removed by following the steps in this guide — Safe Mode, a free scanner, and a backup restore. You do not need to pay a computer repair shop for basic removal. However, if your PC has multiple drives, a business network, or you cannot get into Safe Mode at all, professional help becomes worth considering.

### Should I pay the ransom to get my files back?

No. Law enforcement agencies globally advise against it. Paying funds criminal activity, marks you as someone willing to pay again, and frequently does not result in file recovery. Work through the free decryption tools at nomoreransom.org first — these cover hundreds of known ransomware strains and are genuinely free.

### My files are encrypted and I have no backup. Are they gone forever?

Not necessarily. Check nomoreransom.org for a free decryptor matching your ransomware strain. Also check whether Windows had Shadow Copies enabled — right-click a locked file, select **Properties**, and look for a **Previous Versions** tab. If Shadow Copies were on before the attack, you may find earlier unencrypted versions of your files there.

### How did ransomware get onto my computer?

The most common entry points are: a malicious email attachment (often pretending to be an invoice, delivery notification, or court summons), a link in a phishing email, a download from an unofficial software site, or a fake pop-up ad that prompted you to download something. Ransomware very rarely appears out of nowhere — something you clicked or downloaded allowed it in.

### How do I know if ransomware is still running after the scan?

After running a full scan and removing everything found, restart your PC normally (not in Safe Mode) and open a few files. If they open normally, the ransomware program has been removed. If files are still scrambled, the ransomware itself is gone but the encrypted files remain — those need to be recovered from a backup or decryptor, not scanned again.
