## What Is PII and Why Does It Make You a Target?

PII stands for personally identifiable information. It is any data that can be used to identify a specific person. For insurance agents, that includes:

- Full legal names and dates of birth
- Social Security numbers or tax ID numbers
- Home addresses and phone numbers
- Bank account or credit card information
- Medical history or health-related details (for life or health insurance policies)
- Driver's license numbers

Hackers do not need to break into a bank vault to get this information. They just need to get into your computer. And they are very good at it.

Criminals specifically look for small businesses and self-employed professionals because they assume — correctly, in many cases — that these individuals do not have a dedicated IT team watching their systems. A solo insurance agent working from a home office is, from a hacker's point of view, a soft target with a gold mine of valuable data.

## The Threats You Face Every Day (Even Without Knowing It)

You do not have to click on something suspicious for malware to get onto your computer. Here are the most common ways insurance agents get infected:

### Phishing Emails That Look Real

Criminals send emails that look like they come from your insurance carrier, a banking partner, or even a client. One click on a fake link and your login credentials are handed over. These emails have become so convincing that even experienced computer users get fooled.

### Infected File Attachments

A client sends you a PDF of their documents. You open it. Unknown to you, the file also installs a hidden program that records your keystrokes and sends your passwords to a criminal overseas. This type of malware — called a keylogger — is extremely common.

### Fake Software Updates

A pop-up appears on your screen saying your browser or computer needs an urgent update. You click it. Instead of an update, you download spyware that quietly copies your files.

### Outdated Software Vulnerabilities

Hackers look for computers running old, unpatched versions of Windows, browsers, or common programs. If your computer has not been updated, there are known holes in the software that criminals can exploit.
