## The Real Risk: How Client Files Get Compromised

Most freelance photographers do not lose client files to a dramatic hacking attempt. They lose them to ordinary, everyday habits that criminals have learned to exploit.

### Fake Client Inquiries With Infected Attachments

A message arrives that looks like a new booking inquiry — event details and a "signed contract" attached. The attachment is a disguised file. Opening it can quietly install malware that spreads through your folders, including every client gallery on the drive.

### Presets, Actions, and Plugins From Untrusted Sources

Free Lightroom presets, Photoshop actions, and editing plugins are widely shared online, and not all of them come from trustworthy places. A downloaded file that promises a shortcut to better edits can carry hidden code that scans your system for valuable files — and client photo folders are exactly what it looks for.

### Memory Cards and USB Drives Passed Between Devices

Photographers regularly move memory cards and backup drives between a camera, a laptop, and sometimes a shared computer at home. A single infected device in that chain can pass a virus along without any warning appearing.

### Ransomware That Spreads Through Cloud Backups

If your computer becomes infected and client folders sync automatically to cloud storage, the infected or encrypted files can spread to your backup before you notice — turning one mistake into a total loss.

Yes.
