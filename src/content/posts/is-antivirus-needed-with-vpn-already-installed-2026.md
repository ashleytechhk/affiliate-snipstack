## What a VPN Actually Does (And What It Does Not)

A VPN — short for Virtual Private Network — does one main thing: it hides your internet activity from outsiders. Think of it like drawing the curtains in your house. People outside cannot see in. But if someone is already inside your house, the curtains do nothing.

**A VPN protects you from:**
- Hackers on public WiFi networks (at airports, coffee shops, hotels)
- Your internet provider seeing which websites you visit
- Advertisers tracking your location through your IP address

**A VPN cannot protect you from:**
- A virus that arrives in an email attachment
- Clicking a link on a fake website that looks like your bank
- A scam pop-up that tricks you into downloading malicious software
- Malware already installed on your device
- Ransomware that locks your files and demands payment

This is not a flaw in any particular VPN. It is simply not what VPNs are built to do. A VPN secures your connection. Antivirus software secures your device. They protect against different threats.

## Why Seniors Are Targeted More Often

![How VPN and antivirus work together — diagram showing connection security vs device security](/og/is-antivirus-needed-with-vpn-already-installed-2026-inline-1.png)

Cybercriminals are not random. They target people they believe are more likely to click a suspicious link, trust an official-looking email, or panic when a scary message appears on screen.

Older adults are targeted because:

- **More savings, more to steal.** Retirement accounts, pension deposits, and home equity make seniors high-value targets.
- **Less experience with digital scams.** Many of today's scam techniques — fake invoice emails, IRS impersonation texts, "your account has been suspended" messages — were not around a decade ago.
- **Isolation.** Scammers count on people acting alone without checking with someone younger who might catch the warning signs.
- **Trust in authority.** Fake messages pretending to be from Medicare, the Social Security Administration, or a well-known bank are specifically crafted to feel believable.

A VPN does not block any of these. If a scam email lands in your inbox and you click the attachment, a VPN will not stop what happens next.

## The Threats That Antivirus Stops (That a VPN Cannot)

### 1. Virus-infected email attachments

An email arrives looking like a shipping notification, a Medicare update, or a message from a grandchild. You open the attachment. A virus installs itself quietly, often without any obvious sign. Days later, criminals have access to your saved passwords, your photos, your banking details.

Antivirus software scans email attachments before they can cause harm. A VPN does not scan anything — it just routes your traffic through a different server.

### 2. Fake websites that steal passwords

You search for your bank's phone number. You click what looks like the right result. The page looks exactly like your bank's website. You type your username and password. You have just handed them to a criminal.

Antivirus software with web protection recognizes fake websites and blocks them before you can enter any details. A VPN has no way to tell a fake website from a real one.

### 3. Ransomware

You click a link in an email or download a file that seemed legitimate. Within minutes, your files are locked. A message appears demanding hundreds or thousands of dollars to unlock them. This is ransomware, and it devastated hundreds of thousands of individuals and businesses in recent years.

Antivirus software detects and blocks ransomware at the point of entry. A VPN will route your traffic normally and have no idea anything is wrong.

### 4. Scam pop-ups pretending to be Microsoft or Apple

A window fills your screen. It says your computer is infected and you must call a number immediately. The number connects to a criminal pretending to be tech support. They ask you to give them remote access to your computer. Once they have it, they steal everything.

Antivirus software flags the malicious scripts behind these pop-ups. A VPN does not.

### Does a VPN protect me from viruses?

No. A VPN encrypts your internet connection, which protects your data while it travels between your device and the internet. It does not scan files, block malware, or detect dangerous software on your device. For virus protection, you need antivirus software — these are two different tools that do two different jobs.
