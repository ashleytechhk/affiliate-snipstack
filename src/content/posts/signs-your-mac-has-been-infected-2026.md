## Why Seniors Are Targeted More Than Anyone Else

Cybercriminals do not pick victims randomly. They target people who are most likely to act on fear, least likely to recognize a scam, and most likely to have savings worth stealing.

Seniors tick all three boxes in the criminals' calculations, unfairly or not. Older adults are more likely to trust authority figures — including fake "Apple support" callers. They may be less familiar with what normal Mac behavior looks like, making it harder to notice when something is wrong. And many retired people have accumulated savings that make them attractive targets.

The tactics used against seniors are not sophisticated. They rely on confusion and fear:

- A pop-up that looks exactly like an Apple security warning
- A phone call from someone claiming to be from Microsoft or Apple
- An email that looks like it came from your bank
- A website that suddenly locks your browser and demands you call a number

None of these require a virus in the technical sense. But they all signal that your Mac's security posture needs attention — either because malware is already present, or because your current setup has no [protection against](https://snipstack.io/protection-against-fileless-malware-attacks-2026/) the next attempt.

## The 9 [Signs Your](https://snipstack.io/signs-your-phone-has-a-virus-2026/) Mac Has Been Infected

### Sign 1: Your Mac Has Become Noticeably Slower

A Mac that has always been fast but suddenly feels sluggish — taking longer to open apps, load web pages, or wake from sleep — may have a program running in the background that you never installed. Malware and adware frequently use your Mac's processing power to do things you cannot see: sending spam, mining cryptocurrency, or reporting your activity back to a remote server.

This is different from normal aging. A Mac that slows down gradually over years is just getting old. A Mac that slows down suddenly, especially after you opened an attachment or visited an unfamiliar website, deserves a closer look.

**[What to do](https://snipstack.io/what-to-do-after-malware-infection-2026/):** Open Activity Monitor (found in Applications > Utilities) and look for any process using a very high percentage of CPU that you do not recognize. Take a screenshot and search the process name online if it looks unfamiliar.

---

### Sign 2: Pop-Ups Keep Appearing — Even When Safari Is Closed

Occasional pop-up ads while browsing are annoying but normal. Pop-ups that appear on your desktop when Safari is not even open are not normal. This is a strong indicator of adware — a category of software that displays advertising without your permission and often without you knowing how it got there.

Adware usually arrives bundled with a free download: a free PDF converter, a browser update prompt from a website, or a "free tool" that seemed useful. You may not remember installing it because it was buried in the fine print of something else you agreed to.

### Sign 3: Your Browser's Homepage or Search Engine Changed Without Your Permission

You have always used Google. Now, every time you open Safari, it opens to a page called "SearchBaron" or "MyWay" or something else you have never heard of. Your search results look different — more ads, less useful answers.

This is a browser hijacker. It is a type of malware that changes your browser settings to redirect your searches through a different engine, earning advertising revenue for the criminal while showing you results that may include links to further scams or malware.

Browser hijackers are frustratingly persistent. Changing your homepage back in Safari's settings may not be enough — the hijacker often reinstalls itself through a hidden browser extension or a background process.

---

### Sign 4: New Toolbars or Extensions in Your Browser That You Did Not Install

Open Safari and click the puzzle-piece icon (or go to Safari > Settings > Extensions). If you see extensions listed there that you do not remember adding — especially ones with generic names like "Search Helper," "Smart Search," "Price Finder," or "NewTabSearches" — these are red flags.

Legitimate extensions come from the App Store or are installed when you consciously set up a known tool like a password manager. Extensions you do not recognize should be removed immediately.

---

### Sign 5: Your Mac's Fan Runs Constantly and Gets Very Hot

Your Mac's cooling fan runs faster when the processor is working hard. If the fan runs loudly all the time — even when you are just checking email or reading a website — something is using your computer's power in the background.

Cryptocurrency mining malware is particularly guilty of this. It hijacks your Mac's processing power to generate digital currency for a criminal, 24 hours a day. You pay the electricity bill; they collect the profits. Your Mac gets hot, runs slower, and may have a shortened lifespan as a result.

---

### Sign 6: Programs You Did Not Install Are Appearing in Your Applications Folder

Open Finder, click on Applications in the sidebar, and scroll through the list. If you see programs you do not recognize — or programs you definitely did not install — treat them with suspicion. Common examples include fake "Mac Cleaner" apps, "MacKeeper" (which installs itself aggressively through pop-ups), or utilities with names you simply do not recognize.

Legitimate software does not install itself without your knowledge.

---

### Sign 7: Friends and Family Are Receiving Strange Emails or Messages From You

If someone contacts you to say they received a strange email from your address — or that you posted something on social media that you have no memory of — your accounts may have been compromised. This can happen when malware logs your keystrokes and captures your login passwords, which are then used to send spam or scam messages through your accounts.

If this happens, change your passwords immediately, starting with your email account. Use a different device (your phone) to change them, in case the Mac is still compromised.

---

### Sign 8: You Are Being Asked to Enter Your Apple ID or Password More Often Than Usual

Your Mac will occasionally ask you to confirm your Apple ID password for legitimate reasons — app updates, iCloud sync, purchases. If it starts asking repeatedly, especially for things that do not seem to require it, or if the password prompt looks slightly different from the usual Apple design, be suspicious.

Some malware creates fake authentication prompts to harvest your Apple ID credentials. Your Apple ID controls access to iCloud, your Photos, your backup files, and your payment method on file with Apple.

---

### Sign 9: A Fake "Virus Alert" or "Apple Warning" Has Appeared on Your Screen

This is the most dangerous sign on the list — not because it means your Mac is definitely infected, but because it is designed to frighten you into taking an action that creates the infection (or a far worse problem).

Fake virus alerts mimic Apple's design. They may display a realistic-looking warning, a countdown timer, or a [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-privacy-for-social-media-2026/) to call. They sometimes lock your browser so it appears you cannot close the tab. If this happens:

1. Do not call any phone number shown on the screen.
2. Do not enter any personal information.
3. Force-quit Safari: press Command + Option + Escape, select Safari, and click Force Quit.
4. If the browser re-opens to the same page, hold Shift while clicking the Safari icon — this prevents it from restoring your previous session.

The warning itself, in most cases, is the threat.
