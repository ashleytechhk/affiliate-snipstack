## Why iPad Users Are Targeted More Than Ever

Many people believe that iPads cannot get viruses. That belief — while partly true for traditional viruses — is exactly what scammers count on. The logic goes: "I have an iPad, so I'm safe," and then the guard comes down.

The truth is more nuanced. Apple's iOS operating system does block most traditional malware from installing itself. However, that protection stops at the door of Safari or whichever browser you use. Once you click a link in an email, tap a pop-up ad, or visit a fraudulent website, you are in territory that Apple's system security was never designed to handle.

Here is what does happen on iPads every single day:

- **Phishing websites** that look identical to your bank's login page, designed to steal your username and password
- **Tech-support scam pop-ups** that claim your device has a virus and ask you to call a phone number
- **Fake parcel-tracking pages** that ask for your credit card to release a delivery
- **Unsecured public Wi-Fi** at cafes or airports that lets strangers snoop on what you are typing

None of these are stopped by iOS alone. They are browser and network threats, not operating-system threats. That distinction is critical.

### Phishing: The Most Common Threat for Seniors

Phishing is when a criminal creates a fake version of a trusted website — your bank, a delivery service, a government agency — and tricks you into typing in your details. The fake site looks completely real. The web address might say something like "secure-bank-login.net" instead of your actual bank's address, but most people do not look that closely.

A security app with real-time web protection checks every website you visit against a constantly updated database of known scam sites. If there is a match, it blocks the page before it even loads and shows you a warning. You never see the fake page at all.

### Unsafe Wi-Fi: The Silent Eavesdropper

Public Wi-Fi at a library, airport, or coffee shop is convenient. It is also one of the easiest places for someone with basic technical skills to intercept what you are doing online. They can see which websites you visit, and in some cases capture passwords or account details.

A VPN — which is included in Surfshark's [security suite](https://snipstack.io/security-suite-vs-paying-for-each-tool-separately-2026/) — encrypts your internet connection so that even if someone is watching the network, all they see is scrambled, unreadable data. ### Spam and Scam Calls

Many seniors report receiving calls from people claiming to be from Apple Support, Amazon, or a government agency. These calls often follow up on something you clicked online.
