## What Is Ransomware, Exactly?

Think of ransomware like a padlock that a criminal secretly snaps onto your filing cabinet. Once the lock is on, only they have the key — and they will only hand it over if you pay.

Technically, ransomware is a malicious program (malware) that encrypts your files. Encryption scrambles the contents so they are unreadable without a special code. The attacker holds that code and demands a ransom, usually in cryptocurrency, to send it to you.

The most common ways ransomware gets onto a senior's computer:

- **Phishing emails** — a message that looks like it is from a bank, delivery company, or government agency, with an attachment or link
- **Fake software updates** — a pop-up on a website telling you to "update Adobe Flash" or "install a security patch"
- **Infected USB drives** — plugging in a thumb drive received from an unknown source
- **Pirated software** — downloaded programs from unofficial websites

Once the ransomware file lands on your computer, it waits for you to open it. That is the moment it starts running. That is also the moment it can still be stopped.

## How Ransomware Behaves Before It Locks Your Files

Here is the part most guides skip: ransomware does not lock your files instantly. It goes through several stages first, and each stage is a window to stop it.

![Ransomware detection stages — step-by-step illustrated guide](/og/how-to-detect-ransomware-before-it-runs-2026-inline-1.png)

**Stage 1: Delivery**
The infected file arrives on your computer — usually via email, download, or a USB drive. It is sitting on your hard drive doing nothing yet.

**Stage 2: Execution**
You open the file or click a link. The ransomware program starts running. This is the critical moment — a real-time antivirus scans the file the instant it tries to execute and blocks it here.

**Stage 3: Establishing control**
The ransomware tries to connect to the criminal's server to get its encryption key. A good antivirus or firewall can block this connection.

**Stage 4: Reconnaissance**
The program quietly maps your hard drive, looking for documents, photos, and important files to encrypt. It avoids system files so your computer keeps running normally — you notice nothing.

**Stage 5: Encryption**
Files start changing. If you happen to be watching, you might notice files suddenly cannot open, or their icons look different. By this point, it is already too late without a backup.

**Stage 6: Ransom demand**
A message appears on your screen. Your files are locked.

The key takeaway: Stages 1 through 3 are where detection tools stop ransomware. After Stage 4, your only hope is a backup or a decryption tool — and decryption tools only exist for a small number of known ransomware types.

## Warning Signs Your Computer May Already Be Infected

Watch for these red flags. If you see them, stop using your computer and seek help immediately:

- **Files you cannot open** — clicking a photo or document gives an error
- **File names look strange** — extensions like `.encrypted`, `.locked`, or random letters added to file names
- **Sudden slowness** — your computer working unusually hard for no clear reason
- **Pop-up warning messages** — especially ones demanding payment or threatening legal action
- **Programs crashing** — applications that normally work fine suddenly fail
- **A new "readme" text file** — ransomware often drops a file with payment instructions

If any of these apply, do not panic. Unplug your computer from the internet immediately (pull out the ethernet cable or turn off Wi-Fi) to stop the ransomware from spreading to other devices. Then call a trusted family member or local computer repair shop.

### 2. Keep Windows or macOS Updated

Software updates are not just about new features. Most updates patch security holes that ransomware uses to get in. Turn on automatic updates so you never fall behind.

On Windows: Go to Settings > Windows Update > Turn on automatic updates.
On Mac: Go to System Preferences > Software Update > Check "Automatically keep my Mac up to date."

### 3. Be Suspicious of Email Attachments

Even if an email looks like it is from a friend, bank, or government office:

- Never open attachments you were not expecting
- Hover your mouse over a link to see the real web address before clicking
- When in doubt, call the sender directly using a phone number you already know — not one in the email

### 4. Back Up Your Files Regularly

A backup does not stop ransomware, but it does mean you can recover everything without paying a criminal. Use an external hard drive (kept unplugged from your computer when not in use) or a cloud backup service. If ransomware hits, you restore from backup and lose nothing.

### 5. Do Not Plug In Unknown USB Drives

If you find a USB drive in a parking lot or receive one in the mail from someone you do not know, do not plug it in. This is a known tactic criminals use to spread malware.
