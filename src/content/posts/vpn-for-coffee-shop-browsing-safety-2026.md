## What Is a VPN and What Does It Actually Do?

VPN stands for Virtual Private Network. When you connect through a VPN, your device routes all internet traffic through an encrypted tunnel to a server operated by the VPN provider. From the perspective of anyone monitoring the local network — or of the websites you visit — your connection appears to come from that server, not from you.

Two outcomes matter in practice:

1. **Encryption in transit.** Your data is scrambled before it leaves your device. Even on a network you don't control, your traffic is protected against interception.
2. **IP masking.** Websites and apps see the VPN server's IP address, not your real one. This changes your apparent location and makes it harder to track you across sessions.

That's the core mechanism. Everything else — streaming access, safer banking, privacy — flows from those two properties.

## What Actually Happens on a Café WiFi Network

Public WiFi is a shared network. In practical terms:

**Unencrypted HTTP traffic is readable.** HTTPS — the secure version — encrypts the content of your connections. But a significant share of web traffic, especially from older apps, still travels unencrypted. On a shared network, that content can be read by anyone monitoring the traffic.

**HTTPS protects content but not metadata.** Even on HTTPS sites, your device broadcasts which domains you're visiting, when, and for how long. Anyone analyzing network traffic can build a clear picture of your browsing patterns from this metadata alone.

**Evil twin attacks are real, if uncommon.** A bad actor can set up a fake WiFi network with a convincing name — "CafeBrew_Guest," say — and intercept connections from users who join it. This is less common than the movies suggest but requires no special hardware.

**Session hijacking via unsecured cookies.** Some older apps and services transmit session tokens over unencrypted connections. These tokens can, in principle, be captured and reused to access your accounts.

A VPN doesn't make you immune to every threat — no single tool does. But encrypting your traffic from the device outward addresses most of the passive risks specific to shared public networks.

## How a VPN Protects You on Public WiFi

Here's what actually happens when you activate a VPN at the café counter:

**Step 1 — Encrypted tunnel.** The moment you tap connect, your VPN app establishes an encrypted tunnel to a VPN server. All traffic — every app, every browser tab — goes through that tunnel before it touches the café's network.

**Step 2 — Traffic is unreadable to local observers.** Even if someone on the same network is running packet-capture software, they see encrypted noise, not readable data.

**Step 3 — DNS requests are protected.** Without a VPN, your device queries DNS servers to translate domain names into IP addresses. These queries are often visible to your ISP or anyone monitoring the local network. A VPN routes DNS through its own servers, keeping these lookups private.

**Step 4 — Your real IP stays hidden.** Websites see the VPN server's IP address. This limits location-based tracking and reduces the chance of targeted attacks based on your home or work address.

Worth noting: a VPN doesn't protect against malware you've already installed, phishing pages you click through voluntarily, or compromised account passwords. It addresses network-layer risks, not every possible threat vector.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is café WiFi actually dangerous, or is this overstated?

It's a real but context-dependent risk. Most modern websites use HTTPS, which protects the content of your browsing. What HTTPS doesn't protect is metadata — which sites you're visiting, when, and for how long — that's visible to anyone monitoring the local network. The higher-risk scenarios involve older apps using unencrypted connections, evil-twin networks, or DNS hijacking. A VPN addresses all of these at the network level.

### Does a VPN make me completely anonymous online?

No. A VPN reduces the amount of information available to passive observers on your local network and hides your real IP from websites. It does not make you anonymous against a determined adversary, logged-in tracking (sites that know who you are because you signed in), or browser fingerprinting. Think of it as a meaningful privacy layer, not a complete solution.

### Will a VPN slow down my connection at the café?

Some speed reduction is normal since traffic routes through an additional server. With a quality provider using WireGuard protocol, the reduction is typically small — fast enough for HD video streaming and video calls. The bigger variable is the quality of the underlying WiFi. A slow café connection will still feel slow through a VPN; a fast one will feel nearly identical.

### Do I need a VPN if I only visit HTTPS sites?

HTTPS encrypts the content of your connections, which is meaningful. However, your device still broadcasts DNS queries and connection metadata — which domains you're visiting — to anyone monitoring the local network. A VPN encrypts this layer as well, and additionally protects traffic from any apps that don't use HTTPS. On a shared public network, the extra layer matters.
