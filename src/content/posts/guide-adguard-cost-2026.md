## What Is AdGuard and What Does It Actually Block?

AdGuard is an [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-extensions-2026/) that operates at two different levels depending on which product you use.

**The browser extension** works inside your browser — Chrome, Firefox, Edge, Safari — intercepting ad requests before the page renders them. It is free, requires no account, and is one of the cleaner implementations of browser-level blocking available. You install it, toggle it on, and most display ads and many trackers disappear. It applies cosmetic filters too, meaning empty ad containers are hidden rather than left as white boxes.

**The desktop and mobile apps** work differently. They create a local filtering layer that intercepts all traffic on the device — not just browser traffic. This means ads in other apps, HTTPS traffic (with a local certificate), and traffic from apps that do not use a browser at all. The apps require a paid license.

What AdGuard blocks across both tiers:

- Display ads and video pre-roll ads on most sites
- Third-party trackers and analytics scripts
- Known malware and phishing domains
- Cookie consent pop-ups (with the Annoyances filter list enabled)
- Social media tracking widgets

What it does not block reliably:

- YouTube ads in the Chrome browser (Google actively fights extension-based blocking)
- First-party ad serving (when the site serves ads from its own domain)
- In-app ads on iOS (the iOS version has stricter system limits)

## AdGuard Pricing Tiers Explained

### Free Tier — Browser Extension

The AdGuard browser extension is completely free. No account, no trial, no expiry. You install it from the Chrome Web Store, Firefox Add-ons, or the Safari extension gallery, and it works indefinitely.

What the free extension includes:

- Ad blocking on web pages
- Tracker blocking
- Phishing domain protection
- Cosmetic filtering (hiding empty ad containers)
- Basic filter list management

What it does not include:

- System-wide blocking (only works inside the browser)
- Mobile app ad blocking
- Custom DNS filtering
- The full range of filter lists available in paid apps

For users who browse primarily on a desktop in one browser, the free extension covers the majority of use cases. The main gaps are YouTube ad blocking (inconsistent in Chrome since Google's 2024 countermeasures) and any ads outside the browser.

### Personal Plan — 3 Devices

The Personal plan covers any three devices across Windows, macOS, Android, and iOS. Devices can be mixed — one Windows laptop, one Android phone, one iPad, for example.

Pricing (check adguard.com for current rates — prices vary by region and promotional period):

- **Annual:** Typically in the $25–$35/year range
- **Lifetime:** Typically in the $55–$75 one-time range

The Personal plan unlocks the full desktop apps (Windows and Mac) and the full Android app, which provides system-wide blocking — covering in-app ads as well as browser ads. The iOS app has more restrictions due to Apple's sandboxing, but still provides Safari content blocking and a DNS-over-HTTPS filtering mode.

### Family Plan — 9 Devices

The Family plan covers nine devices, same platform flexibility. It is designed for households with multiple people or power users who run multiple devices.

Pricing:

- **Annual:** Typically in the $50–$65/year range
- **Lifetime:** Typically in the $110–$140 one-time range

The per-device math at nine devices is significantly cheaper than buying two or three Personal plans. If you have four or more devices to cover, the Family plan is almost always the better unit economics.

### Annual vs. Lifetime: Which Is Better Value?

The lifetime license pays for itself in roughly two to three years depending on current pricing. If you expect to use AdGuard for more than three years — and the product has been around since 2014, so longevity is not a concern — the lifetime option is mathematically better.

The case for annual:

- Lower upfront cost
- Flexibility to switch products if something better emerges
- Easier to expense if this is for a business

The case for lifetime:

- Better long-term value for committed users
- No renewal reminders or payment lapses
- Particularly good value when purchased during AdGuard's periodic sales (they run discounts several times a year)

## Is AdGuard Worth the Cost?

That depends on two things: what devices you are protecting, and whether you need anything beyond ad blocking.

**The case for paying:**

If you have more than one device and want ads blocked in apps — not just in a browser — the paid apps earn their cost quickly. The Android app in particular is one of the most capable system-wide [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-free-chrome-2026/)s available on that platform. For a household with a few Android and Windows devices, the Family lifetime plan works out to less than a decent lunch per device, permanently.

**The case for the free extension:**

If you browse primarily on a desktop in Firefox or Chrome, the free browser extension is genuinely capable. The paid apps add features that matter more on mobile or for system-wide filtering. There is no need to pay if the extension covers your actual use case.

**The case for an alternative:**

AdGuard's pricing structure charges per device. If you want to cover a household — a laptop, two phones, a tablet, and a smart TV — you are looking at the Family plan, plus you still do not have VPN coverage for privacy on public networks.

Surfshark's subscription includes [CleanWeb](https://snipstack.io/surfshark-cleanweb-review-2026/), a DNS-level [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-firefox-2026/) that covers unlimited devices simultaneously. DNS blocking does not offer cosmetic filtering or granular per-site control the way AdGuard's apps do, but it covers devices that AdGuard cannot reach — smart TVs, gaming consoles, anything connected to a network. If you want VPN + ad blocking across every device without per-device licensing, the bundled approach often costs less annually.

## FAQ

### Is AdGuard completely free?

The AdGuard browser extension is completely free with no trial period or expiry — it works in Chrome, Firefox, Edge, and Safari indefinitely. The desktop apps (Windows, Mac) and mobile apps (Android, iOS) require a paid Personal or Family license, though desktop and Android offer a 14-day free trial.

### [How much does](https://snipstack.io/how-much-does-surfshark-one-cost-2026/) AdGuard cost per year?

Pricing varies by region and promotional period. As a general reference, the Personal plan (3 devices) typically runs in the $25–$35/year range, and the Family plan (9 devices) in the $50–$65/year range. Check adguard.com for current rates — AdGuard runs discounts regularly that can lower these figures meaningfully.

### Is AdGuard's lifetime license worth it?

For most users who plan to use AdGuard for three or more years, the lifetime license is better value than renewing annually. AdGuard has been in operation since 2014 and is well-established — longevity risk is low. The lifetime option is particularly attractive during AdGuard's periodic sales.

### What is the difference between AdGuard Personal and Family?

Both plans include the same features and work across Windows, macOS, Android, and iOS. Personal covers 3 devices; Family covers 9 devices. The per-device cost of the Family plan is significantly lower when you have four or more devices to protect. Both are available as annual subscriptions or one-time lifetime purchases.

### Does AdGuard block YouTube ads?

In Chrome, AdGuard's browser extension has inconsistent results with YouTube ads — Google actively updates YouTube to detect and work around extension-based blocking. In Firefox, results are somewhat better. The paid desktop app with HTTPS filtering enabled has more success than the browser extension alone. For YouTube on the Android app or smart TV, a DNS-level approach tends to be more reliable.

### What is a cheaper alternative to AdGuard for multiple devices?

For households wanting ad blocking across many devices without per-device licensing, Surfshark CleanWeb is a common alternative. It is a DNS-level [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-chrome-2026/) included in Surfshark's VPN subscription, covering unlimited devices simultaneously — including smart TVs and gaming consoles that dedicated [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-reddit-2026/) apps cannot reach. It lacks AdGuard's cosmetic filtering and per-site control, but the coverage breadth and bundle value are strong for multi-device households.

### Can I use AdGuard on my smart TV?

AdGuard's apps do not have a dedicated smart TV version. Some users configure [AdGuard Home](https://snipstack.io/adguard-home-setup-on-raspberry-pi-2026/) (AdGuard's self-hosted DNS solution, which is free and open-source) at the router level to cover smart TVs and other devices. This requires more technical setup. DNS-level VPN-based solutions like Surfshark CleanWeb cover smart TVs through the VPN app installed on the TV or through router-level configuration.
