## Why Downloads Are the Biggest Threat Seniors Face Online

Most people think of computer viruses as something you catch by visiting a "bad website." That was true 20 years ago. Today, attackers have gotten smarter.

Modern malware hides inside files that look completely normal:

- A **PDF** that claims to be a Medicare statement
- A **Word document** that looks like a grandchild's school project
- A **photo** (.jpg or .png) with hidden code embedded inside
- A **ZIP file** pretending to contain software you searched for
- A **form** your local government office emailed you a link to

When you open any of these files, the malware activates. Within seconds it can log every key you press, take screenshots of your screen, or quietly copy your saved passwords from your browser.

Seniors are specifically targeted because scammers know that older adults are more likely to trust official-looking documents, less likely to spot technical red flags, and often have larger savings accounts tied to the same devices they use to check email.

The FBI's Internet Crime Report for 2024 found that adults over 60 reported **$3.4 billion** in cyber losses — more than any other demographic. That number has grown every single year.

## The Risks of Having No Download Scanner

Some people rely on Windows Defender — the security software that comes built into Windows 10 and 11. It is better than nothing, but independent lab tests consistently show it misses 5–15% of real-world threats that paid antivirus products catch. For Mac users, the situation is even more concerning: many seniors believe Macs cannot get viruses. That is no longer true. Mac-targeted malware increased by over 50% in 2024.

Here is what can happen when a malicious download slips through:

**Ransomware**: Your files — family photos, tax documents, everything — get encrypted and locked. You receive a message demanding payment to unlock them. Even if you pay, recovery is not guaranteed.

**Banking trojans**: A program runs silently in the background, recording everything you type. The next time you log into your bank, the attacker captures your username and password.

**Remote access tools (RATs)**: A scammer gains full control of your computer and can operate it as if they were sitting right in front of it — opening files, moving money, changing passwords.

**Spyware**: The program watches your screen and transmits screenshots or webcam footage to unknown parties.

None of these announce themselves. You would have no idea anything was wrong until the damage was done.
