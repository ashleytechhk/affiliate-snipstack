## Where App Store Refund Correspondence Actually Leaks

Not every refund request touches the same channel. Apple's report-a-problem flow and Google Play's in-app refund request both run through your signed-in account — nothing to protect there. The exposure shows up in three other places.

### Developer refund-request web forms

Apps outside the standard refund windows often route disputes through their own website contact form instead of the platform's native flow. That form feeds a support desk tool, and support desks are built to capture contact data for follow-up — which is exactly what "follow-up" means once your ticket is marked resolved.

### Third-party refund and subscription-cancellation services

Tools that chase down refunds or cancel subscriptions on your behalf need an address to correspond with the developer and confirm the outcome. That's a second party now holding your contact details, on top of the developer's own records.

### Post-refund surveys and win-back sequences

The most predictable leak isn't a breach — it's the marketing automation that fires once a ticket closes: satisfaction surveys, product updates, and win-back offers are standard CRM behavior, not an accident.

An alias breaks this at the point of contact, giving each channel an address unrelated to anything else in your inbox — one you can shut off the moment it turns noisy.

## Don't Alias Your Actual Apple ID or Google Play Account Email

One clarification before you set anything up: this guide covers correspondence *around* a refund request — developer contact forms, refund-tracking services, and the marketing that follows — not the account email tied to the purchase itself.

Apple and Google both match refund eligibility to the account that made the purchase. Apple's report-a-problem flow runs through your signed-in Apple ID directly — there's no separate email field to swap out, and no reason to touch it. The same applies to a Google Play refund request submitted from your Google Account. Routing that account's email through a forwarding alias adds a variable that could complicate Apple or Google matching the request to your purchase history, and there's no upside to taking that risk.

Where an alias earns its keep is everything downstream: a developer's own refund-request form for disputes outside the standard platform window, a subscription-cancellation or refund-tracking service acting on your behalf, or the survey and win-back emails sent once the ticket closes. None of that needs your primary purchasing account email — keeping it separate is exactly the use case an alias is built for.

There's also no way to shut it off. If a win-back sequence keeps arriving on the tagged address, your only options are a filter rule you maintain forever or blocking senders one at a time. An alias that generates an address unrelated to your real one — and that you can delete permanently — gives you an actual exit.
