## What Is DNS-Level Ad Blocking and Why Does It Matter on Mobile?

When your phone loads a page or launches an app, it first asks a DNS server: "what's the IP address for this domain?" Every image, script, ad call, and tracking pixel triggers its own DNS lookup. DNS-level ad blocking intercepts lookups for known ad and tracker domains and returns nothing — the device never connects to the ad server, because it never gets an address to connect to.

The practical difference from browser-based blocking:

- **Browser extensions** only see traffic inside the browser. Instagram ads, YouTube app pre-rolls, Spotify banners — invisible to them.
- **DNS blocking** operates below the app layer. It covers every app on your phone without you configuring each one.
- **DNS blocking cannot do cosmetic filtering** — empty ad containers may remain visible as whitespace, since the HTML structure is already loaded.

On mobile in 2026, DNS blocking is the only realistic way to get system-wide coverage without rooting your device. The two main approaches are:

1. **Private DNS (free)** — configure a custom DNS server in your phone's network settings. Works across all apps. Limited to domain-level blocking — cannot catch ads served from shared domains.
2. **VPN-layer DNS blocking (paid)** — a VPN app handles DNS filtering as part of its traffic management. Broader coverage, easier to configure, blocks more ad formats.

For ad blocking specifically, **AdGuard DNS** or **NextDNS** are the right choices. Cloudflare's security DNS does not block ads — it only blocks malware domains.

### Setting Up Private DNS on Android

**Step 1 — Open Settings**
Go to Settings > Network & Internet (the exact path varies by Android version and manufacturer, but you're looking for the network or connections section).

**Step 2 — Find Private DNS**
Tap "Advanced" or "More connection settings" depending on your device. Look for "Private DNS."

**Step 3 — Enter the DNS Address**
Select "Private DNS provider hostname" and enter the address. For AdGuard DNS, type: `dns.adguard-dns.com`

For NextDNS, create a free account at nextdns.io and copy your unique hostname — it looks like `[your-id].dns.nextdns.io`

**Step 4 — Save and Verify**
Tap Save. Return to the Private DNS screen — it should show "Connected" next to your hostname. Visit a standard ad-heavy site to verify.

### Setting Up DNS Ad Blocking on iPhone

iOS's Private DNS equivalent is available through DNS profiles. The process is slightly different:

**Step 1 — Get the Configuration Profile**
Visit AdGuard DNS at `adguard-dns.com` in Safari and tap "Connect to AdGuard DNS." This downloads a configuration profile. Alternatively, NextDNS provides a similar download on their setup page.

**Step 2 — Install the Profile**
iOS will prompt you to install the profile. Go to Settings > General > VPN & Device Management, find the downloaded profile, and tap Install. Confirm when prompted.

**Step 3 — Enable the DNS Profile**
After installation, go to Settings > General > VPN & Device Management > DNS. Select the AdGuard or NextDNS profile you just installed and tap to activate it.

**Step 4 — Test**
Safari, Chrome, and apps should now route DNS queries through the filtering server. Load a news site and check whether display ads appear.

## Troubleshooting

### Ads are still showing after enabling CleanWeb

- Confirm the Surfshark VPN is actively connected (the status should show "Connected" on the main screen)
- Toggle CleanWeb off and back on in settings
- Disconnect and reconnect to a different server
- Check that you have CleanWeb 2.0 enabled if the option appears
- Force-quit the browser or app and reopen it — some apps cache ad content from before the VPN connected

### Private DNS shows "Connected" but ads still appear

- Some ad networks serve from the same domain as the main site — these cannot be blocked by DNS
- Confirm you entered the hostname exactly, with no trailing spaces or http:// prefix
- On Android, Private DNS only applies to connections over Wi-Fi or mobile data when not using another VPN. If you have a VPN active, the VPN handles DNS instead
- Try switching to NextDNS and checking your query log to confirm DNS queries are reaching the server

### CleanWeb conflicts with another VPN

Android allows only one VPN active at a time. If you use another VPN app, it will conflict with Surfshark's VPN slot. The fix is to use Surfshark as your VPN — it includes both the VPN and CleanWeb in one connection.

### [YouTube ad](https://snipstack.io/youtube-ad-blocker-2026/)s still appear occasionally

YouTube periodically adjusts its ad delivery infrastructure. CleanWeb's [blocklist update](https://snipstack.io/blocklist-update-schedule-for-ublock-origin-2026/)s regularly to keep pace, but brief windows may exist where new ad domains slip through before the list updates. This is a DNS-blocking limitation that affects all solutions — not specific to CleanWeb. Reconnecting to refresh the blocklist sometimes helps.

### Private DNS hostname not accepted on iPhone

iOS requires DNS configuration through a profile rather than a raw hostname entry. If you are entering a hostname directly in iOS settings, this will not work — follow the profile installation steps in Option B above.

## FAQ

### What is DNS-level ad blocking?

DNS-level ad blocking works by intercepting DNS lookups — the requests your phone makes to find the IP address for any domain. When a known ad domain is looked up, the DNS resolver returns nothing, so your device never connects to the ad server. This happens before any content loads, covering all apps and browsers simultaneously.

### Is DNS-level blocking better than a browser extension?

For mobile, yes — browser extensions only work inside the browser. DNS blocking works across every app on your phone. The trade-off is that DNS blocking cannot do cosmetic filtering (hiding empty ad containers) and cannot block ads served from first-party domains that are shared with site content. On desktop, a combination of DNS blocking and a browser extension gives the most complete coverage. [Get CleanWeb here.](/blog/adblock?sub=best-dns-level-ad-blocker-2026__faq)

### Does DNS blocking work for YouTube ads on mobile?

It works for most YouTube ads, most of the time. Surfshark CleanWeb is particularly reliable here because it operates outside the browser where YouTube's ad-detection scripts cannot identify it. Free DNS servers block some YouTube ad domains but coverage is inconsistent — YouTube serves some ads from domains shared with its player infrastructure, which DNS blocking cannot distinguish.

### Will DNS-level ad blocking slow down my phone?

DNS filtering adds less than 1 millisecond to each DNS lookup — imperceptible in practice. If you are using CleanWeb with the Surfshark VPN active, you may see 5-15ms of added network latency depending on server distance. For browsing, streaming, and app use this is not noticeable. DNS-only solutions (no VPN) have essentially zero impact on speed.

### Can I use DNS-level ad blocking on both Wi-Fi and mobile data?

Yes. Android's Private DNS setting applies to both Wi-Fi and mobile data connections. CleanWeb (via Surfshark) also covers both — the VPN connection routes traffic from either connection type. On iOS, the DNS profile applies globally across both connection types as well.

### Is DNS-level ad blocking legal?

Yes, in the vast majority of jurisdictions. Configuring how your device resolves DNS queries is a standard network setting — there is no legal prohibition on using filtering DNS servers or VPN-layer blocking. Some websites ask you to disable your blocker to support their content; you can choose to whitelist individual sites if you want to do so.
