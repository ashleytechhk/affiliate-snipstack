## Why Remote Freelancers Are High-Value Spam Targets

The problem is structural, not bad luck. Here is why freelance job board sign-ups reliably result in inbox overload.

**You register on many platforms.** An active remote freelancer maintains anywhere from five to fifteen job board accounts — often more when including niche boards for specific skills like writing, design, development, or consulting. Each registration is a data exposure point.

**Job board data gets sold or leaked.** Freelance platforms have experienced data incidents over the years. When breach data circulates, your real email address ends up in credential-stuffing lists that get reused for months or years after the original event.

**Recruiters scrape public profiles.** Headhunters and agency tools crawl public freelancer profiles and harvest contact details. Once your real address is in a recruiter CRM, you receive weekly blasts indefinitely — opt-out links or not.

**You cannot take back your email once shared.** Blocking a spam source is whack-a-mole. A new alias takes thirty seconds to create and can be discarded the moment it becomes a problem. That asymmetry — seconds to create, instant to kill — is the entire value proposition of [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-receiving-government-benefit-update-notices-2026/)ing.

## Gmail Plus-Addressing: Why It Consistently Falls Short

Adding a `+tag` suffix to your Gmail address (e.g., `yourname+remoteok@gmail.com`) is free and instant. It fails the core test: you cannot kill the address.

Any recipient who stores your tagged address can strip the `+tag` portion and reach your real inbox directly. The "alias" is not a separate identity — it is a routing hint that sophisticated spammers routinely bypass. You also cannot disable it; the only recourse is creating Gmail filters that move tagged mail to trash, which is manual, breakable, and does not stop the email from arriving in the first place.

For one-off signups where you expect no ongoing correspondence, it serves a purpose. For job board accounts that persist for months or years and store your address in a database that may eventually be breached or sold, it provides almost no protection.
