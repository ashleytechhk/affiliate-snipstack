## The Three Threats Your Current Setup Is Missing

### Unencrypted Connections Are Still the Default

Every time you connect to a hotel Wi-Fi, a coffee shop network, an airport lounge, or a coworking space, your traffic is visible to anyone on the same network running a packet sniffer. This is not theoretical. Tools to intercept unencrypted traffic are freely available and require no special skills to use.

Your antivirus does nothing to stop this. It scans files and flags malware on your device. It cannot encrypt your connection. The moment your data leaves your device unencrypted, it is out of reach.

A VPN solves this by creating an encrypted tunnel between your device and the internet. Anyone watching the network sees scrambled data. Your actual browsing, your login credentials, your banking session — none of it is legible without the encryption key.

### Malware Is Getting Faster Than Signature Updates

Traditional antivirus works by comparing files against a database of known threats. When a new variant of ransomware or a new trojan is discovered, the vendor adds it to the database and pushes an update. You are protected — from that specific variant, from that moment forward.

The gap between "threat emerges" and "your antivirus knows about it" used to be days. Now, AI-assisted malware tools allow criminal groups to generate new variants at a scale and speed that stretches that gap again. The signature model is still necessary. It is no longer sufficient on its own.

Real-time heuristic scanning — which watches program behavior rather than just matching signatures — closes that gap significantly. It catches malware that your antivirus has never seen before because it recognizes what the software is *doing*, not just what it looks like.

### Ads Are Not Just Annoying — They Are a Threat Vector

This one catches people by surprise. Banner ads and tracking scripts served by ad networks are delivered from third-party servers that the website you are visiting does not control. When those ad servers are compromised — which happens regularly — they can serve malicious code to anyone who loads the page. This is called malvertising, and it has affected legitimate sites including news outlets and government portals.

A network-level ad blocker stops those requests before they reach your device. It is not just about faster page loads and fewer distractions. It is about removing an entire class of attack vector from your threat surface.
