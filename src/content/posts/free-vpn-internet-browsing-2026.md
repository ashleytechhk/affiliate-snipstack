## What Does "Internet Browsing" Actually Mean?

The phrase "internet browsing" conjures up the image of someone opening a browser and visiting websites. But your devices are constantly using the internet for far more than that.

On a typical evening at home, a single household generates internet traffic from: a laptop streaming video in a browser, a smart TV running a native Netflix or Disney+ app, a gaming console downloading updates or playing online, a phone with background app syncs and social feeds, a tablet, smart speakers, a router-connected security camera, and possibly a torrent client or cloud backup process running silently in the background.

Every one of those activities is "internet browsing" in the technical sense — each generates outbound traffic, exposes your IP address to remote servers, and can be observed by your ISP. A VPN that only protects your browser tab leaves all of that other traffic completely visible.

This is the scope gap that separates network-level VPN protection from browser-extension VPN protection, and it is the central question to answer before choosing any free VPN solution.

## Browser-Level vs Network-Level: The Core Difference

### What a Browser Extension VPN Does

A Chrome or Firefox VPN extension creates an encrypted tunnel for traffic that flows through that browser. When you visit a website inside Chrome with the extension active, the site sees the VPN's IP address instead of yours, and the traffic between your browser and the VPN server is encrypted.

That is where the coverage stops. Any application on your device that is not that specific browser — your email client, your gaming platform, your streaming apps, your torrent client, your system update processes — sends traffic on your real IP address, unencrypted by the VPN.

Browser extensions are convenient and fast to install. For the narrow use case of private web browsing on a single browser, they work. For anything broader, they create a false sense of security: you think you are protected, but most of your device's traffic is not.

### What a Network-Level VPN Does

A system-installed VPN app operates at the operating system level. When you connect, it routes all traffic from the device through the encrypted tunnel — not just one browser, but every application. Your torrent client uses the VPN's IP. Your streaming apps use it. Your game client uses it. Background sync processes use it.

This is the coverage model that the term "VPN" originally described, and it is what most people mean — or assume — when they say they want a VPN for internet browsing.

## The Features Free Tiers Leave Out

Network-level coverage is only part of what a VPN needs to protect your full internet activity. There are three features that become important once you move beyond browser-only use — and all three are consistently absent or paywalled on free tiers.

### Kill Switch

A VPN kill switch blocks all internet traffic if the VPN connection drops unexpectedly. Without it, a momentary disconnection during a torrent download, a long gaming session, or a video stream exposes your real IP to whatever service you are connected to. For activities where IP-address privacy matters — P2P file sharing being the clearest example — a kill switch is not optional.

ProtonVPN Free does include a kill switch on desktop. Most other free VPN apps do not.

### DNS Leak Protection

Your DNS queries — the lookups that translate domain names into IP addresses — can bypass the VPN tunnel and resolve through your ISP's DNS servers instead, revealing which domains you are visiting even when everything else is encrypted. DNS leak protection routes these queries through the VPN as well.

Free VPN apps vary in how reliably they implement this. Several popular free options have documented DNS leak issues, which undermine the privacy benefit for all non-browser traffic.

### Split Tunneling

Split tunneling lets you choose which apps use the VPN and which connect directly. This is particularly useful for activities like gaming — where VPN latency can harm in-game ping — while you want other apps encrypted. It is a feature that requires meaningful engineering investment, and it is rare on free tiers.

## FAQ

### Does a free VPN protect my smart TV and streaming stick?

Only if you install a system-level VPN app directly on the device — and most smart TVs and streaming sticks do not support VPN apps. A browser extension VPN on your laptop does nothing for your TV. The practical solution for TV-level coverage is to configure the VPN at the router level (which most free VPN services do not support) or to use a paid VPN that offers a router configuration guide. Surfshark supports router installation on most subscription plans.

### Will a free [Chrome VPN](https://snipstack.io/chrome-vpn-add-ons-2026/) extension cover my torrent client?

No. A browser extension VPN only encrypts traffic inside that browser. Your torrent client opens its own connections directly through your ISP using your real IP address. If IP privacy for torrenting is important to you, you need a system-level VPN app — not a browser extension — and it needs to include a kill switch.

### What is the difference between a VPN and a proxy for internet browsing?

A proxy reroutes your traffic through a different server without encrypting it. Some browser "VPNs" — including built-in options in Opera and some Chrome extensions — are technically proxies. They can change your apparent location but do not provide the encryption that makes a VPN useful on public Wi-Fi or for general traffic privacy. A true VPN encrypts all traffic between your device and the VPN server.

### Can I use a free VPN for gaming?

You can, but with caveats. A system-level free VPN (like ProtonVPN Free) will route your game client's traffic through the VPN, which may increase ping depending on server distance. Free-tier servers are shared infrastructure, which typically means higher and less consistent latency. For gaming, split tunneling — which routes your game client directly while other apps use the VPN — is the practical answer, but it is a paid-tier feature on most providers.

### Does a free VPN cover all internet activity on my phone?

If you install a system-level VPN app (like ProtonVPN Free's mobile app) on your phone and keep it connected, yes — it will encrypt traffic from all apps on that phone, not just your mobile browser. The key is that the VPN must be installed as a system app, not as a browser extension. ProtonVPN Free's iOS and Android apps provide this coverage. Data caps on other free services (Windscribe, TunnelBear) limit how much mobile data can route through the VPN before you hit the monthly ceiling.

### Is free VPN internet browsing actually private?

A VPN shifts what is visible and to whom. Your ISP can no longer see your traffic destinations; the VPN provider can. A free VPN with a verified no-logs policy — ProtonVPN Free being the primary example — means the VPN provider does not store records of your activity either. Services without audited no-logs policies may retain usage data. "Private" in the VPN context means protected from ISP observation and IP-address exposure to the sites you visit — not that your activity is untraceable under all circumstances.
