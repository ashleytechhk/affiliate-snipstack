## How [Email Alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-that-blocks-promotional-emails-instantly-2026/)es Work

An alias is a forwarding address. You create a unique address (something like `deals-amazon-june@yourdomain.alias`) and give it to the retailer. Emails sent to that alias arrive in your real inbox, but the sender never learns your actual address.

When the alias starts receiving junk beyond the original purpose, you delete or disable it. The spam stops. Your real address is untouched. You create a new alias next time.

Different tools handle this differently — and the differences matter for price alert use specifically.

Increasingly, no. Many major retailers and their email service providers automatically strip the plus portion of your address during validation or delivery. Yes, with a quality alias service. **Is it worth paying for an alias service, or is the free tier enough?**

**Can retailers detect that I'm using an alias?**

Some retailers check whether an email address looks like a forwarding alias, particularly on high-value account registrations (financial services, etc.). For price alert signups specifically, this is rarely an issue. The check is far less common on e-commerce sites, and quality alias services use domains that pass standard validation. If a particular retailer does reject an alias, you can always use a different alias domain or check whether the site accepts the specific domain your service uses.
