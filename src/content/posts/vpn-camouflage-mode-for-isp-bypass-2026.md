## What Is VPN Camouflage Mode?

Camouflage Mode is a VPN feature that disguises encrypted VPN traffic so it is indistinguishable from regular HTTPS web traffic.

To understand why this matters, you need to understand what your ISP can actually see on your connection.

### The Two Layers of VPN Visibility

When you use a standard VPN, your ISP loses visibility into *what* you are doing. It cannot see which sites you visit or whether you are accessing Netflix, YouTube, or any specific service. That is the basic VPN promise and it holds.

What your ISP *can* still see is the traffic pattern itself. VPN protocols like OpenVPN and WireGuard have recognizable signatures — specific packet structures, port usage, and handshake patterns that distinguish them from ordinary web traffic. An ISP running deep packet inspection does not need to read inside the encrypted tunnel to identify it as a VPN connection. The shape of the data is enough.

Camouflage Mode works at this second layer. It wraps VPN traffic in an additional layer of obfuscation that removes or disguises the identifying characteristics. To your ISP's inspection tools, the connection looks like standard HTTPS traffic heading to a web server — the same kind of traffic generated by loading a webpage. There is no VPN signature to detect.

### The Distinction That Matters

This is a different problem from standard ISP throttling. The existing [ISP throttling article](/blog/vpn?sub=vpn-camouflage-mode-for-isp-bypass-2026__mid) covers the situation where your ISP identifies *traffic type* — Netflix versus speed test traffic — and throttles video streaming specifically. A standard VPN solves that because it hides the traffic type.

Camouflage Mode addresses the next scenario: an ISP that identifies *the VPN tunnel itself* and acts on that. No content inspection needed — just: this is a VPN connection, apply rules. For ISPs that throttle or block detected VPN traffic, a standard VPN does not help. You need obfuscation.

## How Camouflage Mode Works Technically

Surfshark's implementation of Camouflage Mode operates on top of the OpenVPN protocol. Here is what happens at the packet level:

1. **Your device initiates a VPN connection** using OpenVPN, which normally produces a recognizable handshake and packet structure.
2. **Camouflage Mode intercepts and transforms the traffic** before it leaves your device — removing standard OpenVPN header patterns and restructuring packets to resemble HTTPS traffic.
3. **The obfuscated stream leaves your network** looking identical to ordinary encrypted web browsing. No port 1194 (the standard OpenVPN port), no identifiable VPN handshake, no packet timing signatures associated with VPN protocols.
4. **Your ISP's DPI systems analyze the traffic** and classify it as regular HTTPS — which it effectively cannot distinguish from a standard TLS-encrypted connection to a web server.
5. **The traffic reaches the Surfshark server**, which decodes the obfuscation layer and processes the connection normally.

The overhead from this additional obfuscation layer is modest — typically a few percentage points of throughput — and the trade-off is worthwhile: a slightly slower but fully functional VPN connection beats a faster one that gets throttled or blocked.

### When Camouflage Mode Activates

In Surfshark's implementation, Camouflage Mode activates automatically when the OpenVPN protocol is selected. You do not configure it manually — choosing OpenVPN (UDP or TCP) enables the obfuscation. On networks where standard VPN traffic is not restricted, OpenVPN with Camouflage Mode functions normally. On networks where VPN traffic would be detected, the obfuscation layer prevents detection.

## FAQ

### What exactly is VPN Camouflage Mode?

Camouflage Mode is a traffic obfuscation feature that disguises VPN traffic so it looks like ordinary HTTPS web traffic. Without it, your ISP can identify your connection as a VPN even without seeing inside the encrypted tunnel — based on packet structure, port usage, and protocol signatures. Camouflage Mode removes those identifying characteristics. Your ISP sees what appears to be normal encrypted web browsing and cannot apply VPN-specific throttling or blocking rules.

### Why does my ISP care if I am using a VPN?

ISPs generally do not care about VPN use for privacy purposes. The more common issue is network policy: some ISPs throttle all VPN traffic as part of bandwidth management, or throttle it specifically because it bypasses their traffic shaping rules for streaming video. Others may have commercial arrangements with streaming platforms that create incentives to restrict VPN-based regional access. The result from your perspective is the same: VPN traffic gets singled out for worse performance.

### Is Camouflage Mode always active in Surfshark?

No — it activates automatically when you select the OpenVPN protocol (UDP or TCP) in the app's protocol settings. When using WireGuard or IKEv2, Camouflage Mode is not active. WireGuard is faster for most situations, so if your ISP does not target VPN traffic, WireGuard is the better default. Switch to OpenVPN when you need obfuscation — for example, if you notice VPN-specific slowdowns or connection instability that does not occur when you test on a different network.

### Does Camouflage Mode slow down the VPN connection?

There is some overhead — the obfuscation layer adds processing to each packet. In practice, the slowdown is small enough that it does not meaningfully affect streaming quality. The more relevant comparison is: OpenVPN with Camouflage Mode on a network that throttles VPN traffic versus WireGuard being throttled on the same network. The obfuscated connection that escapes detection will almost always deliver better real-world performance than the faster protocol being actively degraded.

### Can my ISP see that I am using Surfshark with Camouflage Mode active?

Your ISP can see that encrypted traffic is leaving your device — that is unavoidable. What it cannot see with Camouflage Mode active is that this traffic is a VPN connection. The traffic appears as standard TLS-encrypted HTTPS, which is indistinguishable from normal web browsing at the protocol level. The ISP does not know which VPN provider you are using, that you are using a VPN at all, or what traffic is inside the encrypted tunnel. **[Try Surfshark Camouflage Mode](/blog/vpn?sub=vpn-camouflage-mode-for-isp-bypass-2026__faq)** with a 30-day refund window if it does not solve your connection issues.

### Does Camouflage Mode work on all devices?

Surfshark's Camouflage Mode works on Windows, macOS, iOS, and Android — the platforms that support OpenVPN in the Surfshark app. It is not available on browser extensions or on platforms where OpenVPN is not implemented (some smart TV apps use IKEv2 only). For streaming arbitrage on a TV, the practical solution is to run Surfshark on your router instead, which covers all devices on your home network.

### Is it legal to use VPN obfuscation features?

VPN use — including obfuscation features — is legal in most countries. Obfuscation is a technical privacy measure, not an attempt to circumvent laws. It prevents your ISP from identifying your traffic type, which is analogous to using HTTPS to prevent a network observer from reading your web traffic. There are a small number of jurisdictions where VPNs are restricted; if you are in one of those locations, that is a separate legal question specific to your situation.
