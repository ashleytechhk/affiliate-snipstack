## What Is a VPN for Remote Work Security on Hotel WiFi?

A Virtual Private Network (VPN) is software that creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a server operated by the VPN provider. All your internet traffic — emails, Slack messages, video calls, file uploads, and browser sessions — passes through that tunnel before ever reaching the open internet.

For remote workers on hotel WiFi specifically, this changes everything about your exposure:

- **Encryption at the source**: Your data is scrambled using AES-256 encryption before it ever leaves your laptop. Even if someone on the same hotel network intercepts your packets, they see indecipherable noise.
- **IP address masking**: Your device's real IP is hidden. Websites and services see the VPN server's IP address instead, not yours.
- **Protocol-level protection**: Modern VPN protocols such as WireGuard and IKEv2/IPSec actively prevent man-in-the-middle attacks that silently alter data in transit.

Without a VPN, connecting to hotel WiFi is like working in a glass-walled office in the middle of a busy lobby. A VPN closes the blinds.

## Why Hotel WiFi Is a Genuine Threat to Remote Workers

Hotel networks are attractive targets for several practical reasons. They handle hundreds of transient guests sharing a single access point, they rarely rotate network credentials, and they almost never run enterprise-grade intrusion detection. Security researchers consistently rank hospitality WiFi among the riskiest network environments for professionals. Here are the three most common attack vectors you will encounter.

### The "Evil Twin" Hotspot Attack

An attacker sitting in the hotel restaurant sets up a WiFi network named something like "Grand_Hotel_Guest" — nearly identical to the real hotel network name. Your laptop or phone auto-connects, routing all your traffic through their device before it reaches the internet. You see no warning, no slowdown, and no indication anything is wrong. According to published threat intelligence, evil twin attacks have increased sharply alongside the growth of the digital nomad workforce since 2022.

### Packet Sniffing on Open Networks

Most hotel networks are unencrypted at the network layer, meaning any device on the same subnet can run freely available capture tools to read raw data packets from other guests. Login sessions for sites that do not strictly enforce HTTPS can be compromised in seconds. Even fully HTTPS-encrypted sites leak metadata — which domains you visit, at what times, and for how long — which can expose corporate activity to competitors or bad actors on the same floor.

### Session Hijacking via ARP Spoofing

More sophisticated attackers on the same subnet use ARP cache poisoning to redirect your traffic through their own machine at the routing level. This provides a real-time feed of everything your browser sends and receives — including authentication tokens that allow them to take over your active sessions without ever knowing your password. You remain logged in, your work continues, and you have no idea your session has been cloned.
