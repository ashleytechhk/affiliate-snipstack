Most people who search "[how to check](https://snipstack.io/how-to-check-if-a-website-is-safe-2026/) company registration online" are not doing routine corporate due diligence. They are trying to answer a specific, urgent question: *is this company real, or is someone trying to scam me?*

The trigger is almost always an email — or sometimes a letter or phone call — from an organisation they have never dealt with before. Common scenarios:

- A debt collection agency claiming you owe money to a company you do not recognise
- A prize notification from a "foundation" or "fund" asking for processing fees
- A delivery company requesting customs payment before releasing a parcel
- A company offering unexpected work-from-home employment, asking for bank details

In every case, the same instinct kicks in: if this is a real company, it should be registered. If I can find it on an official database, maybe it is legitimate.

That instinct is correct — but it is only half the picture. A sophisticated scammer can register a real company, or can impersonate a real company's name while operating from a completely different email domain. Registration status alone does not make an email safe.

## [How to Check](https://snipstack.io/how-to-check-if-your-pc-is-hacked-2026/) Company Registration Online: Step by Step

### Step 1: Identify the Full Company Name

Copy the exact company name from the email, including any "Ltd," "LLC," "Inc," or "Corp" suffix. Scammers sometimes use names that are very close to real companies — one letter different, or with an extra word inserted. Note any spelling that looks slightly unusual.

### Step 2: Find the Right Registry for the Country

Each country runs its own official company registry. Use the registry that corresponds to the country the company claims to be based in:

- **United Kingdom:** Companies House at find-and-update.company-information.service.gov.uk — free search, shows directors, accounts, registered address
- **United States:** Check the Secretary of State database for the relevant state. Most states have a free online search. The SEC's EDGAR database covers publicly traded companies.
- **Australia:** ASIC Connect at connectonline.asic.gov.au — covers all registered Australian companies
- **European Union:** The European Business Register at ebr.org provides access to national registries across member states
- **Hong Kong:** Companies Registry e-Search at icris.cr.gov.hk — free basic search

If the company claims to be from a country not listed above, search for "[country name] company registry" in your browser to find the official government database.

### Step 3: Search and Review the Results

Enter the company name and review what comes back. A legitimate registration entry will typically show:

- The exact registered name
- A registration or company number
- The date the company was incorporated
- The registered address
- The current status (active, dissolved, dormant)

**Warning signs to look for:**
- The company was incorporated very recently (within the last few weeks or months) — scammers do register real companies to add a veneer of legitimacy
- The company has already been dissolved or struck off
- The registered address is a virtual office or address that appears for hundreds of different companies
- No directors are listed, or the directors' names cannot be found anywhere online

### Step 4: Cross-Check the Email Domain

This is the step most people skip — and it is where many scams succeed. A company can be validly registered and still have nothing to do with the email you received. Scammers routinely impersonate real, legitimate businesses.

Take the email address the message came from. Does the domain — the part after the @ sign — actually match the company's official website? A company called "Greenfields Logistics Ltd" sending from `greenfields-logistics-parcel.net` is not Greenfields Logistics Ltd.

Look up the company's real website independently (do not use any website link provided in the suspicious email). Compare the domain of their official site against the sender's domain in the email you received. If they do not match, treat the email as fraudulent regardless of what the company registry shows.

## What the Registry Cannot Tell You

Government registries are excellent for confirming whether a legal entity exists. They are not designed to protect you from email-based fraud. Here is what they will not tell you:

**Whether the email was actually sent by that company.** Email is trivially easy to spoof. Anyone can put any company name in the "From" display field. The registry has no way to verify this.

**Whether the sender's domain is fraudulent.** A domain like `amazon-security-update.co` can look convincing and will not appear in any company registry. Only a tool that checks DNS records and fraud databases can flag it.

**Whether links in the email lead somewhere dangerous.** The registry confirms a company's registered address, not the safety of the URLs they put in their emails.

**Whether the company's identity has been hijacked.** Criminals sometimes impersonate genuinely registered, well-known companies — copying their logos, replicating their email templates, and sending from look-alike domains. The real company is registered and legitimate; the email is still a scam.

## FAQ

### Is there a single database I can use to check company registration worldwide?

No single global database covers every country, but a few multi-country resources help. The European Business Register (ebr.org) covers EU member states. The GLEIF database (gleif.org) covers companies that have been assigned a Legal Entity Identifier, which is common among financial institutions. For most other countries, you need to search the specific national registry. Searching "[country name] official company registry" will usually find the right government site within seconds.

### Can a company be registered but still be a scam?

Yes. Registration confirms a legal entity was formed — it does not indicate whether the company operates ethically or whether a particular email you received actually came from that company. Some fraud operations do register legitimate companies specifically to appear credible. Registration is one data point, not a clean bill of health. [Checking the email sender separately](/blog/scamcheck?sub=how-to-check-company-registration-online-2026__faq) adds the layer of protection that registry checks cannot provide.

### What if I cannot find the company on any registry?

An unregistered company making financial demands is a significant red flag. Legitimate businesses that operate in regulated industries — finance, debt collection, insurance — are required by law to be registered and often licensed. If you cannot find the company on the relevant national registry, do not engage. Report the contact to your national consumer protection or fraud reporting agency.

### What does "checking email authentication" mean, and why does it matter?

Email authentication refers to technical records (SPF, DKIM, DMARC) that a domain owner sets up to prove their emails are genuine. When these records are present and correctly configured, it is very difficult for someone else to impersonate that domain. When they are missing or misconfigured — which is common among scam domains — it is a strong signal that the sender is not who they claim to be. You do not need to understand the technical details; Surfshark's Email Scam Checker reads these records and translates the result for you.

### How do I report a company or email I believe is fraudulent?

The right reporting channel depends on your country. In the United States, report to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov and the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center at ic3.gov. In the United Kingdom, report to Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk. In Australia, report to Scamwatch at scamwatch.gov.au. Most countries have an equivalent national body — searching "[your country] report email scam" will find the correct authority. Always report both the company details and the email content.

### Can older adults protect themselves from these scams without being technical?

Absolutely. The process described in this article requires nothing more than a web browser and the ability to copy and paste. The Surfshark Email Scam Checker in particular is designed for non-technical users — it returns a plain-English verdict with no jargon. The most important protection is the habit itself: pause before clicking, check before responding, and never give out bank details or personal information based on an unsolicited email alone.
