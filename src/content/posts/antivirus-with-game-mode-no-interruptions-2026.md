Most people think of antivirus popups as a nuisance. For seniors who play games online, those unexpected interruptions are something more dangerous: they are the exact moment criminals choose to strike.

Here is how the scam works, step by step:

**You are in the middle of a game.** Your attention is on the puzzle or the card game. You are not thinking about cybersecurity.

**A window suddenly appears, filling your screen.** It shows the Windows logo, or the logo of a known security company. It plays an alarm sound. The text says something like: "CRITICAL ALERT — Your computer has been infected. Your banking details may be compromised. Call this number immediately."

**You are startled.** You were not expecting anything. The alarm sound and bold red text create a rush of anxiety.

**You call the number.** A calm, professional voice answers. They say they are from Microsoft, or from your antivirus company. They ask to connect to your computer remotely to "fix the problem." Once connected, they either steal your files directly or charge you hundreds — sometimes thousands — of dollars to remove a virus that never existed.

## The Difference Between "Silent Mode" and Real Game Mode Protection

Not all antivirus programs handle interruptions the same way. There is an important distinction that seniors — and the family members who help them with technology — should understand.

### Basic "Silent Mode"

Some antivirus programs have a setting where you can manually turn off notifications. This stops the antivirus from showing you its own alerts, but it does nothing about malicious popups from outside the program. A fake Windows security warning coming from a scam website can still appear on your screen even when your antivirus is in "silent mode."

This kind of silent mode also requires you to remember to turn it on before you play and turn it off afterward. Many users forget, or do not know the setting exists.

### Update Prompts That Look Legitimate — But Are Not

A window appears asking you to update your browser, your Flash player, or your media software. It looks genuine. You click "Update Now." The file it installs is not an update. It is malware.

Antivirus with real-time file scanning catches this at the moment of download — the fake "update" file is flagged and blocked before it can execute.

### Browser Warnings That Lead You Astray

You are browsing normally when your browser shows a red warning page. Some of these warnings are real — your browser is genuinely telling you a site is dangerous. Others are fake pages designed to look like browser warnings, complete with official logos and urgent language.

A proper antivirus includes its own web shield that confirms whether a site is genuinely dangerous, so you can tell the difference between a real browser warning and a fake one.
