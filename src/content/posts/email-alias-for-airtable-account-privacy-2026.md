## Why Airtable Accounts Attract Spam

Airtable's privacy exposure comes from several directions that aren't obvious at signup:

**Shared base collaborator invites.** When you share a base or add collaborators, your email address is visible to every participant in that workspace. If any collaborator's account is later compromised, or if they share that contact data with a third party, your address is in the mix.

**Airtable Forms.** If you use Airtable Forms to collect submissions — whether for internal workflows or public-facing intake — your Airtable account email may appear in confirmation and notification emails that form respondents receive. Depending on how the form is configured, that address can be extracted from email headers.

**Automation notifications.** Airtable's native automation tools send notification emails on behalf of your account. These emails include your Airtable-registered address as the sender or reply-to address. Anyone who receives one of those automations now has your account email.

**Marketplace integrations.** The Airtable app marketplace contains dozens of third-party extensions. Many require creating a separate account with the integration provider, which typically means handing over your email again. Each of these providers has its own data-handling practices and breach risk profile.

**Zapier, Make, and API connectors.** Connecting Airtable to other tools almost always involves OAuth or direct credential exchange. These platforms separately store your email address and often use it for their own marketing sequences.

**Airtable's own marketing.** Airtable sends product announcements, feature updates, and promotional emails. Even with preferences adjusted, the volume adds up — and the address is firmly on their list once you've registered.

The cumulative result: a single Airtable account touches more email-collecting surfaces than most users realize. Your real address is the thread connecting all of them.

## Why the Gmail `+tag` Trick Falls Short for Airtable Users

Gmail plus-addressing — signing up as `yourname+airtable@gmail.com` — is a common first step for people trying to organize their inbox. For real email address privacy with Airtable, it doesn't hold up.

The base address `yourname@gmail.com` is fully visible to anyone who looks at the email header. Spam operations routinely strip the `+tag` before ingesting addresses into their lists. A data breach at an Airtable integration partner that exposes `yourname+airtable@gmail.com` immediately reveals your core Gmail address.

More critically: you can't disable it. If spam starts arriving on `yourname+airtable@gmail.com`, your only options are an inbox filter rule (permanent maintenance overhead) or changing your Gmail address entirely. There's no revocation.

An alias that generates a completely unrelated-looking address — and that you can delete in one click — gives you both protection and control that plus-addressing cannot.
