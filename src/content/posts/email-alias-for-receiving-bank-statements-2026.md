### Gmail's `+tag` Trick

Gmail's `+tag` system (`yourname+bank@gmail.com`) is commonly suggested as a quick alias workaround. For banking, it fails in two practical ways.

First, many bank registration forms and contact-update forms strip the `+tag` suffix before storing the address. You submit `yourname+bank@gmail.com`, the bank stores `yourname@gmail.com`, and your real address enters their records and, potentially, any breach of those records.

Second, even when the tag survives, it provides no meaningful protection. You cannot revoke it. You cannot delete it. If the address is compromised — breached or sold — you have no recourse short of abandoning your Gmail account. A tag is a label, not a layer of separation.

### Apple Hide My Email

Apple's service generates forwarding aliases tied to your iCloud account. It works cleanly within the Apple ecosystem and integrates with Safari's autofill for quick alias insertion at sign-up forms.

For banking, it performs adequately: aliases forward reliably, can be deleted, and Apple's domain passes most bank validation checks. The limitation is lock-in. If your primary inbox is Gmail or Outlook, forwarding from Apple's alias to a non-iCloud address works but adds a dependency — if iCloud has a service disruption, your 2FA codes and statement alerts are affected. There's also no alternative identity generation: your real name still enters the bank's records.

For existing Apple ecosystem users already paying for iCloud+, it's a workable option. For everyone else, the friction is genuine enough to matter when the use case involves time-sensitive authentication codes.

3. **No account persistence.** These services exist to receive a single confirmation and expire. Banking requires a stable address for months or years.

Throwaway services are not an option for financial accounts. They aren't in the running for this use case.

## How to Switch Your Bank's Contact Email to an Alias

1. **Create a new alias** in Alt-ID (or your chosen tool). Label it clearly — something like "Chase checking" or "Main bank."

4. **Click the verification link** in the forwarded email to confirm the address change.
5. **Verify** by triggering a test notification (a password reset, a small transaction alert) and confirm it arrives via forwarding.

From that point, your bank communicates with the alias. Your real address is no longer in their active contact records.

**Can I use one alias for all my bank accounts, or should I use separate ones?**

Separate aliases per institution are the better approach. One alias across all accounts means a single breach or compromise affects all your financial email at once. One alias per bank means a compromised alias at one institution is isolated — you delete and replace that alias without touching the others.

**What if I delete an alias my bank is actively using?**

Deleting an alias immediately stops forwarding. Emails sent to the deleted alias bounce. Before deleting, update your bank's contact email to a replacement alias. Do the migration first, confirm the new alias is working, then delete the old one.
