## Method 1 — Call Your Bank's Official Number

The gold standard for verification is always the same: hang up or ignore the text, then call your bank directly using the phone number printed on the back of your debit or credit card — not any number mentioned in the text itself.

**What it does well:** A real bank representative can tell you immediately whether any alert, request, or action on your account is genuine. There is no way for a scammer to intercept this call if you dial the number from your card. This method will never give you a wrong answer.

**Where it falls short:** It takes time — often 5 to 15 minutes including hold time. For someone who receives many suspicious texts, calling the bank every single time is not practical. It also does nothing to protect you against the link in the text itself: if you tapped the link before deciding to call, the damage may already be done.

**Best for:** Any situation where money, account access, or personal information is involved and you want absolute certainty before acting.

**Tip:** Save your bank's genuine customer service number in your phone contacts right now, before you receive a suspicious text. When one arrives, you will not be tempted to use the number the text provides.

## Method 2 — Google the Sender or Message Text

If you receive a suspicious bank text, copying a few words from the message — or the sender's phone number — and searching Google for them often reveals whether other people have reported the same scam.

**What it does well:** Scam operations typically send the same message to thousands of people. Reports accumulate quickly on sites like Reddit (r/Scams), community fraud-warning boards, and consumer protection pages. If a text with the phrase "your Barclays account has been suspended" is doing the rounds, a Google search will almost certainly surface those reports within days of the campaign launching.

**Where it falls short:** Brand-new campaigns — texts that started circulating hours or days ago — may not have any search results yet. Scammers regularly rotate their wording, phone numbers, and sender IDs to stay ahead of community reporting. A clean Google result does not mean the text is safe; it may simply mean it is new.

**Best for:** A quick second opinion when the text message feels familiar, or when you want to see if a specific phone number has been flagged by other users.

## Method 3 — Check the Link Manually Before Tapping

On an iPhone, you can press and hold a link to preview its destination without opening it. On Android, a similar long-press menu shows the full URL. This reveals whether the link actually leads to your bank's real website or to an unfamiliar domain.

**What it does well:** It is instant. If a text claims to be from HSBC but the link preview shows `hsbc-account-verify.xyz` or some other domain that has nothing to do with the real bank, the scam is immediately obvious without any tool needed.

**Where it falls short:** This method requires you to know what your bank's real web address looks like — and scammers have become skilled at creating domains that look almost right. `hsbc-secure.co.uk` could be mistaken for a real HSBC address by someone unfamiliar with the genuine one. For long or heavily encoded links, the preview is not human-readable at all. And on older phones, the link preview feature may not work reliably.

**Best for:** Tech-comfortable users who know their bank's genuine web address well and want a quick pre-check before using another tool.

## Method 4 — Carrier Spam Filter

Mobile carriers (AT&T, Verizon, EE, Vodafone, and others) operate background spam filters that automatically flag or block messages identified as likely scam texts. Some carriers have dedicated apps — AT&T ActiveArmor, T-Mobile Scam Shield — that add an extra layer.

**What it does well:** It requires nothing from you. If a known scam campaign reaches your carrier's filter, the text may be blocked before it arrives, or labelled "Spam Risk" in your messages app. For high-volume, widely reported scam campaigns, carrier filters work well.

**Where it falls short:** Carrier filters work from known-bad databases, which means they miss anything new. Sophisticated smishing campaigns specifically design their messages to bypass carrier detection — using short links, new sender numbers, and variations in wording. A text that passes a carrier filter is not necessarily safe. The filter's "pass" is not a verification; it is simply the absence of a known flag.

**Best for:** Passive background protection — not a primary verification tool when a text has already arrived and you need to decide what to do with it.

## Red Flags in Fake Bank Texts: What to Look For

Even before running any check, these patterns appear in the vast majority of bank impersonation texts:

**Urgent or threatening language.** "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours." "Immediate action required." Real banks do not operate this way — they send letters for account issues and give you time to respond.

**A link that does not match the bank's known website.** Your bank's real website is printed on the back of your card, on your bank statements, and on any official correspondence you have received from them. If the link domain does not match exactly, treat it as suspicious.

**A request for your PIN, password, or full account number.** Banks will never ask for these via text. Ever.

**A sender number that looks like a random mobile number.** Official bank texts in most countries come through registered short codes or verified business sender IDs — not ordinary mobile numbers.

**Grammar and spelling errors.** Not universal — some scam texts are now written very cleanly — but errors in an otherwise formal message are a reliable warning sign.

**Personalisation that feels slightly off.** A text that addresses you by first name only, or uses your surname without your first name, may be using scraped data rather than a real bank's account records.

## What to Do Immediately If You Tapped the Link

If you have already tapped a link in a suspicious bank text before reading this:

1. Close the browser tab or app immediately. Do not enter any information on the page that opened.
2. If you entered your bank password, card number, or any personal details on the page, contact your bank right away using the number on the back of your card.
3. Change your online banking password from a different, trusted device.
4. Report the text to your carrier by forwarding it to 7726 (SPAM) — this number works in the US and UK for all major carriers.
5. Report it to your national consumer protection agency: the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov (US), Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk (UK), or Scamwatch at scamwatch.gov.au (Australia).

Acting quickly matters. If payment details were entered, most banks have a short window during which transactions can be stopped.

### Do banks send text messages with links?

Yes, some banks do send legitimate texts with links — particularly for two-factor authentication codes or fraud alerts asking you to confirm a transaction. The difference is that legitimate bank texts generally do not ask you to log in through the link or provide your full card details. When in doubt, go to your bank's website directly by typing the address yourself rather than tapping the link in the text.

### What is smishing?

Smishing is SMS phishing — a scam where criminals send text messages impersonating trusted organisations (banks, delivery companies, government agencies) to trick you into clicking a link or calling a fake number. The term combines "SMS" and "phishing." It has grown significantly in recent years because many people are more cautious about suspicious emails than about suspicious texts.

### Can scammers fake my bank's name in the sender field?

Yes. SMS sender ID spoofing allows criminals to display any name or short code they choose in the "From" field of a text message. This means a text can appear to come from "HSBC" or "Chase" even if it was sent by a fraudulent operation with no connection to those banks. The sender name alone is not proof that a text is genuine.

### Is it safe to copy a suspicious link and paste it into a checker?

Yes. When you copy a link and paste it into a tool like Surfshark's Scam Checker, you are submitting the text of the web address — your device does not visit the suspicious page during this process. The risk comes only from tapping the link and having your browser load the page it points to.

### What if the link in the bank text has already expired?

Scammers sometimes use short-lived links that stop working quickly to avoid detection. An expired link is a yellow flag — it does not prove the text was a scam, but it is consistent with scam behaviour. If you cannot check the link and the text is asking you to act on something related to your bank account, call your bank directly to find out whether any action is actually needed.

### Why do scammers target bank texts specifically?

Because the financial motivation is direct and the emotional pressure is high. A text that claims your account is at risk creates immediate anxiety — exactly the state scammers want, because it makes people act before they think. Banking texts also benefit from an existing habit of trust: most people do receive genuine texts from their banks, so the format itself carries credibility.
