## How Package Delivery Scam Texts Work

Before comparing tools, it helps to understand what you are actually up against.

Scammers send messages pretending to be from USPS, FedEx, UPS, DHL, or a local courier. The message says something like "delivery failed," "customs fee required," or "confirm your address." There is a link. That link leads to a fake website — a near-perfect copy of the real carrier's site — where you are asked to enter your name, address, and often a small payment.

The payment is not the main prize. Your card number is.

In 2026, these scam texts are generated by automated tools and sent to millions of people at once. They are personalised enough to feel real. The scam text itself — the message and the link it contains — is what a good package delivery scam text checker inspects.

### 2. Google Safe Browsing and VirusTotal

Google Safe Browsing and VirusTotal are well-known security tools that let you submit a URL and receive a report on whether it has been flagged as malicious.

**Strengths:** Both tools draw on large, frequently updated databases. VirusTotal aggregates results from over 70 antivirus engines.

**Limitations for seniors and everyday users:**

- Results are presented as raw technical data — security engine names, detection tags, scan counts. You have to interpret what the results mean yourself.
- Neither tool checks the email or SMS sender identity — only the link destination.
- On a phone, copying a URL from a text message and navigating between apps to paste it into VirusTotal involves several steps that can be fiddly.

**Bottom line:** Useful for tech-savvy users who can interpret raw scan output. Not the right starting point if you just want a clear yes or no.

### 3. Manual Hover and Domain Inspection

On a desktop computer, hovering your mouse over a link without clicking reveals the real web address in your browser's status bar. You then manually check whether that address matches the courier's real website.

**Strengths:** Costs nothing, requires no tools, works instantly.

**Limitations:**

- Does not work on a phone — the primary device most people use for texts.
- Requires you to know what a legitimate FedEx or USPS URL looks like (it is not always obvious).
- Does not check whether a domain has been flagged for fraud.
- Scammers have become adept at registering domains that look legitimate at a glance: `usps-parcel-track.com` looks plausible but is not USPS.

**Bottom line:** A useful first glance on desktop. Insufficient on its own, especially on mobile.

### 4. Googling the Sender or Tracking Number

Pasting the [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/), email address, or tracking number from a suspicious delivery message into a Google search often surfaces reports from people who received the same scam.

**Strengths:** Free, fast, and often effective for widely distributed scam campaigns.

**Limitations:**

- Works best for scams already widely reported. Brand-new campaigns will not show up yet.
- Does not check whether the link is live and malicious right now.
- Relies on other people having already been targeted and reported it.

**Bottom line:** A good supplementary step, not a standalone checker.

### 5. Carrier Spam Reporting (7726)

In the United States and United Kingdom, you can forward suspicious texts to 7726 (SPAM). This submits the message to your mobile carrier for review.

**Strengths:** One of the simplest actions available — forward and forget.

**Limitations:**

- You receive no result. You will not know whether the message was a scam.
- Does not help you decide whether to interact with the current message.
- Primarily helps carriers block future messages — it is a public-service action, not a personal checker.

**Bottom line:** Worth doing after you have already decided the message is suspicious. Not a checking tool.

## What to Do If You Already Clicked the Link

If you tapped the link before checking it, take these steps immediately:

1. Do not enter any information on the page that opened. Close the tab or app right away.
2. If you entered a password, change it immediately from a different, trusted device or Wi-Fi network.
3. If you entered any card or bank details, call your bank using the number on the back of your card — not any number on the suspicious page — and report it.
4. Run a security scan on your phone using your device's built-in security tools.
5. Report the message to your carrier by forwarding it to 7726.

Acting quickly limits the damage. Do not wait.

### What is smishing?

Smishing is the term for phishing scams delivered by SMS text message rather than email. "SMS" plus "phishing" equals "smishing." Package delivery scam texts are the most common form of smishing in 2026. The tactics are identical to email phishing — fake sender, urgent message, dangerous link — but the delivery method is a text to your phone.
