## What Is a VPN, and Why Does It Not Protect You From Viruses?

Let's start with the basics, because this is where many people get confused.

A **VPN** (Virtual Private Network) is like a secure tunnel between your home computer and your employer's office network. When you connect through a VPN, your internet traffic is encrypted — meaning outsiders cannot easily spy on what you are doing. Many companies require remote workers to use a VPN so that sensitive files and communications stay protected in transit.

Here is what a VPN does **not** do:

- It does not scan your computer for viruses or malware
- It does not block malicious files that you accidentally download
- It does not stop a hacker who is already inside your machine from doing damage
- It does not alert you if your passwords have been stolen

Think of a VPN like a locked car door. It keeps strangers from getting in while you are driving. But if you accidentally pick up a hitchhiker (a virus on a downloaded file), the locked door does nothing — the threat is already inside with you.

**Antivirus software** is the part that actively scans your device, finds threats, and removes them before they cause damage. Together, a VPN and [antivirus for](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-for-drone-hobbyist-laptop-with-firmware-files-2026/)m a proper security setup. Separately, each one leaves you partially exposed.

Cybercriminals are not random. They are strategic, and they know exactly who is most likely to click a bad link or fall for a fake message.

Criminals know that compromising one remote worker's device can open the door to an entire company.

**3. Delayed response to warning signs.** When a computer starts behaving strangely — running slowly, showing odd pop-ups, accessing the internet at unusual hours — many people assume it is normal aging of the machine. ## The Real Risk: Your Home Device Can Infect the Whole Office

Here is something that surprises many remote workers: when you connect your home computer to your employer's network through a VPN, your device becomes part of that network. Whatever is on your device has potential access to what is on the network.

This means:

- A virus that got onto your laptop through a personal email can move through the VPN tunnel to company servers
- Ransomware (software that locks your files and demands payment) can spread to your employer's files, not just yours
- A keylogger (software that secretly records everything you type) can capture your work login credentials and send them to criminals abroad

Employers are aware of this risk, which is why many company IT policies require remote workers to have approved antivirus software installed. If you are not sure whether your employer has a requirement, it is worth checking — and worth protecting yourself regardless.

The good news is that modern antivirus software is far simpler to use than it was even five years ago. You install it once, and it works quietly in the background, checking files and websites automatically without requiring you to do anything.
