## Why Scholarship Scams Are So Dangerous

Scholarship scams are not new, but they have become far more convincing. Scammers now use official-looking logos, real university names, and professional language to create emails that even cautious readers can miss.

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) consistently ranks scholarship and grant fraud among the top scams reported by adults over 60. Why seniors specifically? Because many are helping grandchildren with college costs and are emotionally invested in finding funding. Scammers exploit that hope.

Common tactics include:

- **"You've already been selected"** — real scholarships require applications; you cannot win one you never entered
- **"Pay a small processing fee"** — legitimate scholarships never charge applicants to receive money
- **Urgency pressure** — "respond within 48 hours or forfeit your award"
- **Requests for sensitive data** — Social Security Numbers, bank account details, or copies of ID documents before any award is confirmed

The damage goes beyond money. Once scammers have your personal information, it can be used for identity theft that takes years to untangle.

## The 5 Warning [Signs of a](https://snipstack.io/signs-of-a-fake-website-2026/) Fake Scholarship Email

Before we compare checking methods, know what you are looking at. These five red flags appear in nearly every fake scholarship offer:

### 1. You Never Applied

Legitimate scholarships require an application, essay, or nomination process. If an email says you won something you never entered, it is almost certainly fake.

### 2. They Ask for Money Upfront

No real scholarship foundation charges an "application processing fee," "tax clearance payment," or "administrative deposit." Any request for money before you receive your award is a scam signal.

### 3. The Email Domain Does Not Match the Organization

A real scholarship from Harvard would come from a `harvard.edu` address — not `harvard-scholarship-awards.com` or a Gmail account. Always check the sender's full email address, not just the display name.

### 4. Vague or Unverifiable Organization Details

Real foundations have websites, registered addresses, phone numbers, and 990 tax filings searchable in the IRS database. If you cannot find the organization anywhere, it likely does not exist.

### 5. Grammar Errors and Unusual Formatting

While scammers have improved dramatically, many scholarship scam emails still contain awkward phrasing, inconsistent capitalization, or mismatched fonts — signs of bulk-generated fraud content.

### Option 1: Manual Google Search

Type the scholarship name plus the word "scam" into Google. For example: `"National Education Excellence Foundation" scam`. Real organizations will have a long history of results, news coverage, and official websites. Scam organizations often appear only in fraud warning databases or have no results at all.

This method is free but slow. It works well for catching known scams that others have already reported, but misses new schemes that have not been flagged yet.

### Option 2: IRS Charity Lookup

In the United States, legitimate scholarship-granting foundations must be registered as 501(c)(3) nonprofit organizations. You can verify this at the IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search tool (search "IRS tax exempt organization search" in Google). If the organization is not registered, it is not a legitimate charity.

This check is reliable for US-based scholarships but does not help with international offers or phishing links embedded in the email.

### Option 3: Email Header Analysis

Every email contains hidden metadata called "headers" that show where the email actually originated. You can view these in most email clients. A mismatch between the claimed sender and the actual originating server is a strong fraud indicator.

This is the most technically demanding approach and is not practical for most people — especially those who are not comfortable navigating email settings.

## What Happens If You Have Already Responded?

If you have already clicked a link, provided personal information, or — in the worst case — sent money in response to what you now believe may be a scam, take action immediately:

- **Change passwords** on any accounts you accessed after clicking the link
- **Contact your bank or credit card company** if you sent money or shared financial details
- **Place a fraud alert** with the major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion)
- **Report the scam** to the FTC (reportfraud.ftc.gov) and your state attorney general's office
- **Scan your device** for malware using reputable security software

The sooner you act, the better the outcome.

## FAQ

### How do I know if a scholarship email is real or fake?

Real scholarships require an application — you cannot win one you never entered. Check that the sender's email domain matches the official organization website. Verify the organization exists in the IRS charity database (for US foundations). Never pay any fee to receive scholarship money. [Surfshark Scam Check](/blog/scamcheck?sub=fake-scholarship-offer-scam-how-to-check-2026__faq) can analyze suspicious links in the email instantly.

### What should I never do when I receive a suspicious scholarship email?

Do not click any links, do not reply, and do not provide personal information (Social Security Number, bank account, or copies of ID). Do not pay any "processing fee" or "administrative charge" — legitimate scholarships do not charge applicants.

### Can scam scholarship emails look completely professional?

Yes. Modern scammers use official logos, real university names, and polished formatting. Visual quality alone is not a reliable indicator. Always verify the sender domain, the organization's registration status, and any links in the email before taking action.

### Is Surfshark Scam Check easy enough for someone who is not tech-savvy?

Yes. The process is: copy the suspicious link, paste it into Scam Check, read the result. There is no technical knowledge required and nothing to install. It is designed for everyday users, not security professionals.

### What do I do if I already sent money to a fake scholarship?

Contact your bank immediately to report fraud and request a chargeback if you paid by credit or debit card. File a report with the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov and contact your local law enforcement. Place a fraud alert with the three major credit bureaus to protect against identity theft.

### Are all scholarship fee requests scams?

In the context of scholarships — yes. Legitimate scholarship programs are funded specifically to give money away. They do not charge applicants any fee to receive, process, or claim an award. Any scholarship that asks for money upfront should be treated as a scam.
