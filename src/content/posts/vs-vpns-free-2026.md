## What Free VPNs Get Right (And Where They Fall Apart)

### The genuine upside of free tiers

Some free VPNs — Proton VPN's free tier and Windscribe's 10 GB/month plan are the most credible examples — do offer genuinely useful service with no strings attached. Proton's free tier has no data cap, though it limits you to three server locations and one device. Windscribe gives you more location flexibility within a monthly cap.

For a user who just wants to occasionally mask their IP while checking email on public Wi-Fi, these options are legitimately fine.

### Where every free VPN fails the arbitrageur

The subscription arbitrageur's workflow is specific: find a streaming platform offering the same content at a lower price in another region, switch your apparent location, and purchase or maintain that subscription at the cheaper rate. That workflow has hard requirements:

1. **Stable, consistent speeds** — buffering kills the experience and triggers platform suspicion
2. **Streaming-friendly servers** — Netflix, Disney+, and others actively block VPN IP ranges; free VPNs rotate servers infrequently and get blocked fast
3. **Enough data** — a single HD movie is 4–7 GB; a 10 GB monthly cap is gone in two evenings
4. **Multiple regions** — the best arbitrage opportunities shift; you need options

Free VPNs fail on at least two of these four requirements. Usually all four.

### The hidden cost of "free"

Business models matter. A free VPN that doesn't charge you is generating revenue somewhere. The most common sources: selling aggregated (and sometimes granular) browsing data to data brokers, injecting affiliate tracking into your traffic, or running bandwidth-sharing schemes that use your connection to route other users' traffic. The 2020 revelation that several popular free VPN apps were owned by companies with documented data-harvesting practices was not an anomaly — it was the business model made visible.

Proton and Windscribe are genuine exceptions because they have paid tiers that fund the free service. But even they limit free users from the streaming use case by design — paid subscribers are their product's target user.

## FAQ

### Are free VPNs actually free, or is there a hidden cost?

Technically free at the point of use, but the business model has to work somehow. Reputable free tiers like Proton VPN subsidize free users with revenue from paid subscribers. Less reputable options monetize your browsing data, inject tracking into your traffic, or both. The question to ask any free VPN is: what is the revenue model? If the answer isn't clearly "paid subscribers fund the free tier," proceed with caution.

### Can free VPNs unblock Netflix or other streaming platforms?

Occasionally and inconsistently. Streaming platforms employ active VPN detection that flags and blocks known VPN IP ranges. Free VPNs don't rotate their IP pools frequently, so they get blocked and stay blocked. Paid providers dedicate resources to maintaining undetected server pools. If streaming access is your goal, a free VPN is not a reliable solution.

### Is it legal to use a VPN for streaming arbitrage?

VPN use itself is legal in most countries. Whether purchasing a subscription through a different regional pricing tier violates a streaming platform's terms of service is a separate question — most platforms' ToS do restrict it. The practical consequence is account suspension in rare cases, not legal liability. Research the specific platform's policies and make your own informed decision. This article does not constitute legal advice.

### What should I look for in a paid VPN for streaming arbitrage?

Four things matter: (1) server coverage in the specific regions where your target platforms price cheaply, (2) a verified no-logs policy — ideally independently audited, (3) streaming-optimized servers that maintain undetected IP pools, and (4) fast protocol support (WireGuard is the current standard). ### Will using a VPN slow down my internet connection?

Any VPN adds some overhead — your traffic is being routed through an additional server and encrypted. The amount of slowdown depends on the VPN protocol and server load. Free VPNs tend to run overcrowded servers with older protocols, leading to 40–70% speed reduction in typical tests. Surfshark on WireGuard protocol typically shows under 15% reduction on nearby servers — imperceptible for streaming purposes.
