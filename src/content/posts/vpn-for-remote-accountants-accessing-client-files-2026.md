## What is a VPN, Exactly?

VPN stands for Virtual Private Network. Strip away the jargon and here is what it does: instead of your laptop sending data directly to whatever server you are connecting to, all your traffic first goes to a VPN server. That server forwards the request on your behalf, and any response comes back through the same route.

Two things happen as a result:

1. **Your data is encrypted.** The tunnel between your device and the VPN server uses strong encryption, so anyone monitoring the network between you and that server — a public Wi-Fi operator, an internet service provider, or anyone running a packet sniffer nearby — sees scrambled data they cannot read.

2. **Your IP address changes.** Websites and services see the VPN server's IP address, not yours. This is why VPNs are also used to access region-locked content, which we will come back to later.

For an accountant handling payroll, tax filings, or audit documents, point one is the critical one.

## Why Remote Accountants Face Unique Risks

Most office workers share a building network managed by an IT department. That network has firewalls, monitoring, and policies. When you go remote, that safety layer disappears.

Here are specific scenarios remote accountants encounter that make an unprotected connection risky:

### Working from Public or Semi-Public Networks

Hotel Wi-Fi, co-working spaces, airport lounges, and even home broadband from an internet service provider all carry some level of risk. Public Wi-Fi in particular is notorious for what are called man-in-the-middle attacks, where a malicious actor positions themselves between your device and the router to intercept traffic. Financial data is an attractive target because it has direct monetary value.

### Accessing Cloud Accounting Platforms

Platforms like QuickBooks Online, Xero, FreshBooks, and similar tools are designed to be accessed from anywhere — but that design assumes the connection itself is trustworthy. A VPN adds an encryption layer on top of whatever the platform itself provides.

### Transmitting Documents by Email or File Transfer

Even when files are sent via encrypted email or a secure portal, the metadata around those transmissions — who you are, when you sent it, what service you used — can leak on an unencrypted connection. A VPN wraps that activity too.

### Regulatory Context

Depending on your jurisdiction and client base, you may be operating under frameworks that require reasonable safeguards for client data. While a VPN is not a substitute for proper data governance, it is one of the practical, low-cost technical controls that demonstrates due diligence. Always check the specific rules that apply to your practice.

## How a VPN Works: Step by Step

When you switch on a VPN on your laptop, here is [what happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) in sequence:

1. Your [VPN app on](https://snipstack.io/vpn-app-on-android-2026/) your device establishes a connection to a VPN server using an agreed-upon protocol (WireGuard and OpenVPN are the two most common in 2026).
2. The app and server perform a handshake and agree on encryption keys.
3. From that point, every packet of data leaving your device is encrypted before it even hits your router.
4. The VPN server decrypts the packet, sends the request to the destination (say, your cloud accounting platform), receives the response, encrypts it, and sends it back to you.
5. Your VPN app decrypts it locally and shows you the result.

The whole process adds a small amount of latency — typically 10 to 30 milliseconds on a good server nearby. For accounting work, which involves uploading documents and viewing dashboards rather than streaming 4K video or gaming, this latency is essentially unnoticeable.

## What to Look for in a VPN for Professional Use

Not all VPNs are built equally. Here is what matters specifically for remote accounting work:

### No-Logs Policy

A reputable VPN provider should not store records of what websites you visited or what files you accessed. Look for providers that have had their no-logs claims audited by independent third parties. Surfshark has undergone such audits.

### Kill Switch

A kill switch cuts your internet connection entirely if the VPN connection drops unexpectedly. Without it, your device would briefly revert to your regular unencrypted connection. For accounting work, that brief exposure is unacceptable. Ensure whatever VPN you choose has a kill switch and that you have it enabled.

### Protocol Options

WireGuard is the modern standard — fast, lean, and well-audited. OpenVPN is the older but extremely reliable alternative. Avoid VPNs that only offer proprietary protocols without published audits.

### Device Coverage

Accountants typically work across a laptop, a tablet, and sometimes a phone. A plan that covers unlimited devices means you are not paying twice to protect your full setup. Surfshark is one of the few major providers that offers unlimited simultaneous connections on a single plan.

### What is the difference between a VPN and the HTTPS on websites?

HTTPS encrypts the data between your browser and a specific website. A VPN encrypts all traffic from your device, including traffic from apps that might not use HTTPS, DNS lookups, and metadata that HTTPS does not cover. They are complementary, not redundant.

### Is a VPN enough to protect client data on its own?

No. A VPN is one layer of a broader approach. You still need strong, unique passwords, two-factor authentication on every platform that supports it, encrypted storage for local files, and careful handling of how client documents are shared and stored. A VPN handles the network transmission layer; the rest is up to your operational practices.
