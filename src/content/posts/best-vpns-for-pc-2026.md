## What Is a VPN and What Does It Actually Do?

A VPN — Virtual Private Network — creates an encrypted tunnel between your PC and a server operated by the VPN provider. All your internet traffic passes through that tunnel before reaching its destination.

Two practical things happen when you turn on a VPN:

**Your real IP address is hidden.** Websites and services see the VPN server's IP address, not yours. This makes it harder for advertisers, trackers, and sites to build a profile tied to your physical location.

**Your traffic is encrypted between you and the VPN server.** This matters most on untrusted networks — public Wi-Fi at a hotel, airport, or coffee shop. Without a VPN, anyone on the same network can potentially intercept unencrypted traffic. With one, they see only an encrypted stream they can't read.

What a VPN does not do: it does not make you invisible online, it does not protect against malware or phishing if you click something dangerous, and it does not prevent websites from identifying you through cookies or login sessions. It is one layer of a sensible privacy setup, not a magic shield.

## Do You Actually Need a VPN on Your PC?

The honest answer depends on how you use your computer.

**You probably benefit from a VPN if:**
- You regularly connect to public Wi-Fi (cafes, airports, hotels, libraries)
- You want to prevent your internet provider from seeing and selling your browsing data
- You access streaming platforms or services that vary by region
- You travel and want to access content from your home country
- You work remotely and need to appear to connect from a specific location

**You can probably skip it if:**
- You only ever use your home broadband connection
- You're unconcerned about ISP data collection
- You have no streaming or region-related needs

For most regular PC users, the public Wi-Fi use case alone is enough to justify a cheap annual VPN plan. At $2–3/month, the risk reduction on hotel or airport networks is worth it.

## Can't You Just Use Windows or Mac's Built-In VPN?

This is the question most guides skip. Yes — both Windows and macOS have native VPN clients built right in. Here's what they actually do.

![Windows and Mac native VPN settings compared to a full VPN app interface](/og/best-vpns-for-pc-2026-inline-1.png)

### Windows Built-In VPN (Settings > Network & Internet > VPN)

Windows supports L2TP/IPsec, IKEv2, PPTP, and SSTP protocols natively. You can add a VPN connection without installing any third-party software. The catch: **you need a VPN server to connect to.** The built-in client is just the connection tool — it does not include servers, does not include a no-logs policy, and does not provide any privacy from your ISP or VPN provider.

This is useful if your employer provides VPN credentials for remote work, or if you self-host a VPN server at home (advanced). For general privacy and streaming use, the built-in client alone doesn't help — you still need an account with a service that runs the servers.

### macOS Built-In VPN (System Settings > VPN)

macOS supports the same core protocols: L2TP over IPsec, IKEv2, and Cisco IPSec. Same situation — it is a connection client only. No servers included. If you have corporate VPN credentials, macOS handles them natively and cleanly. For personal use, you need a provider.

**Bottom line on built-in clients:** They are reliable connection managers but are not a standalone privacy solution. You still need a VPN service — the built-in client is just the plumbing.

## How to Set Up a VPN on Your PC

Setup takes under five minutes on either platform.

![Step-by-step VPN setup on Windows and Mac — installing and connecting for the first time](/og/best-vpns-for-pc-2026-inline-2.png)

### On Windows

1. Download the VPN app from the provider's official website (or the Microsoft Store for some providers).
2. Sign in with your account credentials.
3. Select a server location — or use the auto-connect feature which picks the fastest available.
4. Click connect. Look for the VPN icon in your system tray to confirm you're connected.
5. Optional but recommended: enable the kill switch in settings before you start browsing.

You do not need to touch the built-in Windows VPN settings at all if you are using a dedicated app from a major provider. The app handles everything.

### On macOS

1. Download the macOS app from the provider's website or the Mac App Store.
2. Sign in and grant the required network extension permissions when prompted (macOS will ask — this is normal).
3. Connect to a server. The VPN icon will appear in the menu bar.
4. Enable the kill switch in preferences if available for your provider.

macOS may prompt you again about network extensions after system updates — this is expected behavior.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is using a VPN on my PC legal?**

VPNs are legal in most countries. They are widely used by businesses, remote workers, and privacy-conscious individuals. A VPN does not make illegal activity legal — but using one for privacy, public Wi-Fi security, or accessing region-locked content is legal in virtually all Western jurisdictions. If you are in a country with restrictions on VPN use, check local laws.

**Will a VPN slow down my internet connection?**

All VPNs add some overhead due to encryption. On a modern protocol like WireGuard, the slowdown is typically 10–20% on fast connections — which most users do not notice in everyday browsing, streaming, or video calls. If your connection is already slow (under 25 Mbps), you may notice buffering on high-quality streams. Choosing a server geographically close to you minimizes the impact.

**What's the difference between a free VPN and a paid one?**

Free VPNs typically monetize by collecting and selling user data — which defeats the privacy purpose. They also enforce data caps, slower speeds, and restricted server access. ProtonVPN's free tier is a notable exception (open-source, no data caps, independently audited) but limits you to three server locations. Paid mainstream VPNs offer no-logs policies backed by independent audits, faster speeds, and more flexibility. At $2–3/month, a basic paid plan is affordable enough that the free-vs-paid tradeoff rarely makes sense.

**Can I use one VPN account on both my Windows PC and Mac?**

Yes, as long as your provider offers apps for both platforms (all four reviewed here do). With Surfshark's unlimited device policy, you can run the same account on a Windows desktop, a MacBook, your phone, and any other device simultaneously.

**Does a VPN protect me from hackers?**

A VPN protects your traffic from interception on untrusted networks — it encrypts the connection between your device and the VPN server. It does not protect against malware, phishing links, or credential theft. For fuller protection, pair a VPN with a [reputable antivirus](https://snipstack.io/reputable-antivirus-software-2026/) program and basic security hygiene (strong passwords, two-factor authentication). **[See which VPN fits your PC setup best.](/blog/vpn?sub=best-vpns-for-pc-2026__faq)**

**Do I need a VPN if I already have [antivirus software](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-for-iphone-2026/)?**

They solve different problems. [Antivirus software](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-2026/) scans for malware and blocks malicious files on your device. A VPN encrypts your network traffic and hides your IP address. They complement each other — a device with antivirus and a VPN is meaningfully more protected than one with only antivirus.
