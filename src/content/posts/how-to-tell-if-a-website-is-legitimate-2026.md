## The Core Signals of a Legitimate Website

Before comparing tools, it helps to know what legitimacy actually looks like. A website that checks all these boxes is almost certainly run by a real business:

- **HTTPS with a valid certificate** — the padlock icon in your browser address bar. Not proof of legitimacy on its own (scammers can get certificates too), but its absence is an immediate red flag.
- **Verifiable contact information** — a working [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/), physical address, and business email that match a real registered entity.
- **A domain older than six months** — brand-new domains used by "established banks" or "official retailers" are almost always fraudulent.
- **Consistent reviews on independent platforms** — Trustpilot, Google Reviews, or sector-specific review sites. Be suspicious of sites with only five-star reviews and no critical feedback.
- **Transparent policies** — a real returns policy, privacy policy, and terms of service written in readable language, not generic boilerplate.

The tools below help you verify these signals quickly.

## Method 1: WHOIS Lookup — Check Who Owns the Domain

**What it tells you:** When the domain was registered, who registered it (where public), where the servers are hosted, and when the registration expires.

**[How to use](https://snipstack.io/how-to-use-email-aliases-for-security-2026/) it:**
1. Go to `whois.domaintools.com` or `lookup.icann.org`
2. Enter the full domain (e.g., `suspiciousshop.com`)
3. Look at the creation date, registrant organisation, and contact details

**What to look for:**
- A domain registered within the last few months claiming to be a long-established brand is almost certainly fake.
- Legitimate businesses rarely hide all registrant information behind privacy services — some do, but paired with other red flags, full WHOIS privacy is suspicious.
- The registrant country should make sense for the business context.

**Limitation:** WHOIS tells you about the domain, not what the site actually does. A fraudulent site can be months old and still be stealing card details. Use WHOIS as a starting point, not a conclusion.

## Method 2: Google Safe Browsing — Check for Active Threats

**What it tells you:** Whether Google has flagged the URL for hosting malware, phishing pages, or unwanted software.

**[How to use](https://snipstack.io/how-to-use-virtual-number-without-sim-card-2026/) it:**
1. Go to `transparencyreport.google.com/safe-browsing/search`
2. Enter the full URL
3. Read the current status

**What to look for:**
- A "no unsafe content found" result is reassuring but not a clean bill of health — many scam shops pass this check because they do not host malware directly.
- A flagged result is a hard stop. Do not visit, enter details, or click anything on that domain.

**Limitation:** Google Safe Browsing catches technically dangerous sites well. It does not catch scam retailers who take real payments and then simply do not ship anything — those sites may be perfectly "safe" in the technical sense while being completely fraudulent operations.

## Trust Signals to Check Manually — A Quick Checklist

Even with tools, a sixty-second manual scan of the site itself catches a lot:

- **About page exists and is specific** — real businesses name their founders, describe their history, or show team photos. Vague filler text is a warning sign.
- **Contact page has real details** — a street address you can verify on Google Maps, a [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-privacy-for-social-media-2026/) that actually connects, a business email on the same domain (not a Gmail or Yahoo address).
- **Social media links work and are active** — check that the linked accounts actually exist, have real followers, and post regularly. Fake sites often link to empty or cloned social profiles.
- **Return and refund policy is specific** — generic copy-paste policies without brand-specific terms are common on scam sites.
- **Payment page uses recognised processors** — PayPal, Stripe, major card networks. If a site only accepts wire transfer, gift cards, or cryptocurrency, that is a significant red flag for a retail purchase.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does HTTPS mean a website is legitimate?**

No. HTTPS means the connection between your browser and the site is encrypted — it does not mean the site itself is trustworthy. Fraudulent sites routinely obtain free SSL certificates. HTTPS is a necessary condition for a legitimate site, not a sufficient one. Always combine it with other checks.

**Q: How do I check if a website is legitimate for free?**

Start with ScamAdviser (free tier at scamadviser.com) for a legitimacy score, then use a WHOIS lookup tool to check the domain's age and registrant details. Cross-reference with Google Safe Browsing if you suspect active malware. For free ongoing protection, [Surfshark Scamcheck](/blog/scamcheck?sub=how-to-tell-if-a-website-is-legitimate-2026__faq) offers a free tier that covers basic link scanning.

**Q: What makes a website look fake?**

Common indicators: a domain registered very recently, no verifiable contact information, a "contact us" page with only a web form and no [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-tinder-privacy-2026/) or address, prices dramatically below market rate, spelling errors in legal pages, and social media links that lead to empty or newly created accounts. A scam shop can look convincing at first glance — the domain age and contact verification are the checks that catch most of them.

**Q: Can a website have good reviews and still be a scam?**

Yes. Review manipulation is common. Scammers bulk-create fake positive reviews on Trustpilot or Google, or only operate long enough to collect payments before disappearing. Look for reviews that mention specifics (product names, delivery times, customer service interactions) and check that reviews span more than a few weeks. A flood of generic five-star reviews posted within a short window is a warning sign.

**Q: Is ScamAdviser always accurate?**

ScamAdviser is a useful screening tool, not a definitive verdict. It works best on sites that have been active long enough to appear in its databases. A new scam site launched this week may score reasonably well until victims report it. Combine it with a WHOIS lookup and manual trust-signal checks for the most reliable picture.

**Q: What should I do if I already paid a website that turned out to be fake?**

Act quickly. Contact your bank or card issuer immediately and request a chargeback — most card providers have a dispute process for non-delivery or fraud. If you paid by bank transfer, contact your bank's fraud team. Report the site to your national consumer protection authority and to ScamAdviser or similar databases so others are warned.
