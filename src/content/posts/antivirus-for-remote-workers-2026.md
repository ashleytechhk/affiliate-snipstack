## Why Working From Home Makes You a Target

Office buildings have layers of security that most people never think about — firewalls, monitored networks, IT teams watching for unusual activity in real time. When you work from home, all of that disappears. You are on a consumer-grade router that may not have been updated in years, on a network shared with every other device in your house, with no one monitoring what comes in or goes out.

Criminals figured this out early in the remote work boom, and they have refined their tactics ever since. According to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center, cybercrime targeting remote workers increased sharply from 2020 onward and has not slowed down. Adults over 60 who work from home now represent one of the highest-value targets — they often have access to employer systems, handle sensitive data, and are statistically less likely to have dedicated security software in place.

The risk is not abstract. A single compromised home computer can give an attacker access to company email, shared documents, customer records, and financial systems — all through your legitimate login credentials, which means the intrusion looks like normal activity until it is far too late.

### Your Home Network Was Never Designed for This

A home Wi-Fi router is built for streaming and browsing. It is not built to stop sophisticated attacks. Most routers ship with default passwords that millions of people never change. Many have firmware vulnerabilities that manufacturers have patched but users have never applied. Some have been quietly compromised for months or years without the homeowner ever knowing.

When your work laptop connects to that network, it is sitting alongside every other device in your home — smart speakers, phones, tablets, children's gaming devices. Any one of those devices can serve as an entry point for a criminal who then moves laterally across the network to reach your work machine. This is called a lateral movement attack, and it is far more common than most people realize.

## The Three Threats You Actually Face

Understanding exactly what you are up against makes it easier to see why basic protection is not enough.

### Targeted Phishing That Uses Your Employer's Name

The most effective attack against remote workers is not a random spam email. It is a carefully crafted message that uses your company's name, your manager's name, and the exact kind of request your job involves. Criminals research LinkedIn profiles, company websites, and email footers to build convincing fakes.

You might receive an email that appears to come from your company's IT helpdesk, asking you to update your VPN credentials through a link. Or an invoice from a supplier you recognize, with a PDF attachment containing hidden malware. Or a calendar invite to a video call, with a link that silently installs spyware when you click to join.

These are not crude scams. They are specifically designed to fool experienced professionals. And they work.

![How phishing attacks target remote workers using employer impersonation in 2026](/og/antivirus-for-remote-workers-2026-inline-1.png)

### Malware That Hides in Ordinary Files

Remote work involves a constant flow of documents — PDFs, spreadsheets, presentations, forms. Each one is a potential delivery vehicle for malicious code. A file that looks like a project brief can contain software that silently records everything you type. A spreadsheet that appears to be a budget can install a program that monitors your screen and sends screenshots to a criminal every 30 seconds.

Without real-time [antivirus](https://snipstack.io/vs-antivirus-2026/) scanning, there is no way to know a file is dangerous until after it has already run. By that point, the damage is done.

### Credential Theft Through Fake Login Pages

When you work remotely, you log in to systems constantly — your company's email platform, project management tools, HR portals, accounting software. Criminals create exact copies of these login pages and deliver links to them through email or text message. When you enter your credentials, they are captured instantly.

Your real login page looks exactly like the fake one. The only reliable way to catch this is a browser-level web protection tool that checks the destination before the page loads — something a dedicated antivirus provides and that no built-in operating system protection does reliably.
