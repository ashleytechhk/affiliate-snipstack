## What Number Spoofing Actually Is

Spoofing means falsifying the caller ID information that displays on your phone. The call is real. The number that shows up is not.

This is technically trivial. Anyone with a VoIP account and the right software can transmit any caller ID string they choose. The FCC has logged over 2,400 enforcement actions against illegal robocallers and spoofed-number operations since 2015, and the volume of spoofed calls has grown, not shrunk.

The most common spoofing patterns targeting online daters:

**Neighbor spoofing.** The call shows a number with your area code and first three digits. Your brain pattern-matches it as local and trustworthy. You answer.

**Contact spoofing.** Scammers who've obtained your contact list data — from a data broker, from an app breach, from a compromised match — can call you from a number that looks like someone you already know. You answer because you think it's them.

**Own-number spoofing.** A call that shows your own number. Alarming enough that most people answer out of confusion. The script on the other end usually starts with "we've detected suspicious activity on your account."

None of these can be faked without one prerequisite: your real [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-privacy-for-social-media-2026/). To neighbor-spoof effectively, attackers need to know your area code and prefix. To contact-spoof, they need your contacts. To run a targeted romance-scam operation, they need your actual number to verify you're a viable target.

That's the entry point Alt-Number addresses.

## Why Online Daters Are a High-Value Target

Dating app users share more sensitive data than almost any other category of app user. You provide your real name, approximate location, age, photos, and phone number — often within the first week of signing up. That data sits in a database that may or may not be adequately secured.

Between 2021 and 2024, several major dating platforms disclosed breaches or data incidents. Even apps that haven't been breached routinely sell anonymized (or not-so-anonymized) user data to third parties including data brokers. Once your number enters that ecosystem, it is sold and resold. There is no opt-out that actually works.

The FTC's Consumer Sentinel Network shows that people who report romance scams are significantly more likely to have also reported being targeted by spoofed calls in the same period. The correlation isn't coincidental — scammers acquire number lists, verify which are active dating app users via cross-referencing broker data, and then begin contact via phone.

![Data flow diagram showing how a real phone number travels from dating app signup through data brokers to a scammer's targeting list](/og/how-to-stop-number-spoofing-calls-2026-inline-1.png)

The attack chain looks like this:

1. You sign up for a dating app using your real number
2. The app shares data with third-party analytics or advertising partners
3. Data brokers aggregate and sell that data
4. A scammer purchases a list that includes your number, age range, relationship status, and app usage
5. Targeted spoofed calls begin — appearing local, or appearing as someone you trust

The problem isn't that your number got "hacked." It's that you gave it to an entity that distributed it, voluntarily and legally, through a chain you had no visibility into.

## Layer 1: Carrier-Level Spoofing Filters (STIR/SHAKEN)

The FCC mandated STIR/SHAKEN implementation for major carriers starting in 2021. This is a framework that cryptographically verifies whether the caller ID on an incoming call actually matches the originating number.

When STIR/SHAKEN is working, your phone or carrier assigns a "verified," "partially verified," or "unverified" status to incoming calls. Calls with forged caller IDs often display as "Spam Risk," "Scam Likely," or show no label at all instead of the spoofed name.

**[What to do](https://snipstack.io/what-to-do-after-malware-infection-2026/):**

- On AT&T: Enable **ActiveArmor** through the AT&T app. It uses STIR/SHAKEN data plus AT&T's own fraud network to label and optionally block suspected scam calls.
- On Verizon: Enable **Call Filter** through the My Verizon app. The free tier labels spam; the paid tier adds automatic blocking.
- On T-Mobile: Enable **Scam Shield** in the T-Mobile app. Scam ID labels suspicious calls in real time; Scam Block can auto-reject them.

**Limitation:** STIR/SHAKEN only works when both ends of the call are on participating networks. Calls that transit overseas infrastructure, or originate from non-compliant carriers, may bypass verification entirely. It reduces spoofing. It does not eliminate it.

## What to Do If You're Already Receiving Spoofed Calls

If spoofed calls are already reaching you, the response is:

**Do not engage.** Don't press numbers, don't stay on the line to argue, don't call back an unknown number that called you. Any engagement signals that the number is active and attended, which increases targeting.

**Report to the FCC.** Go to fcc.gov/consumers/guides/filing-informal-complaint and file a complaint. Include the spoofed number shown, the date and time, and any details about the call content. FCC enforcement uses complaint data to identify high-volume operations.

**Report to the FTC.** At reportfraud.ftc.gov — especially if the call involved any attempt to obtain financial information or personal data.

**Forward to 7726.** Text the spoofed number to 7726 (SPAM). This shortcode is accepted by all major US carriers and feeds their fraud databases.

**Consider your number's exposure.** If spoofed calls are arriving at volume, your number is on a list. That's not fixable retroactively — your options are to layer up your carrier and app-based filtering, or to start using an alias number for new contacts and gradually reduce the footprint of your current number.

## FAQ

**Does Alt-Number actually stop spoofed calls from reaching me?**

Not directly. No tool completely blocks spoofed calls because the spoofed number isn't yours — it's what the caller displays. What [Alt-Number](/blog/altnumber?sub=how-to-stop-number-spoofing-calls-2026__faq) does is prevent your real number from ending up on the targeting lists that generate spoofed call campaigns in the first place. Fewer entities holding your real number means fewer breach-and-sell events that put you in a scammer's crosshairs.

**Can I use an alias number for dating app SMS verification?**

Surfshark Alt-Number uses real mobile numbers rather than VoIP lines. Most apps accept real mobile numbers for verification. That said, each app's behavior varies — test with your specific app before making it your default setup.

**What is STIR/SHAKEN and do I need to do anything to activate it?**

STIR/SHAKEN is a carrier-level protocol that cryptographically verifies caller ID information. Major US carriers have implemented it as of 2021. Your carrier may or may not surface its results visibly — enabling tools like AT&T ActiveArmor, Verizon Call Filter, or T-Mobile Scam Shield brings those results to your screen as call labels or automatic blocks. You do need to enable those apps; the underlying protocol is automatic.

**Is it illegal for someone to spoof a phone number?**

Under the Truth in Caller ID Act, it is illegal to transmit false caller ID information with the intent to defraud, cause harm, or wrongly obtain anything of value. Legitimate uses — like a business calling from its general number instead of an employee's direct line — are permitted. Spoofing with fraudulent intent carries FCC fines up to $10,000 per violation. Enforcement exists but is difficult when spoofing operations run from outside US jurisdiction.

**What if I've already given my real number to multiple dating apps?**

You can't un-ring that bell, but you can stop adding to your exposure going forward. Start using an alias for any new apps or new contacts. With time, your real number's circulation decreases relative to your alias — and your alias is the one that absorbs any resulting spam or spoofing attempts. If things get bad enough, you can retire the alias and start fresh.

**Does forwarding my alias number add noticeable delay to calls?**

Alt-Number call forwarding typically adds a fraction of a second — not noticeable in normal conversation. If you're in a low-signal area, any forwarding adds latency, but that's a network issue rather than an alias-specific one.
