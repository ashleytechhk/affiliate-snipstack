## SimpleLogin: The Power User's Choice

SimpleLogin is an open-source email alias service with a generous free tier and a paid plan at around $4/month. It is the tool of choice for people who take inbox management seriously.

For sports bettors, the standout features are reply-from-alias (you can contact sportsbook support without exposing your real address) and custom domain support (handy if you run multiple accounts or manage betting for clients). The browser extension works across Chrome, Firefox, and Edge, and alias creation is nearly as fast as Alt-ID.

The free tier is limited to 10 aliases, which works if you are active on fewer than ten platforms. Serious multi-platform bettors will hit that ceiling quickly. The paid plan removes all limits and adds priority support.

SimpleLogin was acquired by Proton in 2022, which improves its long-term sustainability but means you are now trusting the broader Proton ecosystem rather than a fully independent service.

**Verdict:** Best for power users who want open-source auditability, reply-from-alias, or custom domain routing. Slightly more setup friction than Alt-ID.

## Apple Hide My Email: The Frictionless Option (With Caveats)

If you are an iPhone user betting via Safari on iOS or macOS, Apple Hide My Email is the path of least resistance. It is built into the system, costs nothing beyond an iCloud+ subscription (which most iPhone users already have), and generates aliases automatically when you fill in email fields on Safari.

The problem for sports bettors is ecosystem lock-in. Most sportsbook apps are native Android or iOS apps, not Safari web pages, and Apple Hide My Email only works seamlessly within the Apple Sign In flow or Safari autofill. Standalone app registrations where you type an email manually are less friction-free.

Alias management is also basic. You can view and disable aliases in iCloud settings, but there is no per-alias statistics view, no custom forwarding rules, and no way to use aliases outside Apple's ecosystem.

**Verdict:** Good zero-cost option for bettors who exclusively use Safari on Apple devices. Not flexible enough for multi-platform or cross-device betting.

## Firefox Relay: Solid Free Tier, Lightweight Tool

Firefox Relay offers five free aliases with unlimited email forwarding for each. The paid plan at $1.99/month removes the alias cap and adds a phone masking feature. It is open-source and audited.

For casual bettors who maintain accounts on five or fewer platforms and use Firefox as their browser, Relay is a perfectly reasonable free option. The extension is clean, alias creation is quick, and the forwarding is reliable.

The ceiling is the five-alias free limit and the absence of custom domain support. Bettors who move across platforms frequently will hit the limit and need to either delete old aliases or upgrade.

**Verdict:** The best free option for Firefox users with a small number of active betting accounts.

## FAQ: Email Aliases for Sports Betting Signups

**Does using an email alias violate sportsbook terms of service?**
Using an alias email address that forwards to a real, personal inbox does not violate standard sportsbook terms. The registration is still yours, tied to real identity and payment details. You are not creating multiple accounts — you are managing how promotional mail reaches you. That said, always read the specific platform's terms for any edge cases.

**Will I still receive verification emails and password resets?**
Yes. All of the tools in this comparison forward every incoming email to your real inbox, including account verification, two-factor authentication codes, and password reset links. The alias is transparent to the sender — they have no way to know it is a forwarding address.

**Can I use a different alias for every sportsbook promotion?**
Yes, and that is actually the best practice. Creating a unique alias per promotion lets you track which platform sold your address or triggered an uptick in spam. When an alias becomes too noisy, disable it — no other inbox is affected.

**What happens if a sportsbook rejects alias email domains?**
A small number of platforms run email domain blocklists and may reject some alias providers. If this happens, try a different alias service (Alt-ID and SimpleLogin use different domain pools) or use a dedicated Gmail address for that platform only.

**Is it possible to hide my identity completely using an alias?**
An email alias masks your real email address but not your identity with the sportsbook itself. Regulated platforms require identity verification (KYC) for withdrawals, so your legal name, address, and payment details are still on file. The alias protects your inbox from spam and data broker exposure — it does not make the account anonymous.

**Do email aliases expire or stop working?**
Aliases stay active as long as your account is active and you have not manually disabled them. If you cancel your Alt-ID or SimpleLogin subscription, forwarding stops. It is good practice to note which alias is linked to which sportsbook account so you can update your contact email before canceling.
