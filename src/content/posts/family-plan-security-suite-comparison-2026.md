## The Norton Problem: You Are Paying for a Brand, Not a Bargain

Norton's name recognition is enormous. Its actual value proposition in 2026 is shakier.

The introductory pricing looks reasonable — around $49.99 for the first year on a 5-device Deluxe plan. The renewal rate is where the frustration starts. Year two often runs $99–120 before any promotional adjustment, a price increase of 50–100% that many families do not notice until the charge hits their card.

What you get for that money: a competent antivirus engine, a passable VPN with a 50GB monthly data cap on lower tiers, and a password manager that is functional but not exceptional. The VPN cap is the part that annoys security-conscious users most — 50GB disappears fast when you have two or three devices routing traffic through it, and upgrading to remove the cap costs more.

Norton's interface regularly pushes upgrade prompts, add-on services, and renewal notifications. For users who remember when Norton was simply an antivirus, the current product can feel cluttered.

The antivirus engine itself tests well in independent lab results. AV-TEST and SE Labs consistently give Norton high scores for malware detection. If antivirus performance alone is your benchmark, Norton earns it. But families shopping for a complete [security suite](https://snipstack.io/security-suite-for-unlimited-devices-2026/) — not just malware protection — are paying a significant premium for features that are either restricted or require a higher tier to unlock fully.

## The McAfee Situation: Better Than It Was, Still Bloated

McAfee has genuinely improved over the past few years. Its antivirus detection rates are competitive, its interface is cleaner than it was in the era that earned it a reputation as the king of system slowdowns, and its Total Protection plan covers more devices on higher tiers.

That said, the bloatware reputation did not come from nowhere. McAfee still installs browser extensions aggressively, still pushes notifications about threats and performance issues that often feel exaggerated, and still has a renewal pricing model that mirrors Norton's: attractive for year one, painful from year two onward.

The VPN included in McAfee Total Protection is among the most limited of any bundled security suite. It lacks a kill switch on some configurations, and its server network is significantly smaller than standalone VPN services. For families who actually want to use the VPN — for public Wi-Fi, for travel, for kids' device security — McAfee's offering is more checkbox than tool.

Where McAfee does better than Norton: its identity protection features are available to more users across more plan tiers, and the multi-device coverage on its Premium plan (up to 10 devices) is more family-friendly than Norton's 5-device ceiling.
