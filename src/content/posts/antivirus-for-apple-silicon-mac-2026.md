## What Is Apple Silicon — and Why Does It Matter?

If you bought a Mac after late 2020, it almost certainly runs on Apple Silicon. This is Apple's own processor chip — the brain inside the computer. It replaced the older Intel chips.

Apple Silicon Macs are faster, run cooler, and last longer on battery. They are genuinely excellent machines. But here is what the advertisements do not tell you:

**The chip does not protect you from the internet.**

Criminals do not attack your chip. They attack you — through emails, websites, fake phone calls, and downloaded files. No matter how powerful your Mac is, these threats arrive the same way: through your browser and your inbox.

## The Real Threats Facing Mac Seniors in 2026

### Phishing Emails That Look Exactly Like Apple

You receive an email. It looks just like an official Apple message — correct logo, correct colors, a warning that your account has been locked. There is a button: "Verify Now."

You click it. You type your Apple ID and password. And just like that, a criminal has access to your Apple account, your photos, and potentially your payment method.

Apple's built-in defenses do not stop this. The email arrives in your inbox. The fake website loads in your browser. Nothing warns you — unless you have active protection running.

### Fake Tech Support Pop-Ups

A large red warning fills your screen. It says your Mac is infected. It shows a phone number to call immediately.

Do not call. Do not click anything.

These pop-ups are a scam. The moment you call, someone will ask for remote access to your Mac and then charge you hundreds of dollars to "fix" a problem that never existed.

### Password-Stealing Software

Sometimes a harmful program arrives hidden inside something that looks harmless — a free game, a PDF reader, a "system update" that pops up on a website. Once installed, it can record everything you type, including your bank passwords.

Apple Silicon Macs are not immune to this. Researchers found 21 new malware families specifically written for Apple Silicon in 2024 alone.

## Why Apple's Built-In Tools Are Not Enough on Their Own

Apple does include security tools on every Mac. They are called XProtect, Gatekeeper, and the built-in Firewall. These tools are good. But they have clear limits:

- They update slowly — sometimes weeks after a new threat is discovered
- They do not scan your email or block phishing websites in real time
- They do not alert you if a file you downloaded two months ago turns out to be harmful
- They offer no protection against scam links sent through text messages or WhatsApp

Think of Apple's tools as a lock on your front door. Good to have. But a lock does not stop someone from tricking you into opening the door yourself.
