## Why Seniors Are the Primary Target for Investment Fraud

Criminals are not random. They choose their targets carefully, and older adults with retirement savings represent the highest-value, easiest-to-reach group available.

There are three reasons seniors are disproportionately targeted:

**Accumulated wealth.** Decades of saving means retirement accounts often hold more money than the accounts of younger adults. A successful attack on a 67-year-old's IRA can net a criminal far more than stealing from a 30-year-old's checking account.

**Less familiarity with digital threats.** Malware, phishing pages, and keyloggers are concepts many older adults have not had to think about before. Criminals exploit that gap. They create [fake website](https://snipstack.io/fake-website-detector-tool-2026/)s that look identical to legitimate brokerage portals — Fidelity, Vanguard, Charles Schwab — down to the logo and color scheme.

**Slower fraud detection.** Many seniors check their investment accounts less frequently than their bank accounts. A criminal who gains access on a Monday may have days or weeks before the theft is noticed — time enough to move funds beyond recovery.

## How Hackers Actually Get Into Your Investment Accounts

Understanding the attack methods makes the solution much clearer. There are three main ways criminals steal investment account credentials from seniors.

### Keyloggers: The Silent Recorder

A keylogger is a piece of software that hides on your computer and records every key you press. When you type your brokerage username and password, the keylogger captures it and sends it to a criminal somewhere in the world. You feel nothing. Your computer appears to work normally. The theft happens days later when the criminal logs in using your credentials.

Keyloggers arrive through innocent-looking email attachments — a "statement from your financial advisor," a "tax document from the IRS," a "special offer from your phone company." One click opens the document and installs the malware silently.

### Phishing Pages: Fake Brokerage Logins

Phishing is when a criminal sends you an email that looks like it came from your brokerage, your bank, or even the [Social Security](https://snipstack.io/social-security-scam-call-verify-2026/) Administration. The email contains a link. You click it and arrive at a page that looks exactly like your account login screen. You type your username and password. The page captures your credentials and redirects you to the real site, so you never suspect anything went wrong.

Modern phishing pages are frighteningly convincing. The URL (web address) in the bar at the top of the browser is slightly different — perhaps "f1delity.com" instead of "fidelity.com" — but it is easy to miss, especially on a small screen or in a hurry.

### Trojan Software: Waiting for the Right Moment

Some malware does not act immediately. A Trojan is a program that installs itself disguised as something useful — a "free PDF reader," a "Windows update," a software update notification. Once installed, it waits. When it detects that you have opened a financial website, it springs into action: capturing your session, intercepting two-factor authentication codes, or taking screenshots of your account balances.

Once installed, it runs automatically in the background.

### Step 2 — Check Your Passwords

Use a different password for your investment accounts than you use for email, social media, or shopping sites. If a criminal steals your email password in a data breach and you use the same password for your Fidelity account, they now have access to both. Your investment account password should be at least 12 characters long and not contain your name, birth date, or address.

### Step 3 — Turn On Two-Factor Authentication

Most major brokerages offer two-factor authentication (2FA). When you log in, the site sends a code to your phone by text message. Even if a criminal has your password, they cannot get in without that code. Look in your account settings under "Security" to enable it — or call your brokerage's customer service line and ask them to walk you through it.

### Step 4 — Type Your Brokerage Address Directly

Never click a link in an email to reach your investment account. Instead, open your browser and type the address directly — for example, "fidelity.com" or "vanguard.com" — every single time. This removes the risk of landing on a fake phishing page entirely.

### Step 5 — Check Your Accounts Weekly

The sooner you spot unusual activity, the better your chances of recovering funds. Set a reminder on your phone or calendar to log into your investment accounts once a week and review recent transactions. Look for anything you do not recognize — small withdrawals, address changes, or beneficiary updates you did not make.
