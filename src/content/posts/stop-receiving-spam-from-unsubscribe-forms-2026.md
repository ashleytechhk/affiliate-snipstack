## Why Unsubscribe Forms Make Spam Worse

Before jumping into the steps, it helps to understand the mechanics. Not all unsubscribe links are created equal.

**Legitimate senders:** A newsletter from a business you actually signed up with is required under CAN-SPAM to honor opt-outs within 10 business days. Unsubscribing from these works — eventually.

**Bad actors:** Spam operations use unsubscribe links differently. When you click, they record that your address was checked, the email was opened, and the unsubscribe was clicked. That is three confirmation signals that your inbox is live. Your address becomes more valuable, not less, and gets resold to other senders at a premium.

**Data brokers and list resellers:** Even if the original sender honors your opt-out, they may have already sold your address to a third party. That third party has no obligation to apply your unsubscribe preference.

The result: you unsubscribe from one list and land on five more.

## Step 1 — Stop Clicking Unsubscribe on Unknown Senders

The first and most important step costs nothing and takes no time: stop clicking unsubscribe links on email from senders you do not recognize or did not directly sign up with.

If you do not remember subscribing to it, do not click anything inside the email. No links, no images, no unsubscribe button. Mark it as spam using your email client's built-in button instead. This signals your provider's filter without confirming your address is active to the sender.

**How to mark as spam without opening:**

- **Gmail:** Check the checkbox next to the email in list view, then click the stop-sign icon (Report Spam) in the toolbar. The email is removed and the sender is flagged.
- **Outlook:** Right-click the email in the list, select **Mark as Junk**, then **Block Sender**. Do not open the email first.
- **Apple Mail:** Right-click (or Control-click) the email in the list, select **Move to Junk**. Apple's filter learns from the action.

## Step 2 — Use the "Unsubscribe" Button in Your Email Client (Not the Link in the Email)

Gmail, Outlook, and Apple Mail all surface a safe unsubscribe option that works differently from the link inside the email.

### Gmail

When you open an email from a mailing list, Gmail often shows a small **Unsubscribe** link next to the sender's name at the top — not at the bottom of the email. This goes through Google's infrastructure, not the sender's tracking link. Use this version.

If you do not see it: click the three-dot menu in the top-right corner of the email, select **Block [sender name]**, then go to **Settings → Filters and Blocked Addresses** to confirm.

### Outlook

Open the email, look for the **Unsubscribe** button that appears in the reading pane header (above the email body). This uses Microsoft's List-Unsubscribe header processing — again, through Microsoft's servers rather than the sender's click tracker.

Alternatively: select the email without opening it, click **Junk → Block Sender** from the Home tab.

### Apple Mail

Open the email. At the top of the message Apple Mail often shows a banner with **Unsubscribe** as a button. Tap or click it. Apple handles the opt-out request directly when this banner appears.

If no banner: click the sender's name in the From field, then select **Block Contact**.

## Step 3 — Create Filters to Auto-Delete Repeat Offenders

For senders that keep getting through even after blocking, create a filter that automatically deletes their emails before they reach your inbox.

### Gmail Filter

1. In the Gmail search bar, enter `from:(spammer@domain.com)` and press Enter.
2. Click the dropdown arrow in the search bar to open Advanced Search.
3. Click **Create filter** at the bottom of the Advanced Search panel.
4. Check **Delete it** (or **Skip Inbox** and **Mark as read** if you want a record).
5. Click **Create filter**.

Repeat for each repeat offender. You can also filter by subject line or by keywords common across multiple spam senders.

### Outlook Rule

1. Right-click the email, select **Rules → Create Rule**.
2. Check the **From** condition (the sender's address is pre-filled).
3. Click **Advanced Options**, then in the action list select **Delete it** or **Move to Deleted Items**.
4. Click **Finish**.

### Apple Mail Rule

1. Open **Mail → Preferences (or Settings) → Rules**.
2. Click **Add Rule**.
3. Set the condition: **From** → **Contains** → paste the sender's address.
4. Set the action: **Move Message → Trash**.
5. Click **OK**.

## Step 4 — Audit What You Are Actually Subscribed To

![Screenshot of an inbox filtered by "unsubscribe" showing a long list of newsletter subscriptions across multiple senders](/og/stop-receiving-spam-from-unsubscribe-forms-2026-inline-2.png)

Search your inbox for the word `unsubscribe`. Every result is something that has your email address and is legally required to offer opt-out. Go through the list and categorize:

- **Still want it?** Keep it, do nothing.
- **Signed up yourself, no longer want it?** Use the client's unsubscribe button (Step 2 above) — this is safe for legitimate senders.
- **Do not remember signing up?** Mark as spam (Step 1), do not click the link.

For most inboxes with significant spam, roughly half the results in this search will be senders you did not consciously sign up with. Those addresses got your email from a third party. Unsubscribing from them is pointless — they will either ignore it or note the activity. Spam-report them and filter them out.

## Troubleshooting

**I unsubscribed but the same sender keeps emailing me.**
Check whether the emails are coming from the same address or different addresses within the same domain. Some senders rotate sending addresses to defeat blocks. Create a filter on the domain rather than a specific address (e.g., `@spammerdomain.com`).

**I blocked a sender but the email still arrives.**
Blocks in email clients apply to new mail. Emails already in your inbox remain. Go to the spam or junk folder and delete in bulk. Also check whether your email client distinguishes between "block sender" (filter to junk) and "delete all from sender" — the behavior varies by client.

**Forwarded mail from my Alt ID alias is going to the spam folder.**
This happens occasionally when a forwarded message does not pass the receiving server's authentication checks. Open the misfiled message, click the three-dot menu, and tell your client to never send messages from that forwarding domain to spam. You only need to do this once.

**The site rejected my Alt ID alias.**
Some aggressive sign-up forms block addresses containing keywords like "alias" or from domains they do not recognize. Surfshark's `surfshark.id` domain passes most real-time validation checks. If a specific site rejects it, try a different alias with a different generated username — the domain stays the same but a fresh alias sometimes clears finicky validators.

**I forgot which alias I used on a site.**
Check the "To:" header on any forwarded email from that service. It will show the alias address, not your real one. Cross-reference it in the Alt ID dashboard to find and manage it.

**My spam volume dropped but did not disappear.**
The steps above stop new spam at the source but do not retroactively remove your address from lists where it already exists. Your existing address will continue to attract some volume from lists already in circulation. Reduce your real address exposure going forward — over time the existing lists go stale and the volume drops.
