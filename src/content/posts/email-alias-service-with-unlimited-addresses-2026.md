### SimpleLogin

SimpleLogin is an open-source alias service acquired by Proton in 2022. The free tier is capped at 10 aliases — enough to test the concept but not enough for the one-alias-per-service strategy. Paid plans remove the cap entirely and add custom domain support.

**What it does well:**

- Fully open source — the code is auditable and self-hostable
- Custom domain support on paid plans (you can alias from your own domain)
- Strong Proton integration if you use Proton Mail
- Browser extension for auto-generating aliases at sign-up

**Limitations:**

- Free tier caps at 10 aliases — you hit the limit fast if you use one per service
- The paid plan adds a separate monthly cost unless you are already on Proton Unlimited
- More configuration required than consumer-facing tools

**Best for:** Technically inclined users who want open-source transparency, use Proton Mail already, or need custom domain aliases for professional use.

### Firefox Relay

Mozilla's Firefox Relay offers five free aliases with a "premium" tier for unlimited. The free tier is useful for testing but inadequate for a full alias strategy. The premium tier is reasonably priced but lacks custom domain support.

**What it does well:**

- Tight browser integration — Firefox users get one-click alias generation when filling forms
- Mozilla's reputation for user-first privacy policies
- Simple, approachable interface

**Limitations:**

- Free tier: five aliases only — that runs out quickly
- No custom domain support on any tier
- Reply-from-alias is limited and does not work in all configurations
- Less developed outside the Firefox browser ecosystem

**Best for:** Firefox-only users who want a quick, low-commitment way to test aliasing. Not recommended as a primary tool for heavy spam problems.

### Apple Hide My Email

If you are inside the Apple ecosystem, Hide My Email is already available through any iCloud+ subscription (which starts at a low monthly price). It creates random Apple-domain aliases that forward to your Apple ID email.

**What it does well:**

- Deeply integrated into Safari, iOS Mail, and iCloud sign-in flows
- No extra app or setup — aliases appear automatically when you sign in with Apple
- Unlimited alias creation on iCloud+
- Apple's privacy-first reputation

**Limitations:**

- Only useful if you are in the Apple ecosystem (Mac, iPhone, iPad)
- Aliases use Apple's domain — no customisation
- No web dashboard for non-Apple devices
- Forwarding is Apple-to-Apple: if you ever leave iCloud, alias management becomes complicated

**Best for:** iPhone and Mac users who want frictionless alias creation for Apple-native sign-ins. Not a full replacement for a dedicated service if you use cross-platform accounts.

### DuckDuckGo Email Protection

DuckDuckGo Email Protection gives you a `@duck.com` forwarding address — effectively a single alias that strips trackers from incoming mail before forwarding. It also lets you generate unlimited disposable `@duck.com` subaddresses for individual services.

**What it does well:**

- Free, no subscription required
- Actively strips email trackers from forwarded mail (a unique feature)
- Unlimited generated addresses
- No account required — just a DuckDuckGo browser install

**Limitations:**

- No reply-from-alias support — you cannot reply from behind the duck address
- All aliases share the `@duck.com` domain — no custom domain
- Tracker stripping, while useful, means DuckDuckGo processes your mail headers
- Less management dashboard than dedicated services

**Best for:** Users who want tracker stripping and are comfortable with receive-only aliases. Not ideal if you need to reply from aliases or manage many active addresses via a dashboard.

### Fastmail

Fastmail is a paid email provider, not a free add-on. It offers unlimited alias support as a native feature of its email accounts, including custom domain aliasing. If you are willing to pay for a proper email host with aliases built in, Fastmail is the most full-featured option.

**What it does well:**

- Aliases and email hosting in one service — no separate tool to manage
- Unlimited aliases including on custom domains
- Full two-way email from any alias
- Strong deliverability (as an established mail host)

**Limitations:**

- No free tier — subscription-only
- Requires migrating to Fastmail as your primary inbox, which is a significant commitment
- Higher cost than standalone alias tools

**Best for:** Power users who want to rebuild their email infrastructure from scratch around privacy, or businesses needing custom domain aliasing at scale.

## How Many Aliases Do You Actually Need?

The answer depends on your strategy:

**Category-based system (starter):** One alias per category of service — shopping, newsletters, social apps, work inquiries, one-off signups. Five to ten aliases covers most people's online footprint.

**Per-service system (advanced):** One alias per website. If a retailer leaks your data, you know exactly which retailer. This forensic-level tracking requires many more aliases — 50 to 100+ is realistic for someone who has been signing up for things for years.

**Unlimited is the only sensible cap for the per-service approach.** If you hit a limit mid-system, you break the strategy because you start reusing aliases across services, which defeats the purpose.

This is why the "unlimited" distinction matters. A service capped at 50 or even 200 aliases sounds generous until you realise you have been signing up for things since 2005. Only truly unlimited services scale with your actual online footprint.

## FAQ

**What is the difference between an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-grubhub-and-food-delivery-signups-2026/) and a [disposable email](https://snipstack.io/disposable-email-that-receives-messages-2026/) address?**

A disposable address is designed to expire — you use it once, it receives a confirmation email, then it stops working. An [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-real-estate-agent-property-alerts-2026/) is persistent: it stays active until you deliberately delete it, and it forwards everything to your real inbox so you can maintain ongoing relationships (order confirmations, newsletters you actually want, account notifications) from behind the alias. For a spam-control strategy, you want persistent aliases you control, not throwaway addresses that expire automatically. [Alt ID](https://snipstack.io/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-service-with-unlimited-addresses-2026__faq) creates persistent aliases with on-demand deletion.

**Can I use multiple alias services at the same time?**

Yes. Some people use Apple Hide My Email for Apple sign-ins and a dedicated service like Alt ID for everything else. The main overhead is remembering which service generated which alias — a password manager that stores the alias alongside the account login solves this neatly.

**Will services accept [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-receiving-payslips-and-hr-notices-2026/)es, or do they block them?**

Most services accept aliases. Some, particularly loyalty programs and services that monetise email lists, block domains associated with known alias services. If a service rejects your alias domain, the practical workaround is to use an alias from a service with multiple domain options, or to flag that service as one that does not deserve your real address anyway.

**[Does using](https://snipstack.io/does-using-email-alias-protect-from-spam-2026/) an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-healthcare-appointment-reminders-2026/) affect email deliverability — will I miss emails?**

Forwarding adds a small latency (seconds, not minutes) but does not cause missed emails in normal operation. The main risk is overly aggressive spam filters on your primary inbox treating forwarded mail as suspicious. Check your spam folder during the first week of using a new alias service to make sure forwarded mail is landing correctly.

**Is unlimited actually unlimited, or are there hidden soft limits?**

"Unlimited" claims vary. For the services listed here: Alt ID, DuckDuckGo Email Protection, and Apple Hide My Email impose no documented alias cap as of 2026. SimpleLogin's paid tier is unlimited per their terms. Fastmail's alias system is limited by practical inbox management, not a hard cap. As always, verify current terms before committing — pricing and limits change.
