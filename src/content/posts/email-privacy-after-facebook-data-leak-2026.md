## Why Facebook Data Leaks Are Especially Bad for Your Inbox

Most people have had a Facebook account for over a decade. That means the email address they used to sign up is old, widely associated with their real identity, and linked to dozens of other services that used "Sign in with Facebook." When that address appears in a breach database, it does not just attract spam — it attracts targeted spam, because it comes bundled with profile data (name, location, sometimes [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/)) that makes phishing attempts more convincing.

Native inbox tools were not built for this scenario. Gmail's spam filter is good at catching known spam patterns. It is not good at handling a fresh wave of senders using brand-new domains and personally addressed subject lines. Within two to three weeks of a major breach, your spam folder starts catching most of it — but by then, you have already clicked something you should not have, or missed a legitimate email buried in the noise.

The right fix is upstream: stop your real address from being useful to the next attacker, not just the current one.

## Option 1: Native Spam Filters (Gmail, Outlook)

**What it does:** Automatically identifies likely spam based on sender reputation, content patterns, and your manual feedback, then diverts it from your primary inbox.

**Where it works:** Legitimate marketing email, obvious phishing, bulk newsletters. If you are already registered with a company and they are over-mailing you, filters handle this cleanly.

**Where it breaks:** Breach-sourced spam. Spammers responding to fresh breach data rotate sender domains constantly, making reputation-based filters lag by days or weeks per new sender. Plus-addressing tricks (`you+tag@gmail.com`) do not hide your actual domain — a spammer who sees `you+facebook@gmail.com` knows your real address is `you@gmail.com`.

**Verdict:** Good for maintenance, not for recovery. Use filters as a backstop, not a solution.

---

## Option 2: Gmail Plus Addressing

**What it does:** Lets you append a tag to your Gmail address (`yourname+facebook@gmail.com`) so you can filter by tag. The email still arrives in your main inbox.

**Where it works:** Identifying which service sold your data. If spam arrives on `yourname+facebook@gmail.com`, you know Facebook was the source.

**Where it breaks:** It does not hide your real address. Anyone who sees the alias can strip the `+tag` portion and recover `yourname@gmail.com` instantly. Many services also reject plus addresses outright during signup. There is no kill switch — you cannot delete a tag alias.

**Verdict:** Useful for forensics, not for protection. It tells you where the leak came from; it does not stop the spam.

---

## Option 3: SimpleLogin / addy.io

**What it does:** Creates proper randomized alias addresses (`trq84x@simplelogin.com`) that forward to your real inbox. The sender never sees your real address. You can disable or delete any alias from a dashboard.

**Where it works:** Standard alias use cases — one alias per service, kill any alias that gets spammy. Free tiers are generous. Both services are open-source and have strong privacy reputations.

**Where it breaks:** No identity masking. These services generate a masked email, but the alias is not tied to a generated name, date of birth, or other profile data. If a service demands more than an email address during signup, you are still providing your real details. Also, some platforms have started blocking known alias domains from SimpleLogin and addy.io.

**Verdict:** Solid free option for most people. The lack of identity masking is a real gap if your threat model includes profile-level data correlation.

## Option 4: Proton Pass Aliases

**What it does:** Proton's password manager includes an alias generator that creates `@passmail.net` addresses. Integrated into the Proton ecosystem alongside ProtonMail and [ProtonVPN](https://snipstack.io/vs-protonvpn-2026/).

**Where it works:** If you are already a Proton user, this is a seamless addition. The free tier includes ten alias addresses. The interface is clean and the privacy track record is strong.

**Where it breaks:** Ten aliases on the free tier limits per-service isolation unless you upgrade. Like SimpleLogin, there is no identity masking — just email masking. The kill switch works, but alias management is slightly less intuitive outside the Proton app.

**Verdict:** Great within the Proton ecosystem. Limited by the free-tier alias cap for heavy users.

## Option 5: Temp-Mail and Disposable Address Services

**What it does:** Generates a short-lived inbox at a random address. Useful for one-time signups where you need to receive a verification email and nothing else.

**Where it breaks:** Not designed for ongoing services. Temp inboxes expire, are often publicly accessible (anyone who guesses your address can read your mail), and cannot be used for accounts you need long-term. They do nothing for protecting an address that has already been leaked.

**Verdict:** Wrong tool for this job. Temp addresses are for throwaway signups, not for replacing a leaked real address.

## How Alt ID Compares When a Breach Actually Happens

Here is the scenario most alias tools do not fully account for: you have used a masked alias, and the service holding that alias gets breached. What does the attacker actually have?

With a **plain alias service** (SimpleLogin, addy.io): they have a masked email address. They cannot trace it back to your real address unless they breach the alias service itself. Good, but the alias is still tied to whatever real-name data you gave the service during signup.

With **Alt ID**: they have a masked email address tied to a fictional name and date of birth. Even if a second breach somewhere else exposes the same fictional identity, it does not connect back to you — the fictional person does not exist. Cross-database identity correlation, which is [how data brokers](https://snipstack.io/how-data-brokers-get-your-email-address-2026/) build detailed profiles on real people, has nothing to work with.

This matters more than most alias comparisons acknowledge. Single-service isolation is good. Cross-database identity protection is better.

## FAQ

**Will any of these tools stop existing spam on my current address?**
No tool can retroactively remove your address from spam lists already in circulation. Inbox filters manage the symptom. Masked aliases prevent the next wave by keeping your real address off future lists entirely. The two approaches work in parallel.

**Can I use a masked alias for Facebook itself?**
You can use an alias for new accounts or secondary accounts. If you want to update the email on an existing Facebook account to a masked alias, you would need to go into account settings and replace the email — Facebook will send a verification to the alias, which will forward to your real inbox normally.

**Does identity masking actually stop data brokers?**
It significantly reduces their ability to connect records across databases. Data brokers build profiles by matching fields like name, email, date of birth, and location across multiple breach sources. If each service has a different fictional identity, the matching process breaks down. It does not make you invisible, but it makes profiling you substantially harder.

**Which is better for a non-technical user — SimpleLogin or Alt ID?**
Both are straightforward. SimpleLogin has a longer track record and a more active open-source community. Alt ID is simpler to set up if you are already a Surfshark subscriber, and the added identity masking layer gives it an edge for users specifically worried about post-breach profiling.

**Should I use a VPN alongside a masked alias?**
They protect different things. A VPN hides your IP address and network traffic. A masked alias hides your email identity. For inbox spam specifically, the alias is more directly useful. If you want comprehensive privacy coverage — IP, email, identity — combining both makes sense, and Surfshark bundles both in one subscription.
