### SimpleLogin

SimpleLogin is an open-source alias service with a genuinely useful free tier: 10 aliases with full reply support, no expiry. The web interface is clean, [browser extension](https://snipstack.io/browser-extension-to-check-scam-sites-2026/)s are solid, and the service has a good reputation in the privacy community.

**Free plan specifics:** 10 active aliases, reply-from-alias supported, one mailbox. For light users — say, protecting your inbox from a handful of sites — 10 aliases is workable. Heavy users who want aliases per service will hit the limit fast.

**Who it's for:** Privacy-conscious users who want open-source software and don't mind a modest free cap. The paid tier (around $4/month) unlocks unlimited aliases and custom domains.

**Limitations:** 10 aliases is restrictive once you start using the category-alias strategy seriously. No standalone app on mobile — browser-based and extension-dependent.

---

### Apple Hide My Email

Apple's built-in alias feature is genuinely excellent — unlimited aliases, seamless integration with iCloud Mail and Safari, automatic fill-in on iOS and macOS. If you're Apple-first, it's arguably the best experience on this list.

**Free plan specifics:** Available with any iCloud+ plan, starting at low cost. Not free in the "zero dollars" sense, but most iPhone users already pay for iCloud storage, making it effectively included.

**Who it's for:** iPhone and Mac users who want zero friction. Setup is automatic — Safari offers to generate an alias every time it detects an email field.

**Limitations:** Completely tied to Apple's ecosystem. Useless on Android or Windows. Aliases are managed through iCloud settings, which is buried. No catch-all domain. If you leave Apple's ecosystem, migrating aliases is painful.

---

### Firefox Relay

Mozilla's alias service is simple and trustworthy. The free tier gives you five [email mask](https://snipstack.io/email-mask-to-reduce-phishing-on-main-account-2026/)s, which forward to your real inbox. The Premium tier adds phone masking and unlimited aliases.

**Free plan specifics:** 5 masks, forwarding only (no replies from the mask on the free tier), Mozilla branding on the forwarding domain. Five is enough for basic use but tight for anyone serious about compartmentalising their inbox.

**Who it's for:** Firefox users who want a quick trust-and-forget option for signing up to sites. The Mozilla brand provides credibility for users who care about who handles their data.

**Limitations:** No reply support on the free tier is a meaningful restriction — you can receive email through the alias but can't respond from it. Five masks runs out quickly.

---

### DuckDuckGo Email Protection

DuckDuckGo's email service gives you a `@duck.com` forwarding address that strips trackers from emails before they reach your inbox. It's free and unlimited, but it works differently from a full alias service.

**Free plan specifics:** One `@duck.com` address, plus the ability to generate random aliases from that address. Tracker removal is the headline feature — every email forwarded has tracking pixels stripped.

**Who it's for:** People who want tracker removal as the priority, not just address masking. Good as a secondary layer on top of another alias service.

**Limitations:** No reply-from-alias support. You get one address, not truly separate aliases per service. Primarily extension-and-browser-dependent. Not a complete alias management system.

---

### Addy.io (formerly AnonAddy)

Addy.io is the most feature-rich open-source option. The free tier includes 10 shared subdomain aliases and unlimited bandwidth, with reply support. Paid tiers unlock custom domains and unlimited aliases.

**Free plan specifics:** 10 aliases on shared subdomains (e.g., `yourname.anonaddy.com`), full reply support, bandwidth unlimited. The shared subdomain means the domain itself isn't unique to you, which some services may flag as suspicious.

**Who it's for:** Power users who want maximum control and open-source auditability. The self-hosting option appeals to people who want zero third-party data handling.

**Limitations:** The interface is more complex than Alt-ID or Apple's system. Shared subdomains can get blocked by aggressive spam filters on receiving services.

## Feature-by-Feature Comparison: What Actually Matters

![Feature comparison chart: Alt-ID vs SimpleLogin vs Apple vs Firefox Relay](/og/best-email-alias-service-free-plan-2026-inline-2.png)

### Alias Limit on Free

If you're using the category-alias strategy (one alias per type of service), five to ten aliases is tight but workable. If you want one alias per service — which gives you forensic-level leak identification — you'll need unlimited. Only Alt-ID (via Surfshark subscription), Apple Hide My Email, and DuckDuckGo offer unlimited at their respective entry points.

### Reply-from-Alias

This matters more than people expect. If you buy something online and the seller emails your alias, you want to reply from that alias — not your real address. SimpleLogin, Alt-ID, and Addy.io all handle this on their free/included tiers. Firefox Relay does not on the free plan.

### Ease of Setup

Apple wins on zero-friction for existing iPhone users. Alt-ID and SimpleLogin both offer clean web dashboards and browser extensions. Addy.io requires more initial configuration. DuckDuckGo is straightforward but limited in scope.

### Privacy and Trust

SimpleLogin and Addy.io are open-source — code is auditable. Surfshark and Mozilla are established privacy-focused companies with public track records. Apple operates under its own privacy framework. All are meaningfully better than Google or Microsoft knowing your alias routing.

Alt-ID is included with Surfshark subscriptions, which are paid. Surfshark does offer a money-back guarantee period, letting you test the full product including Alt-ID before committing. It is not a standalone free service.

**Can I use an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-real-estate-inquiries-2026/) to stop all spam?**
An alias service stops spam that arrives via addresses you've shared going forward. Spam already hitting your real address (from past breaches or harvesting) won't be affected unless you also change your primary address. The strategy works best when you start fresh: never give out your real address again, only aliases.

**Do [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-steam-account-privacy-2026/) services work with Gmail and Outlook?**
Yes. All the services in this comparison forward to any inbox — Gmail, Outlook, iCloud, ProtonMail, or anything else. Your email provider doesn't need to know you're using an alias service. The alias is just a forwarding layer.

**Will websites accept [alias email](https://snipstack.io/alias-email-for-linkedin-signup-without-inbox-spam-2026/) addresses?**
Most will. Some aggressive anti-spam systems blocklist known alias domains (particularly obvious ones like temp-mail domains). Alt-ID and SimpleLogin use professional-looking domains that pass most filters. If a site rejects your alias, that site itself may be worth avoiding.

**How do I choose between SimpleLogin and Alt-ID?**
If you need a free standalone tool with no other purchases, SimpleLogin is the answer. If you're buying a VPN anyway or want a broader privacy solution, Alt-ID gives more for the same spend. Both handle the core job — forwarding aliases with reply support — equally well.
