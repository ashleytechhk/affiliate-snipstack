## The 10 Red Flags — What to Look for and [How to Check](https://snipstack.io/how-to-check-if-a-website-is-safe-2026/)

### 1. Typo Domains and Lookalike URLs

Fake websites rarely invent a new name. They impersonate a brand you already trust. The domain will be one character off — `arnazon.com` instead of `amazon.com`, a letter "rn" that looks like "m", or an added hyphen: `pay-pal-account.com`.

**Manual check:** Read the domain character by character in the address bar before you interact with the page. Look for hyphens where there should be none, numbers replacing letters (g00gle), and extra words appended to the brand name.

### 2. Missing HTTPS

A site without HTTPS — shown by `http://` instead of `https://` in the address bar, or the absence of a padlock icon — does not encrypt the connection between you and the server. Anything you type can be intercepted.

**Manual check:** Look at the address bar immediately after the page loads. The padlock icon should be present. If you see "Not Secure" or just `http://`, leave the site.

**One important caveat:** HTTPS alone does not prove a site is legitimate. Scammers can and do get valid SSL certificates for fake websites. HTTPS means the connection is encrypted, not that the site is honest. It is a necessary condition, not a sufficient one.

---

### 3. Broken or Missing Contact Page

Legitimate businesses want to hear from customers. Scam sites do not. A contact page with a generic form and no address, [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/), or working email is a red flag. Sometimes the contact link goes nowhere at all.

**Manual check:** Click the Contact or About link. Try the contact form. Look for a physical address and verify it on Google Maps. If the address is a residential street or a virtual office with no business history, treat it as suspicious.

### 4. Prices That Defy Belief

A designer handbag for $35. A brand-new laptop at 80% off. If the price is so far below market rate that it would be impossible for a legitimate retailer to turn a profit, the site is almost certainly fraudulent. The goal is to collect payment and deliver nothing — or to capture your card details.

**Manual check:** Search for the same product on two or three known retailers. If the price on the suspicious site is dramatically lower than everywhere else, that disparity is the red flag, not the deal.

---

### 5. Suspicious Payment Methods Only

Legitimate e-commerce sites accept credit cards and trusted payment processors. A checkout that insists on wire transfer, cryptocurrency, gift cards, or prepaid debit cards only is a major warning sign. These methods are preferred by scammers precisely because transactions cannot be reversed.

**Manual check:** Reach the checkout page and look at the payment options. If you see no credit card option and no PayPal or recognisable payment processor, stop before entering any details.

---

### 6. Copy-Pasted or Fabricated Reviews

Fake review sections are easy to spot once you know what to look for. Common signs: all reviews posted on the same day, identical five-star ratings, generic language that does not mention any specific product features, and reviewer names that sound randomly generated.

**Manual check:** Copy a distinctive phrase from a review and paste it into Google with quote marks. If the same text appears word-for-word on a dozen other sites, it is copy-pasted filler. Also look for the site on Trustpilot or Google Reviews — a real business usually has a presence on at least one independent review platform.

---

### 7. Brand-New Domain Registration

Scam websites are typically disposable. They go up, collect victims for a few weeks, and disappear before enough complaints accumulate. A domain registered last month claiming to be an established retailer or financial institution is almost always fraudulent.

**Manual check:** Go to `whois.domaintools.com` or `lookup.icann.org`, enter the domain, and check the creation date. A legitimate business website for a company that has operated for years will show years of domain history. A site registered in the last 90 days with no history is high-risk.

---

### 8. No Privacy Policy or Terms of Service

Any website that collects your data — including your email address, payment details, or browsing behaviour — is legally required to have a privacy policy in most countries. Scam sites skip this because they have no intention of complying with data protection laws.

**Manual check:** Scroll to the footer. Look for Privacy Policy and Terms of Service links. Click them. A placeholder page with Lorem Ipsum text, a page that does not load, or a policy that is one paragraph long and says nothing meaningful is a red flag.

---

### 9. Stock Photo "Team" Pages

A real company employs real people. Their About Us page usually has actual photographs of staff or at minimum a genuine description of the organisation. Scam sites populate team pages with stock photos — professional headshots of people who do not work there.

**Manual check:** Right-click on a team member's photo and select "Search image" (or save and upload to Google Images reverse search). If the same photo appears on multiple unrelated websites under different names, it is a stock image being reused fraudulently.

---

### 10. Urgent Language Designed to Override Your Judgement

Countdown timers. "Only 3 left in stock." "This offer expires in 10 minutes." "Your account will be locked unless you act now." Urgency is a deliberate psychological tool. Scammers use it to prevent you from stopping to think or to run any of the checks in this list.

**Manual check:** When you feel pressured to act immediately, that feeling itself is the red flag. Stop. Close the tab. Visit the brand's official website through a search engine instead of through any link you were given. Legitimate deals do not disappear in seconds.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the fastest single check I can do to spot a fake website?**

Look at the domain name character by character in your address bar. Typo domains — where one or two characters have been substituted or added to impersonate a real brand — are the most common technique used by fake sites. If the domain is even slightly different from what you expect, that is enough reason to leave immediately. [Surfshark Alert](/blog/scamcheck?sub=signs-of-a-fake-website-2026__faq) automates this check and catches known lookalike domains before the page loads.

**Q: Does HTTPS mean a website is safe to use?**

No. HTTPS means the connection between your browser and the server is encrypted, which prevents interception. It does not verify that the site is legitimate. Scammers routinely obtain valid SSL certificates for fraudulent websites, so a padlock icon is a necessary check but not a sufficient one. Always combine the HTTPS check with a domain inspection and other red flag checks.

**Q: How do I check how old a domain is?**

Use a free WHOIS lookup tool. Go to `whois.domaintools.com` or `lookup.icann.org`, enter the domain name, and look at the Creation Date in the results. Any domain registered in the last 60 to 90 days that is presenting itself as an established retailer or financial institution should be treated with strong suspicion.

**Q: I already entered my details on a suspicious site. What should I do?**

Act quickly. If you entered payment information, contact your bank or card issuer immediately and ask them to flag or cancel the card. Change the password for any account you entered credentials into. If you used the same password elsewhere, change those too. Report the site to your national cybercrime authority or the Anti-Phishing Working Group at `reportphishing@apwg.org`.

**Q: Can automatic link scanning catch brand-new scam sites that have never been reported?**

Not with certainty — no tool can identify a site that has never been seen before. However, tools like Surfshark Alert use multiple signals beyond simple blocklists, including domain age, hosting patterns, and behavioural indicators that are characteristic of fraudulent sites. For genuinely novel sites that have not yet been reported, manual checks — particularly domain age and contact page inspection — fill the gap.

**Q: Are review sections on websites reliable for judging legitimacy?**

Reviews displayed on the site itself are unreliable, since the site owner controls what appears. Copy a distinctive phrase from a review and search for it in quotes on Google to test whether it is original. For independent verification, check the business on Trustpilot, Google Business Reviews, or the Better Business Bureau. A business with no presence on any independent review platform is suspicious.
