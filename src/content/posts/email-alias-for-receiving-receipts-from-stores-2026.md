## The Problem: Why Store Receipts Destroy Inboxes

Retailers have a structural incentive to collect your email address. A receipt is a legitimate reason to ask for it, but the moment you hand it over, you've opted into a marketing relationship that's very hard to exit. Unsubscribe links work inconsistently, addresses get sold to third-party mailers, and even when you do unsubscribe, your address remains in their database for future "transactional" messages.

The result: the average active online shopper receives 40–60 promotional emails per week from stores alone, according to inbox analytics reported by email security firms. That noise drowns out emails that actually matter.

The cleanest solution is to never give stores your real email in the first place.

## How [Email Alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-veteran-benefit-portal-registration-2026/)es Work For Store Receipts

An [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-grubhub-and-food-delivery-signups-2026/) is a forwarding address that isn't your real inbox. You give the alias to a store, the store sends the receipt to the alias, and the alias service forwards it to your real inbox. If the store starts spamming you, you delete or disable the alias — the spam stops, and your real address is never exposed.

![How email aliasing works for retail receipts — step-by-step flow](/og/email-alias-for-receiving-receipts-from-stores-2026-inline-1.png)

The practical flow looks like this:

1. You're at checkout (online or in-store) and they ask for your email.
2. You provide an alias like `shopper-nordstrom@altid.example` instead of your real address.
3. The receipt arrives at the alias and gets forwarded to your real inbox.
4. Two weeks later the promotional emails start. You disable or delete the alias.
5. Your real inbox is untouched.

The key difference between tools is how many aliases you can create, whether you can reply from them, how reliable the forwarding is, and what data the service itself retains.

## SimpleLogin — Best Open-Source Alternative

SimpleLogin (now part of Proton) is the strongest open-source contender. The free tier gives you 10 aliases — enough to cover your most-shopped retailers. The paid tier removes that cap and adds features like custom domains.

**Strengths:** Fully open source (you can self-host), reply-from-alias support, detailed per-alias statistics showing how much mail each alias receives, and strong community trust.

**Limitations:** The free tier's 10-alias cap fills up quickly for active shoppers. Setup requires more configuration than Alt-ID. No identity layer — it's email-only.

Best for: privacy-focused users who want to audit the code themselves or self-host.

## Apple Hide My Email — Seamless If You're In The Apple World

Apple's Hide My Email is built into iCloud+ and integrates directly into Safari autofill. When you're signing up for something in Safari on an iPhone, iPad, or Mac, Apple offers to generate an alias on the spot.

**Strengths:** Zero friction within Apple devices, unlimited aliases, decent forwarding reliability.

**Limitations:** You cannot reply from Apple aliases. Auto-generated addresses like `abc123def@privaterelay.appleid.com` look suspicious to some store systems and occasionally get rejected. Entirely useless if you're on a non-Apple device. No identity layer.

Best for: iPhone-primary users who shop mainly through Safari.

## DuckDuckGo Email Protection — The Free Forwarding Layer

DuckDuckGo's Email Protection service gives you a `@duck.com` forwarding address and lets you generate per-sender aliases from it. It's free and strips tracking pixels from forwarded emails, which is a useful bonus for receipt emails that often contain open-tracking links.

**Strengths:** Free, tracking pixel removal, simple interface.

**Limitations:** No reply-from-alias support. Aliases are tied to your `@duck.com` address so there's less isolation between them. No custom naming, no identity layer.

Best for: Users who want free forwarding with basic tracker removal and don't need reply capability.

## Fastmail Masked Email — Best For Power Users

Fastmail's Masked Email feature integrates tightly with the 1Password password manager. When 1Password generates a login for a new store, Fastmail can simultaneously generate a masked email for that login. The alias is stored in 1Password alongside the password.

**Strengths:** Tight 1Password integration, reply support, custom alias naming, unlimited aliases on paid plans.

**Limitations:** Requires both a Fastmail subscription and ideally 1Password for the seamless experience. More expensive when you add both services. Overkill if you just want receipt management.

Best for: Users already running Fastmail + 1Password who want aliases to slot naturally into their existing workflow.

## FAQ

**Can I use an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-healthcare-appointment-reminders-2026/) at a physical store checkout?**

Yes. Most in-store receipt requests let you type or verbally spell out an email. You can give the alias address the same way you would your real one. Some stores send receipts via SMS instead — for those cases, an alias service with a masked [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-tinder-privacy-2026/) option (like Alt-ID) covers you.

**Will stores reject alias email addresses?**

Occasionally. Some retailers flag certain alias domains as suspicious, particularly auto-generated Apple Private Relay addresses. Alt-ID and SimpleLogin aliases tend to pass more reliably because they use less obviously generic domain patterns. If a specific store rejects your alias, the fallback is to use a dedicated secondary real inbox just for that retailer.

**Can I reply to a store about an order from my alias?**

Only if your alias service supports outbound replies. Alt-ID and SimpleLogin both support replying from aliases. Apple Hide My Email and DuckDuckGo do not — if you reply from those, your real address is exposed.

**Do [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-investment-account-notifications-2026/)es expire or stop working?**

They stop working when you disable or delete them, which is exactly the point. You control the lifecycle. If a store starts sending unwanted emails, you switch off that alias and the forwarding stops instantly. Your real inbox is unaffected.

**Is it safe to use an alias for transactional emails like order confirmations?**

Yes. Aliases forward all incoming mail, including order confirmations, shipping updates, and return confirmations. As long as you don't disable the alias before your order is complete, you'll receive everything you need. A useful practice is to keep retail aliases active for 30 days after a purchase, then disable them.

**How many aliases do I actually need?**

More than you think. If you shop at 15 different stores, you ideally want 15 separate aliases so you can trace which store is responsible for any spam and shut off only that one. A paid tier on SimpleLogin or an Alt-ID plan makes unlimited aliases practical.

**[Does using](https://snipstack.io/does-using-email-alias-protect-from-spam-2026/) an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-to-separate-shopping-from-work-email-2026/) stop loyalty program tracking?**

Partly. Your alias prevents your real email from being linked across databases, but retailers can still track you through your loyalty account name, payment card, or shipping address. Pairing an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-managing-subscriptions-one-inbox-2026/) with the Alt-ID alternate name feature removes the name-linking layer too.
