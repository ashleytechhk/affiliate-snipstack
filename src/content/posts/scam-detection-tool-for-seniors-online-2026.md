## Why Seniors Are the Top Target for Email Scams

Before comparing tools, it helps to understand what you are actually up against.

Modern phishing emails are not the obvious misspelled messages they used to be. Scammers now use AI to write polished, convincing messages that impersonate Medicare, the IRS, [Social Security](https://snipstack.io/social-security-scam-call-verify-2026/), banks, delivery services, and even family members. They create fake urgency — "your account will be suspended in 24 hours" — to push recipients into clicking before they think.

Common scam types targeting seniors by email include:

- **Phishing** — fake login pages that steal passwords and banking credentials
- **Grandparent scams** — emotional appeals pretending a grandchild is in trouble
- **Prize and [lottery scam](https://snipstack.io/lottery-scam-email-how-to-spot-2026/)s** — claims of winnings that require payment of fees upfront
- **[Tech support scam](https://snipstack.io/tech-support-scam-phone-call-identify-2026/)s** — warnings about viruses that pressure you to call a fake helpdesk
- **Subscription renewal scams** — fake receipts for services you did not purchase

Knowing the categories is useful. But when you receive an unfamiliar email and need a definitive answer right now, a scam detection tool is far more reliable than instinct alone.

### Google Safe Browsing

Google Safe Browsing is built into the Chrome browser and many Android devices. When you click a link, it checks the destination against Google's database of known malicious sites and warns you before the page loads.

For seniors who use Chrome as their primary browser, this provides a useful baseline layer of protection at no cost. The problem is that it only catches sites already flagged in Google's database. Brand-new phishing pages — which scammers often create and discard within days — frequently slip through during the window before they are reported and indexed.

It also does not analyze the text content of an email itself, only the links you actively click.

---

### Norton 360 and Full Antivirus Suites

Norton, McAfee, and similar products offer broader device security: real-time malware detection, firewall protection, password managers, and email scanning. For seniors who want one comprehensive solution covering everything, a reputable antivirus suite is worth considering.

The trade-offs are cost (typically $40 to $100 per year), required installation, and periodic update prompts. Some seniors find these tools confusing to navigate or intrusive in their notifications.

If someone in the family handles the technical setup and maintenance, a full suite can make sense. If you want something you can manage entirely on your own without support, a simpler dedicated tool is more practical.

### Built-In Email Spam Filters

Gmail, Outlook, and Apple Mail all have spam filters that automatically sort suspected junk into a separate folder. These filters are constantly updated and catch a large volume of mass-distributed scam emails.

The gap: targeted scams — messages crafted to look like they come from your specific bank, your doctor's office, or even a family member — often land in your inbox because they do not match known bulk-spam patterns. These are precisely the emails most likely to fool seniors, and spam filters alone will not stop them.
