## Why Newsletters Are the Worst Offender in Your Inbox

Before comparing tools, it helps to understand exactly why newsletter signups produce such outsized spam.

**List resale is standard practice.** Many newsletter operators monetise their subscriber lists by sharing or selling them to "partner brands." You opted into one publication; you're now on twelve lists. The original newsletter's unsubscribe link doesn't touch any of those partner lists.

**Acquisitions spread your data.** A newsletter gets acquired. The new owner sends a "we're moving" email and enrolls your address in their existing marketing infrastructure. You have no relationship with this company and no obvious way to opt out of their other campaigns.

**Data broker pipelines.** Email lists get ingested by data brokers who normalise, verify, and resell them. Your newsletter signup from three years ago could appear in a fresh outreach campaign today, with no trail connecting it back to the original subscription.

**The breach multiplier.** Newsletter platforms hold large subscriber lists and are attractive breach targets. When a list is exposed, every email on it ends up on dark-web marketplaces. Marketers buy these dumps and blast them.

The result: your real email address, given to one newsletter, can generate spam from hundreds of unrelated senders over a period of years. [Email aliases](/email-alias-example-2026/) break this chain cleanly — you give each newsletter a forwarding address, not your real one, so your real address is never in the pipeline to begin with.

## Tool 2: Apple Hide My Email

For iPhone and Mac users, Hide My Email is built directly into the operating system. Safari autofills a unique `@privaterelay.appleid.com` address whenever a sign-up form is focused. Each address is different; all of them forward to your Apple ID inbox.

**Why it works:** Zero friction for iOS users. No separate app, no dashboard to navigate. Safari handles alias creation automatically at sign-up.

**Where it falls short for newsletters:** The `@privaterelay.appleid.com` domain is widely recognised as a privacy relay. Some newsletter platforms flag it and ask for a "real" email address — particularly smaller publications using basic email validation. You may occasionally need to use a different alias tool for those cases.

Hide My Email is also tightly tied to iCloud+. If you ever cancel iCloud+, your aliases stop forwarding. Alt ID and DuckDuckGo don't have that dependency.

## Why the Free Workarounds Don't Actually Work

### Gmail Plus-Addressing

`yourname+newsletters@gmail.com` looks like a clever trick. Most email marketers strip everything after the `+` before storing the address — so the newsletter's Mailchimp or Klaviyo account records `yourname@gmail.com`, not the tagged version. Your real address goes straight into their database and every downstream list buyer gets it.

Even in cases where `+` addressing survives, you can't revoke it. You can filter incoming mail, but you can't stop the sender from having the address.

### Secondary "Junk" Gmail Account

A second Gmail account keeps your primary inbox clean. The problem is that you now have a second inbox to manage — and most people stop checking it within a few weeks, missing legitimate newsletters they actually wanted. The junk account also accumulates spam, gets breached, and remains permanently linked to any real information you provided during sign-up.

More importantly: you can't kill a Gmail account cleanly. It's a permanent address tied to Google's identity system, not a disposable forwarding layer.

### Free Throwaway Email

Services like Guerrilla Mail and 10 Minute Mail are excellent for single-use confirmations. They are useless for newsletter subscriptions. Newsletters send weekly or monthly — a throwaway inbox that expires in ten minutes can't receive next week's issue. Most newsletter platforms also blocklist known throwaway domains and reject them at sign-up.

## FAQ

**Does an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-twitch-account-privacy-2026/) work with any newsletter platform?**

Alt ID aliases are standard email addresses and pass signup validation on virtually all newsletter platforms. The `@altid.surfshark.net` domain is not on public throwaway blocklists — unlike Guerrilla Mail or 10 Minute Mail domains — so it clears the email validation checks that most newsletter forms run.

**Will I still receive newsletters I actually want?**

Yes. Aliases forward all incoming mail to your real inbox in real time. Your newsletters arrive exactly as they would to your real address. The alias is invisible to the newsletter operator; you see no difference in your inbox.

**[What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) to a newsletter's confirmation email when I sign up?**

It forwards to your real inbox through the alias. Click the confirmation link as normal. From that point on, every issue that newsletter sends goes through the alias to your inbox. The newsletter operator never sees your real address.

**Can I use one alias for all newsletters or do I need one per newsletter?**

One alias for all newsletters works and is the easiest starting point. One alias per newsletter is more powerful — when a specific publication sells your address, you can identify exactly who leaked it and delete only that alias without affecting your other newsletter subscriptions. [Alt ID's](/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-for-newsletter-subscriptions-2026__faq) unlimited alias allowance makes the per-newsletter approach practical.

**[Is using an](https://snipstack.io/is-using-an-ad-blocker-legal-2026/) alias for newsletter sign-ups against the rules?**

No. You're providing a valid, functional email address — the newsletter can deliver to it, and you receive every issue. That it routes through a forwarding layer is invisible to the sender and does not violate standard newsletter terms of service.

**What if I want to keep receiving a newsletter after I delete an alias it was linked to?**

Before deleting the alias, go to that newsletter's preference centre and update your subscription email to a fresh alias. Then delete the old one. The newsletter continues arriving on the new alias; the old one — and every buyer of that list — is cut off.

**Does this protect me from spam that's already hitting my inbox?**

Aliases protect your real address for future signups. If your real address is already being spammed from past newsletter signups, you have two options: aggressively filter and unsubscribe on the real address while moving all new signups to aliases, or migrate to a new primary address and use aliases for everything going forward.
