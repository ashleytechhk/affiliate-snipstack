## Why Shared Computers Are a Serious Security Problem

When one person uses a computer, the risk is manageable. When three, four, or five people share the same Windows machine — employees, family members, part-timers, delivery staff who need to check something quickly — the risk multiplies fast.

Here is why shared computers are so dangerous:

**Each user is a potential entry point.** One employee clicks a suspicious link in an email. Another downloads what looks like a legitimate invoice template. A third connects a USB drive they used at home. Any of these actions can silently install malware that spreads to every file on that computer — and potentially to other computers on the same network.

**Shared computers rarely get updated properly.** In a busy small business, nobody has time to manually run Windows updates or verify security settings between customers. Outdated software is full of known vulnerabilities that cybercriminals actively exploit. Attackers scan for unpatched systems automatically.

**Sensitive business data lives on these machines.** Customer payment information, employee records, supplier contracts, banking login details — if ransomware locks your files or a thief steals your data, the financial and legal consequences can be severe and long-lasting.

The 2024 Sophos State of Ransomware report found that the average cost of a ransomware attack on a small business now exceeds **$1.85 million** when accounting for downtime, recovery costs, and lost customers. For a small family-run business, that number is devastating.

## The Threats Targeting Small Business Seniors in 2026

Cybercriminals are not random. They specifically target older business owners and senior staff because this group is statistically less likely to recognize warning signs — and more likely to act quickly when frightened by a fake alert.

![Common cyber threats targeting small business owners on shared Windows PCs](/og/antivirus-for-small-business-shared-windows-computers-2026-inline-1.png)

Here are the most common attacks hitting small businesses right now:

### Phishing Emails That Look Completely Legitimate

You receive an email that appears to come from your bank, your accountant, the IRS, or a familiar supplier. It asks you to click a link and verify your account details. These emails are designed with convincing logos and professional language specifically to fool you. In 2024, phishing accounted for over 36% of all data breaches tracked by Verizon. One wrong click is all it takes.

### Fake Security Pop-Ups and Tech Support Scams

A warning appears on your screen in large red text: "VIRUS DETECTED — Your computer is infected! Call this number immediately for assistance." This is a scam. No legitimate antivirus program alerts you through a browser pop-up asking you to call a phone number. These fake tech support scams are designed to panic you into calling criminals who then charge you hundreds of dollars or gain remote access to your machine. The FBI reported over **$924 million in losses** from older Americans targeted by tech support scams in 2023 alone.

### Ransomware Spreading Through Shared Files

When employees share files on a shared Windows computer — or through a shared folder on the network — one infected file can encrypt everything on the machine and lock every user out. Ransomware spreads rapidly on shared systems with no real-time antivirus monitoring file activity. By the time you notice, the damage is done.

### Keyloggers That Record Everything You Type

Some malware programs run invisibly in the background and record every keystroke you make — including passwords, banking logins, customer information, and anything else you type on that shared computer. You would never know it was there until the damage appeared on your bank statement or in a customer complaint.
