## Why Freelancers Over 60 Are Being Targeted Right Now

It is not random. Cybercriminals pick their targets carefully, and senior freelancers tick every box they look for.

You work independently, which means there is no company IT department watching out for you. You handle real money — client invoices, PayPal transfers, bank details. You communicate by email dozens of times a day, which gives criminals many chances to slip a fake message through. And statistically, people who did not grow up with the internet are more likely to trust a convincing-looking email or website that a younger person might immediately recognise as fake.

A 2024 survey by the AARP found that 1 in 3 adults over 60 had experienced an attempted online scam in the previous twelve months. For those who also do freelance work, the number is higher — because more online activity means more exposure.

The threats you face as a freelancer are specific:

- **Fake client emails** — A message that looks like it is from a real client, asking you to click a link or open an attachment. The link installs malware. The attachment steals your passwords.
- **Invoice fraud** — Criminals intercept or fake invoices and redirect your payments to their own accounts.
- **Tax season phishing** — Scam emails claiming to be from the IRS or HMRC, threatening fines unless you click immediately.
- **Wi-Fi attacks** — If you ever work from a coffee shop or library, criminals can sit on the same network and intercept what you send and receive.

A good antivirus stops most of these threats before you even see them.

This is not meant to frighten you unnecessarily — but you deserve to know what is at stake.

If malware gets onto your computer, here is what can happen in a matter of hours:

**Your banking details are copied.** Many types of malware watch what you type. When you log in to your bank or PayPal, your username and password are silently sent to a criminal overseas.

**Your client files are locked.** Ransomware is a type of attack that locks all the files on your computer and demands payment — often hundreds or thousands of dollars — to unlock them. Freelancers lose contracts, work history, and client data.

**Your email is used to attack others.** Once criminals control your email account, they send scam messages to everyone in your contact list. Your clients receive fake invoices or dangerous links — appearing to come from you.

**Your identity is used to open accounts.** With enough of your personal details, criminals can apply for credit cards or loans in your name.

None of this requires you to do anything dramatically careless. A single click on a well-crafted [fake email](https://snipstack.io/fake-email-sender-with-attachment-2026/) is enough.

## Extra Steps to Protect Your Freelance Business

Antivirus is the foundation, but here are three additional habits that cost nothing and significantly reduce your risk.

**Use a separate email for client work.** If your personal email is compromised, your client communication stays clean — and vice versa.

**Never open email attachments you were not expecting.** Even if the email appears to come from a known client, call them quickly to confirm before opening anything unusual. Criminals can fake the "from" address.

**Use different passwords for different accounts.** If one account is breached, others remain safe. A free password manager (Surfshark also offers one) can remember them all for you.
