### SimpleLogin (by Proton)

SimpleLogin is the strongest standalone alternative for business users who need custom domain support. You can route `contact@yourdomain.com` through SimpleLogin's alias layer, which is useful if your business email is also your public-facing address.

The free tier allows 10 aliases. The paid plan (~$4/month) unlocks unlimited aliases, multiple custom domains, and PGP encryption for the privacy-focused. Two-way reply works cleanly. If you're already in the Proton ecosystem (ProtonMail, [ProtonVPN](https://snipstack.io/vs-protonvpn-2026/)), integration is seamless.

The main downside versus Alt-ID: it's an additional subscription rather than a bundled feature. If you're not already paying for Proton, you're adding another monthly line item.

### addy.io (formerly AnonAddy)

addy.io is the most flexible option on the technical side — open source, self-hostable, and the cheapest paid tier of any service compared here (~$1/month). It supports unlimited aliases, custom domains, and two-way reply on paid plans.

For a spam-exhausted small business owner who just wants things to work without configuring anything, addy.io's interface feels more developer-oriented than the others. It's excellent if you want granular control or want to host it yourself. It's overkill if you want a clean dashboard and quick setup.

### Firefox Relay

Firefox Relay gives you five free aliases with forwarding, then unlimited on the premium tier (~$1.99/month). The free tier is fine for testing the concept, but five aliases covers almost nothing for a real business workflow.

The main gap: two-way reply is limited. You can receive to an alias but replies leak your real address unless you've configured reply masking, which isn't as clean as Alt-ID or SimpleLogin. For casual personal use it's convenient. For business use where you're having ongoing email conversations through aliases, it falls short.

---

### Apple Hide My Email

Available to iCloud+ subscribers on Apple devices, Hide My Email generates aliases at the system level when you're signing up via Safari or Sign in with Apple. Setup is frictionless if you're in the Apple ecosystem.

The limitation for business use: it's built around Apple's apps and services. Using it outside of Safari or Apple-integrated sign-in flows is clunky. Two-way reply works for iMessage-adjacent use cases but isn't designed for back-and-forth email correspondence through aliases. Fine as a supplement; not a primary business alias solution.

---

### DuckDuckGo Email Protection

DuckDuckGo's [free email](https://snipstack.io/free-email-alias-2026/) protection service generates a `@duck.com` alias that strips trackers from forwarded emails before they reach your inbox. It's the only service here that actively processes email content for tracker removal, which is a genuine differentiator.

The deal-breaker for business use: no two-way reply. Everything that comes in to your duck.com alias appears in your real inbox, but when you reply, your real address is exposed. For one-way newsletter signups and form fills it's excellent. For any service where you need ongoing communication, it doesn't work.

## Why Alias Services Matter More for Business Users

Spam isn't just annoying for a business owner — it's operationally expensive. Here's what actually happens:

**Client messages get buried.** When your inbox volume is 100+ emails a day and 60% are spam, you miss things. A client following up on a proposal, a supplier sending revised pricing — these land in the same pile as the pharmacy ads and SEO pitch spam.

**Your address gets on lists faster.** A business email on your website, in forum signatures, on directory listings, in contractor estimate platforms — each touchpoint is a collection opportunity. Data brokers aggregate these. Spam follows.

**Each unsubscribe attempt is a data confirmation.** When you hit "unsubscribe" on illegitimate spam, you're confirming the address is active. The volume often increases. Aliases break this loop — delete the alias, and there's nothing to confirm.

![Inbox cleanup workflow using email aliases for business communications](/og/best-email-alias-service-for-business-2026-inline-2.png)

The alias strategy that works for business: create a dedicated alias for each category of external contact. One for vendor signups, one for newsletter subscriptions, one for directory listings and lead gen sites, one for contractor platforms. When a category starts generating spam, you kill that alias and create a fresh one. Your real inbox address — the one your actual clients and colleagues know — never appears anywhere public.

## FAQ

**What is the best [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-newsletter-subscriptions-2026/) service for business if I need a custom domain?**

SimpleLogin is the strongest option for custom domain support. You can route a domain you own through their alias layer, which is useful if your public-facing email (like `contact@yourcompany.com`) doubles as a spam-collection point. For users who don't need custom domains, [Alt-ID](/blog/altid?sub=best-email-alias-service-for-business-2026__faq) is the better overall value because it's bundled with a broader privacy toolkit.

**Can I use [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-car-dealership-contact-2026/)es for client-facing business communication?**

Yes, with caveats. Alt-ID and SimpleLogin both support two-way reply masking, so you can receive and respond through an alias without your real address being exposed. This works well for initial vendor contacts or one-off inquiry handling. For ongoing client relationships where trust matters, some users prefer to transition to their real address after an initial contact.

**Will websites reject business [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-rewards-program-signup-2026/)es?**

Some will. Certain services (particularly financial platforms, government portals, and enterprise SaaS with email verification) flag known alias domains and reject them. This happens with most alias services, not just Alt-ID. The practical answer: use your real address for high-trust, long-term accounts; use aliases for everything else.

**How many aliases do I actually need for a business workflow?**

Start with six: one each for vendor sign-ups, newsletter subscriptions, lead-gen and directory listings, contractor and estimate platforms, event registrations, and a throwaway for one-time downloads. Add per-service aliases for high-volume sources once you've identified where most of your spam originates. Most business users find 10-20 active aliases covers their full workflow without becoming unmanageable.

**Does using an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-github-account-privacy-2026/) slow down email delivery?**

Forwarding adds a negligible delay — typically under a second. In practice it's not noticeable. The more relevant concern is deliverability: make sure your real inbox doesn't filter forwarded mail as spam. Add the forwarding domain to your trusted senders list after setup.

**Is Alt-ID worth it if I already pay for a VPN separately?**

If you're paying for a VPN subscription and want alias functionality, it depends on what you're paying. Surfshark's plan includes VPN, Alt-ID aliases, antivirus, and data removal at a competitive price. If switching to Surfshark from your current VPN saves money or breaks even, the alias tool comes effectively for free.
