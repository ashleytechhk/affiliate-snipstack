## Why Puppy Scams Are So Hard to Spot

Puppy scam operators are not amateurs. They run professional-looking websites with stolen photos of real dogs, fake breeder certifications, and customer testimonials pulled from legitimate breeders. The listings appear on real classified sites, [Facebook Marketplace](https://snipstack.io/facebook-marketplace-scam-how-to-avoid-2026/), and even Google search results.

What makes these scams especially tricky:

- **Prices look reasonable.** Scammers price puppies just below market to seem legitimate, not suspiciously cheap.
- **Communication feels warm and personal.** Emails include the dog's name, personality details, and backstory.
- **Payment pressure arrives fast.** Once you express interest, a deposit request follows within hours — usually via wire transfer, Zelle, or gift cards.
- **They delay the red flags.** Everything looks fine until the moment you pay. Then the "shipping" complications begin.

For someone who grew up reading physical newspapers and trusting handshake deals, the polished digital facade of a puppy scammer is genuinely difficult to penetrate. That's not a character flaw — it's a design feature of the scam.

## Step-by-Step: [How to Verify](https://snipstack.io/how-to-verify-a-website-before-buying-2026/) an Online Puppy Seller

Use these steps in order. If any step raises a red flag, stop and do not pay.

### Step 1: Reverse Image Search Every Photo

Copy the URL of each puppy photo and paste it into Google Images (click the camera icon) or TinEye. If the same image appears on a different website with a different dog name, breed, or breeder — that's a stolen photo and an immediate red flag.

Scammers frequently steal photos from legitimate breeder websites or social media pages. The dog is real; the seller is not.

### Step 2: Research the Seller's Email Address

Copy the seller's email and search it in Google, exactly as written, inside quotation marks. For example: `"happypuppies@example.com"`. Look for:

- That address appearing in scam warning forums (Reddit's r/Scams is a good resource)
- The same email connected to different names or breeds
- The email domain being only a few weeks old (check Whois)

A legitimate breeder typically uses the same email for years and may have reviews tied to it.

### Step 3: Check the Domain Age

Go to a Whois lookup tool (whois.domaintools.com is free) and enter the seller's website URL. A domain registered within the last 60-90 days is a serious warning sign. Established breeders don't launch new websites every season.

### Step 4: Search the Business Name and [Phone Number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-privacy-for-social-media-2026/)

Search the breeder's business name plus the word "scam" or "review." Do the same with their [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-tinder-privacy-2026/). Check the BBB (bbb.org) and the FTC's complaint database (reportfraud.ftc.gov). Even one complaint is worth taking seriously.

### Step 5: Request a Live Video Call

Ask to see the puppy on a live video call — not a pre-recorded clip, not photos, not a video sent over WhatsApp. A live call where you can ask the puppy to be moved to different lighting or positions is very hard to fake. Legitimate breeders almost always agree to this. Scammers almost always refuse or invent excuses.

## Red Flags Checklist: What Legitimate Breeders Do Differently

Use this checklist when evaluating any [online pet](https://snipstack.io/online-pet-adoption-scam-warning-signs-2026/) seller.

**Legitimate breeders typically:**
- Have a verifiable physical address and welcome in-person visits
- Provide health records, vaccination documentation, and breeder registration numbers
- Use a stable, consistent email and phone number with a traceable history
- Accept payment via traceable methods (credit card, PayPal with buyer protection)
- Are registered with a recognized breed organization (AKC, UKC, or equivalent)
- Offer a health guarantee and contract
- Welcome video calls and show you the puppy's environment

**Scam sellers typically:**
- Pressure you to pay a deposit quickly before "someone else takes the puppy"
- Request wire transfer, Zelle, Venmo, or gift cards — all non-reversible
- Have a website with no physical address or a generic template design
- Send photos that appear in reverse-image searches on other sites
- Invent shipping complications after you've paid, requesting more money
- Refuse video calls or send pre-recorded clips
- Have no presence on breeder registries

If a seller matches three or more items on the second list, treat it as fraud regardless of how legitimate the website looks.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I get my money back if I paid a puppy scammer?**
Wire transfers, Zelle, and gift cards are generally not recoverable once sent. Credit card and PayPal payments have more recourse — contact your bank or PayPal immediately and file a chargeback. Report to the FTC and IC3 regardless of payment method. [Learn more about scam prevention tools](/blog/scamcheck?sub=puppy-scam-online-how-to-verify-seller-2026__faq)

**Q: Are puppy scams only on shady websites?**
No. Scammers operate on Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, PuppyFind, and even Google-indexed breeder websites that look entirely professional. Always verify the seller regardless of where you found the listing.

**Q: How do I know if a puppy photo is stolen?**
Use Google Images or TinEye reverse image search. Paste the image URL or upload the photo and see where else it appears online. If it shows up under a different dog name or breeder, the seller stole it.

**Q: What payment methods are safest when buying a puppy online?**
Credit card payments offer the most buyer protection because you can dispute the charge. Avoid wire transfers, cryptocurrency, Zelle, and gift cards — once sent, those funds are extremely difficult to recover if the seller is fraudulent.

**Q: Can a legitimate breeder refuse to do a video call?**
Some breeders are camera-shy or have limited technical comfort, but a complete refusal to show you the live dog before payment is a meaningful warning sign. A legitimate breeder has nothing to hide. If they won't do a live call, consider that a red flag.
