## Why Your Phone Number Is More Dangerous Than Your Password

Most people change their passwords after a breach. Almost nobody can change their phone number.

That is the core problem. Your phone number is permanent. It is tied to your bank, your Apple ID, your Google account, and — thanks to SMS two-factor authentication — it functions as the master key to nearly every account you own. Once it lands in the wrong hands, the damage escalates fast.

Here is what bad actors do with a leaked phone number:

- **SIM swapping**: A fraudster calls your carrier, impersonates you, and convinces the support rep to transfer your number to a new SIM. From that point, they control every SMS-based login on your accounts.
- **Stalking and harassment**: On dating apps, sharing your real number with someone you barely know creates a permanent contact channel — one that survives even after you unmatch or block them inside the app.
- **Spam pipelines**: Shopping sites sell verified phone numbers to data brokers. One sign-up on a US e-commerce platform can trigger months of spam calls to your Canadian number.

For Canadian shoppers specifically, there is an additional wall: many US platforms — flash sale sites, US-only retailers, reseller marketplaces — require a **US phone number** at checkout or during account creation. Your Canadian number, even though it starts with +1, gets flagged or rejected outright because it is registered to a Canadian carrier.
