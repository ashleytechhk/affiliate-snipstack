## Why Seniors Are the Primary Target

Cybercriminals follow the money. Older adults, on average, hold more savings, have better credit scores, and are less likely to notice unusual financial activity quickly. That makes them the most attractive target for three of the most common online attacks:

**[Tech support scam](https://snipstack.io/tech-support-scam-phone-call-identify-2026/)s:** A pop-up or phone call claims your computer has a virus and urges you to call a toll-free number. The "technician" then asks for remote access to your screen and drains your bank account or installs real malware in the process. This scam costs American seniors hundreds of millions of dollars annually.

**Phishing emails:** You receive an email that looks exactly like it came from your bank, Medicare, or a delivery company. The link leads to a fake website built to steal your login details. The fakes are so convincing that even experienced computer users are sometimes fooled.

**Ransomware:** Malicious software locks every file on your computer — your photos, documents, everything — and demands payment to unlock them. For someone who stores decades of family photos on their hard drive, this is devastating.

Understanding the threat is the first step. Fixing it is the second.

## The Best Way to Secure a Home Computer: 6 Steps That Actually Work

You do not need to be a technology expert to protect yourself. Each of the following steps is written so that anyone, regardless of tech experience, can follow it.

### Step 1 — Keep Your Operating System Updated

Windows and macOS release security updates regularly. These updates fix known security holes — gaps that criminals actively exploit to break into computers. Many people delay these updates or turn them off because they seem inconvenient. This is one of the most common reasons computers get infected.

**[How to check](https://snipstack.io/how-to-check-if-your-pc-is-hacked-2026/) on Windows:** Click the Start button, open Settings, then go to Windows Update. Click "Check for updates" and install anything listed.

**[How to check](https://snipstack.io/how-to-check-if-a-website-is-safe-2026/) on Mac:** Click the Apple icon in the top left corner, then go to System Settings and Software Update. Install any updates shown.

Setting your computer to update automatically overnight is the easiest long-term solution.

### Step 2 — Use Strong, Unique Passwords

If you use the same password on every account — and most people do — then one data breach exposes everything. Your bank, email, shopping sites, and social media all become vulnerable the moment one of them is compromised.

A strong password is at least 12 characters long and does not include your name, birthday, or common words. A password manager (a program that remembers passwords for you) is the simplest way to keep unique passwords without having to memorise them all.

### Step 4 — Be Suspicious of Unsolicited Contact

No government agency, bank, or technology company will ever call you out of the blue and ask you to give them remote access to your computer, pay a fine in gift cards, or confirm your password over the phone. These are always scams, without exception.

If you receive an unexpected call, email, or pop-up claiming there is an emergency with your computer or accounts:
- Do not click any links
- Do not call any number shown in the pop-up
- Close the browser tab or hang up the phone
- Call the organisation directly using the number on their official website or your bank card

### Step 5 — Secure Your Home Wi-Fi

Your home internet router is the door through which all your devices connect to the internet. If it is not secured, someone parked outside your home could potentially access your network.

Log into your router settings (the instructions are in your router's manual, usually also printed on the bottom of the device) and:
- Change the default admin password to something strong and unique
- Make sure your Wi-Fi is using WPA2 or WPA3 encryption (not WEP)
- Give your network a name that does not identify your address or name

### Step 6 — Back Up Your Important Files

No security system is perfect. If the worst happens and malware damages or encrypts your files, a recent backup means you lose nothing permanently. The simplest approach is an external hard drive that you plug in once a week to copy important documents and photos.

Cloud backup services (Google Drive, iCloud, Microsoft OneDrive) can also do this automatically every night while you sleep, which is even better.

**Can viruses steal my banking information?**

Yes. This is one of the primary goals of a type of malware called a "keylogger" — it records everything you type, including usernames and passwords, and sends that information to criminals without your knowledge. A real-time antivirus detects and blocks keyloggers before they can record anything.

**My computer is running slowly — does that mean I have a virus?**

Slow performance can have many causes, including a full hard drive, too many programs starting up at boot, or an outdated computer. However, some types of malware do cause slowdowns, particularly if they are using your computer's power to mine cryptocurrency or send spam on your behalf. Running an antivirus scan is a sensible first step if you notice unusual slowness.

**How do I know if an email is a scam?**

Scam emails share several common features: they create urgency ("your account will be closed today"), they ask you to click a link to verify your information, and they often have minor spelling differences in the sender's address (support@amaz0n.com instead of amazon.com). When in doubt, go directly to the organisation's website by typing the address yourself — never by clicking the link in the email. A phishing-protection tool in your [antivirus software](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-for-iphone-2026/) will also catch many of these automatically.

**Is it safe to shop online from my home computer?**

Yes, with precautions. Always check that the website address starts with "https" (the padlock icon in your browser). Only shop on websites you recognise and trust, or that you found via a search engine rather than a link in an email. Avoid saving your card details on shopping sites unless you are certain the site is legitimate.
