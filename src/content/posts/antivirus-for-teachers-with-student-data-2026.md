## Why Teachers Are a Prime Target for Hackers

You might think, "I am just a teacher. Why would anyone come after me?"

The honest answer is that hackers are not targeting you personally — they are targeting the data you carry. Student records are valuable. They include full names, dates of birth, home addresses, parent contact details, and sometimes health or disability information. On the dark web, a complete profile of a minor can sell for far more than a stolen adult identity, because minors rarely check their credit history and fraud can go undetected for years.

Teachers are also targeted because they often:

- Use personal laptops that do not have the same security protections as school computers
- Connect to public Wi-Fi in coffee shops, libraries, or airports while grading papers
- Open many email attachments — assignment submissions, parent emails, district notices — which is exactly how viruses spread
- Forget to update software or operating systems, leaving known security gaps open

A 2024 report by a cybersecurity research firm found that the education sector ranked among the top three most attacked industries globally, behind only healthcare and financial services. Schools and individual teachers are soft targets because they often lack dedicated IT security teams.

## What Happens If Your Student Data Is Compromised

This is the part that keeps district administrators up at night — and should concern you too.

**Legal consequences.** In the United States, the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA) requires that schools — and by extension, teachers — protect student records. If a breach happens because you did not take basic precautions, you could face serious disciplinary action or even personal legal liability. Similar laws exist in the UK, Australia, Canada, and across Europe under GDPR.

**Harm to students and families.** A child whose identity is stolen may not discover the problem until they apply for a student loan at age 18 and find their credit already destroyed. This is a real outcome that happens to thousands of young people each year.

**Damage to your reputation and career.** Even if you are not legally at fault, being the teacher whose laptop caused a data breach is not a situation anyone wants to be in. It can follow you for the rest of your career.

**Emotional toll.** Many teachers who have experienced this describe months of anxiety, guilt, and difficult conversations with parents who trusted them with their children's information.

The good news is that all of this is entirely preventable with one simple step.
