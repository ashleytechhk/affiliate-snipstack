### Gmail's `+tag` Trick

Gmail lets you register as `yourname+library@gmail.com`. Mail lands in your normal inbox, and in theory you can filter it later.

In practice, a lot of older ILS software (the backend systems many local libraries still run) strips everything after the `+` before storing the address, or rejects it outright as an invalid format. When the tag survives, it offers no real protection anyway — your base Gmail address is now on file at the library and every third-party platform it shares data with, and you have no way to revoke it without changing your primary email entirely.

### Apple Hide My Email

Apple's built-in service (available from $0.99/month with iCloud+) creates aliases that forward to your iCloud address and can be killed instantly from iOS Settings or iCloud.com. For anyone already using Safari to browse the library's website, autofill makes registration fast.

The catch is platform lock-in. If your household mixes Android phones, Windows laptops, and shared family library cards, managing everything through iCloud gets awkward fast — especially when a parent needs to register a kid's card under a separate alias. Hide My Email also doesn't generate alternate profile data, so your real name still ends up on every optional field the sign-up form asks for.

### SimpleLogin and Firefox Relay

Both are respected privacy-first alias services. SimpleLogin (part of the Proton ecosystem) offers unlimited aliases on paid tiers; Firefox Relay gives five free aliases before charging $1.99/month for more.

Neither is built specifically for the library use case, where you want alternate profile fields alongside a persistent alias and reliable delivery across a consortium of branches. For a single-branch library card with light use, either is a fine free option. For patrons juggling multiple library systems, reciprocal borrowing agreements, and family cards, Alt-ID's unlimited aliases and bundled identity details give you more control per registration.

### Free Throwaway Services (Guerrilla Mail, 10 Minute Mail)

Throwaway inboxes cost nothing and require no sign-up — you just grab an address and check a browser tab. For library digital loans, they fail on the two things that matter most:

1. **Many library systems reject them at registration.** ILS platforms increasingly check submitted addresses against disposable-domain blocklists. Guerrilla Mail and 10 Minute Mail domains commonly appear on those lists, so your card application can be rejected or silently flagged.
2. **They expire before your hold clears.** A digital loan hold can take weeks. A throwaway inbox lasts minutes to an hour. You will not see the "ready for pickup" email in time, and the item goes back to the next person in line.

Throwaway addresses work for a one-off confirmation link. They don't work for an ongoing relationship with a library card that you plan to actually use.

## Why Library Digital Lending Creates a Spam Problem

Public libraries are nonprofits, which makes the spam volume feel counterintuitive — but the digital lending stack behind most local libraries involves several separate companies, each with its own data practices: the ILS vendor, the ebook/audiobook platform (OverDrive/Libby, Hoopla, cloudLibrary), the streaming video partner (Kanopy), and the summer reading or events platform (Beanstack, Eventbrite). Registering once can put your email into all of them.

Add reciprocal borrowing agreements — common in county and regional consortiums — and a single card can technically register you across a dozen branch systems, each running its own newsletter and fundraising list. "Friends of the Library" groups, which are separate nonprofits from the library itself, often maintain their own donor-solicitation mailing lists built from patron sign-up data.

None of this is malicious. It's what happens when a public service runs on private vendor infrastructure. An alias built for this situation needs to:

- **Keep forwarding for weeks or months**, matching how long holds actually take
- **Pass ILS domain validation** instead of getting flagged as disposable
- **Not require a second inbox** to check manually
- **Be deletable per branch or per platform** when one source turns spammy
