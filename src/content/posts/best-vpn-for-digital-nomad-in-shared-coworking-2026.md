## What "Shared Coworking WiFi" Actually Means for Your Traffic

Coworking WiFi is built for convenience, not isolation. Everyone on the same access point is, technically, on the same local network. That is fine for basic browsing. It is a real exposure for anything sensitive — logging into your bank, sending a client invoice, or authenticating a work account.

Most coworking networks:

- **Do not segment users from each other** — your device can, in some configurations, be visible to other devices on the same subnet.
- **Are reused by hundreds of different people per month**, many of whom you will never see and whose devices you know nothing about.
- **Have no consistent security standard** — a coworking space in one city might run enterprise-grade network isolation; another might be running a single consumer router behind the front desk.

None of this means every coworking network is actively dangerous. It means you have no way to verify which kind you are on, and the default assumption should be "shared and unencrypted" rather than "safe."

### Why This Matters More for Digital Nomads Specifically

A nomad's laptop carries more exposure than a tourist's phone. Client files, invoicing logins, banking sessions, sometimes company VPN credentials for a day job — all routed over whatever network the coworking space happens to run that week. And unlike a home office, you rotate through a new network every few weeks, so there is no time to learn its quirks or trust its setup.

## How a VPN Fixes the Network Problem

A VPN wraps your traffic in an encrypted tunnel before it leaves your device, so anyone else on the same coworking WiFi sees only encrypted noise — not your login pages, not your file transfers, not your account sessions.

The setup is the same wherever you are:

1. Open the VPN app before connecting to the coworking WiFi (or right after).
2. Connect to any server — nearby for speed, or a specific country if you need a location.
3. Work as normal. Your traffic is now encrypted end-to-end to the VPN server.

## The Subscription Arbitrage Angle: Why Your VPN's Server Location Is a Money Lever

Here is the part that matters if you are trying to keep your monthly costs down while moving between countries.

Streaming and subscription pricing is set per region, and it varies more than most people realize. A Netflix, YouTube Premium, or Spotify plan billed in one country can cost meaningfully less than the same plan billed in another — sometimes half, sometimes less. This is not a loophole exactly; it is standard regional pricing that most companies set based on local purchasing power. The catch is that your account usually locks to the region where you first signed up or where your billing method is registered.

A VPN changes what a service sees as your location by routing your connection through a server in that country. Two practical patterns nomads use:

**Restoring access to your home catalog.** If your streaming library is registered in your home country, connecting to a VPN server there while abroad keeps your feed looking like it always did — no gaps, no "not available in your region" messages.

**Signing up in a lower-cost region.** Some nomads set up new subscriptions while connected to a VPN server in a country with lower regional pricing, then keep renewing at that rate. This works best done at signup, since changing a region after the fact is inconsistent across services and sometimes blocked outright by the provider's terms.

Either way, the VPN itself typically costs a few dollars a month — often less than the price difference on a single streaming subscription, which is why nomads treat it as a line item that pays for itself rather than an added expense.

## What to Look for in a VPN for This Specific Setup

Not every VPN handles both jobs — network security on shared WiFi and reliable region-switching for subscriptions — equally well. The features that matter for a nomad working out of coworking spaces:

**Large server count across many countries.** You need options in the regions where your subscriptions are cheapest or where your home catalog lives, not just three or four flagship locations.

**No connection limits.** A laptop, a phone, and often a tablet or backup device — all need to be covered on the same plan without juggling device slots.

**Kill switch.** If the VPN connection drops on a flaky coworking router, a kill switch stops your traffic from falling back to the unprotected network instead of just silently failing over.

**Consistent unblocking.** A VPN with a small, easily-flagged IP pool works for a week and then gets blocked by the streaming service, right when you need it most.

**Multi-hop or obfuscation options.** Useful in networks with more aggressive traffic filtering, so your VPN connection is not obviously identifiable as a VPN connection to begin with.

**Price that matches the use case.** Since the whole appeal partly rests on saving money on subscriptions, the VPN itself needs to be cheap enough that it does not cancel out the savings.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I actually need a VPN if I only use coworking WiFi for browsing and email?

Even basic browsing exposes login sessions and account tokens on a shared network. A VPN is low-effort insurance against the networks you cannot verify — which, on a coworking rotation, is most of them. ### Can a VPN actually lower my subscription costs?

Indirectly, yes — by letting you connect through a server in a region with lower published pricing before signing up, or maintaining your home-region rate while abroad. The VPN does not discount anything itself; it changes what location a service sees.

### Will switching VPN servers get my streaming accounts flagged or banned?

Normal use — connecting to a server, watching your existing subscription, disconnecting later — is common practice and not something platforms typically penalize account-by-account. Aggressive region-hopping to repeatedly change a subscription's billing region can run into more scrutiny; check the platform's terms if you are relying on this regularly.

### Is a free VPN good enough for coworking WiFi?

[Free VPNs](https://snipstack.io/vs-free-vpns-2026/) generally cap speed and data, and their small server pools get blocked by streaming platforms quickly. For daily work use in changing locations, the reliability gap becomes a real cost in wasted time.

### Does a VPN slow down video calls or file uploads on coworking WiFi?

A quality VPN typically costs 15–30% of your connection speed. On the fast fiber connections many coworking spaces run, this is rarely noticeable for calls or uploads. On a slower or oversubscribed coworking network, the VPN overhead is usually small relative to the network's own limitations.

### Do I need a different VPN plan for every country I work from?

No. A VPN plan is not tied to a country — you use the same account and switch servers as needed. Surfshark's unlimited-device, all-server-included plan works the same whether you are in a coworking space this month or a different one next month.
