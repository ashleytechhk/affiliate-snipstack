## Why You Can't Tell Who Sold Your Data Right Now

If you use one email address everywhere, every spam message looks identical from your side. The "From" field shows the spammer's domain, not the original source — it doesn't tell you whether the leak traces back to the shoe store you ordered from, the newsletter you signed up for, or the app you tried once and forgot.

Standard advice — unsubscribe, mark as spam, add a filter — treats the symptom, not the source. None of it tells you which of the dozens of services holding your address actually sold it or got breached. And because your real address never changes, the same leak keeps producing new spam from new senders indefinitely.

The only way to get a real answer is to make every site use a different address for you. When one of those addresses starts receiving spam, you've identified the source instantly.

## Detailed Comparison: How Each Method Performs in Practice

### Gmail's Plus and Dot Trick

Adding `+shopname` before the @ in a Gmail address (`you+shopname@gmail.com`) creates a distinct string that still routes to your main inbox. In theory, spam arriving at that exact address would tell you `shopname` leaked it.

In practice, this rarely works. Most marketing platforms strip everything after the plus sign before storing an address, because their own systems treat plus-tags as invalid formatting. Data brokers do the same when compiling lists for resale. By the time spam arrives, it's addressed to your bare `you@gmail.com`, with no trace of which tag was ever used.

The dot trick (`y.o.u@gmail.com` versus `you@gmail.com` — Gmail ignores dots) fails the same way, and depends on the recipient system also ignoring dots, which most don't bother to check. Neither trick survives contact with a determined data broker, and neither lets you delete the compromised identifier — because it was never a separate identifier in their database to begin with.

### Temporary or Burner Email Inboxes

Burner inbox services generate a random address that receives mail for a short window before disappearing. They're useful for one-time verification codes and trials you'll never need again.

For detection, they fall short. Once the address expires, incoming spam simply bounces — you never see it, so you never learn who sold your data. A few services let you extend the lifespan, but they weren't designed for long-term monitoring, and most don't forward to a permanent inbox you actually check day to day.

They're also hard to track across multiple sign-ups. Even if a burner address does receive spam while still active, you have to remember which throwaway address you generated for which site months earlier — often with no record at all.

### Manual Secondary Email Accounts

Some people create a second or third full email account and dedicate each one to a category of sign-up. This does allow detection — if `account2@gmail.com` starts getting spam, you know it came from whatever that account was used for.

The problem is effort. Each account needs its own password and its own inbox to check, plus manual forwarding rules if you want everything centralized. Scaling this to one account per *site* — which real detection requires — is impractical. Most people stop after two or three accounts and drift back to reusing one address everywhere, which erases the entire benefit.

## FAQ

**Will marketers eventually figure out my alias and strip it like they do with Gmail's plus trick?**

No. An Alt ID alias isn't a modified version of your real address — it's a genuinely separate address with no visible connection to your primary inbox. There's nothing to strip, because the alias is the entire address, not an add-on tag.

**What if a site insists I use my real email instead of an alias?**

Most consumer sign-ups accept any valid-looking address. For the small number of services that verify domain reputation, keep a short list of sites where you use your real inbox and reserve aliases for everything else — you'll still catch the majority of leaks.

**How fast can I tell which site sold my data once spam starts?**

Right away. The alias shown in the "To:" field of the spam message is the answer — no investigation needed, because that alias was only ever handed to one site.

**Do I need a new alias for every single site, or can I reuse broad categories?**

Category aliases (one for shopping, one for newsletters) help reduce spam generally, but if pinpoint detection is the goal, a unique alias per *site* — not category — is what tells you exactly which company leaked or sold your address. **Does deleting a leaked alias affect my account with that site?**

No. Deleting the alias only stops forwarding — the site itself has no idea the alias was deleted, and your account there keeps existing. If you still need that account, create a fresh alias and update the contact email on file.
