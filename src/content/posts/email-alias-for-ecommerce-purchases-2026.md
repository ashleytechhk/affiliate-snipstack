## What Is an Email Alias? (Plain English)

An email alias is a second address that silently redirects to your real inbox. When an online store emails your alias, the message arrives in your Gmail or Outlook exactly as normal — but the store never learns your actual address.

Think of it like a call-forwarding number. People reach you at the forwarding number; they never know your direct line. If the number gets passed around to telemarketers, you cancel it and create a new one. Your direct line never changes.

The difference from a standard secondary email account: you don't log into the alias separately. There's no second inbox to manage. Everything forwards to where you already read email. The alias just acts as a disposable front door.

**Key terms:**

- **Alias address** — the address you give to the store (e.g., `shop-xk72@altid.domain`)
- **Real address** — your actual Gmail/Outlook/Apple Mail, which the store never sees
- **Forwarding** — all emails sent to the alias land in your real inbox automatically
- **Kill-switch** — deleting or disabling the alias so no further emails can reach you

## Why Ecommerce Is Particularly Bad for Spam

Not every online form is equally dangerous. Ecommerce sites sit in an especially aggressive category because of how the commercial ecosystem works.

**Retail marketing partnerships.** Online stores routinely share customer data with "trusted partners" — a vague term that in practice means: the brand that made the product you bought, the returns-management platform they use, the loyalty rewards network they belong to, and several ad-tech companies running retargeting campaigns. You accepted this in the terms you didn't read at checkout. Each partner becomes a new email sender.

**Data broker resale.** Retail purchase data is commercially valuable. Your email address, combined with what you bought and when, is worth money to advertisers. Data brokers buy this kind of information, compile it into profiles, and resell it. By the third or fourth resale, the address is on lists you have no connection to.

**Breach frequency.** E-commerce platforms are high-priority targets in data breaches. Customer payment details are usually encrypted, but email addresses often aren't. Breach databases circulate widely; your address can be scraped from one and end up in a spam operation you'd never trace back to the original store.

**Unsubscribe limitations.** Unsubscribing works for the sender you're unsubscribing from. It does nothing about the dozen other parties who already have your address. And it only works when senders comply — many third-party advertisers operating outside strict regulatory environments don't.

The result: **give a real email address at checkout, and within weeks it's circulating in marketing ecosystems you never agreed to.**

## How an Email Alias Fixes This

The alias approach cuts the chain before it starts.

When you check out on any online store, you enter the alias instead of your real address. From the store's perspective, you're a customer with a valid, reachable email — confirmation emails, shipping updates, return receipts all arrive in your inbox normally. But the address the store records, shares, or loses in a breach is the alias, not you.

When spam starts appearing — and eventually it will — you identify which alias is receiving it, delete that alias in your alias manager, and the address stops existing. The spam has nowhere to go. Your real inbox is unaffected.

If you still need to shop at that store, you create a fresh alias for the next order. Takes 30 seconds.

**What you need for this to work:**

1. An alias service that generates addresses instantly
2. Persistent forwarding (not time-limited throwaway addresses — you need order confirmations for months)
3. A kill-switch that actually terminates the address, not just pauses it
4. Unlimited aliases, or enough headroom to give each store its own address

## FAQ

**What is an email alias for ecommerce purchases and how does it work?**

An alias is a forwarding address you give to online stores instead of your real email. The store sends order confirmations and marketing to the alias; those emails forward to your real inbox automatically. When the store's marketing (or their partners' marketing) becomes unwanted, you delete the alias. The store no longer has a working address for you, without any change to your real email account. [Alt ID](/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-for-ecommerce-purchases-2026__faq) handles alias creation, forwarding, and deletion in one dashboard.

**Will I still receive order confirmations and shipping updates?**

Yes. Forwarding is active from the moment you create the alias. Every email sent to the alias arrives in your real inbox — confirmation emails, shipping notifications, return labels, everything. The only thing that changes is what address the retailer has on record.

**What if I need to log into the store's account later?**

You log in using the alias address, not your real one. The alias stays active until you delete it, so logging in with it weeks or months after purchase works normally. If you delete the alias, you'd lose the ability to receive password resets from that store — delete only when you're done with the account entirely, or use Alt ID's toggle-off to pause forwarding without deleting.

**Can a retailer detect that I'm using an alias?**

Some retailers check emails against known alias domains and may reject them at checkout. Alt ID uses its own domain rather than a widely-known disposable-email domain, which means it passes most standard form validation. If a specific store rejects it, you'd need to use your real address for that particular checkout.

**Is using an email alias dishonest?**

No. You're providing a real, functional email address — all emails reach you and replies work. An alias is a privacy tool, not a deception. It's equivalent to using a PO Box instead of your home address for mail, which is widely accepted practice. The retailer has a valid point of contact; you've simply chosen which address they can reach.

**Does this work on mobile shopping apps?**

Yes. Most shopping apps accept any valid email address at sign-up. Enter the alias in the email field during registration or checkout the same way you would on a desktop. The forwarding is handled server-side, so it works regardless of which device the email is sent to.
