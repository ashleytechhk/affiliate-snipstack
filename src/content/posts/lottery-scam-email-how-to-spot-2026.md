## Red Flag 1: You Never Entered

This is the simplest and most reliable test of all. Legitimate lotteries only contact winners who bought a ticket or entered a draw. You cannot win a lottery you did not enter.

If an email tells you that you have won a prize in a draw you have no memory of entering, the email is a scam. No investigation required. No second opinion needed. Delete it.

Scammers sometimes add credibility by naming real lotteries — EuroMillions, the UK National Lottery, the Spanish El Gordo, the American Powerball. None of these organisations send unsolicited emails informing random people they have won. Their winnings are claimed through official channels tied to the ticket purchase.

---

## Red Flag 2: Upfront Fees to Claim Your Winnings

This is the mechanism by which the scam actually steals money. The email congratulates you on a large win, then explains that a small fee — for processing, tax clearance, insurance, administration, or transfer costs — must be paid before the funds can be released.

The fee is always presented as minor relative to the prize. "Pay £200 to unlock your £850,000 prize." The logic is designed to make the payment feel trivially small. But there is no prize. The money you send is gone, and the scammers will often come back with additional fees before eventually disappearing.

**The rule is absolute:** no legitimate lottery charges winners a fee to receive their prize. Tax obligations on lottery wins are handled after the prize is received, through official tax authorities, not through a wire transfer to a private individual. Any upfront payment request is a scam.

## Red Flag 3: The Sender Domain Does Not Match

Scam emails impersonating real lotteries are sent from domains that look similar to official ones but are not. Examples of what this looks like in practice:

- Claiming to be "EuroMillions" but sent from `euromillions-claims-department.net`
- Claiming to be the "UK National Lottery" but sent from `national-lottery-winners.org`
- Claiming to be from a law firm handling prize distributions but sent from a free Gmail or Yahoo address

On a computer, hover your cursor over the sender name in the email to reveal the actual address underneath. On a phone, tap the sender name once to expand the full address. If the domain after the @ symbol is not the official lottery's real website, the email is fraudulent.

## Red Flag 4: Urgency and Demands for Secrecy

Scam emails create artificial pressure for two reasons. First, urgency overrides critical thinking — "you have 48 hours to claim your prize before it reverts to the pool" makes you feel like there is no time to verify anything. Second, secrecy isolates you from people who might warn you off — "do not inform friends or family until funds are transferred, as this is company policy for security reasons."

Legitimate lotteries have no reason to rush winners or demand confidentiality. Prize claims go through verified, documented processes that can take weeks. There is no prize pool clock running down on your unclaimed millions.

If an email tells you to act immediately and say nothing to anyone, those two instructions together should be read as a single signal: this is a scam.

## Red Flag 5: The Lottery Cannot Be Verified

Some scam emails invent entirely fictitious organisations — the "International Lottery Commission," the "Global Prize Draw Foundation," the "United Nations Charity Sweepstake." These sound official but do not exist.

A quick search for the exact lottery name followed by "official website" or "official results" is enough to verify whether the organisation is real. If the search returns no credible results — or returns warnings from fraud watchdog sites — you have your answer.

Even if the lottery name is real, verify that the email matches how that organisation actually communicates. Most national lotteries publish their winner notification procedures publicly. If the email's process does not match what the official site describes, it is fraudulent.

## Red Flag 6: Suspicious Links Inside the Email

Lottery scam emails often include a link to "complete your claim," "verify your identity," or "download your winner's certificate." These links lead to fake pages designed to harvest your personal information — name, address, date of birth, passport number, bank details — or to trigger a malware download.

On a computer, hover your mouse over any link without clicking it. The real destination appears in the status bar at the bottom of your browser window. If it does not match the text of the link or does not lead to an official domain you recognise, do not click.

On a phone, press and hold the link until a preview menu appears, which usually shows the destination URL.

For a definitive check, copy the link and run it through Surfshark's Email Scam Checker. The tool inspects where the link actually points, checks it against phishing databases, and assesses the age and reputation of the destination domain — all within a few seconds.

## Red Flag 7: Requests for Personal Information by Email

Legitimate lotteries verify winner identities through official, documented processes — usually involving certified identification submitted through a secure portal or in person at a claims office. They do not ask for your passport number, bank account details, mother's maiden name, or national insurance number in a reply email.

If a lottery email asks you to reply with personal or financial information, that information will be used for [identity theft](https://snipstack.io/identity-theft-protection-software-2026/) or fraud. Common asks include:

- Full name and date of birth
- Home address
- Copies of ID documents
- Bank account numbers for "direct transfer"
- [Social security](https://snipstack.io/social-security-scam-call-verify-2026/) or national insurance numbers

None of these should ever be sent by email, to anyone, in response to an unsolicited message.

## What to Do If You Receive a Lottery Scam Email

**Do not reply.** Replying confirms your email address is active and will result in more scam attempts. It also opens a line of communication that scammers are trained to exploit.

**Do not pay any fee.** Regardless of the amount or the justification, no payment is legitimate.

**Run it through a scam checker.** Copy the sender address, any links in the email, or the email text itself and submit it to [Surfshark's Email Scam Checker](/blog/scamcheck?sub=lottery-scam-email-how-to-spot-2026__mid). The tool will confirm what the technical signals say about the email's legitimacy.

**Report it.** In the UK, forward it to report@phishing.gov.uk. In the US, report it to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov. In Australia, use Scamwatch at scamwatch.gov.au. Reporting helps authorities track active campaigns.

**Mark as spam and delete.** Your email client's spam filter will learn from the report.

**If you have already paid or shared information:** Contact your bank immediately if money has been transferred. If you shared identity documents, place a fraud alert with the relevant credit bureaux. Change any passwords associated with the email address you used.

## FAQ

### Can you win a lottery you never entered?

No. This is the most fundamental rule: you cannot win a prize draw you did not enter. Any email claiming otherwise is a scam, regardless of how official or convincing it looks. Real lottery winnings are tied to a specific ticket purchase or entry, which the lottery organisation can verify against their own records.

### Do real lotteries contact winners by email?

Some lotteries do send email notifications — but only to people who purchased tickets through accounts registered with that lottery. They will not email random addresses with news of a win. If you have an account with a lottery service, you can log in directly to check your tickets. Always verify through the official site rather than trusting an inbound email.

### [What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) if I reply to a lottery scam email?

Replying confirms your address is active and monitored, which increases the volume of scam emails you will receive. Scammers are also trained negotiators who will work to keep you engaged, build trust, and eventually extract payment or personal information. Do not reply to any lottery email that arrived unsolicited.

### How do lottery scammers get my email address?

Most often from data breaches — lists of real email addresses that have been leaked from hacked databases and sold on underground marketplaces. Scammers also harvest addresses from social media, public records, and mass list purchases. This means receiving a lottery scam email is not targeted — it is a numbers game. [Run your email through a breach checker](/blog/scamcheck?sub=lottery-scam-email-how-to-spot-2026__faq) to see if your address has been exposed.

### Is there any version of a "you've won" email that is legitimate?

Yes — but only in narrow circumstances. If you entered a competition, signed up for a retailer's prize draw, or buy lottery tickets through an online account, a win notification from that specific service is plausible. The test: can you independently verify that you entered, and does the email come from the official domain of that service? If both are true, log in to the service directly (do not use the email's link) to confirm the win through your account.
