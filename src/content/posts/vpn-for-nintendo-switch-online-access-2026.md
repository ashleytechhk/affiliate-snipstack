## What Is a VPN, and Why Does It Matter for Nintendo Switch?

A VPN — Virtual Private Network — is software that routes your internet traffic through a server in a different country, masking your real location and replacing it with the server's IP address. Websites and online stores see the server's location, not yours.

For Nintendo Switch users, this has one very practical benefit: regional pricing.

Nintendo prices its digital games and subscriptions differently across regions. The same title might cost more in North America than it does in Argentina, Brazil, Turkey, or several other markets. Nintendo Switch Online (the paid subscription required for online multiplayer) also varies by region.

By connecting through a VPN server in a lower-priced region before visiting the Nintendo eShop, you appear to be shopping from that country. This lets you create an account tied to that region's store and purchase games or subscription cards at local prices.

This is sometimes called "subscription arbitrage" — using legal regional differences in pricing to buy at a lower cost. It is not a hack or exploit. Nintendo runs regional storefronts with different prices, and you are simply shopping on the one that offers a better deal.

Install Surfshark (or another VPN) at the router level. Every device on your Wi-Fi network, including your Switch, will then connect through the VPN automatically.

2. Navigate to the VPN client section (supported on most DD-WRT, AsusWRT-Merlin, and OpenWRT routers).
3. Enter Surfshark's OpenVPN or WireGuard credentials.
4. Select the server location you want (e.g., Brazil or Argentina for lower pricing).

### Option 2: Share a VPN Connection From a PC or Mac

1. Connect your computer to Surfshark and choose your target server.
2. On Windows: go to Network Settings > Mobile Hotspot, turn on hotspot sharing from your VPN-connected adapter.
3. On Mac: go to System Settings > Sharing > Internet Sharing, share your VPN connection over Wi-Fi.
4. Connect your Switch to the shared hotspot.

### Option 3: Use a Travel Router

A travel router (like a GL.iNet) sits between your modem and your home network. Many support OpenVPN or WireGuard natively and are easier to configure than flashing your main router.
