## Why Conference Signups Are an Inbox Wrecking Ball

Event registrations are among the highest-spam-generating sign-up categories. Here is why.

**Sponsor data packages.** Most conferences offer sponsors tiered packages that include attendee email lists. The higher the sponsorship tier, the more data they receive. When you register for a three-day industry conference, your email may legally land in the hands of 20 or 30 sponsor companies — all of whom have their own mailing lists, their own partners, and their own data-resale arrangements.

**Organizer re-marketing.** Even after the event ends, organizers continue mailing: recaps, next year's early-bird announcements, partner promotions. If you registered with your real email, you're in that database indefinitely. Unsubscribing from one campaign doesn't remove you from the master list.

**Third-party event platforms.** Many conferences use third-party registration tools — Eventbrite, Hopin, Cvent, Bevy. These platforms have their own privacy policies and marketing permissions, which may be separate from the event organizer's. You may be opted into platform-level communications without realizing it.

**Networking app opt-ins.** Event apps that facilitate attendee networking (Whova, Brella, Grip) ask for your email as the login identifier. Some of these send their own promotional messages well after the event. Others share attendee data with future events on the same platform.

The result: a single conference registration can generate ongoing email from five or more distinct senders. Multiply that across the three to five events a typical professional attends per year, and the inbox math gets ugly fast.

Apple's alias service is tightly integrated into Safari and the iOS share sheet, which makes it frictionless to use on mobile — the most common device for last-minute event registration. DuckDuckGo's `@duck.com` service is genuinely free with no subscription required. The browser extension generates unique aliases on any sign-up form — there's no copy-paste step. It also strips email trackers from forwarded messages, which is a useful bonus.

The `@duck.com` domain is increasingly recognizable as a privacy tool, which means some conference platforms may flag or reject it. If domain appearance matters to you, this is a weakness.

**Best for:** Privacy-conscious users who want a free, standalone tool and don't mind occasionally being told the alias format isn't accepted.

Firefox Relay offers five free aliases — enough for someone who attends a conference once or twice a year. Beyond five, a paid plan is required. The `@mozmail.com` domain has similar recognition issues to `@duck.com`. It works well inside the Firefox browser via the extension, but offers no mobile app-native integration.

**Best for:** Occasional attendees using Firefox as their primary browser who don't expect to exceed five total aliases.

## FAQ

**What is an [email alias for](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-insurance-comparison-sites-2026/) conference signups and why do I need one?**

An [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-car-dealership-contact-2026/) is a forwarding address — it receives emails and passes them to your real inbox, without revealing your real address to the sender. For conference signups, you need one because event organizers routinely share or sell attendee email lists to sponsors and partners. Giving an alias instead of your real address means you receive the legitimate event communications but can cut off the spam pipeline with a single deletion when the event is over.

**Will event platforms accept an alias address at registration?**

Most will. Consumer platforms like Eventbrite, Hopin, and Bevy accept any valid email format. Enterprise platforms that run stricter validation are more likely to reject obviously disposable domains like `@duck.com`. Surfshark Alt ID uses a less recognizable domain that passes most event-platform validators. If a particular platform still rejects your alias, using your real address for that one registration and then aggressively filtering afterward is the fallback. [Alt ID is the alias tool with the highest acceptance rate for event platforms.](/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-for-conference-signups-2026__faq)

**Can I still receive event reminders and conference materials through an alias?**

Yes. The alias forwards everything to your real inbox transparently. You receive confirmation emails, calendar invites, schedule updates, speaker slide downloads, and post-event recordings exactly as you would with your real address. The organizer and platform have no idea you used an alias.

**How long should I keep a conference alias active before deleting it?**

A practical window is four to six weeks after the event ends. By then you will have received any legitimate follow-ups: recordings, certificate links, feedback surveys. After that point, incoming mail is almost exclusively sponsor campaigns and re-marketing — safe to cut off by deleting the alias.

**[What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) to my conference registration if I delete the alias before the event?**

Your registration stays valid — it is stored in the conference platform's system linked to the alias address. Deleting the alias just stops new emails from forwarding to your real inbox. If you need to access your registration confirmation or QR code after deletion, check your real inbox's archive for the confirmation email you received at sign-up time. Best practice: delete the alias *after* the event, not before.

**Is an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-twitch-account-privacy-2026/) the same as a disposable/[temporary email](https://snipstack.io/temporary-email-and-password-free-2026/) address?**

No. A disposable address typically expires after minutes or hours and is not linked to your real inbox. An alias is persistent — it stays active until you choose to delete it, and it forwards all mail to your real address in real time. For conference use, you want an alias, not a throwaway: you need to receive registration confirmations, event updates, and day-of logistics reliably.
