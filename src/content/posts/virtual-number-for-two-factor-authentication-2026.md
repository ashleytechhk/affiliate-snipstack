Dating apps require phone verification. When you sign up for Tinder, Bumble, or Hinge, you hand over your real mobile number to confirm your identity. That number then lives in three places simultaneously: the app's database, your app account settings, and — if you ever share it — in the messages of anyone you talk to.

A motivated bad actor with your number can run it through reverse-lookup services and surface your name, home address, and linked accounts within minutes. ## Why Standard 2FA with Your Real Number Creates a False Sense of Security

Two-factor authentication is genuinely useful — it makes account takeover much harder when someone has your password. The problem is what happens when the factor itself is your real phone number.

SMS-based 2FA with a real number is vulnerable to SIM swapping. This is an attack where a bad actor contacts your mobile carrier, convinces them to transfer your number to a new SIM (often using personal details available from prior breaches), and then receives all your incoming texts — including authentication codes. It is not hypothetical: the FBI's 2022 Internet Crime Report documented a sharp increase in SIM swap complaints, with losses exceeding $72 million that year.

Beyond SIM swapping, there is the simpler problem of exposure surface. Every service you verify with your real number adds that number to another database. The more databases hold your real number, the higher the cumulative breach risk.
