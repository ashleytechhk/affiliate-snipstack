## How Email Masking Works

Every tool in this guide does the same thing at a mechanical level: it creates a forwarding address that sits between you and the website. When a site sends email to your masked address, the service forwards it to your real inbox. The site never learns your actual address.

The variables that separate these tools are:

- **Platform availability** — Apple-only vs. cross-platform
- **What gets masked** — email only vs. email plus identity
- **Alias cap** — limited free tier vs. unlimited
- **How mail is routed** — through the provider's servers or not

## Option 1: Apple Hide My Email

Apple Hide My Email is built into iCloud+ (which includes any paid iCloud storage plan). It generates random Apple-hosted addresses and forwards mail to your Apple ID inbox.

### Step 1 — Confirm Your iCloud+ Subscription

On iPhone or iPad: go to **Settings → [your name] → iCloud → iCloud+**. If you are on any paid tier (50 GB and above), Hide My Email is included.

### Step 2 — Use It at Sign-Up

On iPhone Safari: when a website asks for your email, tap the input field. If the site's form is standard HTML, Apple will offer **Hide My Email** as an autofill suggestion above the keyboard. Tap it to generate and fill an address automatically.

On Mac Safari: same flow — click an email field, look for the **Hide My Email** suggestion in the autofill dropdown.

### Step 3 — Manage Your Aliases

Go to **Settings → [your name] → iCloud → Hide My Email** to see all your active aliases, the app or website each is tied to, and the option to deactivate or delete any of them.

**Where it falls short:** Hide My Email only works inside Apple's ecosystem — Safari on iPhone, iPad, or Mac. If you use Chrome, Firefox, or any non-Apple device, the feature simply does not appear. It also only masks your email address; it does not generate an alternative name or other identity details for forms that ask for more than an address.

## Option 2: Firefox Relay

Firefox Relay is Mozilla's email masking service. It is free for up to five aliases, with a paid upgrade for unlimited.

### Step 1 — Create a Firefox Account

Go to relay.firefox.com and sign in with a Firefox account, or create one for free. No payment required for the basic tier.

### Step 2 — Install the Firefox Relay Extension

From the Firefox Add-ons store (addons.mozilla.org), search "Firefox Relay" and install the official extension. Sign in when prompted.

### Step 3 — Generate an Alias

Visit any sign-up page. Click the email field — Firefox Relay places a small icon inside the field. Click it to generate a relay address and fill it in automatically.

Alternatively, go to relay.firefox.com and click **Generate new mask** to create an alias you can copy and paste anywhere.

### Step 4 — Receive Mail in Your Real Inbox

Mail sent to your relay address is forwarded to the email you registered with Firefox. The relay address appears in the "To" header; your real address is not visible to the sender.

**Where it falls short:** The free plan caps you at five aliases. The extension is Firefox-specific — it does not work in Chrome, Edge, or Safari. Once you hit the five-alias limit, you need to delete an existing alias or upgrade to the paid plan (currently ~$1.99/month).

## Option 3: DuckDuckGo Email Protection

DuckDuckGo Email Protection gives you a `@duck.com` forwarding address that strips tracking pixels from incoming emails before delivering them to your real inbox.

### Step 1 — Get a Duck Address

Open the DuckDuckGo browser app on iOS or Android, or go to duckduckgo.com/email on desktop and join the waitlist. Once approved, you receive a personal address like `yourname@duck.com`.

### Step 2 — Use Private Duck Addresses at Sign-Up

Inside the DuckDuckGo browser, tap any email field on a sign-up form. The browser will offer to fill in a unique Private Duck Address — a randomly generated alias that forwards to your `@duck.com` address, which then forwards to your real inbox.

### Step 3 — Manage Addresses

In the DuckDuckGo app, go to **Settings → Email Protection** to see your forwarding addresses and deactivate any that start attracting spam.

**Where it falls short:** The full alias-generation flow works best inside the DuckDuckGo browser. In third-party browsers, you typically use your single `@duck.com` address rather than per-site aliases, which reduces the ability to isolate a compromised address to one service. All your mail routes through DuckDuckGo's servers for tracker removal — which is fine if you trust them, but adds a dependency that some users prefer to avoid.

## Troubleshooting

**The site is rejecting my alias address.**
Some sites block known alias domains. Free relay services (simplelogin.com, relay.firefox.com) appear on more blocklists than newer domains. Surfshark's `surfshark.id` domain clears most real-time validation checks. If you are seeing rejections on free tools, switching to Alt ID usually resolves it.

**Forwarded mail is going to spam.**
Add the forwarding domain to your safe senders list. In Gmail: open the misfiled message, click the three-dot menu, select **Filter messages like these**, then **Never send to spam**. In Outlook: go to **Settings → Mail → Junk email → Safe senders** and add the forwarding domain.

**I forgot which alias I used on a site.**
Check the "To:" header of any email from that service — it will show the alias address used, not your real one. Match it against your alias manager to find and control it.

**I hit the alias cap on the free plan.**
Firefox Relay's free tier caps at five aliases. If you need more without upgrading, consider switching to [Surfshark Alt ID](/blog/altid?sub=hide-email-address-when-signing-up-2026__mid), which does not publish a hard per-account alias limit for standard subscribers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is it legal to use a masked email address when signing up?**
Yes. Choosing which email address to share with a service is not illegal. Some services prohibit disposable addresses in their terms of service, but using an alias is not a legal violation. [Learn more about Alt ID here](/blog/altid?sub=hide-email-address-when-signing-up-2026__faq).

**Will masked aliases stop all spam?**
Masking your address prevents new spam from reaching your real inbox through that alias — you can kill any alias that goes bad. It does not retroactively remove your address from lists where it already exists. For addresses already in circulation, use a fresh alias for every new sign-up going forward and treat your real address as private.

**Can I reply from a masked alias?**
It depends on the service. Surfshark Alt ID and Firefox Relay are forwarding-only — replies go from your real address unless you configure your email client otherwise. Apple Hide My Email allows sending from aliases via Apple Mail on iOS and Mac.

**What happens to forwarded mail if I cancel my Surfshark subscription?**
If you cancel, your Alt ID aliases stop working and mail sent to them will no longer be forwarded. Before cancelling, update any important accounts to your real address or an alternative alias service.

**Which tool works on Android?**
Apple Hide My Email does not work on Android. Firefox Relay works in Firefox for Android. DuckDuckGo Email Protection works in the DuckDuckGo Android browser. [Surfshark Alt ID](/blog/altid?sub=hide-email-address-when-signing-up-2026__faq) works in Chrome and Firefox on Android — the same extension, no platform restriction.

**Does Surfshark Alt ID work without the VPN being on?**
Yes. The Alt ID alias and forwarding function works independently of the VPN. You do not need to have the VPN connection active to generate aliases or receive forwarded mail.
