## What is a VPN, Exactly?

A **VPN (Virtual Private Network)** is software that creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and the internet. When you connect through a VPN server in a different country, two things happen:

1. **Your traffic is encrypted** — no one on the same network can read what you are doing.
2. **Your apparent location changes** — websites and streaming services see the VPN server's location, not yours.

Think of it like mailing a letter inside a sealed diplomatic pouch. The postal workers handling it know it came from you and is going to a destination, but they cannot read the contents — and the return address shows the embassy, not your truck stop.

For truck drivers, that second point is where the real subscription savings come in.

## Why Cab WiFi Creates a Real Problem

Most cab WiFi setups — whether a carrier-provided hotspot, a truck stop network, or a lot lizard signal booster — share a few uncomfortable characteristics:

- **Shared networks**: Dozens or hundreds of devices connect to the same access point. Anyone with basic tools can intercept unencrypted traffic.
- **No WPA3 encryption**: Many older truck stop networks still run outdated security protocols.
- **DNS leaks**: Your browsing history can be visible to the network operator even if individual pages are HTTPS-encrypted.

This is not hypothetical. Security researchers routinely demonstrate that coffee shop and transport-hub WiFi networks are trivially easy to attack. A cab parked at a busy distribution center or truck stop is the same environment at scale.

A VPN encrypts everything before it leaves your device, so even if someone is sniffing the network, they see nothing useful.
