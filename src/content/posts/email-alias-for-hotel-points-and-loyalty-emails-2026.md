## SimpleLogin: The Free Alternative

SimpleLogin (now owned by Proton) is a strong open-source option. The free tier gives you ten aliases and unlimited forwarding on those ten. For someone who only needs hotel-specific aliasing and nothing else, ten may be enough.

Where it falls short for frequent travelers:

- **No bundled VPN.** If you are managing loyalty accounts on hotel Wi-Fi, you are doing so without traffic encryption unless you pay for a separate VPN.
- **No alternate identity generation.** You get a forwarding address, not a fake name or DOB. Some programs require a legal-sounding name to match a credit card — SimpleLogin does not assist with that.
- **Free tier cap.** Ten aliases disappear quickly when you count hotel chains, airline programs, rental car clubs, cruise lines, and the occasional casino rewards program.

SimpleLogin paid ($4/month) removes the alias cap and is a credible alternative if you already have a VPN you trust and want an open-source forwarder. ## Apple Hide My Email: Excellent but Ecosystem-Locked

If you use an iPhone and pay for iCloud+ (starting at $0.99/month), Hide My Email is already available to you. Apple generates random alias addresses (`randomstring@privaterelay.appleid.com`) and forwards to your real iCloud or Apple ID email.

It works well. Aliased mail is clearly labeled in Mail, and you can disable any alias in Settings without touching any other account.

The catch: it only works inside the Apple ecosystem. If you use Gmail or Outlook as your primary inbox, Hide My Email is not an option. It also does not include a VPN or breach monitoring. For Apple-only users with modest alias needs, it is a tidy choice. For cross-platform travelers or those who want broader protection, it is too narrow.

## Gmail + Alias: Why It Does Not Actually Work

Many guides still recommend using Gmail's `+tag` feature as a free alias. The advice is outdated for privacy purposes. When you use `yourname+hilton@gmail.com`, Gmail delivers the mail to your normal inbox — which is fine for routing and filtering. But the loyalty program receives and stores your actual Gmail address. The `+tag` is visible to them and frequently stripped by their systems anyway.

You get inbox organization. You do not get privacy, identity separation, or the ability to disable forwarding if the program sells your data. For travelers who genuinely want to isolate their loyalty email identity, Gmail tags are not a substitute for a real alias service.

## FAQ

### Can I still earn points if I sign up with an alias?

Yes. The alias forwards all program mail — including points credit confirmations and award certificates — to your real inbox. The loyalty program has no way to tell the difference between an alias and a primary address. Your account functions exactly the same.

### [What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) if I disable an alias after signing up?

Forwarding stops immediately. You will not receive any mail sent to that alias until you re-enable it. If you need to recover your account (e.g., forgot password), you will need to either re-enable the alias or update your account to a new address before disabling the old one.

### Will using an alias cause problems at hotel check-in?

No. Your booking confirmation arrives in your real inbox via forwarding. You can print or show that confirmation as normal. The front desk sees your booking reference number, not your email address.

### Is it legal to use a different name when signing up for loyalty programs?

Loyalty program terms vary. Using an alias email address is universally accepted. Using an alternate name that does not match your ID is more nuanced — some elite tier programs require name-matching for award redemptions, particularly for flights. Use your real name on programs that tie awards to ID documents, and use Alt ID for the email field only.
