## What Is a Deepfake Video Scam?

A deepfake is a video where artificial intelligence has replaced a real person's face or voice with someone else's — or generated a convincing fake from scratch. The technology was once expensive and technically demanding. In 2026 it is freely available, and scammers are using it at scale.

The most common delivery method is email. A message arrives claiming to be from a news outlet, a financial authority, or a person you know. Inside is a video — hosted on a plausible-looking website — showing someone apparently endorsing an investment, warning you about a frozen account, or urgently asking for help.

**Why email is the attack surface:** Most deepfake video scams do not ask you to evaluate a video carefully. They ask you to act fast. The video is a trust-building mechanism. The real damage happens when you click a follow-on link, fill in a form, or transfer money. That means the scam can be disrupted at the email stage — before the video is ever a factor.

## Method 1: Visual Tells — Spotting the Fake Yourself

If you have already opened a video and something feels wrong, these are the most reliable things to look for:

**Unnatural blinking.** Early deepfakes produced faces that almost never blinked. Modern ones overcorrect and blink at irregular intervals. Watch for a rhythm that feels slightly mechanical.

**Skin texture and hairline edges.** AI-generated faces often look waxy or overly smooth, particularly around the hairline and ear edges. The boundary between face and hair can appear to shimmer or blur when the subject moves.

**Lip sync that is slightly off.** The most common deepfake failure: the mouth movements do not quite match the audio. Look for delays of a fraction of a second, or consonant sounds (especially "p," "b," "m") where the lips do not close fully.

**Lighting inconsistency.** A pasted-in face inherits the lighting from the source footage, not the background. Shadows that do not match the room, or reflections in the eyes that do not fit the scene, are reliable tells.

**Unnatural neck and shoulder movement.** Deepfakes focus on the face. The rest of the body is often untouched original footage — and the seam between a faked face and a real neck can produce subtle warping when the subject turns.

**The limitation:** These tells require you to watch the video with sceptical attention. Scammers know this, and they deliberately create urgency so you focus on the message, not the messenger. If someone is telling you an account is frozen or a relative is in trouble, your brain prioritises the content — not whether the lip sync is slightly off.

## Method 2: Reverse Video Search

If the deepfake uses footage of a real public figure — a politician, a CEO, a news presenter — you can sometimes catch it with a reverse image or video search.

**How it works:** Take a screenshot of a frame from the video. Upload it to Google Images or TinEye and search for visually similar results. If the original, unedited footage exists online, it will often surface — and comparing the two makes the manipulation obvious.

**What it does well:** It is free, it requires no specialist tool, and it often exposes scams that use stolen footage of real people as the base layer.

**Where it falls short:** Entirely generated deepfakes — where no real source footage exists — will return no matches. The process also requires you to already be watching the video, which means a link has already been clicked and a potentially malicious website has already loaded in your browser. That is a step most people should not take with an untrusted link.

**Best for:** Verifying a video you have encountered on a website or social media, rather than one delivered via email link.

## Method 3: Dedicated Deepfake Detection Tools

Several specialised tools now exist to analyse video for AI manipulation. The most widely used are Deepware Scanner and Sensity AI.

**Deepware Scanner** is a free tool where you submit a video URL or upload a file. It returns a probability score — the higher the score, the more likely the video has been AI-manipulated. Results typically take one to three minutes.

**Sensity AI** offers more granular analysis, including frame-by-frame breakdowns of where manipulation is detected. It is primarily aimed at enterprise and media verification use cases and requires registration.

**What these tools do well:** They provide a systematic, algorithmic verdict on the video itself — not just a human reading of visual tells. For journalists, investigators, or anyone who needs to confirm a specific video is fake before acting on it, this is the right approach.

**Where they fall short:** Both require the video to be available for submission, which again means you have already opened something. Neither tool monitors your inbox or checks whether the email that delivered the link is legitimate. They are forensic tools — excellent for verification after the fact, less useful for prevention at the point of delivery.

**Cost note:** Deepware's core scanning is free. Sensity operates on a subscription model aimed at enterprise users.

## How to Respond When You Suspect a Deepfake Scam Email

**Step 1: Do not click the video link.** This is the single most important action. The link may lead to a malicious site even before any video plays.

**Step 2: Copy the link and check it.** Right-click the link in the email and choose "Copy link address." Open a new tab, go to Surfshark's Email Scam Checker, paste the link in, and run the scan.

**Step 3: Check the sender address carefully.** Scammers use addresses like `noreply@bbc-news-verify.com` rather than an actual BBC domain. The display name can say anything — the email address itself is harder to fake.

**Step 4: If the video claims someone you know is in trouble, call them directly.** Deepfake scams often impersonate family members or colleagues in distress. Hang up, call back on a number you already have, and verify before doing anything else.

**Step 5: Report and delete.** Use your email client's "Report phishing" function, then delete the email. Do not forward it to anyone else.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do deepfake video scams reach people by email?

Scammers send mass emails with a link to a hosted video — often on a fake news or financial site designed to look legitimate. The email creates urgency: a frozen account, a breaking news item, an investment opportunity closing today. The video is used to add credibility before a follow-on link asks for payment details or login credentials.

### Can I spot a deepfake just by watching it?

Sometimes, particularly with lower-quality fakes. Watch for irregular blinking, skin that looks too smooth, lip sync that is slightly delayed, and lighting that does not match between the face and the background. But skilled deepfakes are increasingly difficult to detect by eye alone, especially when you are distracted by an urgent message. Checking the email link before it loads is more reliable than trying to evaluate the video afterwards.

### Is it safe to paste a suspicious link into a checking tool?

Yes. Pasting a link address into a web form does not cause your device to visit the suspicious page. You are submitting text — the link itself — not clicking through to the destination. There is no security risk in checking a link this way. [Run the check here.](/blog/scamcheck?sub=deepfake-video-scam-how-to-detect-2026__faq)

### What if I already clicked the link and watched the video?

Close the tab and do not enter any information on any page that opened. If you entered a password, change it immediately from a separate, trusted device. If you made any financial transfer, contact your bank directly using the number on the back of your card — not any number shown on the site you visited. Report the incident to your national cybercrime reporting service.

### Are older adults more at risk from deepfake scams?

Scammers target everyone, but people who are less familiar with how deepfakes work are more likely to treat a convincing video as reliable evidence. The most effective protection is not learning to spot fakes visually — it is building the habit of checking the email link before clicking it, regardless of how convincing the message seems.

### Do deepfake detectors work on audio-only fakes (voice cloning)?

Deepware and Sensity are primarily video-focused. Voice clone detection is a separate category — tools like Pindrop and Resemble Detect are designed for audio analysis. For email-delivered scams using voice clips or video, checking the sender and link at the email stage remains the most practical first step for everyday users.
