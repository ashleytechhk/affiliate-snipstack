## Why Twitch Creators Specifically Need Email Aliases

Most privacy guides are written for generic internet users. Creators face a more aggressive threat model.

When you grow a Twitch channel past even a few hundred followers, you become a target for:

- **Sponsor spam**: Agencies scrape creator contact pages and Twitch "About" panels for emails. Once you appear on one outreach list, you end up on dozens.
- **Data broker profiles**: Third-party analytics sites aggregate creator data — including contact information pulled from social profiles — and sell it. Your real email is often part of that package.
- **Platform leaks**: Services you used to manage your stream (clip tools, alert overlays, merch platforms) have varying security standards. A breach at one of them can expose your real email to credential-stuffing attacks.
- **Viewer escalations**: Occasionally a dedicated viewer finds a creator's personal email and the contact becomes unwanted. An alias creates a clean circuit breaker.

An alias address means that when any of these scenarios plays out, you disable one alias and create a new one. Your real inbox stays untouched.

### Setup for Twitch creators

The practical workflow looks like this:

1. Create one alias for your Twitch account registration itself.
2. Create a second alias specifically for inbound brand contact — this goes on your media kit and channel "About" panel.
3. Create a third alias for tool subscriptions (OBS plugins, clip services, stream management dashboards).

Separating aliases by purpose means you instantly know which surface got leaked when spam starts arriving. You disable the compromised alias without touching the others.

Alt-ID is not a full email provider. It cannot send outbound mail using the alias as the From address without some configuration workarounds. If you need to initiate sponsor conversations from a masked identity, you would need to combine it with a separate email hosting service.

It also does not offer a custom domain — if you want aliases at `@yourbrand.com`, SimpleLogin's paid tier is a better fit.

## SimpleLogin: The Free Alternative

SimpleLogin is an open-source email alias service now owned by Proton. The free tier gives you 15 aliases and lets you reply from those aliases — useful if you are actively corresponding with sponsors from a masked address.

The limit of 15 aliases is manageable for smaller creators, but busy streamers who sign up to a lot of services will hit that ceiling quickly. The paid plan ($30/year) removes the limit and adds custom domain support.

**What SimpleLogin does not have:** any form of personal information masking. It protects your inbox address only. Your name and contact details are still floating around creator databases.

For pure inbox protection on a zero budget, SimpleLogin is a solid option. For a broader privacy posture, it falls short compared to Alt-ID.

## Apple Hide My Email: Good, But Walled

If you are on an iPhone or Mac and already pay for iCloud+, Hide My Email is built in and genuinely convenient. Apple generates a random alias whenever you tap "Sign in with Apple" or use Safari's autofill on a compatible form.

The friction: it only works smoothly within the Apple ecosystem. Signing up for a Twitch third-party tool on a Windows PC, an Android phone, or a browser other than Safari is clunkier. Many creator tools do not support "Sign in with Apple" at all.

For creators who live entirely in Apple's world, it is a legitimate option. For everyone else, it is a nice bonus rather than a primary solution.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use an email alias to create my original Twitch account?**

Yes. You can register a Twitch account using a forwarding alias address. Any verification emails will arrive at your real inbox via forwarding. Just make sure the alias service you use does not auto-expire aliases — you will need it to stay active for password resets and security notifications. Alt-ID aliases persist until you manually delete them, which makes them reliable for account registration. **Does using an alias actually stop spam or just redirect it?**

Aliases stop spam at the point of exposure, not at the inbox. When a service you signed up for sells its user list to marketers, the exposed address is the alias — not your real email. You delete the alias, create a new one, and the spam chain is broken. If you never delete compromised aliases, spam still reaches your inbox.

**What happens to my Twitch account if I delete the alias I registered with?**

Before deleting an alias tied to an active account, update the email on that account to a new alias or your real address. Most alias services let you do this in account settings. Deleting an alias that is still set as your Twitch account email would lock you out of password resets.

**Is Alt-ID available outside the UK, Japan, and Canada?**

Yes. Alt-ID works regardless of your location. It is a software feature bundled with Surfshark subscriptions and is not restricted to specific regions. You manage aliases through the Surfshark dashboard from any country.

**Does an email alias affect two-factor authentication (2FA) codes?**

No. 2FA codes sent via email go through the alias forwarding chain to your real inbox in the same way any other email does. SMS-based 2FA is unaffected because it uses your phone number, not your email. Authenticator app-based 2FA (like Google Authenticator or Authy) is also unaffected.
