## What "Anonymous Email" Actually Means (and What It Does Not)

Before diving into steps, let us be precise. "Anonymous email" covers two different intentions:

**Alias-based privacy** — You use a fake or masked address when signing up for services. Emails forwarded to your real inbox, but senders never see your actual address. This is what most people actually need.

**Zero-identity communication** — You want to send a message with no connection to your real identity at all. This is harder, requires more steps, and is not necessary for the spam-protection use case.

This guide focuses on the first category — alias-based privacy — because that is what solves the 50-emails-a-day problem. If you also want to go deeper into zero-identity sending, the Troubleshooting section covers that.

## Troubleshooting

**The forwarded email is going to spam in my real inbox.**
Add your Alt ID forwarding domain to your real email's allowlist/safe senders list. In Gmail: Settings > Filters > Create a new filter > From: the Alt ID forwarding domain > Never send to spam.

**A service is not accepting the alias address.**
Some services blocklist free or alias email domains. If rejected, try generating a new alias with a different pattern, or for services you genuinely trust, use your real address and accept the tradeoff.

**I want to send email with no identity at all (not just an alias).**
This is possible but more involved: use ProtonMail or Tutanota over the Tor network, creating an account without any real identity attached. Note that even these approaches have limits — no email system offers 100% anonymity, and the recipient's email server logs metadata regardless of what you do on your end.

**My alias stopped forwarding.**
Check that the alias is toggled Active in Alt ID. If the issue persists, log out and back into your Surfshark account, or contact Surfshark support.

**Can I use Alt ID on mobile?**
Yes. The Surfshark app on iOS and Android includes Alt ID under the same account. Create aliases from mobile the same way.
