## What "Network Protection" Actually Means — and Why Antivirus Alone Is Not Enough

Most people think of antivirus as the thing that stops viruses. That is true — but viruses are only one way criminals get into your life.

Your home network is the invisible pathway every device in your house uses to reach the internet. Your laptop, your phone, your tablet, and even your smart TV all share it. When that pathway is unguarded, anyone who can reach it — a hacker parked outside your home, an attacker on your street's shared internet, or a criminal who has already broken into a nearby network — can potentially intercept what you send and receive.

- You type in your online banking password. An attacker on the same network sees it before it reaches the bank.
- You receive an email and click what looks like a safe link. Because your network is unprotected, the connection is silently redirected to a fake website.
- Your smart thermostat or Wi-Fi camera — devices that have no antivirus of their own — are used as a back door into everything else on your network.

## The Threats That Live on Your Network (Not Just Your Computer)

Understanding the specific risks helps you see why this matters. You do not need to become a cybersecurity expert. You just need to know what you are up against.

### Man-in-the-Middle Attacks

This is the most common network-level threat for home users. A criminal positions themselves between you and the website you are visiting. Every piece of information you send — passwords, credit card numbers, messages — passes through them before reaching its destination. From your screen, everything looks completely normal.

### Rogue Wi-Fi Hotspots

When you are away from home — at a coffee shop, a doctor's waiting room, or a library — criminals sometimes set up fake Wi-Fi networks with names like "CafeGuest" or "Hospital_WiFi." When you connect, they see everything. Without network protection, your device has no way to warn you.

### Network Scanning by Malware

Some malware does not attack your computer directly. It quietly scans your home network to find other devices — a printer, a smart speaker, a security camera — that have weaker defenses. It uses those devices as stepping stones to reach your computer or to steal information you send across the network.

## Why Seniors Are Targeted More Than Anyone Else

This is not a comfortable fact, but it is an important one. Criminals specifically target older adults because the payoff is higher and the defenses are often weaker.

Adults over 60 are more likely to have retirement savings, fixed income payments arriving on a schedule, and established credit histories. That makes them attractive targets. At the same time, many older devices — laptops five, seven, or ten years old — run software that no longer receives security updates. The hardware still works, but the defenses have gone dark.

There is also the trust factor. Older adults were raised in an era when institutions were reliable. A letter from the bank meant something. An official-looking email or a realistic-sounding phone call triggers the same instinct. Criminals know this and design their scams accordingly.

Network attacks exploit one more vulnerability: the proliferation of smart devices. In 2026, a typical home might have a smart TV, a Wi-Fi doorbell, a wireless printer, a tablet, and two or three phones — all sharing one network. Most of those devices have no antivirus protection. Without network-level security, every one of them is a potential entry point.
