## Why Old Laptops Struggle With Ads

Modern web pages are not just text and images. A typical news homepage fires 40–80 separate ad-network requests before your content even appears. Each request:

- Opens a new connection to an ad server
- Downloads JavaScript to render the ad
- Runs that script on your CPU
- Fetches tracking pixels from a third-party domain

On a machine from 2015 or 2016 with a dual-core CPU and limited RAM, this cascade buries your actual browsing under a mountain of invisible work. The result: tabs take 8–12 seconds to load, the fan spins up, and the battery drains faster than it should.

The fix sounds obvious — install an [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-that-blocks-cookie-consent-popups-2026/) — but the wrong [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-for-android-without-root-access-2026/) compounds the problem. A bulky extension with a 200,000-rule filter list loads those rules into memory every time you open Chrome or Firefox. You trade one memory hog for another.

### The Two Types of [Ad Blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-for-chromecast-streaming-device-2026/)s

Understanding the difference between these two approaches is the key to picking the right one for old hardware:

**Browser extensions** ([uBlock Origin](https://snipstack.io/ublock-origin-not-working-chrome-alternatives-2026/), [AdBlock Plus](https://snipstack.io/adblock-plus-for-android-2026/)) work inside the browser. They intercept requests after the browser has already started processing the page. They are powerful and customizable, but they consume browser memory and can conflict with other extensions.

**Network-level blockers** work before the browser is involved at all. They operate at the operating system or VPN layer, rejecting connections to known ad and tracker domains before any data is downloaded. Because the blocking happens outside the browser, the overhead on your laptop is minimal.

For an old laptop, network-level blocking is almost always the better choice.

## How Network-Level Ad Blocking Works

![How CleanWeb blocks ads at the network level](/og/best-lightweight-ad-blocker-for-old-laptop-2026-inline-1.png)

Think of your internet connection as a series of doors. When you visit a webpage, your browser knocks on hundreds of doors at once — the main site, ad networks, analytics services, social widgets, and tracking pixels. A browser extension stands inside your browser and slams individual doors shut after they are already open. A network-level blocker stands at the building entrance and refuses entry entirely.

Here is how Surfshark's CleanWeb puts this into practice:

1. **You connect to Surfshark VPN.** CleanWeb is a built-in feature — no separate download.
2. **DNS filtering kicks in.** When your device tries to resolve a domain (e.g., `ads.doubleclick.net`), CleanWeb's DNS returns nothing, so the connection never opens.
3. **The ad data is never downloaded.** Your browser renders the page faster because there is less to fetch.
4. **Your CPU stays idle** where it would otherwise be running ad scripts.

The practical result: on older hardware, pages can load 30–50% faster simply because the browser is processing fewer resources. Battery life improves for the same reason — fewer background connections, less radio activity.
