## Why Spam Keeps Coming No Matter What You Do

Before the comparison, it helps to understand why the volume never drops. Your address has been through at least three of the following pipelines:

**Data breaches.** A retailer, forum, or app you signed up for years ago was breached. Your address landed on a dark-web dump, got purchased by spam operations, and has been resold repeatedly. The original site may not even exist anymore.

**List buying.** "Free" services often monetise by selling user lists. A single form fill can end up on a dozen third-party lists within weeks.

**Scraping.** If your address has ever appeared in a public forum, a LinkedIn profile, a website footer, or a GitHub commit, automated bots have harvested it and added it to commercial databases.

**Address validation.** Every time you click an unsubscribe link or open a spam email, you confirm the address is active. That confirmation makes it more valuable to the next buyer.

The common thread: **your real address is permanent and unrevokable.** Once it's out, it's out. The only structural fix is to stop handing out your real address — and to create addresses that you can revoke on demand.

## Method 1: Spam Filters

Spam filters are the passive defence built into every email provider. Gmail, Outlook, Apple Mail, and every major service use machine learning to classify incoming mail and divert probable spam to a separate folder.

### What they do well

- Zero effort on your part after initial setup
- Catch a high percentage of bulk spam from known sending patterns
- Keep your main inbox reasonably clean without any habit change

### Where they fail

- They never stop the spam arriving at your address — they just sort it
- Sophisticated phishing and social engineering often pass the filter
- Legitimate mail occasionally gets caught (false positives)
- The spam folder still exists and still grows; it's not solved, it's hidden
- When you switch email providers, you lose your filter training

### Spam protection verdict

Filters are noise reduction, not protection. Your address is still out there. The volume at the server level is unchanged. Every spammer who has your address still has it.

**Spam stopped at the source: No.**

## Method 2: Unsubscribing

CAN-SPAM (US), GDPR (EU), and equivalent laws in other regions require commercial senders to honour unsubscribe requests. The standard advice is: click unsubscribe, wait 10 days, done.

### What it does well

- Legally compliant senders (reputable retailers, newsletter platforms) do honour requests
- Reduces email from brands you actually signed up for but no longer want
- Works well inside a single platform ecosystem (e.g., unsubscribing from all Mailchimp senders)

### Where it fails

- **It confirms your address is live.** Spam operations use unsubscribe clicks as address verification. You've just told them your address is worth more.
- **It only affects that sender's list.** The twenty other lists your address was sold to are unaffected.
- **Offshore spam operations ignore it entirely.** There's no enforcement mechanism for senders outside your legal jurisdiction.
- **10-business-day windows mean ongoing exposure** while you wait for the opt-out to process.

### Spam protection verdict

Unsubscribing works for the responsible, compliant 20% of your unwanted email. For the other 80%, it either has no effect or actively makes things worse.

**Spam stopped at the source: No.**

## Method 3: Gmail + Tagging (and Similar Provider Tricks)

Gmail lets you add a `+tag` to your address — so `yourname+shopping@gmail.com` delivers to `yourname@gmail.com`. Outlook and iCloud have their own variants. The idea is to create per-service addresses so you can filter on the tag.

### What it does well

- No extra tool required — works with your existing account
- Lets you create inbox filter rules based on the tag
- Free and instant

### Where it fails

- **Marketers strip the + tag.** It's so widely known that most email marketing platforms automatically remove everything after `+` before storing or reselling your address. Your real address (`yourname@gmail.com`) gets through anyway.
- **It exposes your real username.** `yourname+shopping@gmail.com` makes your base address (`yourname@gmail.com`) trivially obvious to anyone who receives it.
- **Many sites reject it at sign-up.** Some forms validate the format and refuse addresses containing `+`.
- **Deleting a tag doesn't stop spam.** You can't delete a Gmail+ alias — you can only create a filter. The address still works; you're just sorting, not revoking.

### Spam protection verdict

A partial tool at best, and increasingly ineffective as spam operations have learned to defeat it.

**Spam stopped at the source: Rarely.**

## Method 4: Disposable / Temporary Email Services

Services like Guerrilla Mail and 10 Minute Mail give you a random address with no account required. You check mail in the browser tab, then it expires.

### What they do well

- Perfect for single-use signups you'll never revisit: downloading a PDF, redeeming a promo code, trying a service for five minutes
- No setup, no account, no commitment
- Your real address is never shared

### Where they fail

- **They expire.** When you need a password reset from a site that has one of these addresses, the inbox is gone.
- **No forwarding.** You have to actively check a separate tab. Any email you don't catch in time is lost.
- **Blocked by many sites.** Services that filter known disposable domains (which is a growing list) reject these addresses at sign-up.
- **Not suitable for ongoing email.** Shopping receipts, account notifications, newsletters you actually want — none of these work with an address that disappears.

### Spam protection verdict

Strong for the specific case of one-time signups you'll never need again. Useless for anything that requires ongoing email receipt.

**Spam stopped at the source: Yes (for that one signup only).**

## FAQ

**Does using an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-service-that-works-with-apple-mail-2026/) actually stop spam, or just move it?**

A properly deployed alias stops spam at the source for that alias. When you delete a compromised alias, the spam has nowhere to go — it bounces, not forwards. Your real inbox never sees it. This is categorically different from filters, which move spam to a folder but leave your address exposed and the volume unchanged at the server level.

**Can I use [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-legal-document-service-signup-2026/)es alongside my existing Gmail account?**

Yes. Aliases forward to whatever inbox you designate — Gmail, Outlook, Apple Mail, or any other provider. You manage everything from your existing inbox. The alias layer sits in front of it invisibly. You don't need to change your email provider.

**What happens to emails I'm already receiving on my real address?**

Aliases stop *future* exposure. Spam already arriving on your compromised real address won't be affected by a new alias system. For that existing spam, filtering or migrating to a fresh primary address are the options. Going forward, every new signup uses an alias — so the real address gradually stops accumulating new sources.

**Will services reject Alt-ID alias addresses?**

Most services accept them without issue. Some high-security services — banks, government portals, certain healthcare platforms — block known alias domains as a policy. For those services specifically, use your real address. Reserve aliases for the vast majority of signups where that restriction doesn't apply.

**Is one alias per service better than one per category?**

Per-service gives you forensic precision: when spam arrives, you know the exact service that leaked your data, not just the category. Per-category is simpler to manage. The recommended approach is to start with per-category aliases, then go per-service for any service you consider high-risk (major retailers, apps with your payment details, platforms with a breach history).

**Does replying to an email expose my real address?**

When you receive a forwarded email and reply using your normal mail client, your real address is used unless you configure otherwise. Alt-ID supports reply masking in supported configurations — replies go out from the alias, not your real address. Check your Surfshark setup to confirm this is enabled if reply privacy matters for a specific use case.
