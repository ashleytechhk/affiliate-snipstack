## What Are Switzerland's Data Protection Laws?

Switzerland is not a member of the European Union, but it has long maintained privacy standards that parallel — and in some areas exceed — EU requirements. The revised Federal Act on Data Protection, which replaced Switzerland's original 1992 data protection law, is the framework that matters most for VPN users today.

Here are the key points in plain language:

**The revFADP took effect 1 September 2023.** It modernised Swiss privacy law to align with GDPR principles, introducing stricter rules on data processing, new individual rights (access, correction, deletion), and enhanced obligations for organisations handling personal data.

**Switzerland is not in the EU, but it has EU adequacy status.** This means the EU has formally recognised Switzerland as providing an adequate level of data protection. Swiss-based companies can receive data transfers from EU countries without additional legal mechanisms — a recognition that carries significant weight.

**No mandatory VPN activity logging.** Unlike some jurisdictions that legally require internet service providers and VPN operators to retain user connection logs, Switzerland does not impose this obligation. A VPN provider based in or operating under Swiss law can operate a genuine no-logs policy without violating local regulations.

**Outside the 14 Eyes intelligence alliance.** The "Eyes" agreements refer to intelligence-sharing arrangements between Western governments. Switzerland is not a signatory. While this doesn't make Swiss-served connections immune from all scrutiny, it removes one specific layer of mass surveillance exposure that affects VPN providers based in, say, the US, UK, or Australia.

## How a VPN Works With Swiss Data Protection Principles

Understanding the mechanism helps you make better decisions about how and why to use one.

When you connect to a VPN:

1. Your device creates an encrypted tunnel to a VPN server in the location you choose.
2. All your internet traffic travels through that tunnel — encrypted, so your ISP sees only that you're connected to a VPN, not what you're doing.
3. Websites and streaming services see the VPN server's IP address, not your real one. They identify your apparent location as wherever that server sits.
4. When you disconnect, the session ends. A no-logs provider retains no record of what sites you visited or what you streamed.

Under the revFADP framework, a VPN provider with a genuine no-logs policy and Swiss-compliant data handling practices has no connection activity to hand over — even if asked. The law doesn't require them to collect it, so they don't.

This is meaningfully different from a VPN provider based in a jurisdiction that legally requires data retention. The no-logs promise means something different when the law supports it versus when the law works against it.
