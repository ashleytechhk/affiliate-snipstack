## Why a Grandparent's Tablet Is Such an Easy Target

Most tablets given to older relatives are set up once — by a son, daughter, or grandchild — and then used for years with almost no oversight. That is exactly the environment scammers look for.

**They are rarely updated.** Software updates patch security holes. Someone who isn't confident with technology often taps "remind me later" every time an update prompt appears, sometimes for months.

**They come with no built-in "scam sense."** Younger tablet users have usually seen dozens of phishing attempts and know to be suspicious. Someone who got their first tablet at 68 has not built up that instinct yet — and criminals know it.

**They are used for exactly the things scammers want.** Photos of grandchildren, banking apps, email, Facebook — a grandparent's tablet often holds more sensitive personal and financial information than people realize.

**They are trusted without question.** If a warning appears on the screen, a grandparent's first instinct is usually to believe it and follow the instructions — not to question whether it is real.

## How the Scam Actually Works

![How a tech support pop-up scam targets a grandparent's tablet](/og/best-antivirus-for-grandparent-tablet-in-2026-inline-1.png)

Understanding the mechanics makes the danger much clearer. Here is the pattern reported over and over again:

### Step 1 — A Fake Warning Appears

While browsing a normal website, a full-screen pop-up appears claiming the device is infected. It often uses official-looking logos and even plays a loud alert sound to create panic.

### Step 2 — A [Phone Number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/) Is Offered as the "Fix"

The pop-up lists a phone number to call for "immediate support." This is not a real support line — it connects directly to the scammer.

### Step 3 — Remote Access Is Requested

The person on the phone asks the grandparent to install a remote-access app "so I can see what's wrong." Once installed, the scammer can see and control the entire tablet.

### Step 4 — Money or Data Is Taken

From there, the scammer may ask the grandparent to log into a banking app "to confirm the fix worked," or may quietly install spyware to collect passwords over time.

The entire process can take under thirty minutes, from the first pop-up to the money leaving the account. This is why relying on "just be careful" is not enough — the warning itself is designed to look completely legitimate.
