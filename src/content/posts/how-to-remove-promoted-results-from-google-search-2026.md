## What Are "Promoted Results" on Google?

Promoted results — officially labelled "Sponsored" since Google's 2022 redesign — are paid advertisements that appear at the top and sometimes the bottom of search engine results pages (SERPs). Advertisers bid on keywords through Google Ads, and when your search matches a targeted keyword, those ads surface first.

On desktop, you might notice them easily because you have more screen real estate. On mobile, a single sponsored result can occupy your entire visible area before you scroll. Google often shows three to four ads above the first organic result on mobile screens, which means you might scroll through 400–600 pixels of ads just to reach unpaid content.

**Why does mobile get hit harder?**

- Smaller screens mean ads take up a higher percentage of visible space
- Mobile click-through rates for ads tend to be lower, so advertisers bid more aggressively to compensate
- Google's mobile layout is optimised for engagement, and ads drive revenue

This is not a bug. It is by design. Which means the fix has to come from your end.

## Can You Turn Off Promoted Results Inside Google?

Short answer: no. Google does not offer a native toggle to disable sponsored results. You can be signed into a Google account, turn off personalised ads in your account settings, and reduce *how targeted* the ads are — but the ad slots themselves will still fill with something. Non-personalised ads are still ads.

Some users try switching to alternative search engines like DuckDuckGo or Brave Search, which show fewer or no ads. That works, but it means giving up Google's search quality, which remains best-in-class for many query types.

The more practical middle ground: keep using Google, but block the ad delivery at the network level.

## How Network-Level Ad Blocking Works on Mobile

A network-level [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-for-slow-internet-connections-2026/) sits between your device and the internet. When your phone requests a resource — say, an ad-serving script from `googleadservices.com` — the blocker checks that request against a blocklist. If it matches a known ad domain, the request is dropped. The ad never loads. The slot either disappears entirely or collapses, leaving only organic results.
