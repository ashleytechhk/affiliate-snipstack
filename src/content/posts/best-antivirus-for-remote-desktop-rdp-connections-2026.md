## What Is RDP and Why Should You Care?

Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) is a built-in Windows feature that lets one computer control another over the internet. It is incredibly useful — your grandchild can help fix your computer from across the country, or you can access your home PC while traveling. Many home health aides, accountants, and tech support workers use it regularly.

The problem is that RDP was designed for convenience, not security. When it is left switched on and exposed to the internet without proper protection, it becomes a door that criminals actively try to break down.

### How Hackers Exploit RDP

Hackers use automated tools that scan billions of internet addresses every day looking for open RDP ports (usually port 3389). When they find one, they try thousands of common passwords automatically — a method called a "brute force attack." If your password is something like "Password1" or your name plus your birth year, they will crack it within minutes.

Once inside, they can:

- Install ransomware that locks all your files until you pay
- Steal saved passwords, bank account details, and personal photos
- Use your computer to attack others (making you look like the criminal)
- Watch everything you do in real time

This is not a scare story. The FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center recorded billions of dollars in losses linked to RDP-based attacks in recent years, with retirees and seniors disproportionately affected.

## Why Seniors Are Especially Vulnerable

Scammers and hackers know that older adults are more likely to:

- Have RDP enabled after a "tech support" call they received
- Use simpler passwords that are easier to remember
- Not realize their computer has been compromised for weeks or months
- Have valuable accounts — retirement funds, pension portals, Medicare accounts

A common scam works like this: you receive a phone call or a pop-up on your screen saying your computer has a virus. A "helpful" technician asks you to enable Remote Desktop so they can fix it. They connect, pretend to clean your machine, steal your banking credentials, and sometimes install hidden software so they can return later.

Even if you would never fall for that scam, legitimate RDP use still carries risk without proper antivirus protection.
