## What Is a VPN, Exactly?

A VPN, or Virtual Private Network, creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a server operated by the VPN provider. All your internet traffic travels through this tunnel before reaching its destination.

Two things happen as a result:

1. **Your traffic is encrypted.** Anyone on the same network — a hacker, a nosy ISP, or someone running packet-sniffing software — sees only encrypted noise instead of your actual activity.
2. **Your IP address changes.** Websites and services see the VPN server's IP address and location, not yours. This is what enables region-switching for streaming subscriptions.

These two functions are connected but distinct. Security is about protecting what you send. Region-switching is about where the world thinks you are located. A good VPN does both simultaneously.

## Why Public Wifi Is a Real Security Risk

Public wifi networks — in airports, hotels, cafes, libraries, and transit hubs — are almost always unencrypted. That means traffic flowing over them can be intercepted by other users on the same network.

The most common attacks on public wifi include:

**Man-in-the-middle attacks.** An attacker positions themselves between your device and the network router, intercepting data passing in both directions. Login credentials, payment details, and session tokens can all be captured this way.

**Evil twin networks.** A malicious hotspot named something like "CoffeeShop_Free" sits alongside the legitimate network. Users connect without realizing the difference, and the attacker sees all their traffic.

**Packet sniffing.** Free tools exist that capture and analyze all unencrypted data packets on a network. On an open wifi network, anyone who downloads one of these tools can see unencrypted traffic from every other connected device.

HTTPS protects individual website connections, but it does not protect everything — DNS queries, app traffic, and connections to poorly configured sites can still leak information on a public network.

A VPN encrypts everything that leaves your device, regardless of whether the destination website uses HTTPS or not.

## How VPN for Public Wifi Security Works: Step by Step

When you connect to a VPN on public wifi, here is [what happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) in sequence:

1. **You open the VPN app and connect to a server.** The app establishes an encrypted tunnel to a VPN server in your chosen location.
2. **Your device routes all traffic through the tunnel.** Every request — browser, app, background sync — goes through the encrypted channel before hitting the internet.
3. **The VPN server forwards requests on your behalf.** The destination website sees the VPN server's IP address, not your device's address.
4. **Responses come back through the same tunnel.** Data from the destination is encrypted in transit back to your device.
5. **The VPN app decrypts and delivers the data.** Your browser or app sees the response as if nothing unusual happened.

From the perspective of anyone else on the public wifi network — including the network operator — all they see is an encrypted connection to a VPN server. The content of your traffic is invisible.

The encryption standard that matters here is AES-256, the same standard used by financial institutions and government agencies. Surfshark uses AES-256-GCM by default. Breaking this encryption is computationally infeasible with current technology.
