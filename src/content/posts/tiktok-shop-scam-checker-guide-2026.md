## Why TikTok Shop Emails Are Especially Risky

TikTok Shop phishing works because people are already expecting emails. When you order something, you expect shipping confirmations, delivery updates, and refund notices. Scammers exploit that expectation — they send fake versions of exactly those emails at exactly the right moment.

The tell-tale patterns to watch for:

- **Urgency pressure** — "Your refund will expire in 24 hours." "Verify your account within 12 hours or your order will be cancelled."
- **Links that look right but are not** — The URL might read `tiktok-shop-orders.com` or `tiktokshop-verify.net` rather than `shop.tiktok.com`.
- **Requests for payment or card details** — A legitimate platform refund never requires you to re-enter payment information to receive it.
- **Vague subject lines** — "Your order requires attention" without an order number is a red flag.

The safest habit is to treat every email link as unverified until you have checked it — or to open TikTok Shop directly through the app rather than clicking email links at all.

## Tool 1: Google Safe Browsing — The Invisible Baseline

Google Safe Browsing is already running whenever you use Chrome, Safari, or Firefox. Every link you open gets checked against Google's database of known phishing sites and malware hosts. If a match is found, your browser stops the page from loading and shows a red warning screen.

**What it does well:** Zero setup, completely invisible, no extra steps. It handles the vast majority of well-known, widely-reported scam domains without you having to do anything.

**Where it falls short:** Safe Browsing works from a cached blocklist. A brand-new phishing domain — registered last week specifically for a TikTok Shop campaign — may not be in the database yet. Fresh scam sites frequently operate for days or weeks before they get flagged. If the link in your suspicious email points to a new domain, Safe Browsing will likely let you through without a warning.

It also tells you nothing about emails themselves — it only kicks in once you click a link.

**Best for:** Catching previously-reported, widely-known scam domains as a passive safety net.

## Tool 2: VirusTotal — The Most Thorough Manual Check

VirusTotal lets you paste a URL into its website and scans it simultaneously against more than 70 [antivirus](https://snipstack.io/vs-antivirus-2026/) and security engines. Results show you exactly which engines flagged the link and what category they assigned — phishing, malware, spam — along with WHOIS data and historical scan results.

**What it does well:** Depth and transparency. If a URL clears 70 independent engines, that is a meaningful signal. The multi-engine approach is particularly useful for TikTok Shop [phishing link](https://snipstack.io/phishing-link-checker-free-2026/)s because even if one database has not caught up yet, others often have.

**Where it falls short:** You have to copy the URL and paste it into the VirusTotal website manually. For an email you are reading on your phone at 7am, that is two or three extra steps at the exact moment when friction kills the habit. There is no automatic background scanning.

**Best for:** Tech-comfortable users who want to run a thorough check on a specific link before clicking, or anyone who has already received a suspicious email and wants a definitive scan before deciding [what to do](https://snipstack.io/what-to-do-after-malware-infection-2026/).

## Tool 3: ScamAdviser — Trust Scores for Non-Technical Users

ScamAdviser gives each website a trust score between 0 and 100, drawing on domain age, hosting signals, blacklist status, user reviews, and whether the site has visible contact information or a return policy. A score below 40 is a red flag; above 80 is generally trustworthy.

**What it does well:** The output is easy to read — a number with a plain-English explanation of why the site scored the way it did. "Domain registered 6 days ago" or "no contact page found" are the kinds of signals that tell you something useful without requiring technical knowledge.

**Where it falls short:** ScamAdviser checks domains, not the email itself. You need to extract the destination URL from the email link, open a separate tab, and paste it into the ScamAdviser site. New scam domains may not have enough data to score accurately, so very fresh phishing sites sometimes receive a neutral rather than a low score.

**Best for:** Non-technical users who want a readable verdict on an unfamiliar shopping or service site before engaging with it.

## Tool 4: URLVoid — Multi-Blacklist Lookup

URLVoid checks a URL against more than 30 established security databases — including Sucuri, PhishTank, OpenPhish, and SURBL — and shows pass/fail results for each. It also returns WHOIS registration data, hosting country, and IP address details.

**What it does well:** It is fast, free, and pulls from a wide set of well-maintained lists. The multi-source approach means you can see whether a domain has been specifically flagged for phishing (via PhishTank), general malware distribution (via Sucuri), or spam operations (via SURBL) — useful context when you want to understand what kind of threat you are dealing with.

**Where it falls short:** Like ScamAdviser, URLVoid requires a manual lookup every time. There is no browser integration and no alerting system — it is purely a lookup tool. New scam domains will return clean results until they have been reported to the databases URLVoid queries.

**Best for:** A second opinion when another tool flags a domain, or a quick multi-blacklist check for a link you received outside a browser session.

## FAQ

### How do I tell if a TikTok Shop email is real?

Check the sender's email address first — legitimate TikTok emails come from `@tiktok.com` domains, not variations like `tiktok-support.net` or `tiktokshop-verify.com`. Then hover over any links in the email before clicking to see where they actually point. If the destination domain does not exactly match `tiktok.com`, treat it as suspicious. When unsure, [run the link through a scam checker](/blog/scamcheck?sub=tiktok-shop-scam-checker-guide-2026__faq) before clicking anything.

### What should I do if I already clicked a suspicious TikTok Shop link?

Do not enter any information on the page if it opened. Close the tab immediately. If you entered a password, change it now — for TikTok and for any other account where you use the same password. If you entered payment details, contact your card issuer and request a new card number. Run a scan on the URL using VirusTotal to understand what the site was, and report it to your email provider as phishing.

### Are free scam checker tools reliable enough?

Free tools like VirusTotal and URLVoid are genuinely effective for manual, on-demand checks. The limitation is not accuracy — it is consistency. These tools only protect you when you remember to use them, and that is harder than it sounds when you are reading email quickly. Always-on tools like Surfshark Scamcheck protect you even when you do not stop to check, which is most of the time.

### Can scammers make [fake email](https://snipstack.io/fake-email-sender-with-attachment-2026/)s that pass scam checks?

Phishing emails themselves are harder for scanning tools to assess — the tools check URLs and domains, not the email's visual design or wording. A fraudulent email can look perfect while pointing to a malicious domain. This is why checking the destination URL of any link is more reliable than judging the email by appearance.

### [Does Surfshark](https://snipstack.io/does-surfshark-one-include-email-protection-2026/) Alert work on mobile?

Yes. Surfshark Alert is available as a mobile app on both iOS and Android. Scamcheck runs in the background on mobile the same way it does on desktop, flagging dangerous sites before you land on them even when you tap a link in a mobile email app.

### Is TikTok Shop phishing a new threat?

Phishing targeting e-commerce platforms has existed for years, but TikTok Shop specifically has seen a significant rise in targeted scams as the platform's shopping features have grown. The tactics are not new — fake order confirmation and refund emails are among the oldest phishing templates — but the volume targeting TikTok users specifically has increased sharply.
