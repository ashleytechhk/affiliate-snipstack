## Why Spam Finds You: How Email Harvesting Works

Before the tutorial, it helps to understand *why* your address is everywhere. There are three main pipelines that feed spam operations:

**1. Form fills and account creation.** Every time you sign up for something — a newsletter, a giveaway, a free trial — that service stores your email. Some sell it. Some get acquired and the new owner monetises the list. Some just have lax security.

**2. Data breaches.** A retailer, a forum, a dating site gets breached. Millions of addresses land on dark-web marketplaces. Spammers buy the dump and start blasting. You may not even know the breach happened until your inbox turns hostile.

**3. List buying and reselling.** There's a whole industry of data brokers who compile, verify, and sell email lists. A "free" service signs you up, then sells your address to advertisers. That address then gets resold again. By the time you're on the fifth buyer's list, you have no idea who the original collector was.

The common thread: **your real email address is a permanent liability** because you can't revoke access once it's shared. An alias changes that equation entirely — you can revoke it at any time without changing your real address.

## Step-by-Step: Build a Spam-Proof Alias System

### Step 1 — Audit Your Current Inbox Damage

Before creating aliases, take five minutes to understand what you're dealing with.

Go to your spam folder and your promotional tab. Scroll through the senders. You're not looking to unsubscribe — you're looking for patterns. Which *categories* of service are sending junk? Typical buckets:

- E-commerce and flash sales
- Newsletter subscriptions
- One-time accounts (forums, downloads, trials)
- Dating or social apps
- Work-related or freelance inquiries

Write those categories down. They become your alias map.

### Step 2 — Create One Alias Per Category

Log into Alt ID and create a dedicated alias for each category you identified. A clean naming structure makes management obvious:

| Category | Example Alias |
|---|---|
| Shopping / e-commerce | `shop-yourname@altid-domain` |
| Newsletters | `news-yourname@altid-domain` |
| Dating / social apps | `social-yourname@altid-domain` |
| Work / freelance | `work-yourname@altid-domain` |
| One-time throwaway | `temp-yourname@altid-domain` |

The naming matters. When you get a spam email, you see immediately which alias received it, which tells you which category leaked.

All aliases forward to your real inbox. You see everything in one place, but your real address is never exposed.

### Step 3 — Use the Right Alias Every Time

This step is pure discipline — and it becomes automatic within a week.

The rule is simple: **match the alias to the context, every single time.**

- Buying something online? Use `shop-`.
- Subscribing to a blog? Use `news-`.
- Trying a new app or game? Use `temp-`.
- Applying for freelance work? Use `work-`.

If you're unsure which category something falls into, default to `temp-`. Throwaway aliases are disposable by design.

A browser autofill trick helps: save each alias as a separate autofill entry with a label. Most password managers (1Password, Bitwarden, etc.) let you store multiple email identities. You pick the right one from a dropdown — no typing.

### Step 4 — Identify the Leaker When Spam Arrives

This is where the strategy pays off. When a spam email lands in your inbox, the "To:" field tells you exactly which alias received it.

Say you start getting pharmacy spam to `shop-yourname@altid-domain`. That alias was used exclusively for shopping sites. One of those sites sold or leaked your data. You now know the *category* of the breach, even if you can't identify the exact service.

Check: did the spam start after a specific purchase or sign-up? If you've been meticulous about using separate aliases per *service* (not just per category), you can narrow it even further — one alias per service means pinpoint identification.

**[Screenshot: Spam email with "To:" field showing the shop alias, illustrating identification]**

### Step 5 — Kill the Leaker Alias and Replace It

Once you've identified which alias is compromised, delete it in the Alt ID dashboard. One click. The spam stops instantly because the address no longer exists.

Then create a fresh alias for that category and update your accounts that legitimately need it (your actual online shopping logins, for example).

This is the fundamental advantage over filtering or unsubscribing: **you're not blocking individual senders, you're revoking the address entirely.** No filter can keep up with the volume of new spam domains. Killing the alias does.

### Step 6 — Harden With Per-Service Aliases (Advanced)

Once you're comfortable with category aliases, you can go one level deeper: one alias per *service*, not just per category.

For example, instead of one `shop-` alias for all e-commerce:

- `amazon-yourname@altid-domain`
- `ebay-yourname@altid-domain`

This gives you forensic-level identification. When spam arrives, you know the exact service that leaked your address, not just the category. Alt ID's unlimited alias allowance makes this practical — create as many as you need.

## Troubleshooting

**Legitimate emails aren't arriving.** Check that the alias is active and forwarding is enabled in the Alt ID dashboard. Also verify your real inbox isn't filtering forwarded mail into spam.

**I forgot which alias I used for a service.** Search your password manager or sent mail for the service name. Going forward, store the alias alongside the login credentials in your password manager.

**Spam is arriving on my real address, not an alias.** Your real address is already compromised from before you started using aliases. Consider creating a fresh primary address and migrating only your trusted contacts to it, while using aliases for everything new going forward.

**A service rejects the alias format.** Some services block disposable-address domains. If this happens, use a different category alias or check if Alt ID offers multiple domain options for your aliases.

**I deleted an alias but still receive some spam.** Mail already in transit before deletion may still deliver briefly. Wait 24 hours — if it continues, the real address may be exposed from a separate source.

## FAQ

**What is an email alias and how does it differ from a disposable address?**

An alias is a persistent forwarding address — it stays active until you delete it, and it forwards to your real inbox. A disposable address is typically temporary and expires after a set time. For the category-alias strategy, you want *persistent* aliases you control, not time-limited throwaways. [Alt ID](https://snipstack.io/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-to-avoid-spam-2026__faq) provides persistent aliases you can delete on demand.

**Does the recipient see my real email address when I reply?**

When you reply from your inbox, your mail client uses your real address by default unless you configure it to send *from* the alias. Check whether your email provider and Alt ID support reply-from-alias — some configurations support this, keeping your real address hidden in both directions.

**Can I use this strategy with Gmail's existing "+" trick?**

Gmail's `yourname+tag@gmail.com` format is a partial workaround, but it's widely filtered by marketers who strip everything after the `+`. A proper alias service uses a completely different address, which cannot be stripped or guessed back to your real account.

**How many aliases do I actually need?**

Start with five (the five categories in Step 2). Add per-service aliases for sites you consider high-risk. Most people find 10-20 aliases covers everything without becoming unmanageable.

**Is this the same strategy used for online shopping specifically?**

Shopping is one category within this system, but the strategy here is broader: it's about mapping every corner of your online life to a separate address so *any* leaker can be identified and removed. The mindset is surveillance and revocation, not just checkout protection.
