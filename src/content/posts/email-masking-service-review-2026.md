## Why Spam Is a Structural Problem, Not a Filtering Problem

Most spam-exhausted inbox owners treat the symptom rather than the source. Here's why that never works:

**Spam filters are reactive.** They catch known patterns. Spammers rotate domains, swap sender names, and mutate subject lines faster than any filter can keep pace. You'll block one wave and the next one gets through.

**Unsubscribing is counterproductive on breach-sourced spam.** Clicking any link in an email sent by an unknown sender confirms your address is active. For legitimate marketing email from brands you recognise, unsubscribing works. For spam that arrived because your address was in a breach database or sold by a data broker, it makes you a higher-value target.

**Your real email address cannot be revoked.** Once it's shared, it's permanent. It will be passed between data brokers, included in breach databases, and acquired by spam operations you will never be able to identify or contact. The only move that actually changes the equation is ensuring your real address was never given out in the first place.

Email masking fixes this at the source. You give a masked address — the service forwards legitimate mail to your inbox. When the masked address attracts spam, you delete it in seconds. Your real address was never involved.

### SimpleLogin

SimpleLogin is an open-source [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-privacy-without-creating-new-account-2026/) service with a generous free tier and strong credibility among privacy-focused users. It was acquired by Proton (the encrypted email company) in 2022, which improved infrastructure reliability and added integration with Proton Mail.

**What it does well:**

- Free tier allows up to 10 aliases — enough for cautious testers
- Open-source codebase means the privacy claims can be independently verified
- Supports reply-from-alias, keeping your real address hidden in both directions
- [Browser extension](https://snipstack.io/browser-extension-to-check-scam-sites-2026/) makes alias creation frictionless during signups
- Works with any email provider — Gmail, Outlook, or anything else

**Where it falls short:**

- Free tier caps at 10 aliases, which isn't enough for a full per-service alias system
- No identity masking — the masked address exists, but no fake name or details come with it
- Paid plan is affordable but not bundled with other privacy tools
- Customer support response times are slower than commercial alternatives

**Best for:** Privacy-conscious users comfortable with a technical product who want open-source assurance and are willing to manage a modest paid plan.

### Apple Hide My Email

Apple Hide My Email generates random addresses that forward to your iCloud inbox. It's baked into iCloud+, available across iOS, iPadOS, and macOS — and it works with minimal setup for anyone inside the Apple ecosystem.

**What it does well:**

- Deep system integration — available natively in Safari, Mail, and Sign in with Apple
- Generates addresses automatically during form fills; no separate app needed
- Reply-from-alias is supported (replies leave from the masked address, not your real one)
- No additional cost if you already pay for iCloud+ (any tier)

**Where it falls short:**

- Locked entirely to Apple devices — no Windows, Android, or cross-platform support
- Cannot be used with Gmail or any non-iCloud inbox as the real address destination
- No identity masking, browser extension, or dark web monitoring
- Limited alias management visibility — Apple's interface doesn't show which alias is receiving spam by source
- Exists as a feature, not a standalone service — no support channel beyond general Apple Support

**Best for:** iPhone-only users who already pay for iCloud+ and want something that works without any setup. Not suitable for anyone with a mixed device environment or a non-Apple email address.

## What "Email Masking" Actually Protects You From

Understanding the protection model helps set expectations correctly.

**What masking protects:**

- Your real email address — it never leaves your hands
- Your identity on low-trust services — they only ever know the masked address
- Your inbox from a compromised service — kill the masked address, the spam stops

**What masking does not protect:**

- The content of emails in transit (use encrypted email for that)
- Your IP address or browsing activity during signups (use a VPN for that)
- Your masked address from being breached at the receiving service — it's still stored there

This is where Alt ID's generated identity component becomes meaningful. If `tempname-abc123@masked-domain.com` ends up in a breach database alongside the name "Alex Morgan" and a date of birth that isn't yours — that breach record can't be linked back to you, even by someone actively trying.

It's not an absolute guarantee of anonymity. But it makes correlation significantly harder.

## FAQ

**What is an email masking service and how is it different from a spam filter?**

A spam filter sits between your inbox and the junk that's already addressed to you. An email masking service prevents your real address from being given out in the first place. Filters are reactive — they catch known patterns. Masking is structural — it removes your real address from the equation entirely. For anyone dealing with serious spam volume, masking addresses the root cause while filtering only manages symptoms.

**Is email masking legal?**

Yes. Using a masked or [alias email](https://snipstack.io/alias-email-for-linkedin-signup-without-inbox-spam-2026/) address is not illegal in any jurisdiction that regulates normal email use. You're not misrepresenting yourself in any legally significant way — you're simply receiving mail at a forwarding address. The same concept applies to PO boxes for physical mail.

**[What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) if I delete a masked address — do I lose emails I've already received?**

Emails already in your real inbox are unaffected. Deleting a masked address stops future emails from being forwarded — anything already delivered stays in your inbox. Mail in transit at the moment of deletion may or may not arrive depending on the service's processing window; most deliver for a short period before the deletion takes full effect.

**Can services tell I'm using a masked email address?**

Some services explicitly block known alias or masking domains. This is more common with financial services, gaming platforms, and some subscription services that want to prevent account sharing. If a service blocks a masked address, the most practical option is checking whether the masking service offers multiple domain options for the masked address. [Alt ID](/blog/altid?sub=email-masking-service-review-2026__faq) provides options for this scenario.

**Does masking protect me if the masking service itself gets hacked?**

This is a legitimate concern. A masking service holds the mapping between your masked addresses and your real inbox — that mapping is a target. The risk is real but manageable: check whether the service has a published privacy policy that minimises data retention, whether it's been independently audited, and whether it uses encrypted storage. SimpleLogin's open-source codebase allows independent review. Alt ID's generated identity feature means that even if the mapping were exposed, the associated identity data doesn't point to your real self.

**How many masked addresses do I actually need?**

Start with one per major category: shopping, newsletters, account signups, work inquiries. That's four to five addresses. Power users prefer one per service — so if one leaks, you know the exact source. Most email masking services with unlimited aliases make this practical; the 10-alias free tier on SimpleLogin is the main constraint.
