## The 7 Warning [Signs of a](https://snipstack.io/signs-of-a-fake-website-2026/) Romance Scam

### 1. They Appear Instantly Perfect

Romance scammers open with intense attention. They respond to every message quickly, say exactly what you want to hear, and escalate affection faster than any real relationship would. This is called love-bombing, and it is a deliberate tactic designed to build trust before the manipulation begins.

A real person getting to know someone takes time. They have bad days, slow replies, and opinions that do not always align with yours. If someone online seems to perfectly mirror your values, your sense of humour, and your preferences within the first week, be cautious.

### 2. They Cannot Meet In Person — Ever

Video calls are awkward. Meeting up is always complicated. There is a work trip, a medical emergency, a family crisis, a passport issue, a military deployment. Scammers maintain elaborate backstories to explain why they are always unreachable in person.

If you have been messaging someone for weeks or months and every attempt to meet or video call has been blocked by some new obstacle, that pattern is a significant warning sign. Ask for a live video call. A real person will find a way to say yes.

### 3. Their Photos Look Too Good

Professional-quality headshots, glamorous travel backdrops, model-adjacent appearance — scammers choose photos that are appealing and credible, but they steal them from real people elsewhere on the internet.

Test this with a reverse image search. On Google Images, click the camera icon and upload the profile photo. On a phone, long-press the image and choose "Search image." If the same photo appears under a different name on another site, or in a stock photo library, it is stolen. TinEye (tineye.com) is an alternative reverse search engine worth trying if Google returns no results.

![Running a reverse image search to verify a dating profile photo](/og/how-to-spot-romance-scam-online-2026-inline-1.png)

### 4. They Move the Conversation Off the Dating Platform Quickly

Dating apps have fraud detection and reporting systems. Scammers know this. Within a day or two, they typically push to move the conversation to WhatsApp, Telegram, or email — platforms where there is no oversight and no easy way to report them.

Moving to private messaging is not automatically suspicious. But if they are insistent about it, and it happens before you know much about them, note it.

### 5. Their Story Has Convenient, Unchangeable Complications

The details of a scammer's backstory are designed to justify requests later. They are often widowed. Their children live far away. They work in an international field — oil rigging, military service, medical missions, engineering contracts abroad — that keeps them perpetually unavailable and perpetually in need of financial help.

Listen for internal inconsistencies. Does their claimed profession match what they seem to know about it? Do details change between conversations? Write things down if you need to. Inconsistency is hard to maintain across weeks of messaging.

### 6. They Send Links or Emails You Did Not Ask For

As trust builds, scammers often send links — to a supposed verification platform, an investment site, a shared document, or a news article about themselves. These links may look legitimate but lead to phishing pages designed to capture your login credentials or personal information.

Do not click any link from someone you have not met in person without checking it first. **[Surfshark Scam Detector](/blog/scamcheck?sub=how-to-spot-romance-scam-online-2026__mid)** lets you paste a URL and get an instant risk assessment. If the domain was registered recently, has no reputation history, or is flagged in scam databases, you will know before you click.

Similarly, if they contact you by email and the address looks slightly off — a Gmail address claiming to be a verified professional, or a domain that is close to a real organization's name but slightly misspelled — run that address through the Scam Detector email checker.

### 7. The Conversation Leads to a Financial Request

This is the end goal of every romance scam. The request may be direct ("I need money for a plane ticket to come see you") or indirect ("My investment account is frozen and I just need to park some funds temporarily"). It may be framed as a loan, a gift, or an urgent emergency.

Common request formats:
- Gift cards (iTunes, Google Play, Amazon) — chosen because they are irreversible
- Wire transfers to a foreign account
- Cryptocurrency — untraceable and non-refundable
- A "verification fee" on a fake dating site they link you to

If someone you have never met in person asks you for money in any form, stop all contact. Report the profile to the platform. Do not send anything.

## [What to Do](https://snipstack.io/what-to-do-after-malware-infection-2026/) If You Think You Are Being Scammed

If something in this guide made you look at a current online relationship differently, here are the steps to take now.

**Do not send money.** Even if you feel close to this person, even if the emergency sounds genuine, stop. Romance scammers are skilled at making their requests feel reasonable and urgent. Take 24 hours before you do anything.

**Do a reverse image search** on their profile photos. Run their email address through a scam checker. Look for inconsistencies in their story.

**Talk to someone you trust in person.** The emotional dynamic of a romance scam can make it hard to see clearly from the inside. A friend, family member, or counsellor can offer perspective without judgment.

**Report the account.** Dating platforms have reporting tools. Use them. The report may not result in immediate action, but it contributes to pattern detection that protects other users.

**Contact your bank immediately** if you have already sent money. Banks can sometimes reverse wire transfers if you act within hours. Credit card companies have chargeback processes. The sooner you call, the more options you have.

Report the scam to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov and to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center at ic3.gov.

## FAQ

### How do I know if someone online is real or a scammer?

Look for consistency over time — in their story, their photos, and their availability. Run a reverse image search on their profile photos. Ask for a live video call with no filters. If they refuse or always have an excuse, that is a warning sign. Run any email address they give you through a scam checker to see if it has been flagged in fraud databases.

### What are the most common romance scam scripts in 2026?

The most common scenarios in 2026 involve military personnel deployed overseas, oil rig or offshore workers, doctors or engineers on international assignments, and widowed individuals with children living abroad. These backstories are chosen because they justify unavailability and ongoing financial hardship. AI-generated messages have made the writing quality smoother, so poor grammar is no longer a reliable tell.

### Can a romance scammer use a real person's identity?

Yes. Many scammers steal photos and personal details from real people — including social media profiles of real professionals. The real person has no idea their identity is being used. This is why reverse image searching profile photos is worth the two minutes it takes. If the photo belongs to someone with a completely different name, you are talking to a scammer using a stolen identity.

### How do I check if an email from someone I met online is safe?

Look at the full email address — not just the display name. A personal Gmail address claiming to belong to a US Army general or a senior executive at a multinational company is an immediate red flag. Then run the address through **[Surfshark Scam Detector](/blog/scamcheck?sub=how-to-spot-romance-scam-online-2026__faq)** to see if it appears in scam or breach databases. Check any links they send before clicking by pasting them into the link scanner.

### What should I do if I already sent money to a romance scammer?

Contact your bank or card issuer immediately — the sooner you call, the better your chances of a reversal. If you paid with gift cards, contact the card issuer directly (the number is on the back of the card). If you sent cryptocurrency, recovery is very difficult; report to the exchange where you bought it. File reports with the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov and the FBI's IC3 at ic3.gov. You are not the first person this has happened to, and reporting helps investigators identify patterns.

### Are romance scams only a risk on dating apps?

No. Romance scammers operate across social media platforms, online games, WhatsApp groups, Facebook, and even by text message. Any platform where strangers can initiate contact is a potential vector. Some scams begin with a "wrong number" text that quickly turns into a friendly conversation and then a relationship. The same warning signs apply regardless of where the contact began.
