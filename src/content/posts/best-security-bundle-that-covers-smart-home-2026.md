## Why Smart Home Devices Are a Security Blind Spot

Smart home gadgets share two qualities that make them attractive targets. First, they almost never run [antivirus software](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-2026/) — no app store, no scanner, no updates pushed from a security vendor. Second, they sit on the same Wi-Fi network as your phones, laptops, and banking apps. Compromise one device and an attacker can potentially observe traffic from every other device on that network.

The three most common attack paths are:

- **Weak default passwords.** Many smart devices ship with factory credentials that owners never change.
- **Unencrypted network traffic.** Some IoT devices send data without encryption, making it readable to anyone else on the same network or intercepting traffic upstream.
- **Malicious DNS requests.** Malware on a compromised device can silently phone home to command servers using DNS, a channel most household routers leave completely open.

A router-level VPN with a built-in malware-domain blocker closes all three angles at once — for every device, without installing anything on the gadget itself.

## The Router Installation: How to Cover Every Smart Home Device

Setting up Surfshark at the router level is the step that unlocks whole-home smart home coverage. Here is the general approach:

1. **Check router compatibility.** Surfshark publishes guides for common router firmware including DD-WRT, Tomato, and AsusWRT. Many mid-range and above consumer routers support one of these.
2. **Install the VPN configuration.** You upload Surfshark's connection files to your router's VPN client settings. Surfshark's support pages walk through each router model step by step.
3. **Enable Clean Web at the account level.** This activates the malware-domain and tracker-blocking DNS filter across the connection.
4. **Connect your smart devices as normal.** Because the protection runs at the router, Alexa, Ring, Google Nest, and your smart TV are covered automatically — no app install required on the device.

If your current router does not support VPN client mode, a common workaround is to purchase a mid-range router (ASUS RT-AX series, GL.iNet travel routers) that does. The one-time hardware cost is often far less than the Bitdefender Box, and it works with any [VPN subscription](https://snipstack.io/vpn-subscription-cheaper-with-two-year-plan-2026/) going forward.

### Can a VPN really protect smart home devices like Alexa and Ring?

At the router level, yes. When the VPN runs on your router rather than a specific device, all traffic leaving your home — including traffic from Alexa, Ring cameras, and Nest thermostats — passes through the encrypted tunnel. Malicious domains blocked by Clean Web are also blocked for those devices, even though they cannot run an app themselves.

### What smart home threats does a [security bundle](https://snipstack.io/security-bundle-black-friday-deals-2026/) actually stop?

A bundle with a router VPN and DNS-level [malware blocking](https://snipstack.io/malware-blocking-ad-blocker-2026/) stops malicious network traffic, prevents smart devices from connecting to known command-and-control servers, encrypts traffic so others on the same network cannot read it, and (via breach monitoring) alerts you if an account tied to your home's smart home services has been compromised.

### Will running a VPN on my router slow down smart home devices?

There is a small speed trade-off from VPN encryption. For browsing and streaming it is typically negligible on a modern connection. Smart home commands (turning on lights, asking Alexa a question) are low-bandwidth and rarely affected. On older or budget routers with slower processors, the impact can be more noticeable — check your router's specifications before committing.

### Is Bitdefender Box worth it compared to a software bundle?

For users who want per-device IoT risk scores and network anomaly detection, Bitdefender Box offers capabilities that software-only bundles do not. For most households, the higher upfront cost, hardware setup, and absence of VPN and breach monitoring make a software bundle like [Surfshark One](https://snipstack.io/surfshark-one-for-family-of-four-review-2026/) the more practical choice.

The Alert component monitors the email addresses you register with it. If your Ring, Nest, or Amazon account email appears in a known data breach, Alert notifies you so you can change the password before anyone uses it. It covers the account layer, complementing the network-layer protection from the VPN.
