## Why Gamers Are Targeted — Especially Older Ones

You might think hackers only go after people who play violent shooter games or who spend thousands of dollars on virtual items. That is not true.

Criminals target **any** online game where people are relaxed and interacting with others. Word games, solitaire, mahjong, bingo, trivia, chess — all of them. Here is why older adults are especially at risk:

**You are online at predictable times.** Many seniors play in the morning or evening, following a routine. Scammers know this and time their attacks accordingly.

**You are focused on the game, not on warnings.** When you are in the middle of a puzzle or a card game, a pop-up demanding your attention feels urgent and real.

**You are more likely to trust what you see on screen.** Younger people who grew up with the internet have learned to be suspicious of pop-ups. Many older adults have not had the same exposure to these tricks.

**You often store important information on the same device.** If you check your email, do your banking, or store passwords on the same computer you use for games, a criminal only needs to get into that one device to access everything.

## The Four Biggest Threats Inside Online Games

### 1. Fake Virus Pop-Ups (Tech Support Scams)

This is the most common attack. While you are playing, a message appears — sometimes covering the whole screen — claiming your computer has been infected. It may look like it comes from Microsoft or your [antivirus software](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-for-iphone-2026/). It always includes a phone number to call.

If you call that number, the person on the other end will ask to connect to your computer remotely. Once they have access, they can steal your files, install real malware, or simply ask you to pay hundreds of dollars to "fix" a problem that never existed.

**What to do instead:** Close the browser or the game by pressing Alt + F4 (Windows) or Command + Q (Mac). Never call a number from a pop-up. Real security warnings from your computer never ask you to call anyone.

### 2. Fake Game Downloads

You want to try a new word game or a puzzle app. You search for it online and find a download link. The file installs what looks like a game — but in the background it also installs spyware that records everything you type, including passwords.

This risk is highest when you download games from anywhere other than the official App Store (on iPhone or iPad) or the Google Play Store (on Android). On a computer, it also applies to any game website that is not well known.

### 3. Links in Game Chat

Many online games have a chat feature where you can send messages to other players. Criminals join these games specifically to send links. The message might say "Click here for free bonus coins" or "Your account was flagged — verify here." The link takes you to a page that looks real but is designed to steal your login details or install malware.

**The rule:** Never click a link that another player sends you in a game chat, no matter how friendly they seem.

### 4. Infected Game Add-Ons

Some games offer extra features you can download — themes, bonus levels, helper tools, cheat programs. These unofficial add-ons are a very common way to deliver malware. Even if the file looks harmless, it may contain hidden software that activates once installed.

## What Happens When Your Device Gets Infected

Many seniors do not realize their computer has been compromised until significant damage has already been done. Here are warning signs to watch for:

- Your computer runs unusually slowly for no clear reason
- You see new toolbars or programs you did not install
- Your browser opens different websites than you typed
- Ads appear in places where they never appeared before
- You receive strange emails saying your accounts were accessed from a new location
- Money moves from your bank account without your knowledge

By the time you notice these signs, the threat may have been active for days or weeks. This is why **prevention matters far more than reaction**. A good antivirus catches the threat before it can cause harm, not after.
