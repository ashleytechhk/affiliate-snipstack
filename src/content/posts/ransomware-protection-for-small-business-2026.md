## What Is Ransomware? (Plain English Explanation)

Think of ransomware as a digital padlock that criminals put on your own files. Here is how it works in simple steps:

1. You receive an email, a pop-up, or a link that looks legitimate
2. You click it — without knowing it is a trap
3. A hidden program secretly installs itself on your computer
4. That program scrambles every file on your computer so you cannot open them
5. A message appears on your screen demanding payment — usually in cryptocurrency — to unlock your files

The criminals behind these attacks are not amateur teenagers in basements. They are organized criminal groups that run these operations like a business. They have customer service teams (to handle ransom payments), pricing strategies, and even "loyalty programs" for repeat victims.

The word "ransom" is exactly right. Your own data is held hostage. And even if you pay, many victims never receive their files back.

## Why Small Business Owners Are Prime Targets in 2026

You might think: "I am just a small bakery / repair shop / accounting practice. Why would criminals bother with me?"

That is exactly the thinking attackers count on.

Large corporations have entire IT departments, cybersecurity teams, and multi-million dollar defenses. Small businesses — especially those run by owners who are less comfortable with technology — are much softer targets. Criminals know this.

Here are the specific reasons small business owners over 60 face higher risk:

**Customer data is valuable.** If you keep customer names, email addresses, [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/)s, or payment details anywhere on your computer, that information is worth money to criminals. Medical practices, legal offices, insurance brokers, and accountants are especially targeted.

**Business interruption is devastating.** Criminals know that a small business cannot afford to be shut down for days or weeks. The pressure to pay the ransom quickly is much higher for a small operation than for a large corporation that can absorb the disruption.

**Older systems are easier to attack.** Many small business owners use the same computer for years without updating software. Outdated operating systems and programs have known security holes that ransomware exploits.

**Fewer backups exist.** Many small business owners do not have a regular backup system. When ransomware strikes, there is no copy of the files to restore — making the ransom demand feel like the only option.

## How Ransomware Actually Gets In (And How to Shut the Door)

Understanding the entry points makes it much easier to protect yourself. Ransomware typically arrives through one of these routes:

### Phishing Emails

This is the most common method. You receive an email that appears to come from your bank, a supplier, the government, or even a family member. The email contains either an attachment or a link. Opening the attachment or clicking the link triggers the attack.

Modern phishing emails are shockingly convincing. They use your name, reference real companies, and mimic official logos perfectly. Even careful, experienced people get caught.

### Malicious Websites

Visiting certain websites — including sites that look completely normal but have been compromised — can trigger automatic downloads. You do not even need to click anything.

### Fake Software Updates

Pop-up messages telling you to "update your software now" or "your computer is at risk" are often traps. Clicking them installs the ransomware rather than any legitimate update.

### Remote Desktop Vulnerabilities

If your computer uses remote desktop software (allowing someone to connect to your computer remotely), outdated or misconfigured settings create an easy entry point for attackers.
