## Why Seniors Are Targeted More Than Anyone Else

Scammers are not random. They are strategic. Older adults tend to:

- Have more savings and assets than younger people
- Be less familiar with what legitimate emails from banks and companies look like
- Be more likely to follow instructions when the message sounds authoritative or urgent
- Respond to emotional triggers — a "problem" with a grandchild, a frozen bank account, an overdue tax bill

These are not character flaws. They are patterns that scammers have studied and exploit deliberately. Knowing you are being targeted is the first step to not falling for it.

## What a Phishing Email Actually Is

A phishing email is a fake message designed to look like it comes from someone you trust — your bank, Amazon, Medicare, the IRS, PayPal, or even a family member.

The goal is always one of these three things:

1. **Get you to click a link** that takes you to a [fake website](https://snipstack.io/fake-website-detector-tool-2026/) where you type in your password
2. **Get you to open an attachment** that installs harmful software on your device
3. **Get you to reply** with personal information like your [Social Security](https://snipstack.io/social-security-scam-call-verify-2026/) number, date of birth, or card details

The name "phishing" comes from the idea of fishing — they cast a line, and they wait to see who bites.

## [How to Spot](https://snipstack.io/how-to-spot-romance-scam-online-2026/) a Phishing Email: 6 Warning Signs

Learning to recognise the pattern takes less than five minutes. Here are the six things that appear in almost every phishing email.

### 1. The email creates urgency or panic

"Your account will be suspended in 24 hours." "Unusual activity detected — verify now." "Your payment failed — act immediately."

Legitimate companies do not communicate this way. They give you time. They include [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/)s. They do not threaten consequences if you do not click a link *right now*.

**Rule:** If an email is trying to make you panic, that is a sign you should slow down — not speed up.

### 2. The sender address looks almost right — but not quite

Look at the full email address, not just the name displayed. Scammers can make a message *display* the name "PayPal" or "Bank of America" while the actual sending address is something like `noreply@paypal-securityalert.net` or `billing@amaz0n.support`.

**Rule:** Click on the sender name to reveal the full email address. If the domain — the part after the @ sign — does not exactly match the company's real website address, it is a fake.

### 3. There are spelling mistakes or strange formatting

Professional companies proofread their emails. Phishing messages often have odd spacing, grammar errors, mismatched fonts, or sentences that sound slightly unnatural. Not always — AI has made scam emails more convincing — but it is still a useful check.

### 4. The link does not go where it claims

Before you click any link in an email, you can check where it actually leads.

**On a computer:** Move your mouse over the link without clicking. Look at the bottom of your screen — a small bar will show the real web address the link will take you to.

**On a phone or tablet:** Press and hold the link (do not tap). A preview will appear showing the real address.

If the real address looks strange, long, or does not include the company's exact name, do not click it.

### 5. You were not expecting this email

An invoice for something you did not buy. A delivery notification for a package you did not order. A security alert for an account you have not used recently. Unexpected emails deserve extra scrutiny.

### 6. It asks you to open an attachment

Unless you specifically requested a document — a statement, a receipt, a form — do not open attachments from emails you were not expecting. Infected attachments are one of the most common ways harmful software gets onto a computer.

## [What Happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) If You Click: The Real Risk

![Phishing email attack chain — how a single click leads to account theft](/og/how-to-protect-against-phishing-emails-2026-inline-1.png)

This is where the fear is justified. When you click a link in a phishing email, two things can happen:

**Scenario A — The fake login page.** You land on a page that looks exactly like your bank, PayPal, or email provider. You type your username and password. That information goes straight to the scammer. They log in as you, change your password, and lock you out — sometimes within minutes.

**Scenario B — Silent malware.** Some links or attachments install software on your computer without you doing anything else. This software can record every key you press (including passwords), take screenshots, or give the scammer remote access to your files.

Neither scenario requires you to hand over a credit card number or confirm anything. One click, or one opened attachment, can be enough.

## The Four Habits That Stop Most Phishing Attacks

You do not need to become technical. You need four habits.

**1. Never click a link to log in.** If an email says there is a problem with your account, close the email and open your browser. Type the company's web address yourself — for example, `www.wellsfargo.com` — and log in from there. If there is a genuine problem, you will see it after logging in normally.

**2. Look at the full sender address before doing anything.** Not the display name — the actual address. If it does not match the company exactly, delete the email.

**3. When in doubt, call.** Look up the company's [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-privacy-for-social-media-2026/) from their official website (not from the email) and call to check whether the message was real.

**4. Run a security tool that catches what you miss.** Even careful people occasionally click something they should not have. A security layer that checks every link before it loads — and blocks dangerous websites automatically — catches the mistakes that habits alone cannot always prevent.

## Step-by-Step: [What to Do](https://snipstack.io/what-to-do-after-malware-infection-2026/) When a Suspicious Email Arrives

![Checking a suspicious email on a laptop — step by step for seniors](/og/how-to-protect-against-phishing-emails-2026-inline-2.png)

Follow these steps every time you receive an email that makes you feel worried, rushed, or uncertain.

**Step 1 — Stop and breathe.** Do not click anything yet. Urgency is the trap. The scammer wants your finger on that link before your brain catches up.

**Step 2 — Check the sender address.** Click on the sender's name to reveal the full email address. Does the domain after @ exactly match the company's real website?

**Step 3 — Hover over any links.** On a computer, move your mouse over the link and look at the address shown at the bottom of your screen. On mobile, press and hold to see the preview.

**Step 4 — If anything looks wrong, delete it.** You do not need to investigate further. Delete and empty your trash.

**Step 5 — If you are still worried about your account, contact the company directly.** Look up their [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-tinder-privacy-2026/) from a previous statement, your card, or their official website. Do not use the phone number in the email — that can also be fake.

**Step 6 — If you clicked something already, act fast.** Change the password for that account immediately using the official website. If it involved your bank or credit card, call them straight away. Run a virus scan on your device.

## "I Already Clicked" — What to Do Right Now

Do not panic. Speed matters more than perfect decisions.

- **If you clicked a link but did not enter anything:** Close the browser tab. Run a virus scan on your device immediately.
- **If you entered your email password:** Change it right now on the real website. Then check whether you use the same password anywhere else — change those too.
- **If you entered banking or card details:** Call your bank immediately. They can freeze the card and monitor for suspicious transactions.
- **If you opened an attachment:** Run a full virus scan on your device as soon as possible.

Acting within the first hour makes a significant difference. Most account takeovers happen within minutes of a password being captured.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I know if an email is really from my bank?**
Look at the full sender address — not just the display name. Your bank's emails will always come from its exact official domain (for example, `@bankofamerica.com`). If in doubt, log in to your account directly by typing the address yourself in your browser. A genuine security alert will be visible there too.

**Is it dangerous just to open a phishing email without clicking anything?**
In most cases, simply opening an email is low risk. The danger comes from clicking links or opening attachments. However, some older email programs can load images from outside the email — which tells the scammer your email address is active and being read. If you receive a lot of suspicious emails, consider turning off "load remote images" in your email settings.

**What if the email uses my real name — does that make it real?**
Not necessarily. Scammers often purchase or steal personal data that includes names, addresses, and partial account details. An email that uses your first name is not automatically trustworthy. Always check the sender address and never log in through an email link, regardless of how personalised the message looks.
