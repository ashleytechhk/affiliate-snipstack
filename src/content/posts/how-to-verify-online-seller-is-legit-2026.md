## Method 2 — Look Up Business Registration

A legitimate business that sells goods or services is registered somewhere. Most countries have a free public register you can search by company name.

**Where to search:**
- **United States:** Secretary of State business registry for the relevant state (most are freely searchable online)
- **United Kingdom:** Companies House at companieshouse.gov.uk
- **Australia:** ASIC register at asic.gov.au
- **Canada:** Canada's Business Registries at ised-isde.canada.ca

What you are looking for:

- The company name appears in the register and is listed as active (not dissolved or struck off)
- The registered address matches what the seller lists on their website or in their email
- The company was registered more than a few months ago — a business incorporated last month and already claiming thousands of customers warrants scepticism

This check is particularly important for higher-value purchases — electronics, furniture, jewellery, anything above a few hundred dollars where the financial exposure justifies the five minutes it takes to run.

Be aware that many small legitimate sellers operate as sole traders or individual marketplace sellers and may not appear in a corporate register. The absence of a registration is not automatically a red flag for small sellers — but it raises the importance of the other checks.

## Method 3 — Verify Reviews (And Watch For Fake Ones)

Online reviews are the first thing fraudulent sellers manipulate. Understanding what genuine reviews look like — and what fake ones look like — takes practice, but the patterns are consistent.

**Signs of genuine reviews:**
- Spread across different dates over several months or years
- Specific product details mentioned ("the blue version of the XL size arrived well packaged")
- Mixed ratings — even excellent sellers have occasional negative reviews, and those negatives have detailed responses from the seller
- Reviews on multiple independent platforms (Trustpilot, Google Reviews, and sometimes Facebook or Reddit)

**Signs of fake or manipulated reviews:**
- A cluster of five-star reviews posted within a short window — all with similar generic language
- No reviews at all, despite the seller claiming a large customer base
- Reviews only on a platform the seller controls (their own website's review section with no external verification)
- Trustpilot or Google profiles created very recently with no review history

Search the seller's name alongside "reviews" and "scam" on Google before you buy. If other people have been defrauded, there is usually a trail — forum posts, Reddit threads, consumer protection reports, or news coverage.

## Method 4 — Inspect the Contact Details and Email Domain

This check is especially relevant when a seller reaches out to you by email — which is the most common delivery mechanism for online seller fraud targeting older adults.

**What to verify:**

The seller's email address domain should match their business name exactly. If an email claims to be from "GlobalShop Electronics" but the sending address ends in `@gmail.com`, `@yahoo.com`, or any domain that is not the business's own — that is a significant warning sign.

Hover over any links in the seller's email before clicking. The URL that appears at the bottom of your browser should match the seller's stated website domain. Mismatches — a button labelled "View your order" that links to a completely different domain — indicate a phishing attempt rather than a legitimate seller communication.

For sellers directing you to a website, check the domain age. A legitimate established retailer does not have a domain registered six weeks ago. Free WHOIS lookup tools will show you when a domain was registered. Scamcheck surfaces this automatically alongside its other checks.

## Method 5 — Check the Payment Method Before You Commit

The payment method a seller offers tells you a great deal about their legitimacy — and about what recourse you will have if something goes wrong.

**Safer payment options:**
- **Credit card via a recognised gateway** (Stripe, Worldpay, Adyen) — credit cards offer chargeback protection, and recognised gateways have fraud controls built in
- **PayPal** — Buyer Protection covers eligible purchases, and disputes are handled by a third party rather than the seller
- **Apple Pay or Google Pay** — these route through your card's existing protections

**High-risk payment options — avoid for unfamiliar sellers:**
- **Bank transfer / wire transfer** — irreversible, and the seller has your bank details
- **Cryptocurrency** — completely irreversible, no consumer protection exists
- **Gift card codes** — a payment method that no legitimate business uses; a universal scam signal
- **Zelle or similar peer-to-peer apps** — generally not designed for commerce and offer limited recourse

If a seller insists that you can only pay by one of the high-risk methods above, treat it as a definitive red flag. Legitimate businesses accept card payments because it protects both parties. A seller who refuses card payments is either operating outside the formal economy or actively avoiding the chargeback protections that card payments provide to buyers.

## Putting It Together: A Pre-Payment Checklist

Before you send money to any online seller you have not bought from before:

1. **Scam scan the seller's email or website** — paste it into Scamcheck and read the verdict. Takes 30 seconds.
2. **Check business registration** if the purchase is above a threshold that matters to you financially.
3. **Read reviews on independent platforms** — Trustpilot and Google, not just the seller's own site.
4. **Inspect the email address and any links** — domain match, no suspicious redirects.
5. **Confirm the payment method** — card or PayPal, never bank transfer or gift card.

A seller that passes all five is almost certainly legitimate. A seller that fails two or more — especially the first and the last — should not receive your money.

## FAQ

**How do I verify an online seller is legitimate before I buy?**

Start with a quick scan of their website or email address using a tool like Scamcheck — it cross-references against fraud databases and checks domain age in seconds. Then look for genuine reviews on Trustpilot or Google, verify that their contact email matches their stated business domain, and confirm they accept card or PayPal rather than bank transfer or gift cards. **What are the warning [signs of a](https://snipstack.io/signs-of-a-fake-website-2026/) scam online seller?**

The most common warning signs: prices that are dramatically lower than everywhere else, contact emails using free providers like Gmail or Yahoo rather than a business domain, a website domain that was registered very recently, no reviews on any independent platform, and pressure to pay by bank transfer or gift card. Receiving an unsolicited email about a deal you did not inquire about is itself a warning sign — legitimate sellers do not cold-email you about special offers.

**Is it safe to buy from a seller who only accepts bank transfer?**

No. Bank transfers are irreversible and offer no consumer protection. If the seller disappears after receiving payment, you have no mechanism to recover the funds through the payment channel. If a seller will only accept bank transfer, either find an alternative seller or walk away.

**Can scammers make a [fake email](https://snipstack.io/fake-email-sender-with-attachment-2026/) look like it's from a real company?**

Yes — this is called email spoofing, and it is very common. The display name in an email can say anything, but the actual sending domain (visible when you expand the sender details) cannot be faked for a properly secured domain. Check the full sending address, not just the name shown. If you received an unexpected email from what looks like a known retailer or marketplace, go directly to that company's official website to verify — do not click any links in the email itself.

**What should I do if I already paid a fraudulent seller?**

Contact your bank or card provider immediately and report the transaction as potentially fraudulent. If you paid by credit card, ask to initiate a chargeback. If you paid through PayPal, open a dispute via the Resolution Centre as soon as possible. Report the seller to your national consumer protection body — the FTC in the US, Trading Standards or Action Fraud in the UK. The sooner you act, the better your chances of recovery.

**Does Scamcheck work on emails as well as websites?**

Yes. You can paste either a website URL or an email address into Scamcheck. For seller verification specifically, scanning the email address the seller contacted you from is often more revealing than scanning their website — fraud databases flag known sender addresses, and a newly created email domain registers as a risk signal even if the website itself looks professional.
