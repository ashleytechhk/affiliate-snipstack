## The Myth That Has Left Thousands of Mac Users Exposed

You have probably heard it before: "Macs do not get viruses." That idea made some sense in the early 2000s when almost nobody used a Mac. Today, Apple has hundreds of millions of active Mac users worldwide. Attackers go where the audience is.

Between 2023 and 2025, security researchers tracked a significant spike in Mac-specific malware. Threats like **Atomic Stealer**, **AMOS**, and **Cuckoo** were specifically designed to bypass Apple's built-in protections and steal passwords, banking details, and files — all without showing you a single warning.

Apple's built-in tool, called XProtect, updates periodically. But it only catches threats Apple already knows about. New malware can circulate for days or weeks before Apple adds it to the detection list. A dedicated antivirus tool updates its database continuously — sometimes hundreds of times per day.

For anyone over 60 who is freelancing with a Mac, this gap matters enormously.

## Why Freelance Video Editors Face a Higher Risk

You might wonder: why would a video editor be a more attractive target than, say, an accountant? The answer comes down to your workflow.

### You Download a Lot of Files From Many Sources

Freelance video work involves receiving footage from clients, downloading stock clips, pulling royalty-free music from websites, and installing plugins for editing software like Final Cut Pro, DaVinci Resolve, or Adobe Premiere. Each of those downloads is a potential entry point.

A compressed video file, a zipped plugin folder, a shared Dropbox link from a client you just met — any of these could contain a hidden payload. You would not see it. The file would open normally, the footage would play fine, and the malware would quietly get to work in the background.

### You Work With Clients Who Trust You With Their Data

When a client sends you raw footage of a corporate event, a family gathering, or a product launch, they are trusting you with their content. If malware on your Mac accesses those files or, worse, locks them with ransomware and demands payment to release them, you face not just personal financial loss but serious damage to your professional reputation.

Ransomware attacks on freelancers are more common than reported. Most victims never publicise what happened because of the embarrassment.

### You Likely Use the Same Mac for Personal and Professional Tasks

Most freelancers — especially those working from home — use one computer for everything. The same Mac where you edit client videos also holds your banking login, your saved passwords in Safari, your email history, and years of personal photos.

A single successful attack does not just affect your work. It can drain a bank account, expose your identity, or lock you out of accounts you have had for decades.
