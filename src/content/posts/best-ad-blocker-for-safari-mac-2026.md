## AdGuard for Safari — Best Free Extension

AdGuard's Safari extension is free, open source, and maintained by a team that has been building [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-free-chrome-2026/)s since 2009. It uses Safari's native content-blocking API, which means it runs efficiently without accessing your browsing history — Apple's API is sandboxed by design.

The default setup blocks ads and basic trackers. Dig into settings and you can activate additional filter lists: EasyPrivacy for tracking scripts, regional lists for ad networks that EasyList misses, and anti-social-widget filters that remove Facebook and Twitter embed trackers from third-party sites. Advanced users can write custom filtering rules directly in the app.

![AdGuard for Safari filter list configuration](/og/best-ad-blocker-for-safari-mac-2026-inline-1.png)

On mobile, AdGuard offers a free Safari content blocker for iPhone and iPad. The same filter lists apply, and if you set up AdGuard on both Mac and iPhone, you get consistent blocking behaviour across devices. The iOS free version covers Safari only — for system-wide blocking on iPhone (in-app ads, Mail tracking pixels) you need AdGuard Pro, which is a separate paid purchase.

**Verdict:** The strongest free option for Mac Safari. Excellent filter coverage, transparent about what it blocks, and zero cost. Limitation: Safari-only on Mac, and extending to system-wide coverage on iPhone requires an extra purchase.

## 1Blocker — Best-Designed Paid Extension

1Blocker has been on Mac and iPhone since 2014 and shows it in the interface quality. The app is split into clearly labelled sections: Ads, Trackers, Social Widgets, Cookie Notices, Annoyances, and Custom Rules. You toggle categories on or off rather than hunting through raw filter lists.

The tracking protection is thorough. 1Blocker maintains its own curated blocklist rather than relying purely on EasyPrivacy, and it updates frequently. On Safari for Mac, it operates through Apple's content-blocking API with a companion extension that handles more complex cases like cosmetic filtering (hiding ad placeholder containers after the ad URL is blocked).

iCloud sync means your rules and settings travel automatically from your Mac to iPhone and iPad. For families or anyone with multiple Apple devices, that removes a meaningful friction point. The iOS version provides full Mobile Safari support — same categories, same UI, same blocking quality.

The subscription model (or an optional one-time "Forever" purchase) means you are paying for active maintenance, which is the right model for ad blocking — filter lists need constant updates as ad networks adapt. The price is fair for what you get.

**Verdict:** Worth paying for if you want a polished experience that works identically on Mac and iPhone. Limitation: still browser-only, meaning ads in apps like News or Podcasts pass through unblocked.

## Wipr — Minimal and Affordable

Wipr takes a different philosophy: no configuration, no settings, just block. Install it, enable it in Safari Extensions preferences, and walk away. The developer maintains a single curated blocklist that he updates regularly, and the extension passes those rules to Safari's content-blocking engine.

The result is fast and lightweight. Because there are no settings to tune and no large multi-list infrastructure to load, Wipr adds negligible overhead to Safari. It covers ads and the most common trackers on mainstream sites. Where it falls short is in depth — it does not [block fingerprinting](https://snipstack.io/block-fingerprinting-trackers-browser-2026/) scripts or the more obscure tracking pixels that a tool like AdGuard or 1Blocker catches with its extended lists.

Mobile Safari support is included: a separate Wipr app on the App Store extends the same blocklist to iPhone and iPad Safari. The one-time purchase covers both Mac and iOS versions through a universal purchase if you buy through the App Store.

**Verdict:** Excellent for users who want something that works without any setup. Not the deepest [tracker blocker](https://snipstack.io/tracker-blocker-browser-extension-2026/), but fast, cheap, and zero-maintenance. Limitation: light on advanced tracking protection; browser-only.

## Better — Privacy-Focused, Ethically Sourced

Better is built by a two-person studio (Aral Balkan and Laura Kalbag) with a clear privacy-first philosophy. The blocklist is hand-curated — no automated scraping — and the team publishes their blocking criteria openly. Every entry in the list is there because it was manually reviewed and found to violate user privacy.

The upside of curation: very low false positives. Better rarely breaks websites because each block is deliberate. The downside: the list is smaller than EasyList-based tools, so some ad networks and trackers that AdGuard or 1Blocker catch will slip through.

Better works on Mac Safari and iPhone Safari (universal purchase). Like the other extensions here, it is browser-only — no system-level coverage. For users who care about the ethics behind the tool as much as the technical effectiveness, Better is the most transparent option in this comparison.

**Verdict:** The right choice if you want a blocker you can fully audit and trust on principle. Lighter block rate than AdGuard, but with strong integrity. Limitation: smallest blocklist; browser-only.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do Safari [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-firefox-2026/)s work differently from Chrome extensions?

Yes. Chrome extensions can intercept network requests directly. Safari uses a sandboxed content-blocking API where extensions submit a list of rules to the browser, and Safari applies them — the extension cannot read your browsing history or see which pages you visit. This makes Safari extensions more private by design, but also means they cannot do some of the dynamic filtering that [uBlock Origin](https://snipstack.io/ublock-origin-not-working-chrome-alternatives-2026/) does on Chrome. For most users, the difference in blocking quality is minimal.

### Can I use AdGuard for Safari on iPhone for free?

Yes. AdGuard offers a free Safari content blocker for iPhone and iPad that uses the same filter lists as the Mac extension. It covers ads and trackers within Safari. In-app ads (in games, free apps, etc.) and tracking pixels in Mail are not covered by the free version — those require AdGuard Pro or a system-level tool like CleanWeb.

### Will a Safari [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-chrome-2026/) break websites?

Occasionally. Paywalled news sites sometimes detect content blockers and refuse to show articles. Some sites with heavy custom ad implementations may display blank spaces where ads were. Well-maintained tools like AdGuard and 1Blocker have whitelists and issue fixes quickly when a popular site breaks. If a site stops working, temporarily disabling the extension for that domain (most tools offer a per-site toggle) is the standard workaround.

### Does CleanWeb [block ads in](https://snipstack.io/block-ads-in-free-games-ios-2026/) Safari the same way an extension does?

CleanWeb blocks at the DNS layer, which means it prevents your device from connecting to ad-serving domains at all. This catches the majority of ads in Safari. Some ads that are served from the same domain as the content (common in streaming apps) may not be blocked at the DNS level. For Safari specifically, combining CleanWeb with a lightweight extension like AdGuard gives the most complete coverage. ### Is it legal to use [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-reddit-2026/)s in 2026?

Yes, in all major jurisdictions. Ad blocking is a personal choice about what software runs on your device. Courts in Europe have consistently upheld users' right to block ads. Publishers may prefer you do not, but there is no legal issue with using any of the tools in this guide.

### Which option is best if I also use Windows or Android?

Safari extensions are Apple-platform only. If you work across Mac, Windows, and Android, CleanWeb is the only tool in this list that covers all three platforms through a single app and subscription. AdGuard also has separate Windows and Android apps, but those are distinct purchases from the Safari extension.
