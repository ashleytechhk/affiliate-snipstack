## Why a Phone Number Is More Dangerous Than You Think

Most people treat a phone number as low-stakes contact information. That instinct is wrong, and the asymmetry between what you think you're sharing and what you're actually exposing is significant.

A mobile number links to:

**Your carrier account.** Your carrier ties your number to your legal name and billing address. That relationship is the foundation of SIM swap fraud — where an attacker convinces your carrier to transfer your number to their device, then uses account recovery flows to take over your email, bank accounts, and social profiles. SIM swap attacks have surged because mobile numbers became the universal second factor for authentication.

**Reverse-lookup databases.** Dozens of services aggregate public records, data-broker files, and social platform data into searchable profiles accessible by phone number. Many are free. A reverse lookup on your number can surface your full name, current and historical addresses, family members, and in some cases workplace information — in under a minute, with no account required.

**Every service using SMS for two-factor authentication.** Your phone number is the recovery mechanism for most of your important accounts. Control over it is effectively control over your digital identity.

Dating apps create a specific window of exposure. You have spent a short time with someone you selected from a pool of strangers. You felt enough of a connection to move off the app. That judgment call about someone's character is made with very limited information. And once you share your real number, it stays with them permanently — even if you unmatch, even if things go badly, even if you block them on every other platform.

## The Three Things That Go Wrong

**Harassment.** A match that ended badly can text and call indefinitely. Blocking on the app closes one channel but leaves the number open. You end up in a reactive loop — blocking individual messages from an address you cannot revoke access to.

**Stalking.** This is where the reverse-lookup risk becomes physical. Studies of dating app safety incidents consistently identify phone number exchange as one of the primary escalation points for stalking situations. The path from "I have your number" to "I know where you live" is shorter than most people realize and requires no technical skill — just a free website.

**SIM swap and account takeover.** A determined bad actor who has your number and some patience can attempt to port it to a new device through social engineering at the carrier level. Once they have your number, they have your two-factor authentication. Banking, email, and social accounts become vulnerable through perfectly legitimate recovery processes designed to help people who lose their phones. The attacker is not hacking anything — they are using the system exactly as intended, with your number as the key.

None of these scenarios require the other person to have criminal intent from the start. Harassment often comes from someone who handles rejection poorly. Stalking can begin as curiosity. The problem is that sharing your real number creates a connection you cannot cleanly sever later.
