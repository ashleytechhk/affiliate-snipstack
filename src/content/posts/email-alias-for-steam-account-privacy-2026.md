## Why Steam Accounts Generate So Much Spam

Steam isn't the only source, but it's a reliable one. Here's what's feeding your inbox:

**Steam's own marketing.** Wishlist sale notifications, friend activity digests, group event invitations, publisher announcements. These are legitimate emails, but the volume adds up — and your preferences get reset with every platform update.

**Third-party trading and marketplace sites.** SteamTrades, Skinport, DMarket, CSGOFloat, and dozens of others all require account registration. Each is a separate data-breach surface. Several have been compromised in the past. When they leak, your email lands on spam lists.

**Phishing operations.** Fake "Steam Support" emails impersonating Valve's billing or trade confirmation system are a known, active threat. These are only effective if attackers have your email. They get it from the marketplace breaches above.

**Game key and giveaway sites.** Humble Bundle, Fanatical, key resellers, free key aggregators — most require account creation. Many sell address data to third-party marketers.

**Forum accounts.** Every game community forum, subreddit-companion site, or clan portal you've registered on is another potential source. Some are run by hobbyists with no security expertise.

The result: you've handed your real address to dozens of services, any of which could have already leaked it. Aliases let you contain the damage and trace the source.

## Why the Gmail `+tag` Trick Fails for Gamers

The Gmail plus-addressing trick is widely recommended in productivity circles. For Steam privacy, it's close to useless.

When you sign up as `yourname+steam@gmail.com`, mail still arrives in your regular Gmail inbox. That part works. But the base address — `yourname@gmail.com` — is visible to anyone who looks. Spam operations routinely strip everything after the `+` symbol before adding addresses to their lists. A breach that exposes `yourname+steam@gmail.com` immediately reveals your core address.

More critically: you can't kill it. If the alias leaks, you can't disable `yourname+steam@gmail.com` without disabling your entire Gmail account. There's no revocation. Your only options are inbox filters, which require constant maintenance, or changing your Gmail address entirely.

A proper alias service gives you an address that looks completely unrelated to your real account and can be deleted with one click the moment it turns bad.

## Dealing With Steam Phishing Emails

Fake "Steam Support" emails are one of the most common phishing vectors targeting gamers. They impersonate trade confirmations, account bans, item disputes, and billing issues. They're designed to look exactly like real Valve emails.

If your real email address is in the hands of phishing operators — which is likely if you've used any trading or marketplace site — you'll receive these regularly. No filter catches all of them, because the senders constantly change domains.

An alias strategy reduces this attack surface significantly. If your Steam alias is only known to Valve, phishing emails that arrive on that alias are clearly fake (since no third party has it). Phishing emails that arrive on your trading-site alias or your forum alias can be identified by source and the alias deleted. Either way, you have more signal and more control than inbox filtering gives you.
