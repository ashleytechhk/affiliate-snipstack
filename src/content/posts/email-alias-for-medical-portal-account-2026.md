## Why Medical Portals Are Spam Magnets

Healthcare platforms collect your email to send lab results, appointment confirmations, and billing notices. That is legitimate. The problem is what happens after: portals share data with affiliated networks, pharmacies, insurance partners, and marketing vendors. The legal basis is usually buried in a terms-of-service document nobody reads.

The result: your real email address ends up in data broker databases, where it can be bought, sold, and leaked. A 2024 breach report found that healthcare data — including email addresses — had a longer average "shelf life" in dark-web marketplaces than financial records, because victims are slower to notice and act.

An email alias breaks this chain at the source. The portal only ever sees the alias. Even if the portal sells its list, only the alias is exposed. You can delete or disable the alias at any time.

## SimpleLogin: Best for Power Users

SimpleLogin is an open-source alias service with a generous free tier (10 aliases) and a paid plan that removes limits. It integrates with Bitwarden's password manager and supports custom domains, so you can receive mail at `portal@yourprivatedomain.com` instead of a shared service domain.

The catch: setup takes longer. You need to configure DNS records if you use a custom domain, and the interface has a steeper learning curve than Alt-ID. For someone who just wants to stop spam from one healthcare portal with minimal friction, SimpleLogin is probably more than they need.

That said, if you manage email privacy across dozens of services and want open-source auditability, SimpleLogin is the better long-term tool.

## AnonAddy: Cheapest Paid Option

AnonAddy charges roughly $1 per month for unlimited aliases. It supports catch-all addresses (any email sent to `anything@yoursubdomain.anonaddy.com` arrives in your inbox), which is useful if you register with many portals quickly.

The main limitation is that the free tier restricts bandwidth, which can cause forwarding delays. For a medical portal where a password-reset email needs to arrive instantly, that is a real concern. The paid tier resolves it, but at that point Alt-ID's bundled pricing (which also covers a VPN) becomes more competitive on a per-feature basis.

## Feature Deep Dive: What to Check Before Choosing

### Reply Capability

When you contact a portal's support team or need to confirm an appointment change, you want to reply from the alias address. Alt-ID and SimpleLogin both support this. AnonAddy supports it on paid plans. Mailinator does not.

Medical records and portal accounts often span years. Your alias needs to stay active indefinitely, not expire after 24 hours. Any alias service you choose should let you keep aliases alive for as long as your portal account exists.

## FAQ

### Is it safe to use an email alias for a medical account?

Yes. An alias forwards mail to your real inbox just like a regular address. You still receive all legitimate communications from the portal — lab results, appointment reminders, billing notices — but your real address is never exposed. The alias acts as a buffer between the portal and your inbox.

### Will the portal reject an alias address?

Most portals accept any valid email format. Alias addresses from services like Alt-ID are fully functional email addresses that pass standard validation checks. If a portal specifically blocks known alias domains (rare, but possible), SimpleLogin's custom domain option lets you use an address that looks like a personal domain.

### Can I reply to emails from my medical portal using the alias?

Yes, with Alt-ID, SimpleLogin, and AnonAddy (paid). When you reply, the portal's helpdesk sees the alias as the sender. Your real address is not disclosed.

### What happens if I delete the alias by mistake?

Most alias services allow you to restore recently deleted aliases within a grace period. Alt-ID and SimpleLogin both offer this. That said, it is worth labeling aliases clearly (e.g., "Memorial Clinic Portal") so you do not delete the wrong one. If you do lose access, the process is to create a new alias and update your email in the portal settings — the same five-minute process described above.

### Do I need a separate email alias for every medical portal I use?

You do not have to, but it is good practice. If you use one alias across multiple portals and that alias is sold by one of them, all portals are affected. A unique alias per portal means a breach at one site cannot be traced back to your other accounts.
