## "But I Heard Macs Don't Get Viruses"

This is the most common reason seniors skip [antivirus](https://snipstack.io/vs-antivirus-2026/) protection on a Mac — and it is exactly what scammers count on.

Here is what is actually true:

- **Macs do get malware.** Adware, spyware, and ransomware all exist for macOS. Independent security researchers tracked over 670,000 Mac-specific malware samples in 2024 alone.
- **Phishing does not care what computer you use.** A [fake email](https://snipstack.io/fake-email-sender-with-attachment-2026/) from "Apple Support" asking you to verify your ID works just as well whether you are on a Mac, a Windows PC, or an iPhone. The link it sends you to is a website — and websites do not check what kind of computer opened them.
- **Fake tech-support scams are surging.** These begin with a pop-up on your screen that says something like "YOUR MAC HAS BEEN INFECTED — CALL THIS NUMBER IMMEDIATELY." The pop-up is a webpage trick. It is not a real warning from Apple. But it looks terrifying, and every year thousands of seniors call the number and hand over remote access to their computer.
- **The M3 chip is new, and some older security tools do not yet understand it.** Apple's own protections are well-optimized for M3. But gaps exist, particularly around browser-based threats and social engineering attacks.

The message is not "panic." The message is: your MacBook Air M3 is good hardware with solid baseline security, and it needs one extra layer of real-world protection to cover what Apple cannot.

## The Threats That Actually Affect Seniors in 2026

![Common online scams targeting MacBook Air seniors in 2026](/og/antivirus-for-macbook-air-m3-2026-inline-1.png)

Understanding the risk is the first step to protecting yourself. Here are the four threats most likely to affect a senior using a MacBook Air M3.

### 1. Phishing Emails That Look Like Real Ones

You receive an email from what appears to be Apple, your bank, or Medicare. It uses the correct logo. The text looks professional. It asks you to click a link and confirm your account details.

The link goes to a [fake website](https://snipstack.io/fake-website-detector-tool-2026/) that records everything you type — your password, your [Social Security](https://snipstack.io/social-security-scam-call-verify-2026/) number, your credit card number.

In 2026, these emails are generated by AI. They no longer have the spelling mistakes that used to make them easy to spot. They are personalized, polished, and convincing.

### 2. Fake Tech-Support Pop-Ups

While browsing a normal website, your screen suddenly fills with a warning: your Mac is infected, your personal files are at risk, you must call a [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/) immediately to fix it.

This is always a scam. Apple never communicates this way. The pop-up is a webpage that cannot actually do anything to your computer — but it is designed to frighten you into calling. If you call, the person on the other end will ask for remote access to "fix" the problem and then steal your financial information, install real malware, or demand payment for fake services.

A good antivirus program blocks the websites that display these pop-ups before they ever load.

### 3. Fake Shopping Websites

You search for a product and click a link that looks like a legitimate store. The prices are attractively low. You enter your credit card details and receive nothing — or receive a cheap counterfeit. More importantly, your card details are now in the hands of criminals.

### 4. Password Theft Through Malicious Downloads

A free PDF reader, a cooking recipe downloader, a font pack — small, innocent-looking files that come bundled with software that quietly steals saved passwords from your browser.

## Why Apple's Built-In Tools Are Not Enough

Apple builds two security tools into every Mac: **XProtect** and **Gatekeeper**. They are genuinely useful, and they are always running without you having to do anything.

But they have clear limits:

- **XProtect only knows threats Apple has already catalogued.** When a brand-new scam website goes live at 9 a.m., XProtect will not know about it until Apple issues an update — which can take days or weeks.
- **XProtect does not protect you while browsing.** It checks files you download and apps you open. It does not check every website you visit, every link you click in an email, or every pop-up that appears on your screen.
- **Gatekeeper only checks apps from unidentified developers.** It does not touch web-based threats at all.
- **Neither tool gives you any warning.** They work silently in the background. You will not see an alert saying "this website is dangerous" before you accidentally type in your banking password.

This is the gap that a dedicated antivirus fills. It adds a real-time layer on top of Apple's baseline that checks websites as you visit them, warns you about suspicious links before you click them, and blocks known malware even if Apple has not yet added it to XProtect's list.
