## What Exactly Is a VPN?

A VPN is software that creates a private, encrypted tunnel between your device and the internet. When you connect through a VPN server in another country, websites and apps see that server's location rather than your actual one in New Zealand.

Think of it like this: you are in Auckland, but you connect to a VPN server in a country where streaming prices are lower. From the streaming service's perspective, you appear to be a local customer in that region — and you get access to that region's pricing or catalogue.

There are three things a VPN does simultaneously:

- **Hides your real location.** Your IP address is replaced with the VPN server's IP.
- **Encrypts your traffic.** Data between your device and the VPN server is scrambled, making it harder for third parties to see what you are doing.
- **Routes around geographic restrictions.** Content or pricing locked to a specific region becomes reachable.

For the average New Zealander who just wants to save on subscriptions, encryption is a nice bonus. The location-switching feature is the real draw.

The result: a Netflix Standard subscription in New Zealand can cost nearly double what the equivalent tier costs in markets like Turkey, Argentina, or parts of Southeast Asia. Multiply that across three or four streaming services and the annual gap is significant.
