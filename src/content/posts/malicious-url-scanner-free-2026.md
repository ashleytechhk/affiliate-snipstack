## What Is a Malicious URL?

Before diving into the tools, it helps to understand what you are actually checking for.

A URL is the web address you see in your browser or inside a link — for example, `www.yourbank.com/login`. A malicious URL is an address that leads somewhere dangerous instead of somewhere legitimate.

Malicious URLs come in a few forms:

- **Phishing URLs** — designed to look like a real website so you enter your password or payment details on a fake page
- **Malware URLs** — visiting the address automatically downloads harmful software onto your device
- **Scam URLs** — pages designed to trick you into paying money for something fraudulent

The address itself often contains clues. A real bank might use `www.hsbc.com`. A fake version might look like `www.hsbc-account-verify.net` or `www.hsbc.com.update-now.xyz` — legitimate-looking words, but with something subtly wrong. These differences are easy to miss, especially on a phone screen where the full address is truncated.

A malicious URL scanner checks the address for exactly these patterns — and cross-references it against databases of known dangerous sites.

## How a Malicious URL Scanner Works

You do not need to understand the technical mechanics to use one effectively — but knowing [what happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) in the background helps you trust the results.

When you submit a URL to a malicious link scanner, it performs several checks almost simultaneously.

**It queries databases of known malicious sites.** Security researchers and companies maintain constantly updated lists of URLs that have been confirmed as dangerous. If the address you submitted matches an entry, the scanner flags it immediately. These databases collectively track millions of threats and update in near-real time.

**It examines the domain's age and history.** Legitimate businesses typically maintain websites that have been registered and active for years. Criminals set up new domains quickly — a site registered yesterday that claims to be your bank is a major red flag. A scanner can see this registration history.

**It analyses the URL structure itself.** Suspicious patterns include very long addresses packed with random characters, domains that mimic well-known brands with slight variations (replacing an "l" with a "1," for instance), and addresses using free hosting services that established companies rarely rely on.

**It checks email sender authentication records.** When an email arrives, the sending server leaves a digital signature — a technical record that proves whether the email genuinely came from the company it claims to be from. Surfshark's Email Scam Checker reads these records automatically, so you get a verdict on the email as a whole, not just the individual link.

The result of all these checks is a plain-language verdict. You do not need to know what DMARC records or DNS reputation scoring means — the tool translates the technical findings into a clear answer: this looks safe, or this looks dangerous.

## Types of Emails That Commonly Contain Malicious URLs

Knowing the most common formats helps you recognise them faster.

**Parcel delivery notifications.** A message claiming your package is held, awaiting a small customs fee, or needs address confirmation. These are among the most commonly sent scam emails globally. Genuine delivery companies do not request payment by clicking an email link.

**Bank and account security alerts.** "Your account has been locked. Verify your identity immediately to restore access." Real banks contact you through their official app or by telephone if there is a genuine problem. Urgency in an email is almost always a manipulation tactic.

**Tax and government correspondence.** Fake notices claiming you are owed a refund, or that you owe an outstanding amount. Government agencies communicate through official post or their own authenticated portals — not through email links asking for immediate payment.

**Invoice and supplier payment requests.** An unexpected invoice or request to change payment details for an existing supplier. These are often targeted at small business owners or anyone who manages regular payments.

**Subscription renewal warnings.** Fake alerts claiming a service you use is about to expire or that a charge is pending. These exploit the fact that most people have multiple active subscriptions and may not remember the exact renewal dates.

When one of these arrives and you feel any uncertainty at all, copy the link and run it through a scanner before doing anything else.
