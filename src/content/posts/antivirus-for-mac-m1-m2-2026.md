## Why the "Macs Don't Get Viruses" Idea Is Dangerous

For many years, the low number of Mac users meant criminals focused their efforts on Windows computers. That changed. By 2023, Macs accounted for roughly 15% of the personal computer market — a large enough audience to be worth targeting. Security researchers at Malwarebytes reported a 61% increase in Mac-specific threats between 2022 and 2024.

The M1 and M2 chips, introduced from 2020 onwards, added new hardware-level security features. Apple deserves credit for that. But hardware security is only one layer. The threats most likely to harm a senior Mac owner do not attack the chip at all. They attack you — through your inbox, your browser, and your trust.

Here is what the real threat landscape looks like for Mac users over 60.

## The 4 Threats That Target Older Mac Owners

### 1. Phishing Emails and Fake Login Pages

You receive an email that looks like it came from Apple, your bank, or a delivery company. The message says your account has been suspended, a payment failed, or a parcel could not be delivered. There is a link. You click it. The page looks identical to the real website. You type your email and password. The criminal now has your login credentials.

This attack works on every computer — M1, M2, Windows, or anything else. The chip does not matter. Neither does how careful you are, because the best phishing pages today are near-perfect copies of real websites.

In 2024, phishing was the single most reported cybercrime type in the United States, accounting for over 298,000 complaints to the FBI. Older adults were the most financially harmed group.

### 2. Fake Tech Support Scams

A pop-up appears on your screen. It says your Mac has been infected. It displays a phone number and tells you to call immediately. You call. A friendly person answers, explains the "problem," and asks to connect to your computer remotely to fix it.

They are not fixing anything. They are searching your files for bank account numbers, passwords, and personal information. Sometimes they install software that lets them return to your computer whenever they want, without asking.

Apple will never contact you by pop-up. Microsoft will never call you unsolicited. Any message that creates urgent panic and asks you to act immediately is almost certainly a scam.

### 3. Adware and Browser Hijackers

You download a free program — a PDF reader, a video converter, a recipe app — and a few days later your browser starts behaving strangely. A new search engine appears that you did not choose. Advertisements pop up on pages that never had them before. Your home page changed overnight.

These are signs of adware or a browser hijacker. They are not as dramatic as ransomware, but they collect your browsing history, expose you to more dangerous ads and links, and slow down your Mac. They arrive through downloads that look completely innocent.

### 4. Data Breaches That Expose Your Passwords

This one does not involve you clicking anything wrong. A company you have an account with — a retailer, an airline, a healthcare provider — suffers a data breach. Your email address and password are now available for criminals to buy on underground markets. If you use the same password on multiple sites (which most people do), one breach becomes access to many accounts.

According to the Identity Theft Resource Center, there were over 3,200 data breaches in the United States in 2023 alone. Most people find out months later, or never.

## What Apple's Built-In Protection Does (and Does Not) Do

Every Mac, including M1 and M2 models, comes with three security tools built in.

**XProtect** is a background malware scanner. It checks files as they are opened and compares them against a list of known malware signatures that Apple updates regularly. It handles the most common, well-documented threats automatically.

**Gatekeeper** stops apps from unknown sources from opening. When you download an app that Apple has not reviewed, Gatekeeper blocks it and asks whether you really want to open it.

**The Firewall** controls which programs are allowed to send and receive data over the internet.

These tools are genuinely useful. For a Mac owner who only visits a small number of trusted websites and never clicks links in emails, they may be sufficient.

The gaps matter for most real-world users:

- XProtect does not block phishing websites in real time. If you click a link in an email that takes you to a fake bank page, XProtect does not warn you before you type your password.
- XProtect only catches threats it already knows about. A brand-new piece of malware that has not yet been added to Apple's database passes through silently.
- None of Apple's tools monitor data breaches. If your email and password from a past breach are being used against you right now, Apple has no way to tell you.
- Tech support scam pop-ups are not stopped by XProtect or Gatekeeper — they appear in your browser and require no download at all.

This is the gap that an antivirus fills.

## Does the M1 or M2 Chip Change Anything?

The short answer: for most of these threats, no.

The M1 and M2 chips include Apple's Secure Enclave, a dedicated hardware module that protects things like Face ID and encryption keys. This is genuinely excellent security against certain types of attack — particularly those trying to extract stored credentials at the hardware level.

But phishing attacks, tech support scams, browser hijackers, and data breaches do not operate at the hardware level. They operate at the human level — through an email you open, a link you click, a number you call. The chip architecture is irrelevant to a phishing page that tricks you into typing your own password.

The M1 and M2 are faster chips, not invisible shields. Treating them as a reason not to run antivirus software is a misunderstanding of how modern attacks actually work.
