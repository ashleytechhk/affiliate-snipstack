## What Is a Network Attached Storage Device?

A Network Attached Storage device — most people call it a NAS — is a small box that connects to your home Wi-Fi router. It stores files just like a USB hard drive, but every device in your house (phones, tablets, laptops) can access those files wirelessly.

Many seniors use a NAS to:

- Back up family photos and home videos
- Store important documents like tax returns and medical records
- Share files between a laptop and a tablet without emailing them

It sounds convenient. And it is — until someone uninvited finds their way in.

The problem is that most NAS devices are connected to the internet by default. That means they are visible to anyone who knows where to look. Criminals know where to look.

## [How Does a](https://snipstack.io/how-does-a-vpn-protect-you-2026/) NAS Attack Actually Happen?

You do not have to do anything wrong for your NAS to be attacked. Here is how it typically happens in plain language:

**Step 1: The scanner.** A criminal runs an automated program that scans millions of home internet connections, looking for NAS devices that are visible online. This takes seconds per address and costs almost nothing.

**Step 2: The entry point.** If your NAS has a default password (like "admin" or "1234"), or if the software running on your NAS has not been updated, the attacker can walk straight in.

**Step 3: The infection.** The attacker installs ransomware or spyware onto your NAS — or onto a laptop connected to it. The malware spreads silently.

**Step 4: The lock.** You wake up one morning and find that all your files are scrambled. A message appears demanding payment to unlock them.

What makes this especially frightening is the speed. The entire process — from scan to encryption — can happen overnight while you sleep. You would have no warning.

## Why Your NAS Itself Is Not Enough Protection

You might be thinking: "But my NAS has a built-in firewall." Or: "My router has security settings."

These features help, but they are not enough on their own. Here is why:

**Built-in NAS firewalls** block some types of traffic but cannot detect malware that arrives through a legitimate-looking connection — like an email attachment you opened on your laptop that then spread to the NAS.

**Router security** protects traffic coming in from outside your network, but it does not scan files for viruses. Once malware is inside your home network (on your phone, tablet, or laptop), your router cannot stop it from reaching your NAS.

**No antivirus on connected devices** means that when you connect your laptop to the NAS to move photos, any virus on your laptop can transfer directly to the NAS drive.

The only way to close this gap is to run real-time antivirus protection on every device that connects to your NAS.
