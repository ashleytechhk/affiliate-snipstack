## Why "Just Use the App" Is Not Enough Privacy Protection

Every major dating app claims to keep your personal information private. The reality is more complicated.

When you sign up for Tinder, Bumble, Hinge, or any of the major platforms, your phone number enters an ecosystem that extends far beyond the dating app itself. Your number is a persistent identifier — one that ad-tech platforms, data brokers, and people-search services use to link your dating profile to your real name, your home address, your employer, your social media accounts, and your relatives.

The 2023 Mozilla Foundation privacy report on dating apps found that the majority of major platforms share or sell user data with third-party advertising partners in ways that most users do not anticipate when reading the signup screen. Phone numbers were specifically identified as high-risk data because they are permanent, tied to real-world identity through carrier records, and almost never changed the way an email address can be.

Once your number is in the data broker pipeline, it stays there. Opt-out requests are slow, manually intensive, and frequently reversed by automated scrapers that re-harvest data within weeks. The number you typed into a dating app signup form two years ago is likely indexed across a dozen people-search sites right now.

This is the exposure that happens before you ever give your number to a specific match. The moment you actually share it — in a chat, before a first call, when you want to "move off the app" — the risk escalates from ambient to direct.
