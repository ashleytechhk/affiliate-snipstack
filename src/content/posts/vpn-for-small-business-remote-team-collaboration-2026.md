## What Is a VPN, and Why Does "Business" Change Anything?

A VPN — Virtual Private Network — creates an encrypted tunnel between a device and the internet. Instead of your team member's laptop sending raw traffic through whatever network they're on, the VPN wraps every packet in strong encryption and routes it through a private server. Anyone snooping on the coffee-shop Wi-Fi sees noise, not your Slack messages or client files.

A **consumer** VPN is designed for one person streaming or browsing privately. A **business** VPN is designed for a team. The practical differences:

- **Multiple simultaneous connections** without paying per seat
- **Consistent server options** so every team member exits from the same country when needed
- **Stable performance** for video calls and file transfers, not just web browsing
- **Centralized billing** so one invoice covers the whole team

Surfshark hits all four points and adds something rare: truly unlimited device connections under a single subscription. A five-person team with two work devices each? One account. A twenty-person team where half also work from personal laptops? Still one account.

## Why Remote Teams Are Specifically at Risk

When your team worked in one office, traffic flowed through one managed network. One firewall, one IT team, one set of rules. Remote work shatters that model.

Consider what happens on a typical remote workday:

1. A designer logs into Figma from a rental apartment in Lisbon on the building's shared Wi-Fi.
2. A developer pushes code to GitHub from a co-working space in Buenos Aires over their mobile hotspot.
3. A sales rep joins a Zoom call from a hotel in Tokyo using the hotel's unencrypted network.

Each of those connections is a potential interception point. Without a VPN, credentials, client data, and internal documents travel over networks you have no control over. A single credential capture can compromise your entire stack — your CRM, your cloud storage, your billing system.

A VPN running on each device means none of that matters. The traffic is encrypted before it leaves the device, regardless of what network it's on.

Buy a single subscription and keep the credentials in your team's password manager (1Password, Bitwarden, etc.). Every team member installs the app on their devices using the same account login.

### Step 3: Set Connection Standards

Tell your team which server location to use for day-to-day work (usually the country closest to your main clients or servers), and which locations to use when accessing regional software pricing. Write this down — even a one-page Notion doc works.

### Step 4: Enable Auto-Connect

Every major VPN app, including Surfshark, has an auto-connect feature that fires up the VPN whenever the device joins a network. Turn this on for everyone. The human factor is the biggest security gap: people forget to connect manually.

### Step 5: Use Split Tunneling for Performance

Split tunneling lets you route specific apps through the VPN while everything else goes direct. For video calls where latency matters, you might want Zoom or Google Meet to bypass the VPN while your Slack and cloud storage go through it. Surfshark supports this on all major platforms.

## What to Look for in a VPN for Small Business Remote Teams

Not every VPN is built for team use. Here's a checklist of features that actually matter:

**Unlimited simultaneous connections** — Non-negotiable if you're covering a team. Per-device pricing destroys the value proposition.

**Strong encryption** — AES-256 is the current standard. Any reputable VPN uses it. Don't settle for less.

**No-logs policy** — Your business data is your business. A VPN that keeps activity logs is a liability. Surfshark has an audited no-logs policy.

**Kill switch** — If the VPN connection drops, a kill switch cuts internet access entirely rather than letting traffic leak unencrypted. Essential for anyone handling sensitive client data.

**Wide server network** — More countries means more flexibility for both security and regional pricing access.

**Multi-platform apps** — Your team uses Windows, Mac, iOS, Android, and probably a Linux box somewhere. The VPN needs native apps for all of them.
