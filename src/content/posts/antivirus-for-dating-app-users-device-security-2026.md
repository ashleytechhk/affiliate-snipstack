## Why Dating Apps Are a Goldmine for Hackers

### You Are More Trusting When You Are Hopeful

Dating apps work by encouraging you to open up to strangers. You share photos, talk about your life, and gradually lower your guard. Criminals exploit this dynamic on purpose. A scammer who has spent two weeks building a warm relationship with you has a much better chance of getting you to tap a link than a cold phishing email from a stranger.

This is why malware attacks through dating apps follow a predictable pattern: build trust first, then strike.

### Common Tactics Used Against Seniors

**Malicious links in messages.** Your match sends you a link — "Look at this article I found" or "Here are more photos of me on this site." One click and a file begins downloading in the background.

**Fake app downloads.** They ask you to switch to a different app to "chat more privately." The app they send you is not from an official store. It is malware disguised as a messenger.

**Phishing pages.** They share a link to what looks like a normal website — a news article, a photo album, a gift they want to send. The page is a fake designed to steal your login details or install tracking software.

**QR codes.** Increasingly, scammers ask you to scan a QR code they "accidentally" sent. QR codes can redirect your browser to dangerous sites just as easily as a typed link.

## What Happens If Your Device Gets Infected

This is where things get serious, and it is worth being direct.

If malware lands on your phone or tablet, here is what it can do:

- **Log your keystrokes** — recording every password you type, including online banking
- **Access your camera and microphone** — without your knowledge
- **Read your messages and emails** — including two-factor authentication codes that unlock your accounts
- **Copy your photos** — which may include photos of documents, credit cards, or personal images

None of this requires you to hand over any information voluntarily. The malware does it quietly, in the background, while your phone sits on the kitchen counter.

For seniors who use their phone for banking, health records, or family communication, the damage from a single infection can be severe and long-lasting.

## Why Built-In Phone Security Is Not Enough

Both iPhones and Android phones come with some built-in safety features. Apple's App Store and Google's Play Store screen apps before listing them. Android has Google Play Protect running in the background.

These are useful. But they have gaps.

**They do not scan links you receive in dating apps.** If someone sends you a malicious URL through a chat message inside the app, your phone's built-in tools will not flag it before you tap.

**They do not catch all malicious apps.** Harmful apps have made it through official stores before, and if a scammer talks you into installing something from outside the store, there is no official screening at all.

**They cannot warn you about phishing pages** that are freshly created. Scammers regularly build new fake websites specifically to slip past block lists.

A dedicated antivirus layer fills these gaps. It is not a replacement for your phone's built-in defenses — it works alongside them.
