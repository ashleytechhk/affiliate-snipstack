## Why Your [Phone Number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-alias-for-work-2026/) Is More Dangerous Than You Think

Most people treat their phone number like a username — public, throwaway, harmless. It is not.

Here is what someone can do with your number in under ten minutes using free tools:

- **Reverse-lookup your full name and current address** via data broker aggregators
- **Find your social media profiles** linked to the same number (most platforms use phone as a secondary identifier)
- **SIM-swap your mobile account** by calling your carrier, impersonating you, and redirecting your two-factor authentication texts to their own device
- **Cross-reference your number against leaked databases** to find the email address and passwords you reused on other sites

The scariest part? None of this requires hacking skills. It requires patience and a Google search.

## The Bigger Risk: SIM Swapping

SIM swap fraud deserves its own mention because it is the most financially damaging attack that starts with your phone number.

Here is how it works: an attacker calls your mobile carrier, claims to be you, and asks to transfer your number to a new SIM they control. Carriers often ask basic verification questions that can be answered using information freely available on social media or data broker sites. Once they have your number, every SMS-based two-factor authentication code — for your bank, your crypto wallet, your email — goes to them instead of you.

Losses from SIM swap attacks have reached into the hundreds of thousands of dollars for individual victims. The FBI flagged a sharp rise in cases in both 2022 and 2023.

Using an alt number for public-facing activity means your real number is less visible online. Fewer strangers have it. Fewer data broker records list it. That reduces the surface area for a SIM swap attempt.
