## Why Campaign Volunteers Share Their Numbers Without Thinking

Political campaigns run on personal contact. When you sign up to phone-bank, canvass a neighborhood, or coordinate voter registration drives, sharing your number feels like a basic part of the job. Field organizers need to text the shift schedule. Other volunteers need to reach you when plans change. Local journalists sometimes contact volunteers for quick comments. All of that seems reasonable in the moment.

The problem is what happens to your number once it enters campaign infrastructure. A volunteer coordinator exports the contact sheet to a personal cloud folder. That folder gets shared with five more people to streamline coordination. One of those people leaves the campaign after a disagreement — and still has access. A 2024 digital security audit by the Electronic Frontier Foundation found that volunteer contact lists are among the least protected data sets in grassroots political organizing, largely because they are built in a hurry and rarely deleted after the campaign wraps.

For volunteers who are also active on dating apps or social media, the exposure stacks. If your phone number appears in two separate contexts — a campaign contact roster and a Hinge profile — it becomes straightforward for anyone motivated to cross-reference both and build a detailed picture of your workplace, your schedule, and your home neighborhood.

## What Actually Happens When the Wrong Person Gets Your Real Number

The threat is not hypothetical. Here is the concrete chain of events that plays out when your number reaches the wrong hands.

**Persistent contact harassment.** Someone with your number can call or text from any device they own or borrow. Blocking works only until they switch numbers. Your real number does not change.

**Location inference.** There are documented cases of abusers purchasing aggregated location data from data brokers who work with carrier-linked records. Your phone number is the key that unlocks that lookup. This is not a theoretical attack — it has been used against domestic abuse survivors repeatedly.

**Identity bridging.** Most major messaging and social platforms — WhatsApp, Telegram, Signal, Snapchat — display a profile when a phone number is added to someone's contacts. One reverse lookup can immediately surface your profile photo, display name, and mutual connections across multiple platforms.

**Smishing and phishing escalation.** Political volunteer contact lists, when leaked or sold, are common targets for SMS phishing campaigns impersonating the original campaign. Volunteers who used their real number receive credential-harvesting texts that look exactly like legitimate campaign updates.

None of these scenarios require a sophisticated attacker. They require only that one person — out of the dozens who may have seen your number on a shared spreadsheet or in a screenshot — decides to act on it. You cannot control that. You can control whether the number they have is your real one.
