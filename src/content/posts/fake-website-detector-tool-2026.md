## Google Safe Browsing — The Background Safety Net

Google Safe Browsing runs silently inside Chrome, Firefox, and Safari. Every time you visit a site, your browser checks the address against Google's database of known phishing pages and malware sites. If there is a match, a red warning screen appears before the page loads.

**What it does well:** You never have to think about it. The protection is there whether or not you remember to check, covering billions of visits per day. For widely reported, high-volume scam sites that have accumulated enough victim reports to appear in Google's database, it works well.

**Where it falls short:** Safe Browsing operates from a cached blocklist. A fake website created three days ago — the kind set up specifically for a targeted email campaign — is unlikely to appear in that list yet. If a scammer registers a lookalike domain today, sends you an email tonight, and you click the link tomorrow, Safe Browsing may show nothing unusual. It also tells you nothing about whether the email that sent you the link was legitimate — it only acts once you are about to load the page.

**Best for:** A safety net for high-volume known threats. Not a substitute for checking unfamiliar email links before clicking them.

## VirusTotal — The Most Thorough Manual Check

VirusTotal (owned by Google) passes a URL through more than 70 [antivirus](https://snipstack.io/vs-antivirus-2026/) and security engines simultaneously and shows you exactly which ones flagged it and what category they assigned — phishing, malware, spam, or clean. The depth of the result is unmatched by any free tool.

**What it does well:** If a URL comes back clean across 70 independent engines, that is a meaningful confidence signal. The interface also shows WHOIS data (who registered the domain, and when), community annotations, and historical scan results — context that helps you understand not just whether a site is dangerous, but why.

**Where it falls short:** The output is designed for people comfortable reading security tool data. Seeing rows of engine names, detection categories, and confidence levels is not useful if you do not know how to interpret them. VirusTotal also has no concept of email authentication — it checks URLs, not whether the sender of an email was who they claimed to be. Every check requires manually copying the URL and pasting it into the site.

**Best for:** Users comfortable with technical output who want the most thorough one-off URL check available.

## ScamAdviser — Readable Trust Score

ScamAdviser assigns websites a trust score from 0 to 100 using signals like [domain age](https://snipstack.io/domain-age-checker-scam-prevention-2026/), hosting location, contact information, and whether the site appears on known blacklists. The score is displayed with a brief explanation — for example, "this domain was registered 12 days ago" or "no return policy detected."

**What it does well:** The format is intuitive. You do not need to know what DKIM stands for — a score of 22 out of 100 with a note saying the domain is very new communicates the risk clearly. For checking shopping or services sites you are unfamiliar with, the trust score format is beginner-friendly.

**Where it falls short:** ScamAdviser is slower to flag new scam domains than engine-based tools. The trust scoring system can also be influenced by surface-level signals — a well-designed fake site can score higher than its actual risk level warrants. There is no email sender verification. Every check requires a manual copy-and-paste.

**Best for:** Non-technical users investigating an unfamiliar shopping or service website, rather than analysing an email link under time pressure.

## URLVoid — Fast Multi-Blacklist Lookup

URLVoid checks a domain against more than 30 security databases simultaneously — PhishTank, Sucuri, SURBL, OpenPhish, and others — and returns a pass/fail from each one. Basic WHOIS data (registration date, country) is shown alongside the results.

**What it does well:** Speed and breadth. For a quick cross-reference against established blocklists, URLVoid covers more databases in one check than most people would access individually. If a domain is actively listed as a phishing host by multiple services, URLVoid will surface it.

**Where it falls short:** URLVoid only reports what already exists in the databases it queries. A scam domain registered this week, not yet reported and added to blocklists, will return a clean result even if it is actively being used in phishing campaigns. There is no email authentication checking, no [browser extension](https://snipstack.io/browser-extension-to-check-scam-sites-2026/), and no alert system. Like the other manual tools, it requires you to remember to check and to take the time to do it.

**Best for:** A quick secondary lookup when another tool has flagged a domain and you want confirmation from additional sources.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a fake website detector tool?

A fake website detector tool checks whether a web address is likely to be fraudulent, phishing-related, or associated with malware. It cross-references the domain against security databases, checks domain registration history, and looks for technical signals associated with scam sites. Results may come as a pass/fail, a trust score, or a breakdown across multiple security engines depending on the tool.

### Can a fake website look completely professional?

Yes. Modern scam sites can be nearly indistinguishable from the real organisations they impersonate. Scammers copy logos, layouts, and even customer support chat widgets from legitimate sites. The visual presentation of a website is not a reliable indicator of whether it is safe. Domain-level checks — including how recently it was registered and whether the sender authentication records match — are more reliable signals than how polished the design looks.

### Why do suspicious emails land in my inbox instead of spam?

Email spam filters catch a large volume of obvious scam messages, but they are not perfect. Phishing emails that use freshly registered domains, impersonate legitimate senders convincingly, or are sent in targeted small batches (rather than millions at once) can pass through filters before enough reports accumulate to trigger blocking. This is why a separate checking step — using a tool like Surfshark's Email Scam Checker — adds meaningful protection that inbox spam filtering alone does not provide.

### Is it safe to paste a suspicious link into a checker?

Yes. Copying a URL from an email and pasting it into a checker is not the same as clicking the link. You are sending the address as text to the checking service. Your browser never loads the suspicious page, and no files from that site can reach your device through this action. It is completely safe to do.

### What should I do if a link is flagged as dangerous?

Do not click it. Delete the email. If the message claimed to be from your bank, a delivery company, or any other organisation you deal with, do not use any [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/) or link from that email — go directly to their official website by typing the address yourself, or call the number printed on your card or a previous statement. If you already clicked before checking and entered any details, contact your bank immediately and change the passwords for any accounts you entered credentials into.

### Do I need to pay to use a fake website detector tool?

Several tools on this list are free, including Google Safe Browsing (built into your browser), VirusTotal, ScamAdviser, and URLVoid. Surfshark's Email Scam Checker is also free for individual link and email address checks — no account required. A Surfshark subscription adds ongoing protection, including alerts if your email address has appeared in a data breach. ### Can any tool catch a scam site that was set up this week?

No tool can identify a site with complete certainty if it has never been seen before. Tools that rely entirely on existing blocklists — including Google Safe Browsing, URLVoid, and parts of ScamAdviser — will return clean results for brand-new scam domains. Surfshark's checker also analyses domain registration patterns and technical signals beyond simple blocklist matching, which gives it better coverage of newer threats. For very fresh domains, checking manually whether the domain is a few days old (via a WHOIS lookup) and whether the sender address passes authentication checks adds another layer of protection.
