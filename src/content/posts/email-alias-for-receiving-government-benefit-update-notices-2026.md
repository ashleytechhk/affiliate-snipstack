### Gmail's `+tag` Trick

Appending `+benefits` to your Gmail address (`yourname+benefits@gmail.com`) is free and mail still lands in your primary inbox, which makes it tempting for anything you don't want to fully commit an alias service to.

The catch: many benefit navigator apps and nonprofit sign-up forms strip everything after the `+` before storing your address. The tag disappears, your base Gmail address goes into their CRM, and now every partner newsletter that follows lands in the one inbox you can never revoke. There's no way to tell which sign-up caused the flood, and no kill switch once it starts.

It's a reasonable habit for services you already trust. It's the wrong tool for a nonprofit navigator app or a benefit-related newsletter you're testing out for the first time.

### Apple Hide My Email

Included with iCloud+ (from $0.99/month), Hide My Email creates aliases that forward to your iCloud address and delete instantly from Settings or iCloud.com. Safari's autofill makes sign-up frictionless if you're already in the Apple ecosystem.

The limitation is platform lock-in — if your daily inbox is Gmail or Outlook, routing through iCloud adds a step most people skip. It also doesn't generate alternative identity data, so your real name still lands in a navigator app's records even when the email doesn't.

For Apple users who already pay for iCloud+, it's a workable option for benefit-adjacent newsletters. For everyone else, it adds friction that a dedicated alias tool avoids.

### Free Throwaway Services (Guerrilla Mail, 10 Minute Mail)

Temporary inboxes work for one-off confirmation links, not for anything you'll need to reference weeks later.

For benefit update notices, they fail on the two points that actually matter:

1. **Blocked at sign-up.** Nonprofit and third-party benefit tools increasingly reject known disposable domains outright, so registration for a navigator app or newsletter may simply fail.
2. **They expire before the next update arrives.** A recertification reminder that lands three weeks after you created a 10-minute inbox goes nowhere. You'd need to keep recreating addresses and re-registering, which defeats the point of getting updates in the first place.

They're fine for a single click-through. They're the wrong choice for anything you expect to hear from again.
