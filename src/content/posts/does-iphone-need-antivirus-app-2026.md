## Why Scammers Love Targeting People Over 60

Fraud targeting older adults has grown every year for the past decade. The reasons are not flattering to the scammers, but they are worth understanding so you can spot the tactics.

Older adults are more likely to:
- Have savings that make a theft meaningful
- Answer phone calls from unknown numbers
- Trust official-looking emails and texts
- Feel embarrassed about reporting a scam once it has happened

The scams themselves have evolved. They no longer look like the old "Nigerian prince" emails. Today's attacks arrive as:

- A text message from "Apple" saying your account is locked
- An email from "your bank" asking you to verify your details
- A pop-up on a website warning you that your phone has a virus (it doesn't — but they want you to call a number)
- A fake delivery notification asking you to click a link to rebook

None of these attacks need to install a virus on your phone. They just need one moment of confusion to get your password, your card number, or access to your account.

## The Truth About Viruses on iPhones

Here is something you might find surprising: iPhones almost never get traditional viruses.

Apple builds iPhones so that every app runs in its own sealed box. One app cannot look inside another, and no app can reach deep into the operating system the way a virus needs to. Apple also checks every app before it appears in the App Store.

This is genuinely good news. It means the pop-ups you sometimes see saying "your iPhone has 3 viruses — call this number immediately" are lies. Your phone does not have a virus. That pop-up is the scam.

But here is the catch: **"no viruses" does not mean "no risk."** The threats that actually target iPhone users are different — and many people have no [protection against](https://snipstack.io/protection-against-fileless-malware-attacks-2026/) them at all.

## The Three Real Threats iPhone Users Face in 2026

### 1. Phishing Links

A phishing link is a fake website designed to look exactly like a real one. The scammer sends you a link by text, email, or social media. You click it. The page looks like your bank's website. You type in your details. They now have your password.

No virus was installed. Your iPhone was not "hacked" in the dramatic Hollywood sense. But your account may now be accessed by someone else.

A good security tool checks links before you open them. If the destination is a known fake or dangerous page, it blocks it and warns you. That warning — the five seconds between your tap and landing on a fake page — can save your account.

### 2. Data Breaches and Stolen Passwords

Every year, large companies get hacked and millions of passwords are stolen. These lists of stolen passwords are then sold to other criminals who try them on banking sites, email accounts, and shopping platforms.

You may have an account on a website that was breached years ago, and not even know about it. If you used the same password on multiple sites — which most people do — that one stolen password opens many doors.

A breach monitoring tool watches databases of stolen passwords and emails. If your details appear, it sends you an alert so you can change your password before someone uses it.

### 3. Unsafe Public Wi-Fi

When you connect to Wi-Fi at a café, a hotel, or an airport, you are on the same network as strangers. On many public networks, someone with the right software can see what information you're sending — including login details, if the website you're visiting doesn't protect them properly.

A VPN (Virtual Private Network) fixes this. It wraps your internet traffic in a private tunnel so that nobody on the same Wi-Fi network can read it. Think of it like sending a sealed envelope instead of a postcard.

## Three Simple Habits That Help More Than Any App

Even the [best security](https://snipstack.io/best-security-suite-for-mixed-device-household-2026/) tool works better when you pair it with a few habits:

**Be suspicious of unexpected messages.** If a text or email is urgent — "your account will be closed," "verify now," "you have won a prize" — pause before clicking. Real banks and real companies do not rush you. Call the company directly using the number on their official website, not any number given in the message.

**Check the web address before you type your password.** Fake websites often have addresses that look almost right — "app1e.com" instead of "apple.com," or "natwestsecure.com" instead of "natwest.com." Look at the full address in the browser bar before entering any details.

**Use different passwords for important accounts.** At minimum, your email account and your bank account should have passwords you use nowhere else. A password manager app can remember them for you.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does my iPhone really need an antivirus app?

Not a traditional virus-scanning app — those cannot function on iPhone due to Apple's security design. But your iPhone does benefit from tools that block phishing links, monitor for stolen passwords, and protect your connection on public Wi-Fi. Those threats are real for iPhone users.

### Can my iPhone get a virus from a website?

Extremely unlikely if your iPhone is not jailbroken. Visiting a website can expose you to phishing (a fake page designed to steal your password), but it cannot install a virus through the Safari browser on a standard iPhone. If a pop-up tells you that you have a virus, close the browser tab — the pop-up itself is the scam.

### What is phishing and how do I avoid it?

Phishing is when a scammer creates a fake website that looks like a real one — your bank, Apple, Amazon — and tricks you into typing your login details. A security tool with a link filter can block known phishing sites before you land on them. Your own habit of checking the web address also helps enormously.

### Is a VPN really necessary on an iPhone?

It is most useful when you connect to Wi-Fi away from home — at a café, airport, hotel, or library. On public Wi-Fi, others on the same network could potentially intercept your traffic. A VPN prevents that. On your own home broadband, a VPN matters less, but it adds no harm.
