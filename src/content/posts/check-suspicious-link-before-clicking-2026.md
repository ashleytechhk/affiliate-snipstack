## What Does It Mean to Check a Suspicious Link Before Clicking?

A "link" in an email is the underlined or highlighted text you tap or click to go to a website. Scammers create fake websites that look identical to real ones — your bank, Amazon, the post office — and send emails with links pointing to those fake pages. When you enter your password or payment details there, the scammer captures them.

**Checking a suspicious link before clicking** means running that link through a safety tool before you visit it. The tool inspects where the link actually leads — not where the email claims it leads — and tells you whether the destination is known to be dangerous.

Think of it like checking the address on an envelope before opening the door. The label might say "your bank," but the tool can see the real return address. If they do not match, you know not to open it.

You do not need to understand the technical details to use one — but knowing [what happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) in the background helps you trust the result.

When you submit a suspicious link to a checking tool, it does several things almost instantly:

**1. It looks up the destination address.** A link in an email can hide its true destination. "Click here to view your parcel" might actually lead to a domain like `parcel-track-confirm-secure.xyz` — nothing to do with any courier company. The checker reveals this real address before you visit it.

**4. It checks the email sender's authenticity.** Legitimate companies are required to set up technical records that prove their emails actually come from them. The checker verifies these records without you needing to know what they are.

## Other Signs a Link or Email Is Suspicious

Even before running a check, these patterns are reliable warning signals:

- **The sender address does not match the company name.** "Amazon Customer Service" sending from `noreply@amazon-secure-update.net` is not Amazon.
- **Generic greetings.** Real companies who have your account know your name. "Dear Valued Customer" is a red flag.
- **Urgent or threatening language.** "Your account will be suspended immediately." Scammers manufacture pressure to stop you thinking clearly.
- **Links that show a different address when you hover over them.** On a computer, hovering your mouse over a link without clicking shows the real destination in your browser's status bar. If it does not match the displayed text, do not click.
- **Requests for personal information by email.** Banks and government offices will not ask for passwords, PINs, or full account numbers via email.

When something feels off, it usually is. The habit of checking a suspicious link before clicking it adds a final layer of confirmation to that instinct.
