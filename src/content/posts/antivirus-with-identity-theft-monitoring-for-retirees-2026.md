## Why Retirees Are a Prime Target

Scammers are not choosing older adults at random. There are practical reasons retirees show up disproportionately in fraud reports:

- **Retirement savings are a lump sum worth chasing.** A scammer would rather target one account holding decades of savings than dozens of accounts with small balances.
- **Medicare and Social Security numbers rarely change.** Once a retiree's number is exposed in a breach, it stays valuable to criminals for years.
- **Phone scams work better on landlines and unfamiliar callers.** Many retirees still answer unknown numbers, which younger generations have largely stopped doing.
- **Passwords get reused for decades.** A password created in 2010 for an old shopping account may still protect a banking login today.
- **Trust in official-sounding callers.** Scammers impersonate the IRS, Medicare, Social Security, or a bank's fraud department because those names carry authority.

None of this means retirees are careless. It means the people running these scams have done the math and picked their targets deliberately.

## How Identity Theft Actually Happens to a Retiree

Here is a realistic sequence, based on patterns reported to consumer protection agencies:

1. A company you signed up with years ago — a pharmacy loyalty program, an old email provider, a retailer — gets hacked.
2. Your email address and an old password leak into a breach database that criminals buy and sell.
3. Since many people reuse passwords, that old password still opens your current bank or Medicare portal login.
4. A scammer either logs in directly, or uses your leaked email and name to build a convincing phishing call or text — "This is Medicare, we need to verify your account."
5. Once they have your Social Security number or bank details, they open credit accounts, redirect benefit payments, or drain savings.

The entire chain often starts at step one — a breach you never heard about, tied to a service you barely remember signing up for.

## What to Do When You Get an Alert

Getting an alert is not a reason to panic — it means the system worked and you found out early. Here is the short version of what to do next:

1. **Read what was exposed.** The alert tells you which service was breached and what type of data was involved.
2. **Change that password immediately.** Go to the website directly rather than clicking a link in an email.
3. **Change it everywhere else too, if reused.** This is the step people skip, and it is the one that matters most.
4. **Call your bank if financial details were exposed.** Ask about a new card number or extra monitoring on the account.
5. **Consider a credit freeze if a Social Security number was involved.** This is free and can be done directly with each of the three credit bureaus.

Most of this takes under fifteen minutes once you know the steps.
