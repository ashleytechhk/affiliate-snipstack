## How Streaming Geo-Pricing Works

Every major streaming service adjusts its subscription price by country. This is called purchasing power parity pricing, and it is a deliberate business decision: charging the same USD price globally would price out most of the world's potential subscribers. So Netflix charges $15.49/month in the US and the equivalent of $11 in Mexico, not because the service is worse but because the local economy supports different price points.

The platform enforces this through IP geolocation. When you visit Netflix from a browser or app, the first thing their servers do is check your IP address against a geolocation database. That lookup tells them your country, which determines:

- Which pricing tier you see at signup
- Which regional catalog you can access
- Which payment currencies are available

A VPN intercepts this process at the source. Instead of Netflix seeing your real IP — say, a US residential address — it sees the IP of the VPN server you connected to in Mexico. The rest of the process flows from there: Mexican pricing, Mexican catalog, MXN payment options.

## Why Many VPNs Fail at Mexico Streaming

Not every VPN can reliably deliver Mexico-region streaming access. Here is why the gap between good and bad VPNs is so stark:

**IP blocklists.** Netflix, Disney+, and Max all maintain actively updated databases of known VPN datacenter IP ranges. When they detect that an incoming connection comes from a flagged datacenter IP — rather than a genuine residential or commercial address — they either block playback or refuse to show regional pricing. [Free VPNs](https://snipstack.io/vs-free-vpns-2026/) and low-budget paid VPNs burn through their Mexico server IPs quickly because they do not have the resources to rotate them when flagged.

**Server volume.** A VPN with only two or three Mexico servers cannot spread load or swap to clean IPs when one gets blocked. A provider with 50+ Mexico server nodes can route users to unblocked IPs within seconds.

**DNS leaks.** Even with a VPN connected, if your device sends DNS queries to your ISP's resolver rather than the VPN's, streaming platforms can cross-reference your visible IP (Mexico) against your DNS resolver location (US, UK, wherever you actually are) and flag the mismatch. A quality VPN routes DNS through its own servers by default.

**Speed.** Mexico-to-global routing can add latency depending on where you are physically located. A VPN with a well-distributed server network handles this better than one with a single overloaded Mexico datacenter.
