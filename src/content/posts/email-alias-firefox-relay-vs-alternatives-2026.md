## What Is an [Email Alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-privacy-without-creating-new-account-2026/) and Why Does It Matter?

An [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-healthcare-appointment-reminders-2026/) is a forwarding address that sits between you and the websites you sign up for. When a site asks for your email, you give the alias instead of your real address. Everything sent to that alias gets forwarded to your real inbox. To the site, the alias looks like a normal address. To you, it is a disposable shield.

The key benefit is revocability. If a particular alias starts attracting spam, you delete it. The spam stops immediately. Your real address was never involved, so it stays clean. You can then create a fresh alias for any legitimate accounts that used the old one.

This is fundamentally different from Gmail's `+tag` trick, which spammers learned to strip years ago. A proper alias service uses a completely different domain that cannot be reverse-engineered to your real address.

## The Four Contenders: What Each One Does

### Firefox Relay

Firefox Relay is Mozilla's masked email product. It generates random forwarding addresses on the `mozmail.com` domain and forwards received email to your real inbox. The free tier gives you five masks — enough to get started, not enough to cover most people's full online footprint.

The paid "Premium" tier (part of Mozilla's subscription bundle) unlocks unlimited masks, lets you reply to masked emails anonymously, removes email trackers before forwarding, and adds custom subdomain creation. Firefox Relay also has a [browser extension](https://snipstack.io/browser-extension-to-check-scam-sites-2026/) that offers one-click mask generation when you encounter an email field.

What it does well: browser integration is clean, the interface is beginner-friendly, and Mozilla's privacy reputation is genuine.

What it does not do: there is no fake name or alternate identity generation, it cannot create [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/) masks, and the five-mask free ceiling is low.

### SimpleLogin

SimpleLogin is an open-source alias service that was acquired by Proton in 2022. Its free tier allows 10 aliases — double Firefox Relay's free cap. The premium plan ($36/year or approximately $4/month) unlocks unlimited aliases, custom domains, catch-all addresses, and PGP encryption for forwarded mail.

Because the code is open source, security-focused users can audit it or self-host. The Proton ownership adds credibility in the privacy community: Proton Mail, Proton VPN, and [Proton Pass](https://snipstack.io/proton-pass-email-alias-review-2026/) are well-regarded products.

What it does well: 10 free aliases is genuinely useful; open-source is a meaningful trust signal; the Proton ecosystem integration (Proton Pass can autofill SimpleLogin aliases) is convenient for existing Proton users.

What it does not do: no identity generation beyond the alias itself; the free tier still runs out for heavy users.

### Apple Hide My Email

Apple Hide My Email generates random `@privaterelay.appleid.com` forwarding addresses from inside Safari, iPhone settings, and iCloud. It requires an active iCloud+ subscription, which starts with any paid iCloud storage plan.

The integration is the strongest of any option here for Apple device users — it appears directly in iOS sign-up flows with no separate app needed. However, Hide My Email is locked entirely to the Apple ecosystem. You cannot use it on Android, Windows, or non-Safari browsers on a PC. And if you ever leave Apple's subscription, you lose access to the service.

What it does well: frictionless on iPhone and Mac; no separate account needed; Apple's privacy track record is solid.

What it does not do: works only within Apple devices and Safari; no fake name generation; no cross-platform use.

### Surfshark Alt-ID

Alt-ID [is Surfshark](https://snipstack.io/is-surfshark-one-better-than-separate-apps-2026/)'s masked identity feature, included in [Surfshark One](https://snipstack.io/surfshark-one-vs-bitdefender-total-security-2026/) and One+ plans. It generates unlimited alias addresses that forward to your real inbox, and it pairs each alias with an optional alternate name and profile details — useful for sites that ask for a full name alongside an email.

There is no mask limit, which matters when you start applying the one-alias-per-service approach (the most effective spam-control strategy). You can toggle aliases off without deleting them, or delete them permanently. It works on any device and any platform — there is no ecosystem lock.

What it does well: unlimited aliases with no per-alias pricing; fake name generation included; platform-agnostic; included with a broader [privacy suite](https://snipstack.io/privacy-suite-for-couples-shared-household-2026/) (VPN, [Antivirus](https://snipstack.io/vs-antivirus-2026/), Alert).

What it does not do: requires an active Surfshark subscription; the Surfshark domain used for aliases is not as widely recognised as Mozilla or Apple (though it functions identically for forwarding purposes).

## How to Choose: Matching Tool to Situation

**You are testing the concept and want zero cost:** Start with SimpleLogin's free tier. Ten aliases gets you further than Firefox Relay's five, and the open-source code is a genuine trust signal. Expect to hit the ceiling if you use more than ten services with different addresses.

**You are fully inside the Apple ecosystem:** Apple Hide My Email is the path of least resistance — it works without installing anything extra. The catch is that this only holds as long as you stay on Apple devices and maintain your iCloud+ subscription.

**You want the audit trail (open source matters to you):** SimpleLogin's premium plan at $36/year is a clean choice. Proton's track record in privacy is strong, and the Proton Pass integration makes alias autofill convenient.

**You are already on a Surfshark plan, or you want unlimited aliases without a separate per-alias subscription:** [Alt-ID](https://snipstack.io/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-firefox-relay-vs-alternatives-2026__mid) is the practical winner. Unlimited masks, the fake-name generation feature (which the other three do not offer), and no ecosystem restriction. If you are starting from scratch with 50+ spam emails a day and want to build a one-alias-per-service system, the lack of any alias ceiling matters.

**You want Firefox Relay specifically:** The free tier is a good zero-commitment test. If you find yourself needing more than five masks within the first month — and you likely will — you will need to either upgrade or switch to something without a low cap.

## Setting Up Your Alias System: The Core Strategy

Whichever tool you choose, the system works the same way:

**Create one alias per category or service.** Shopping, newsletters, social platforms, trials, work contacts — each gets its own address. All of them forward to your real inbox.

**When spam hits, check the "To:" line.** That alias tells you exactly which category or service leaked your data. You do not have to guess.

**Delete the compromised alias, not your real address.** The spam stops instantly. Your real inbox is untouched. Create a fresh alias for any legitimate accounts that need updating.

The reason this works where filters fail: you are revoking the address entirely, not blocking individual senders. Spammers rotate domains constantly. A filter has to keep up with every new domain they spin up. Deleting the alias terminates all of them simultaneously.

## Making the Switch from Firefox Relay

If you are already using Firefox Relay and hitting the five-mask ceiling, here is how to transition without losing any forwarding:

1. **Identify which of your current Relay masks are in active use.** Log into relay.firefox.com and check which aliases have received legitimate mail recently.
2. **Create equivalent aliases in your new tool** (SimpleLogin, Alt-ID, or whichever you are moving to). Label them identically.
3. **Update your accounts one by one.** Go into the account settings for each service and change the email on file from the old Relay mask to your new alias.
4. **Once all accounts are updated, disable the old Relay mask.** Do not delete it immediately — leave it for a few weeks to catch any stragglers, then delete it once you confirm nothing important is being forwarded.
5. **Going forward, use only the new tool for any new sign-ups.** The Relay masks you did not port can simply be left to expire or deleted.

This is a one-time migration that takes an hour or two but pays off permanently.

## FAQ

**Is Firefox Relay free forever?**

The five-mask free tier has no stated expiry — Mozilla has offered it since Relay launched. The paid premium features (unlimited masks, anonymous replies, tracker removal) require a subscription. Whether the free tier stays free long-term depends on Mozilla's product decisions, which is a factor worth considering if you plan to build your alias system around it.

**Can I use SimpleLogin without a Proton account?**

Yes. SimpleLogin works as a standalone service. A Proton account gives you tighter integration with Proton Pass for alias autofill, but it is not required to use SimpleLogin's web interface or browser extension independently.

**Does Apple Hide My Email work on Android or Windows?**

No. Apple Hide My Email is only available within Apple's own apps and Safari on Apple devices. If you use Android, Windows, or any non-Apple browser on a desktop, you cannot generate or manage Hide My Email addresses through those platforms. This makes it unsuitable as a primary alias tool for anyone with mixed-device workflows.

**Will sites reject Alt-ID alias addresses as disposable?**

Some sites run checks against known disposable-address domains and reject them. This is an issue all alias services face, not just Alt-ID. If you encounter a rejection, the common workaround is to try a different alias domain if your provider offers multiple options, or use a less strict sign-up flow (mobile app vs. web form can sometimes differ). In practice, most mainstream services accept Alt-ID aliases without issue.

**How many aliases do I actually need to manage 50+ spam emails a day?**

The volume of spam you receive today is a symptom of past exposure — your real address is already compromised on many lists. Aliases will not clean up that backlog. What they do is stop new exposure from compounding the problem. Start with five to ten category aliases going forward. Over time, as you build a one-alias-per-service habit, you may have 30, 50, or more active aliases. The tools with hard limits (Firefox Relay free at 5, SimpleLogin free at 10) will not scale to that without an upgrade. [Alt-ID](https://snipstack.io/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-firefox-relay-vs-alternatives-2026__faq) has no mask ceiling, which makes it suited to the long-term approach.

**Can I reply to emails from an alias address?**

Depends on the tool. Firefox Relay Premium and SimpleLogin both support replying from the alias so the recipient never sees your real address. Alt-ID supports this as well. Apple Hide My Email's reply behavior depends on your mail client configuration. On the free tier of Firefox Relay, anonymous replies are not available.
