## Path A: Setting Up Brave Browser (Built-In Blocking, Free)

Brave is a Chromium-based browser with Shields — its native ad and tracker blocker — turned on by default. It looks and feels like Chrome. Setup takes under two minutes.

### Step 1 — Download Brave

**Android:** Open the Google Play Store, search "Brave Browser," tap Install. Wait for the download to complete.

**iOS:** Open the App Store, search "Brave Private Browser," tap Get.

*[Screenshot placeholder: Play Store / App Store showing Brave Browser install page]*

### Step 2 — Open Brave and Let It Initialize

Launch Brave from your home screen. On first open it will show a brief onboarding flow — you can skip through these screens. No account sign-up is required.

### Step 3 — Verify Shields Is On

Tap the address bar and navigate to any ad-heavy website (a tabloid news site is a good test). After the page loads, tap the **lion icon** in the address bar (on Android) or the Shields icon at the top of the screen (on iOS).

You will see a panel showing "Ads & Trackers Blocked" with a number. If it shows a count, Shields is active.

*[Screenshot placeholder: Brave browser address bar with lion/Shields icon highlighted]*

### Step 4 — Adjust Shields Settings (Optional)

The default Shields settings block third-party ads and trackers. If a site breaks (some banking apps and form-heavy sites occasionally do), tap the Shields icon and toggle "Shields" off for that site only. Everything else stays protected.

To access global settings: tap the three-dot menu → Settings → Shields. You can upgrade to "Aggressive" blocking here, which catches more trackers but has a slightly higher chance of site breakage.

### Step 5 — Import Your Bookmarks (Optional)

If you're migrating from Chrome, tap the three-dot menu → Bookmarks → Import from Chrome. Your saved sites carry over. Passwords can be imported via Settings → Passwords → Import.

**What Brave blocks:** Third-party display ads, trackers, cross-site cookies, fingerprinting scripts, and auto-play media. What it does not block: ads inside the YouTube app (because that's a separate app, not a browser tab), in-app ads in other apps, or ads on your Wi-Fi network beyond the browser.

## Troubleshooting

![Troubleshooting ad blocking on mobile — common issues and fixes](/og/browser-with-built-in-ad-blocker-2026-inline-2.png)

**Ads still appearing in Brave:**

- Confirm Shields is toggled on — tap the lion icon in the address bar. If it shows "Shields Down," tap it to re-enable.
- Try upgrading to Aggressive blocking: Settings → Shields → Trackers & ads blocking → change to Aggressive.
- Some sites serve ads from first-party domains (same domain as the site), which content blockers cannot block without also breaking the site. This is a structural limitation, not a Brave bug.

**CleanWeb toggle is grayed out:**

- Make sure you are signed into your Surfshark account in the app.
- On Android, check that the app has permission to run in the background: Settings → Apps → Surfshark → Battery → Allow background activity.

**CleanWeb enabled but ads still showing in a specific app:**

- Some in-app ad networks use domain patterns not yet on Surfshark's blocklist. DNS-level blocking catches the majority of domains, but not all ad networks are covered. Coverage improves over time as Surfshark updates the blocklist.
- For complete ad blocking inside apps like YouTube, pair CleanWeb with the Brave Browser for web browsing and accept that some app-level ads may still slip through.

**Brave breaks a website I need:**

- Tap the Shields icon in the address bar → toggle Shields off for that site. Brave remembers the per-site setting so you only have to do this once per site.
- Alternatively, lower the blocking level for that site from Aggressive to Standard.

**Battery drain after enabling Surfshark:**

- The VPN tunnel has a small battery cost. If you enable CleanWeb without connecting to a VPN server, the battery impact is minimal — DNS filtering alone consumes very little power.
- For full VPN use, Surfshark's implementation is among the more efficient options on iOS and Android.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is there a browser with built-in [ad blocker for](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-for-iphone-browser-2026/) both Android and iPhone?**

Yes — Brave works on both platforms and has Shields enabled by default. Download it from the Play Store (Android) or App Store (iOS) and ads are blocked immediately on first use with no additional setup. **Can I keep using Chrome and still [block ads on](https://snipstack.io/block-ads-on-youtube-tv-app-2026/) my phone?**

Chrome for Android and iOS does not support extensions, so you cannot add a traditional [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-free-chrome-2026/) to Chrome on mobile. The workaround is a network-level solution like Surfshark CleanWeb, which blocks ad domains before they reach Chrome (or any other browser). You keep Chrome; the ads just don't load.

**Does Brave Browser block [YouTube ad](https://snipstack.io/youtube-ad-blocker-2026/)s?**

Brave blocks YouTube ads when you watch YouTube through the Brave browser's built-in YouTube web view or when visiting youtube.com in Brave. It does not block ads in the standalone YouTube app, because the YouTube app is a separate application outside Brave's scope. For YouTube app ad blocking, a DNS-level solution like CleanWeb is more effective.

**Will blocking ads break websites I use regularly?**

Most sites work fine with ad blocking enabled. A small fraction of sites — particularly those that actively detect [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-extensions-2026/)s — may show a "disable your [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-firefox-2026/)" message or hide content. In Brave, you can turn Shields off per-site in one tap. With CleanWeb, there is no per-site control, but DNS-level blocking is harder for sites to detect, so anti-ad-blocker warnings appear less often.

**Is it safe to use a browser with built-in ad blocking?**

Yes. Both Brave and Surfshark are established, reputable products. Brave is open-source and has been independently audited. Surfshark is a commercial VPN provider with a no-logs policy independently verified by third parties. Ad blocking itself makes browsing safer by preventing malicious ad networks (malvertising) from delivering scripts to your device.

**Do built-in mobile [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-chrome-2026/)s affect page load speeds?**

Generally, page load times improve with ad blocking enabled because a large portion of page weight is ad content and tracking scripts. Blocking these before they load means fewer HTTP requests, less JavaScript to parse, and faster rendering. Some ad-heavy pages load 30–50% faster with a blocker active.
