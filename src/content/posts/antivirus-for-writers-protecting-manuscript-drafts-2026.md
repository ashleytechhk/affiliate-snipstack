## Why Writers Are at Greater Risk Than They Realize

Most people assume hackers are interested in bank accounts and corporate servers. The reality is more unsettling: cybercriminals also target ordinary home computer users who are less likely to have protections in place and more likely to pay a ransom when precious personal files are threatened.

Writers fall squarely into this profile.

Your manuscript — especially a memoir, a family history, or a novel you have been writing for years — carries enormous personal value. You cannot replace it from a store. You cannot ask a bank to refund it. A criminal who locks your files with ransomware understands that you may pay whatever they demand just to get your words back.

Beyond ransomware, there are other everyday threats:

- **Viruses** that corrupt files silently, causing documents to become unreadable over time
- **Spyware** that monitors your keystrokes, capturing passwords and personal details as you type
- **Phishing emails** disguised as messages from publishers, writing groups, or Amazon, tricking you into handing over account credentials
- **Fake software downloads** bundled with malware, often disguised as free writing tools or grammar checkers

None of these threats require you to visit a dangerous website or do anything obviously wrong. A single click on the wrong email attachment is enough.

## The Three Biggest Threats to Your Manuscript in 2026

### 1. Ransomware: The File-Locker

Ransomware is software that silently encrypts every file on your computer and then demands payment — usually in cryptocurrency — to unlock them. It spreads through email attachments, infected USB drives, and compromised websites.

For a writer, ransomware is catastrophic. Every chapter, every note, every draft — locked and unreadable unless you pay strangers an untraceable sum of money, with no guarantee they will actually restore your files.

According to security firm Malwarebytes, ransomware attacks on home users increased by over 70% between 2022 and 2024. Seniors are disproportionately represented among victims.

### 2. Cloud Sync Does Not Always Save You

Many writers store drafts in services like Google Docs or iCloud and believe this makes them safe. This is only partially true. If ransomware encrypts your local files and those encrypted versions sync to the cloud before you notice, your cloud backup may also become corrupted. Some cloud services keep version history, but not all do — and not forever.

[Antivirus software](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-with-parental-controls-2026/) stops ransomware before it encrypts anything, which is the only reliable line of defense.

### 3. Phishing: The Fake Email Trap

Phishing attacks have grown increasingly convincing. You might receive an email that appears to be from your writing group, a literary magazine, or a well-known retailer, asking you to log in to your account or download an attachment.

These emails are designed to look real. The logos, the formatting, even the sender name may look familiar. But clicking the link or downloading the attachment installs malware on your computer, or steals your username and password.

A good antivirus solution includes real-time web and email scanning that flags these threats before you interact with them.

## Backing Up Is Not Enough on Its Own

You may already back up your work — perhaps to an external drive or a USB stick. This is a good habit. But backup alone does not protect you for two important reasons.

First, if malware infects your computer and you do not notice immediately, your backups may capture infected versions of your files. An older, clean backup is only useful if you can identify exactly when the infection occurred.

Second, some malware specifically targets backup drives and network storage. If your external drive is connected to your computer when ransomware strikes, it may encrypt the backup as well.

Antivirus software and regular backups work together. One stops threats before they cause damage; the other provides a recovery option if something does slip through. You need both.

## Simple Steps to Protect Your Manuscript Today

You do not need to be a technology expert to protect your writing. Follow these five steps and you will have addressed the most common risks:

**Step 1: Install reputable antivirus software.** Choose a product from a well-known provider with a track record of strong independent test results. Look for real-time protection and automatic updates.

**Step 2: Keep your operating system updated.** Windows and macOS regularly release security updates that patch vulnerabilities. Enable automatic updates so your system stays current.

**Step 3: Back up your manuscript in at least two places.** Use one external drive and one cloud service. Disconnect the external drive from your computer when you are not actively backing up.

**Step 4: Be cautious with email attachments.** If you receive an unexpected attachment, even from someone you know, verify with them by phone before opening it. Their email account may have been compromised.

**Step 5: Use strong, unique passwords for your writing accounts.** If you use an online writing platform, literary agent portal, or publisher submission system, use a password that is at least 12 characters long and different from any other password you use.

These are not complicated steps. And most of the ongoing protection happens automatically once you have completed them.

Potentially, yes. If ransomware encrypts your local files and those encrypted files sync to the cloud before you notice, your cloud copy may also become unusable. ### How do I know if my computer is already infected?

Signs of infection include: your computer running much slower than usual, strange pop-up messages you do not recognize, programs opening or closing on their own, or files that suddenly cannot be opened. If you notice any of these, stop using your computer for sensitive tasks and run a full scan with antivirus software immediately.
