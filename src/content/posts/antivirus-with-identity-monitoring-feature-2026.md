## Why Seniors Are the Primary Target

Scammers are not random. They are strategic. Older adults are specifically targeted for several reasons, and understanding those reasons helps explain why standard antivirus software alone falls short.

First, seniors tend to have more savings — retirement accounts, fixed incomes, property equity — making them financially attractive targets. Second, many older adults grew up before the internet and may not immediately recognise the signs of a phishing email or a fake website. Third, and perhaps most importantly, seniors are less likely to monitor their accounts as frequently as younger users, which gives criminals more time to act undetected.

The most common attacks are not viruses in the old-fashioned sense. They are:

- **Phishing emails** that impersonate your bank, Medicare, the IRS, or a courier service
- **Data breach exploitation** — criminals buy stolen login credentials from leaked databases and try them on your real accounts
- **Social engineering scams** that trick you into handing over passwords or one-time codes over the phone
- **Fake tech support** alerts that claim your computer is infected and ask you to pay for a "fix"

A standard antivirus can block some malicious downloads. But it cannot tell you that your email address and password are currently for sale on a fraud marketplace. That is what identity monitoring adds.

## What Does "Identity Monitoring" Actually Mean?

The phrase sounds technical, but the concept is straightforward.

When companies get hacked — and large companies get hacked regularly — the stolen data (email addresses, passwords, phone numbers, sometimes financial details) often ends up in databases that criminals share or sell. These databases are sometimes called "dark web" collections, though they circulate in various underground corners of the internet.

Identity monitoring services continuously scan these databases for your personal information. When your details appear — say, your email address from a shopping site that was breached two years ago — the service sends you an alert. You then know to change that password before anyone uses it to access your bank, your email, or your superannuation account.

Without monitoring, you would likely never know your data had been exposed. Breaches are often not disclosed for months, and by the time you read about them in the news, criminals may already have acted.

## The Real Risk: Credential Stuffing and Account Takeover

Here is a specific scenario worth understanding, because it is extraordinarily common.

Imagine a supermarket loyalty app you signed up for years ago was hacked. You used the same email address and password there that you use for your online banking — a habit that is very common and very dangerous. Criminals take that leaked email and password combination and try it on hundreds of banking, email, and shopping sites automatically. This is called credential stuffing, and it works precisely because most people reuse passwords.

If you have identity monitoring active, you get an alert the moment your credentials appear in that leaked database. You change your password. The criminal tries the old one, it fails, and they move on. Without monitoring, you remain exposed indefinitely.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between antivirus and identity monitoring?

Antivirus software scans your device for malicious files and blocks harmful software from running. Identity monitoring watches external databases — breach records, fraud marketplaces — for your personal information. Both address different threats. Antivirus protects your device; identity monitoring protects your data. A combined product handles both from a single subscription.

### How do I know if my personal information has already been stolen?

You may not know without a monitoring tool in place. Signs can include unfamiliar charges on bank statements, unexpected password reset emails you did not request, or calls from your bank about unusual activity. An identity monitoring service actively scans for your details and alerts you — rather than waiting for you to notice something wrong.

### Is it safe to give my email address to a monitoring service?

Reputable services like Surfshark, Norton, or McAfee use your email address solely to check it against breach databases. They do not share it with third parties for marketing purposes. Read the privacy policy before signing up, and use well-known providers rather than unknown free tools.

### Do I need to be tech-savvy to use these tools?

No. The best services are designed for everyday users. Once set up, they run in the background and only contact you when action is needed. Setup typically involves downloading an app, creating an account, and entering the email addresses you want monitored — a process that takes under ten minutes.

### Can identity monitoring prevent identity theft entirely?

No tool can guarantee prevention — that language would be misleading. What monitoring does is reduce the window of opportunity. The faster you know your data has been exposed, the faster you can change passwords and secure accounts. Early warning is not a guarantee, but it is meaningfully better than finding out months later.

### My computer already has [Windows Defender](https://snipstack.io/windows-defender-download-2026/). Is that enough?

Windows Defender handles malware scanning reasonably well for basic threats. It does not include breach monitoring or VPN. If you want to know when your passwords are exposed in a data breach, or if you want encrypted protection on public Wi-Fi, you need an additional service — or a bundle that covers those gaps.
