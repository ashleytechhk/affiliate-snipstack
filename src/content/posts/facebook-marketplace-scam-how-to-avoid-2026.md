## The Most Common Facebook Marketplace Scams in 2026

Scammers rotate tactics, but a handful of patterns show up again and again. Here is how each one works and what it looks like in your inbox.

### The Fake Payment Email

A buyer "pays" via a third-party service — often spoofed to look like PayPal, Zelle, or a shipping company. An email lands in your inbox confirming the payment and asking you to click a link to complete the transaction or release goods. The email is fabricated. No money has moved. The link either harvests your login credentials or installs malware.

### The Overpayment Check

A buyer sends a physical check or digital transfer for more than the asking price, then asks you to refund the difference before the original payment clears. The original payment bounces. You have already sent real money.

### The Shipping Courier Request

A buyer says they cannot collect in person and will send a courier. They request your personal details — full name, address, sometimes your email and phone number — to "arrange pickup." The courier never arrives; the details go to a fraud database.

### The Phishing Notification

You receive what looks like a genuine Facebook notification about your listing — a message, a policy warning, an account restriction. The email looks convincing. The link inside goes to a lookalike login page designed to steal your Facebook credentials.

## How to Check a Suspicious Email Manually

![Step-by-step guide to inspecting a suspicious Facebook Marketplace email header and sender address](/og/facebook-marketplace-scam-how-to-avoid-2026-inline-1.png)

### Step 1: Look at the actual sender address

The display name can say anything — "PayPal Security Team," "Facebook Marketplace," "Zelle Payments." The real address is what matters. In Gmail, click the three dots or the down arrow next to the sender name to reveal the full address. In Outlook, hover over the name.

A genuine PayPal notification comes from `@paypal.com`. If you see `paypal-secure-confirm@mail3.ru`, `facebook-marketplace@notify-check.net`, or any address that does not match the official domain exactly, stop.

### Step 2: Hover over every link before you click

Hover your cursor over any link in the email — do not click. Your browser or email client will show the destination URL in the status bar. If the link claims to go to PayPal but the destination is `paypai-verification.com` or a string of random characters, it is a phishing link. Close the email.

### Step 3: Search the message text in quotes

Copy a distinctive sentence from the email — something specific, not generic. Paste it into Google surrounded by quote marks. Scammers recycle message templates across thousands of victims. If the exact text has been flagged on scam-reporting forums like Reddit, Scam Detector, or consumer complaint sites, you will find it within seconds.

### Step 4: Trust your instincts about urgency

Legitimate payment processors do not email you to say your account will be closed in 24 hours if you do not verify your details. Facebook does not send emails requiring you to click a link or your listing will be deleted today. Urgency is a manufacturing technique, not a genuine indicator of a problem. Any email that demands immediate action — especially one that arrived right after a Marketplace interaction — deserves an extra 60 seconds of scrutiny.

## Practical Rules for Safer Marketplace Transactions

Beyond email checking, a few behavioral rules significantly reduce your exposure.

**Never accept payment outside the platform.** Facebook Marketplace Checkout offers purchase protection. Buyers who insist on Venmo, Zelle, wire transfer, or mailing a check are either bypassing protection intentionally or following a scam script. Either way, stay inside the platform.

**Meet locally for high-value items.** For items over a few hundred dollars, an in-person handover at a public place is the safest transaction. No courier, no shipping address, no payment email chain.

**Do not send any refund before a payment clears.** If a check has not fully cleared your bank — typically two to five business days — any money you send back comes out of your own funds. Banks are required to make funds available before they confirm the payment is genuine.

**Verify any notification email by going directly to the platform.** If Facebook emails you about a policy issue, do not click the link in the email. Open a new browser tab, go to facebook.com directly, and check your notifications there. If the issue is real, it will appear on the platform. If it does not, the email was fake.

## FAQ

### What is the most common Facebook Marketplace scam in 2026?

The fake payment email is the most frequently reported variant. A buyer appears to pay via a spoofed payment notification — often mimicking PayPal or Zelle — and the email asks the seller to click a link to "release" the funds or complete the transaction. No payment has been made. The link leads to a credential-harvesting page or a request for the seller's banking details.

### How do I tell if a PayPal email from a Marketplace buyer is real?

Check the actual sender domain — not the display name. Genuine PayPal emails come from addresses ending in `@paypal.com` exactly. Log into your PayPal account directly (not through any link in the email) to see if the payment appears there. If it is not showing in your PayPal account, the email is fake regardless of how convincing it looks. [Run the email through a scam checker](/blog/scamcheck?sub=facebook-marketplace-scam-how-to-avoid-2026__faq) if you are not certain.

### Is it safe to give a buyer my email address on Marketplace?

It carries risk. Scammers use Marketplace interactions to collect email addresses for phishing follow-ups. If a buyer insists on moving the conversation to email rather than staying in Messenger, that is itself a warning signal. Keep communication inside the Marketplace platform where possible. If you do share an email, consider using an alias rather than your primary address.

### What should I do if I already clicked a link in a scam email?

Act quickly. Change the password for any account you may have entered credentials into, starting with Facebook and any email or payment accounts. If you entered banking details, contact your bank immediately. Run a malware scan on the device you used. Report the email to Facebook (forward to phish@fb.com) and to your national cybercrime authority.

### Does Surfshark Email Scam Checker work on mobile?

### Can scammers fake the Facebook sender address entirely?

Yes. Email spoofing allows a sender to display any "from" address — including official-looking ones — while the actual sending server is somewhere entirely different. A scam email can display `no-reply@facebookmail.com` as the visible sender while originating from an unrelated server. Checking the full email header (not just the display address) reveals the true origin. Surfshark's Email Scam Checker includes header analysis as part of its scan.
