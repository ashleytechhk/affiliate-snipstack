## What "Antivirus" Actually Does on iPhone (And What It Can't)

Before comparing apps, let's be honest about what's happening under the hood.

Apple's iOS sandbox prevents any third-party app from scanning other apps or intercepting system processes the way [antivirus software](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-2026/) does on Windows or Android. So when Norton or McAfee says "[antivirus for](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-for-android-free-2026/) iPhone," they're really selling web threat detection, Wi-Fi monitoring, and identity services — not traditional virus scanning.

This matters because you should be comparing products on the features they can actually deliver on iOS:

- Malicious link / phishing protection
- VPN (encrypted browsing on public Wi-Fi)
- Dark web / data breach monitoring
- Spam call and SMS filtering
- Private search

That's the real battleground. Here's how the three main options stack up.

## Norton 360: The Brand You Know, With Caveats

Norton has decades of brand equity and a genuinely good product on Windows. On iPhone in 2026, it's a different story.

The Norton 360 iOS app delivers web protection, a VPN, and dark web monitoring. But the VPN on lower-tier plans caps out at 60GB per month — which sounds like a lot until you're streaming video on hotel Wi-Fi for a week. The unlimited VPN is gated behind the more expensive Premium or Deluxe tiers.

The bigger issue is the pricing model. Norton aggressively discounts year-one subscriptions, then renews at full price — sometimes 2–3x what you originally paid. If you've been a Norton customer for more than a year, you've probably experienced this. It's a legitimate product, but the post-discount renewal is the moment most people start shopping around.

Norton's iOS app has improved but remains heavier than it needs to be. On an older iPhone SE or an iPhone with limited storage, you'll notice it.

**Bottom line on Norton:** Best suited for households that also run Windows machines and want a single vendor across everything. For iPhone-only or iPhone-primary users, you're paying for Windows-era infrastructure you'll never use.
