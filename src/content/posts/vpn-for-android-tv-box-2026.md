## What a VPN Does on an Android TV Box

A VPN — Virtual Private Network — routes your internet traffic through a server in a country you choose. Every website and streaming platform you visit sees the VPN server's location instead of your real one. On an Android TV box, the VPN runs as a system-level app: once it is connected, every other app on the device — Netflix, Disney+, YouTube — behaves as though it is physically located wherever you pointed the VPN.

Android TV boxes run a version of Android specifically built for television use. Most current boxes — the Nvidia Shield TV, Xiaomi Mi Box S, Chromecast with Google TV, and a wide range of third-party Android TV boxes — have the Google Play Store built in. This matters because a VPN provider with a dedicated Android TV app can be installed in under a minute, maintained automatically through Play Store updates, and navigated entirely with a standard TV remote.

There are two distinct reasons streaming viewers install a VPN on their TV box:

**Content access.** Streaming services license content by territory. A title available on Netflix in one market may not be licensed in another. Switching your VPN server location gives you access to a different regional catalogue.

**Subscription price arbitrage.** This is the bigger opportunity for cost-focused viewers. Streaming platforms price their plans in local currencies based on purchasing power in each market. The identical Netflix or Disney+ plan can cost $5–8 per month in some markets versus $15–18 in others. A VPN lets you appear to be in a lower-priced region, which means you can subscribe or renew at that region's price rather than your home market rate.

## Method 2: SmartDNS — When You Need Speed Over Encryption

SmartDNS is a different technology from a VPN, and understanding the distinction helps you pick the right tool for each situation.

A VPN encrypts all your traffic and routes it through a remote server. This provides full privacy and geo-switching, but adds a small processing overhead. SmartDNS does not encrypt your traffic — instead, it intercepts and reroutes only the DNS queries that streaming services use to detect your location. The result is near-zero speed impact, since your actual video data travels a direct path to the content server.

**When SmartDNS is the better choice:**

- Your Android TV box is older or underpowered and struggles with VPN encryption overhead on 4K streams
- Your internet connection is at the edge of what 4K requires and every megabit counts
- You want the simplest possible setup — no app install, just a DNS settings change
- Your box does not have Play Store access and you want to avoid sideloading

**When the native VPN app is the better choice:**

- You want full traffic encryption for privacy (SmartDNS does not provide this)
- You need to switch regions frequently and want a one-tap interface
- You want protection on non-streaming traffic too

Surfshark includes SmartDNS at no extra cost with any subscription. Here is how to configure it on an Android TV box:

1. Open your Android TV box's **Settings**, then navigate to **Network** or **Wi-Fi**.
2. Select your connected network and open its advanced options.
3. Change the DNS setting from automatic to **Manual**.
4. Enter the Surfshark SmartDNS server addresses (available in your Surfshark account dashboard under "SmartDNS").
5. Save the settings and restart the network connection or reboot the box.

That is the entire setup. No app required, no sign-in on the TV box itself. The box's streaming apps will now appear to be in the region Surfshark's SmartDNS is configured for.

One important note: SmartDNS locks you to a single configured region. To change regions, you update the DNS entry again, which takes a minute but is less immediate than switching servers in the VPN app.

## Does a VPN Slow Down Streaming on an Android TV Box?

Speed impact from a VPN depends on three things: your base internet speed, the geographic distance to the VPN server, and the VPN provider's infrastructure quality.

In practice, modern VPN protocols — particularly WireGuard, which Surfshark uses by default — are efficient enough that the overhead on a fast broadband connection is imperceptible for streaming. 4K HDR content requires approximately 25 Mbps. Most home broadband connections run well above that, giving plenty of headroom for VPN processing.

The main scenario where you will notice slowdown: connecting to a VPN server on the opposite side of the world on a connection that is already at or near your streaming service's minimum requirement. The fix is to pick a geographically closer server in your target region, or switch to SmartDNS, which adds no processing overhead at all.

For underpowered Android TV boxes (older hardware with slower processors), SmartDNS is worth considering simply because the device handles encryption better than continuous AES encryption at high bitrates.
