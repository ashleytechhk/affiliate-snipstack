## Why Fake Hotel Booking Sites Are So Common

The travel industry moves enormous amounts of money online. Scammers follow the money. They build sites that look exactly like real hotel booking platforms — complete with star ratings, fake guest reviews, and photos copied from the real property's own website. Some even appear in paid search results, which can make them look official.

The Federal Trade Commission reported that travel scams cost consumers hundreds of millions of dollars in recent years, and hotel booking fraud is one of the top three categories. Seniors are disproportionately targeted because they tend to trust polished-looking websites and are less likely to be familiar with the red flags.

The problem is not that older travellers are less careful. The problem is that fake sites have become convincingly realistic. A scammer can buy a professional website template for a few dollars, copy hotel photos from Google Images, and have a believable booking site live within a day.

## [How to Spot](https://snipstack.io/how-to-spot-online-shopping-scam-2026/) a Fake Hotel Booking Site: The Manual Checklist

Before using any tool, run through this checklist yourself. These are the warning signs that appear on almost every fake booking site.

### Check the Web Address Very Carefully

Look at the address bar — the part that starts with `https://`. Scammers often register addresses that look almost right:

- `booking-hotels-deals.com` instead of `booking.com`
- `hotells.com` instead of `hotels.com`
- `marriot-official.com` instead of `marriott.com`

A single misspelling or an extra word like "deals," "official," or "discount" is a red flag. Legitimate major hotel chains and booking platforms do not use unusual domain names.

### Look for HTTPS — But Do Not Trust It Alone

You will see a padlock icon in the address bar on secure sites. This means the connection is encrypted, which is good. However, fake sites also use HTTPS these days — it is free and easy to set up. A padlock does not mean the site is honest, only that your connection to it is encrypted. Do not let the padlock reassure you on its own.

### Search for the Hotel Independently

Open a new browser tab and search for the hotel's name directly. Find the hotel's official website or look it up on a well-known platform like Expedia or Booking.com. Compare the [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/), address, and pricing. If the booking site you found has different contact details, that is a serious warning sign.

![Step-by-step guide: verifying a hotel site manually](/og/fake-hotel-booking-site-how-to-check-2026-inline-1.png)

### Read the Fine Print

Scroll to the bottom of the page and look for a terms and conditions page, a refund policy, and a physical business address. Fake sites often have no refund policy or list a nonsensical address. If you cannot find any of these, leave the site immediately.

### Check How Old the Website Is

Brand new websites registered in the past few months should be treated with caution. You can look up when a domain was registered using a WHOIS lookup tool (just search "WHOIS lookup" in your browser). A hotel booking site that was registered two weeks ago is almost certainly not trustworthy.

## The Most Common Fake Hotel Booking Scam Patterns in 2026

Knowing how scammers operate helps you recognise them faster.

### The Fake Confirmation Email

You book a hotel through a legitimate site. Then you receive an email claiming there was a problem with your booking and asking you to re-enter your payment details on a link provided. The email looks official. The link goes to a fake site.

If you ever receive an email like this, do not click any links in it. Go directly to the booking platform's official website by typing the address yourself or using a bookmark you set up previously.

### The Too-Good-To-Be-True Deal

A site offers a four-star hotel at a fraction of what every other platform charges. The catch: you pay upfront, receive a confirmation, and when you arrive at the hotel, your reservation does not exist.

Real discounts of 10 to 20 percent are common. Discounts of 50 percent or more on rooms that are fully priced everywhere else should trigger immediate suspicion.

### The Lookalike Domain

As mentioned above, scammers register domains that look almost identical to well-known booking platforms. They may even run paid advertisements so their fake site appears at the top of search results.

Always double-check the full web address before entering any payment information. Take your time — scammers count on you being in a hurry.

## [What To Do](https://snipstack.io/what-to-do-after-malware-infection-2026/) If You Have Already Paid

If you suspect you have already paid a fake hotel booking site:

- Contact your bank or credit card company immediately and report it as fraud. Many cards have fraud protection that can reverse the charge.
- Report the site to the relevant consumer protection authority in your country (the FTC in the US, Action Fraud in the UK).
- Keep screenshots of the transaction and any confirmation emails you received.
- Alert the real hotel directly so they are aware their brand is being used fraudulently.

Acting quickly gives you the best chance of recovering your money.

## FAQ

**Q: How do I know if a hotel booking site is legitimate?**

Check the web address carefully for typos or unusual words. Search for the hotel independently. Look for a physical address and refund policy at the bottom of the site. Run the URL through a scam-checking tool like Surfshark's [scam checker for](https://snipstack.io/scam-checker-for-text-messages-with-unknown-links-2026/) a fast, plain-language result. **Q: Can a site with HTTPS and a padlock still be fake?**

Yes. HTTPS means the connection between your browser and the site is encrypted, but it does not verify that the site is honest or that the business behind it is real. Scammers routinely use HTTPS on their fake sites because it makes them appear more trustworthy.

**Q: What is the safest way to book a hotel?**

Book directly through the hotel's official website or through a platform you already know well, such as Booking.com or Expedia. When you receive any follow-up emails about your booking, never click links inside them — always log into your account directly by typing the website address yourself.

**Q: I received an email about a hotel deal. How do I check if it is real?**

Do not click any links in the email. Instead, copy the destination link (by hovering over it without clicking) and paste it into a scam-checking tool. Surfshark's scam checker can analyse both URLs and email senders, which makes it particularly useful for evaluating suspicious emails.

**Q: Are ads in search results safe to click for hotel bookings?**

Not always. Scammers sometimes pay to advertise their fake sites, which can place them at the top of search results. Always look at the actual web address before clicking, and consider going directly to a trusted platform rather than clicking on advertisements.

**Q: What should I do if I paid a fake hotel site and the hotel has no record of my booking?**

Contact your bank or credit card company straight away and report fraud. Explain that you paid for a service you did not receive. Most major card issuers have chargeback processes for this situation. Also report the fraudulent site to your national consumer protection authority.
