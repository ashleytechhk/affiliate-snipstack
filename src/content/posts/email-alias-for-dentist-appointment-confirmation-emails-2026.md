## What to Look for in an [Email Alias for](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-privacy-without-creating-new-account-2026/) Appointment Confirmations

Not all alias tools are built the same. For the specific use case of giving an email to a dentist, here's what actually matters:

**1. Forward-and-forget reliability.** The confirmation email has to arrive. Some free alias services have deliverability issues — test yours before relying on it for an appointment you can't miss.

**2. One-click disable.** The whole point is to cut off access when the relationship ends. If disabling an alias takes more than two taps, you won't bother, and the tool fails at its job.

**3. No distinctive domain.** Some alias domains (`@relay.firefox.com`, `@privaterelay.appleid.com`) signal to the recipient that you're using a privacy tool. That's fine for most use cases, but worth knowing.

**4. Reply-from-alias (optional but useful).** If the dental office emails to ask you to reschedule, being able to reply without exposing your real address keeps the alias intact end-to-end.

The alias receives the confirmation email and forwards it to your real inbox. Just make sure the alias is active at the time you book, so you receive the confirmation.

### Will the dental office know I'm using an alias?

Probably not, unless they inspect the full email header — which front-desk staff have no reason to do. The alias looks like a normal email address. Some alias domains (like `@privaterelay.appleid.com`) are more obviously privacy-focused, but most forwarding aliases appear as standard addresses.
