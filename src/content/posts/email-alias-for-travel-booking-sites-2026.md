## Why Travel Booking Sites Are a Spam Problem of Their Own

Travel booking platforms are not just selling you flights and hotels. They are running multi-sided marketplaces where your email address is inventory.

**The partner network problem.** When you book on Expedia, your contact details may be shared with Hotels.com, Vrbo, Travelocity, Hotwire, and a rotating cast of car hire, [travel insurance](https://snipstack.io/travel-insurance-scam-warning-signs-2026/), and experiences partners — many of which are Expedia subsidiaries or affiliates. One sign-up, one real address, and you are on a dozen lists before your flight departs.

**Price-alert addiction bait.** Sites like Kayak and Google Flights encourage you to set fare alerts "so you never miss a deal." Alerts are a legitimate feature — and also the mechanism that locks you into receiving daily promotional emails. Turning off alerts often requires navigating three separate preference screens, none of which are labeled clearly.

**Loyalty re-engagement loops.** Booking.com Genius, Expedia Rewards, and similar programs are designed to re-engage lapsed customers. If you have not booked in 90 days, expect a sequence of win-back campaigns. Most platforms allow partners to trigger these campaigns on their behalf — meaning unsubscribing from Booking.com does not stop a hotel chain partner from re-contacting you through the Genius program.

**Post-trip review requests and upsell chains.** After every stay you can expect a review request, a follow-up if you do not review, a "build on your trip" upsell for the next destination, and a seasonal promotion anchored to wherever you just went. Each email is technically "transactional" — which some platforms use to justify sending them without consent.

### Apple Hide My Email

Built into iCloud+ (starts at $0.99/month), Hide My Email generates persistent aliases ending in `@privaterelay.appleid.com`. Safari on iPhone and Mac prompts you to use a new Hide My Email address when filling a registration form.

**Strengths:** Seamless on Apple devices, no extra app required, deletion is simple.

**Weaknesses for travel booking:**
- Android users cannot use it
- The `@privaterelay.appleid.com` domain is well-known and occasionally blocked by form validators on travel sites that restrict obvious alias domains
- You cannot reply from the alias, which matters if a travel site asks you to reply to confirm a change request

**Best for:** iPhone-first travelers who book through Safari and live in the Apple ecosystem.

### Firefox Relay

Five free aliases, unlimited with Firefox Premium ($1.99/month). Aliases end in `@mozmail.com`.

**Strengths:** Free tier covers basic use, integrates with Firefox autofill.

**Weaknesses for travel booking:**
- Five aliases fill up fast if you use Booking.com, Expedia, Airbnb, Kayak, and one airline separately
- `@mozmail.com` is blocked by some booking sites
- Locked to the Firefox browser; no Safari or Chrome integration

**Best for:** Firefox users who only need a few aliases and want to keep costs at zero.

### Gmail `+tag`

The format is `yourname+kayak@gmail.com`. Gmail delivers it to your normal inbox. You can filter by tag.

**Why it fails for travel booking sites:**

Booking platforms — Expedia in particular — routinely strip the `+tag` suffix when storing your email. The database entry becomes `yourname@gmail.com`. When Expedia shares with a partner, they share the stripped version. You cannot trace the leak, you cannot kill the address, and the `+tag` label you created for filtering is gone.

Additionally, some travel sites reject the `+` character in email fields entirely. The approach is unreliable at sign-up and breaks down at the privacy layer.

**Verdict:** Useful only for inbox organization within a single, trusted service. Not viable for travel booking privacy.

---

### Throwaway Addresses (10MinuteMail, Guerrilla Mail)

These give you a temporary inbox that expires after minutes or hours.

**Why they fail for travel booking:**

You will need to receive emails from your booking for weeks: pre-trip reminders, check-in links, boarding pass updates, post-trip receipts for expenses. A dead address means missed itinerary changes and lost access to your own booking — potentially at the worst possible moment.

Throwaway addresses solve the spam problem by also breaking the service. That is not a trade-off worth making for travel.

## FAQ

**What is an [email alias for](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-ecommerce-returns-tracking-2026/) travel booking sites?**

An [email alias](https://snipstack.io/how-email-alias-2026/) is a forwarding address that routes incoming email to your real inbox without revealing your real address. You give the alias to a booking platform at sign-up. You still receive all legitimate emails — booking confirmations, check-in links, itinerary updates — but the platform and its partners only know the alias. If the alias starts generating spam, you delete it and the channel is gone. [Alt-ID handles this with one click.](/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-for-travel-booking-sites-2026__faq)

**Will travel booking sites accept an alias email address?**

Most do. Booking.com, Expedia, Airbnb, and most airline booking portals accept any standard-format email address. The only consistent exception is sites that block domains known to be associated with [disposable email](https://snipstack.io/disposable-email-that-receives-messages-2026/) services. Surfshark Alt-ID uses a realistic domain that is not on common blocklists, which makes it more reliable at check-in than `@duck.com` or `@mozmail.com` addresses.

**Can I still receive booking confirmations and itinerary emails through an alias?**

Yes. Forwarding aliases work the same as your real address for incoming mail. Booking confirmations, e-tickets, check-in reminders, and receipt emails all land in your real inbox via the alias. The only difference is the platform never learns your actual address.

**[What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) to my booking if I delete the alias before my trip?**

Do not delete an alias while you still need to receive mail from that booking. The correct workflow is: finish the trip, export any receipts you need, then delete the alias. If you need to keep a loyalty account active but want to stop the spam, create a new alias, update the account email, confirm it forwards correctly, then delete the old alias.

**[Does using](https://snipstack.io/does-using-email-alias-protect-from-spam-2026/) an alias prevent travel sites from sharing my data with partners?**

An alias prevents partners from reaching you by email. It does not stop the booking platform from sharing your profile data — name, travel preferences, payment method — under its privacy policy. For email spam specifically, a masked alias is the most effective tool available. For broader data privacy, pairing it with a VPN protects your browsing behavior and connection data when you use those booking sites on any network.

**[Is Surfshark](https://snipstack.io/is-surfshark-one-better-than-separate-apps-2026/) Alt-ID better than Apple Hide My Email for booking sites?**

For cross-platform use, yes. Apple Hide My Email only works on Apple devices and Safari, which is a limitation if you switch between iPhone and a Windows PC, or if you use Chrome. Alt-ID works via the Surfshark browser extension on any browser and any device. If you are exclusively on Apple hardware and Safari, both tools are roughly equivalent — but Alt-ID comes bundled with VPN coverage that adds value whenever you book on public Wi-Fi.
