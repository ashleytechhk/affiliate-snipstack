## Red Flag 1: A Spoofed Sender Domain

The most reliable tell in any LinkedIn job scam is the sender's email address. Scammers register domains that look nearly identical to the real thing: `linkedln.com` (that is a lowercase L, not an i), `1inkedin.com`, or `linkedin-jobs-alert.net`. At a glance, especially on a mobile screen, these are almost impossible to distinguish from `linkedin.com`.

Before you respond to any unsolicited recruiter email, tap or click on the sender name to reveal the full email address. If the domain is anything other than `linkedin.com` or the verified domain of the company the recruiter claims to represent, treat the email as suspicious.

Legitimate corporate recruiters email from company domains — `@google.com`, `@deloitte.com`, `@accenture.com`. A recruiter claiming to represent a well-known company but emailing from `linkedin.recruiter.2026@gmail.com` or a similar free-provider address is not who they say they are.

## Red Flag 3: Generic Greeting With No Personal Detail

Real recruiters who found your LinkedIn profile know your name. They may reference your current role, your most recent employer, or a specific skill listed on your profile. An email that opens with "Dear Candidate," "Dear Job Seeker," or "Hello Professional" was not written for you — it was blasted to a list.

Generic greetings alone are not definitive proof of a scam; some legitimate recruiter tools do send templated outreach. But combined with any other red flag on this list, a greeting that does not use your name should raise your guard immediately.

## Red Flag 4: Requests to Move Off LinkedIn or Off Email

LinkedIn has its own messaging system. Legitimate recruiters are comfortable using it. A red flag appears when an email — or an initial LinkedIn message — quickly pushes you to continue the conversation on WhatsApp, Telegram, or a personal email address outside any traceable platform.

Scammers prefer private messaging apps because those conversations cannot be reported to LinkedIn, leave no platform record, and allow them to build a false sense of personal rapport before the ask for money or personal information arrives. If a recruiter's first or second message includes a [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/) and a request to move to WhatsApp, pause before responding.

## Red Flag 5: A Suspicious "Apply Now" or "Confirm Your Details" Link

Job scam emails frequently include links to fake application portals, document upload pages, or identity verification forms. These pages are designed to harvest your passport number, tax ID, home address, or banking details — information that can be used for identity fraud or that becomes leverage for further extortion.

Before clicking any link in an unsolicited recruiter email, hover over it on a computer to see the real destination in your browser's status bar. On a phone, press and hold the link to preview the URL. If the address does not match the company's official website, do not click.

## Red Flag 6: Requests for Payment Before Employment

No legitimate employer asks you to pay anything before you start work. Scammers use several framings for this:

- "Background check fee" — you pay a third-party service the scammer controls
- "Equipment deposit" — you send money for a laptop or phone that never arrives
- "Training materials" — a fee for access to onboarding documents
- "Security clearance processing" — for roles that supposedly require vetting

Occasionally these come with a twist: the "employer" sends you a cheque first (which will later bounce) and asks you to forward part of it to cover the fee. This is a classic advance-fee fraud pattern adapted for the job market.

Any payment request before a confirmed employment contract — regardless of how it is framed — is a scam.

---

## Red Flag 7: An Unrealistic Salary for Minimal Effort

A $120,000 remote role requiring no specific qualifications, no interview beyond a twenty-minute chat on Telegram, and a start date next week is not a job opportunity. It is bait.

Scammers calibrate salaries to be just high enough that the instinct to apply overrides the instinct to question. Before you respond to any offer, spend two minutes checking market salary ranges on a site like Glassdoor or Indeed for the stated role and location. If the number is significantly above market for the described responsibilities, the opportunity is almost certainly fraudulent.

---

## Red Flag 8: Manufactured Urgency

"This offer expires in 24 hours." "We need to fill this role by end of week." "Please confirm your interest immediately or we will move to the next candidate."

Legitimate hiring processes move at human speed — recruiters schedule calls, wait for responses, and allow candidates time to research the company and consider the offer. Artificial deadlines are a pressure tactic designed to stop you thinking carefully. Real employers do not rescind opportunities because you asked for a day to consider.

When urgency language appears in an initial email from someone you have never interacted with, slow down rather than speeding up.

## FAQ

### How do I know if a LinkedIn job offer email is real?

Check three things first: the sender's full email address (does the domain match the company?), any links in the email (do they lead to the company's real website?), and whether there is any request for payment or personal documents before a formal offer. For a faster answer, [run the email through a scam checker](/blog/scamcheck?sub=linkedin-job-scam-how-to-spot-2026__faq) and get a plain-language verdict in seconds.

### Can scammers fake a LinkedIn profile that looks legitimate?

Yes. Scam profiles often have a profile photo (sometimes stolen from a real person's social media), a list of past employers at recognizable companies, and a small number of connections. The presence of a LinkedIn profile does not confirm a recruiter is genuine. Always verify by checking whether the company actually has the role listed on its official careers page.

### What should I do if I already gave personal information to a fake recruiter?

Act quickly. If you shared financial details, contact your bank immediately. If you provided government ID or tax information, report the incident to your national identity protection agency (in the US, that is identitytheft.gov; in the UK, Action Fraud). Change the password on any accounts associated with the email address you used. Report the LinkedIn profile and the email as phishing so the platforms can investigate.

### Is it safe to paste a recruiter email or link into a checking tool?

Yes. You are submitting the text of the email address or link to a web form — your device does not visit any suspicious page during that process. The risk comes from clicking a suspicious link directly; submitting its text to a checker does not carry that risk.

### Are LinkedIn job scams only sent by email?

No. Scammers also use LinkedIn's own direct message system, WhatsApp, and Telegram. The red flags are the same regardless of the channel: payment requests, urgency pressure, off-platform moves, requests for personal documents before any formal offer.
