## Method 2: Gmail `+tag` Plus-Addressing

Gmail lets you append a tag to your existing address: `yourname+newsletters@gmail.com`. All email sent there arrives in your regular Gmail inbox. You can set filters to label or archive tagged mail automatically.

**Why it partially works:** It's zero-setup and free. You can use `yourname+shopname@gmail.com` for every signup, then filter by tag.

**Why it fails against newsletters:**

Most commercial email platforms — Mailchimp, Klaviyo, Constant Contact — strip everything after the `+` automatically. The email effectively arrives as `yourname@gmail.com`. Your real address is fully visible to the sender. When that list gets sold, the buyer receives your actual address, not the tagged version.

Even where `+` addressing survives, you cannot revoke it. You can filter incoming mail, but you cannot stop the sender from having `yourname+newsletters@gmail.com` — and from selling it onward.

## Method 3: Secondary "Junk" Gmail Account

The classic approach: create a second Gmail account — `yourname.newsletters@gmail.com` — and give it to every newsletter, promo signup, and mailing list.

**Why it works at first:** Your real inbox stays clean. All newsletter volume goes to the junk account.

**Why it degrades over time:**

- You have to actively check the junk inbox to retrieve anything useful (a promo code, a shipping confirmation from a newsletter brand you actually like). Most people stop checking within a month.
- When the junk inbox gets too full to manage, you're back to square one — except now you have two email accounts to deal with.
- The junk address is still a permanent email address. It accumulates spam, gets breached, and can be linked to your real identity if you've used the same name or phone number during signup.
- You cannot kill it without losing everything on that account.

A secondary Gmail solves inbox hygiene at the cost of creating a second inbox management problem.

## Method 4: Unsubscribe + Inbox Filters

The standard advice: click unsubscribe on every newsletter, then set up filters for whatever slips through.

**Why it seems reasonable:** It's built into every email client. No new tools required.

**Why it doesn't actually protect your real email from newsletters:**

Unsubscribing removes you from one specific list. It does nothing about the copies of your address already sold to third parties. Under CAN-SPAM, senders have up to 10 business days to process opt-outs. Offshore senders ignore opt-outs entirely.

Inbox filters are reactive. They catch what you've already seen enough times to create a rule for. Every new sender gets through until you add a new filter. It's permanent maintenance with no finish line.

Neither method prevents your address from being in the wild. Both accept that your real email is already compromised and attempt to manage the fallout — rather than stopping the leak.

## Method 5: Free Throwaway Email (Guerrilla Mail / 10 Minute Mail)

Free throwaway services give you a temporary inbox — no signup, no account — that expires within minutes or hours.

**Why they're useful:** For one-time gated downloads, single-use promo codes, or any signup you'll never need to revisit. Zero setup, zero cost.

**Why they completely fail for newsletters:**

Newsletters send ongoing emails. A throwaway inbox that expires in 10 minutes is useless for receiving next week's issue, a shipping update, or a promo you wanted to catch next month. Most throwaway domains are also on public blocklists — many newsletter platforms reject them at signup.

Free throwaway email is a tool for a five-minute problem. Newsletter spam is a months-long, recurring problem.

**Is using an alias to sign up for newsletters against the rules?**

Alt-ID protects your real address for future signups. Spam arriving on your existing address from past signups is a separate problem. For those, use Gmail filters or consider migrating to a new primary address and using aliases for every new signup going forward.

**Can I reply to a newsletter from my alias?**

Most newsletter communication is one-way — you receive, you don't reply. For the rare newsletter that invites replies, check whether your email client can send from the alias address. In most setups, replies will go from your real address. For newsletters, this limitation almost never matters.
