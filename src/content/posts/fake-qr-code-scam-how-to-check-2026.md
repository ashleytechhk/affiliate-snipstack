## What Is a Fake QR Code Scam?

A QR code is simply a visual shortcut to a web address. The black-and-white square encodes a URL, and your phone's camera reads it and offers to open the link. Every legitimate use — a restaurant menu, a boarding pass, a payment terminal — works exactly this way.

Scammers figured out that QR codes are perfect for hiding dangerous links. Think of it like receiving a sealed envelope with no return address and no label — you cannot tell where it has come from or where it will take you until you have already opened it. With a normal email link, you can at least hover over it and read the address in the corner of your screen. With a QR code, you see nothing until your camera decodes it.

This technique has its own name in cybersecurity: **quishing** — a combination of QR and phishing. Security researchers began tracking it seriously around 2023, and by 2026 it has become one of the most common methods scammers use to redirect victims to fake websites.

The fake website may look identical to your bank's login page, a government portal, a delivery tracking service, or a payment screen. Once you enter your details there, the scammer has them.

## Where Fake QR Codes Show Up in 2026

Scammers place fake QR codes in a surprising number of places.

**Emails asking you to scan.** A message arrives claiming your bank account needs verification, your parcel is being held, or your subscription has a problem. Instead of a clickable link, it contains a QR code. This is the most common delivery method — and the one most relevant to the Email Scam Checker tool covered below.

**Parking meters and public terminals.** Criminals stick a fraudulent QR code label over the legitimate one. Instead of paying for parking, you are taken to a fake payment page that captures your card details.

**Restaurant tables.** A sticker on the table pointing to a "menu" QR code is replaced with a fraudulent one leading to a phishing site or a page that tries to install software on your device.

**Letters and parcel slips in your post.** Physical mail can contain QR codes — fake delivery notices, fake fines, fake prize notifications. People often trust printed material more than email, which makes this particularly effective.

**Posters and leaflets.** In public spaces, scammers print or paste fake charity donation codes, event registration codes, or free-offer codes that lead to credential-harvesting pages.

Older adults are frequently targeted because they have often adopted QR codes during and after the pandemic — for checking into venues, accessing menus, or managing health bookings — and may not yet be alert to the idea that these codes can be tampered with.

## How to Check a Fake QR Code Scam — Step by Step

The process is simpler than most people realise, and you do not need any technical knowledge to do it.

**Step 1: Point your camera at the QR code, but do not tap to open.** When your phone's camera detects a QR code, it decodes the web address and shows it to you as a small preview banner — usually at the top or bottom of the screen. This preview appears before your browser opens anything. This is your checkpoint.

**Step 2: Read the preview address carefully.** Look at the domain — the core part of the address, before any slashes. Ask yourself whether it makes sense for what the QR code claims to be. A parking payment QR code should lead to the parking authority's official site. A bank verification code should show your bank's actual domain. If the address looks unfamiliar, random, or vaguely similar to a real brand but slightly wrong — like `paypa1-secure.com` instead of `paypal.com` — do not tap.

## Other Warning Signs of a Fake QR Code

Even before running a check, these patterns suggest a QR code may be fraudulent.

**A sticker placed over something else.** In physical locations, look at the QR code carefully. If it appears to be a sticker layered over another surface, particularly at a parking meter or ticket machine, treat it with strong suspicion.

**Urgency in the surrounding text.** "Scan immediately or your parcel will be returned." "Account suspended — scan to restore access now." Scammers manufacture urgency to stop you pausing and thinking. Legitimate organisations rarely require immediate scanning of a QR code to prevent account closure.

**An email sender whose address does not match the organisation.** A QR code embedded in an email claiming to be from your bank should come from the bank's official domain — not from `secure-banking-updates.net` or a free webmail address.

**The destination address looks odd in the preview.** As mentioned above, a preview that contains random characters, mimics a brand name with slight variations, or uses an unusual domain extension (`.xyz`, `.ru`, `.top`) where you would expect `.com`, `.gov`, or a known regional domain is a warning signal.

**No QR code was expected.** If you did not initiate a transaction or request a verification, you should not be receiving a QR code to scan. Unexpected QR codes — in emails, texts, or physical mail — warrant extra scrutiny.

### Can I get scammed just by scanning a QR code?

Scanning alone — decoding the image — does not put you at risk. The risk comes from tapping to open the resulting link and then entering information on the page it leads to. The preview moment your phone shows you between scanning and opening is your opportunity to assess and decide. Some pages do attempt automatic downloads when you visit them, which is a reason to check the address before tapping even if you have no intention of entering details.

### Are QR code scams common in email?

Yes. Embedding a QR code in an email is increasingly common among scammers because many email security filters are better at detecting dangerous text links than they are at reading images. A QR code embedded as an image bypasses some of those filters. If you receive an email with a QR code asking you to scan to verify your account, confirm a delivery, or access a service, treat it with heightened suspicion.

### What should I do if I already scanned and opened a suspicious QR code?

If you only opened the page and did not enter any information, the risk is lower — close the browser tab immediately. If you entered a password, change that password right away from a separate, trusted device. If you entered payment card details, contact your card provider immediately using the number on the back of your card. If you entered identity information such as your national ID number or date of birth, consider placing a fraud alert with your relevant national credit agency.
