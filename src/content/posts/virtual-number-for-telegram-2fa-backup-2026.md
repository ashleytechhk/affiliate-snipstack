## Why Telegram 2FA and Dating Apps Are a Dangerous Combination

Most people set up Telegram 2FA once and forget about it. The default fallback for account recovery is an SMS sent to your registered phone number. That sounds reasonable until you think about what can go wrong.

### SIM Swap Attacks Are More Common Than You Think

A SIM swap is when someone convinces your mobile carrier to transfer your number to a SIM card they control. Carriers verify identity by asking security questions — often things like your billing address, account PIN, or the last four digits of your ID. That information is frequently leaked in data breaches or lifted from social media profiles.

Once an attacker controls your number, they can receive any SMS code that Telegram sends. They reset your account password, take over your session, and lock you out. You lose your messages, your contacts, and in some cases your identity is used to scam people in your name.

This is not a theoretical edge case. High-profile SIM swap incidents have hit ordinary users across North America, Europe, and Australia — not just celebrities or crypto holders.

### Online Daters Share Their Number More Than They Realize

The online dating world creates a specific vulnerability loop:

1. You match with someone on a dating app.
2. After a few chats, you move the conversation to Telegram because it feels more private.
3. To add them on Telegram, they may learn your username — and if your username is linked to a searchable profile, your real number sometimes surfaces.
4. Alternatively, you share your number directly to "text outside the app."

Every person you give your number to is a potential data point. Most people are harmless. But it only takes one bad actor — or one data breach at a dating platform — to expose your number to someone who decides to use it maliciously.

If that number is also the one on your Telegram 2FA, you now have a chain of vulnerabilities running from your dating profile all the way to your messaging account.
