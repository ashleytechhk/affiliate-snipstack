## 1. Gmail's Built-In Scam Warning Banner

**Gmail's native phishing detection is your first line of defense — and it costs nothing.**

When Google suspects an email is a phishing attempt, it places a bold red banner across the top of the message: "This message seems dangerous." If you see it, do not click any links and do not download attachments. Gmail's algorithms check the sender's domain reputation, email authentication records, and message content to generate this warning.

You can reinforce this protection by enabling Gmail's "Enhanced Safe Browsing." Go to your Google Account settings, navigate to Security, and turn it on. This tells Google to cross-reference links in emails against its real-time list of known phishing and malware sites. It is one of the fastest zero-click upgrades you can make.

The limitation: Gmail's warnings only appear for emails that have already been classified as suspicious by Google. Brand-new scam campaigns, highly targeted spear-phishing, and emails sent from recently registered lookalike domains often slip through the banner system entirely.

## 3. Google's Message Header Analyzer

**For the cautious reader who wants to see the technical proof behind an email.**

Every email carries an invisible set of "headers" — a trail of metadata that records where the message actually came from, which servers it passed through, and whether it passed or failed authentication checks. Gmail lets you view these headers by clicking the three-dot menu inside any email and selecting "Show original."

The raw text is dense and hard to read. Google's free Message Header Analyzer (available at toolbox.googleapps.com/apps/messageheader/) turns that wall of text into a color-coded summary. Green means the authentication passed; red means something failed. A legitimate email from your bank will show a clean DMARC pass. An impersonation attempt will often show a mismatch between the "From" display name and the actual sending domain.

This method takes three more steps than the Surfshark checker, but it is completely free and requires no sign-up. It is especially useful if you want to understand *why* an email is suspicious, not just *whether* it is.

## 5. Have I Been Pwned — Email Breach Check

**Find out if your own Gmail address is already in criminals' hands.**

Have I Been Pwned (HIBP), created by security researcher Troy Hunt, is the internet's most trusted free database of leaked credentials. You type in your email address, and it tells you whether that address has appeared in any known data breach — complete with which sites were compromised and what type of data was exposed.

Why does this matter for scam emails? Because scammers often use breach data to make their phishing more convincing. A message that knows your name, your old password, or the last four digits of a card number feels far more legitimate than a generic blast. If HIBP shows your email appeared in a breach, you should assume scammers may already have personal details about you and treat any related email with extra caution.

HIBP is completely free. You can also sign up for free breach notifications so you get an email alert if your address appears in a future leak.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can Gmail automatically detect all scam emails?

No. Gmail's filters catch a large percentage of obvious spam and phishing, but targeted scams — especially those that impersonate known contacts or use newly registered domains — frequently bypass automated detection. Layering Gmail's protection with dedicated checkers significantly raises your catch rate.

### What does it mean if an email fails DMARC?

DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance) is a standard that tells receiving mail servers whether an email genuinely came from the domain it claims to be from. A DMARC fail means the email did not pass the sender's own authentication rules — a strong indicator of forgery or impersonation.

### Should I reply to a suspicious email to ask if it is real?

No. Replying confirms to scammers that your address is active, which typically results in more scam attempts. If you need to verify whether a message is legitimate, contact the company directly using contact details from their official website — not any contact information provided in the suspicious email.

### Is it safe to forward a suspicious email to a checking tool?

Forwarding the full email (rather than clicking links inside it) is generally safe. For link scanning, copy-paste the URL directly rather than clicking it. For file attachments, upload them to VirusTotal without opening them locally first.
