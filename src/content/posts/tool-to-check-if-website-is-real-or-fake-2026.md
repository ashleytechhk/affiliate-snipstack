## Why [Fake Website](https://snipstack.io/fake-website-detector-tool-2026/)s Are So Hard to Spot

Before comparing tools, it helps to understand what you are up against. Modern scam websites use several tactics that make them look legitimate:

- **SSL padlock icons** — that green padlock means the connection is encrypted, not that the site is safe. Scammers can and do use SSL certificates on fake sites.
- **Lookalike domains** — a scammer might register `paypa1.com` or `amazon-support-billing.com` to mimic real brands.
- **Copied design** — entire website layouts, logos, and text are stolen from legitimate companies in minutes.
- **Short lifespans** — many scam sites stay live for just days or hours before disappearing, making traditional blocklists slow to catch them.

This means visual inspection alone is not enough. You need a tool that checks more than what you can see.

## Google Safe Browsing: The Invisible Safety Net

Google Safe Browsing is already running quietly in the background if you use Chrome, Firefox, or Safari. When you navigate to a known dangerous site, your browser throws up a red warning page before you get there.

### Strengths

- Built into every major browser at no cost
- Backed by Google's enormous data infrastructure
- Catches the vast majority of well-known phishing and malware sites
- No setup required

### Limitations

Because Safe Browsing works on a blocklist model, it can be slow to catch brand-new scam sites. A fraudulent domain registered this morning may not be flagged yet. It also does not help you proactively check a link before clicking — you only see the warning after you have already navigated to the site.

For checking a specific URL you found in an email before you visit it, you would need to use Google's Safe Browsing transparency report tool manually, which is a less polished experience.

**Bottom line:** Google Safe Browsing is a solid last line of defense built into your browser, but it is not designed for proactive checking of suspicious email links.

## VirusTotal: Powerful but Built for Experts

VirusTotal scans a URL or file against over 70 different antivirus engines and security databases simultaneously. If you paste a link in, you will get a report showing how many of those engines flagged it as malicious.

### Strengths

- Comprehensive multi-engine scanning
- Free to use
- Extremely detailed results
- Well-regarded in the security research community

### Limitations

VirusTotal's results are dense and technical. A typical report might show 2 out of 73 engines flagging a URL as suspicious — but is that worrying or noise? For a trained analyst, that context is useful. For someone who received a strange email and just wants a clear answer, it creates more anxiety than clarity.

VirusTotal is also reactive: it tells you what it knows about a URL, but it does not guide you toward a decision the way a consumer-focused tool does.

**Bottom line:** VirusTotal is a powerful secondary verification tool for users who want to go deep. It is not a great primary tool for everyday email safety checks.

## WHOIS Lookup: The Detective's Tool

WHOIS allows you to look up the registration details of any domain — who owns it, when it was registered, and where. This can be revealing. A website claiming to be a major bank that was registered three weeks ago is a red flag.

### Strengths

- Free and widely available (multiple WHOIS lookup sites exist)
- Can reveal suspiciously recent domain registration dates
- Useful for checking if a domain name is even close to what it claims to be

### Limitations

Modern privacy regulations mean domain registrant information is increasingly hidden behind [privacy protection](https://snipstack.io/privacy-protection-software-review-2026/) services. You may not see a name or contact — just a redacted listing. And interpreting what you see requires some context.

WHOIS is a useful supplementary check, particularly for identifying brand-new domains. It is not a standalone scam detector.

## FAQ

### What is the fastest way to check if a website is real or fake?

Copy the URL and paste it into Surfshark Scam Checker. Results come back in seconds with a clear verdict — no technical knowledge needed. For quick secondary verification, Google's Safe Browsing transparency report is also free and fast.

### Can a website be fake even if it has a padlock icon?

Yes. The padlock means the connection between your browser and the site is encrypted. It says nothing about whether the site itself is legitimate. Scammers routinely obtain SSL certificates for fake websites. Never rely on the padlock as a safety signal on its own. [Surfshark Scam Checker](/blog/scamcheck?sub=tool-to-check-if-website-is-real-or-fake-2026__faq) checks the actual domain reputation, not just the certificate.

### Are free website checkers good enough?

Free tools like Google Safe Browsing and VirusTotal catch many known threats. However, very new scam sites may not yet appear in their databases. A dedicated tool with continuously updated real-time threat intelligence adds an extra layer of protection that free tools alone may miss.

### What should I do if a website is flagged as dangerous?

Do not proceed to the site. Do not enter any personal information. If the link came from an email pretending to be a company you use, report it to that company's fraud team directly (using contact details from their real official website, not the suspicious email). Delete the email afterward.

### How do scammers create such convincing fake websites?

Fraudsters use website copying tools to clone legitimate sites in minutes. They buy domains designed to look like brand names at a glance. Some even use AI tools to refine their copies and make phishing emails sound more natural. The sophistication has increased significantly in recent years, which is why manual inspection is no longer a reliable defense on its own.

### [Is Surfshark](https://snipstack.io/is-surfshark-one-good-for-streaming-and-privacy-2026/) Scam Checker only useful for email links?

No. While it is particularly well-suited for checking links from suspicious emails, you can use it to check any URL — links shared on social media, in text messages, in messaging apps, or anywhere else you encounter a web address you are not certain about.
