## Step 1 — Read the Display Name Carefully, Then Check the Actual Address

**What to do:** Open the email and look at who sent it. Most email apps show a friendly display name like "PayPal Support" — but the real sending address is hidden underneath.

On Gmail, click the sender name to expand the full address. On Apple Mail or Outlook, hover over or tap the name to reveal it.

**What to look for:**
- The domain after the `@` should match the company exactly. `support@paypal.com` is legitimate. `support@paypal-secure-alert.com` is not.
- Watch for lookalike characters: `rn` can look like `m`, and `0` (zero) can look like `O` (letter O).
- If the display name says one company but the actual domain is a random Gmail or Hotmail address, that is a major red flag.

## Step 2 — Do Not Click Any Link — Hover First

**What to do:** Before clicking any link in the email, hover your mouse over it (on desktop) or long-press it (on mobile) to preview the actual URL.

**What to look for:**
- The URL should start with `https://` and match the company's real domain
- Watch for extra words inserted into the domain: `amazon-order-update.com` is not Amazon
- Shortened URLs (bit.ly, tinyurl.com) in unexpected emails are suspicious — you cannot see where they lead without clicking

**Do not** click a link just to see if it is real. That single click can be enough to trigger a malware download or confirm your email address to spammers.

## Step 4 — Inspect the Email Body for Classic Scam Signals

Even without any tools, the content of the email itself reveals a lot.

**Common red flags in the body:**
- **Urgency language:** "Your account will be closed in 24 hours." Scammers pressure you to act fast so you do not think clearly.
- **Generic greetings:** "Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name suggests a mass-sent phishing blast.
- **Requests for personal information:** Legitimate banks and services will never ask for your password, full card number, or [Social Security](https://snipstack.io/social-security-scam-call-verify-2026/) number via email.
- **Poor spelling and grammar:** Not every scam email has bad spelling in 2026 — AI tools have improved scammer copy — but obvious errors are still a warning sign.
- **Mismatched branding:** Compare the email's logo and colours against the company's real website. Slightly off fonts and blurry logos are common in phishing emails.

## Step 5 — Verify Directly Through the Official Website

If you are still unsure after the checks above, do not reply to the email or call any [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/) listed in it. Instead:

1. Open a new browser tab
2. Manually type the company's real website address (e.g., `paypal.com`, `amazon.com`)
3. Log in to your account directly
4. Check whether the alert or message in the email actually shows up in your account notifications

If the email was real, you will see the same information inside your account. If your account shows nothing unusual, the email was almost certainly a scam.

## Step 6 — Report the Scam Email

Reporting phishing emails helps protect other people. It takes less than a minute.

- **Gmail:** Open the email, click the three-dot menu, select "Report phishing"
- **Outlook:** Click "Report" → "Report phishing" in the toolbar
- **Apple Mail:** Drag the email to your Junk folder, then forward it to `reportphishing@apple.com`
- **Global reporting:** Forward phishing emails to `reportphishing@apwg.org` (Anti-Phishing Working Group)

Then delete the email. Do not keep it in your inbox.

## Troubleshooting: Common Questions

**The email looks real but something still feels wrong. What should I do?**

Trust your instinct. Go directly to the company's official website by typing the URL yourself, log in, and check your account. If nothing matches what the email described, it was a scam.

**I already clicked the link. What now?**

Do not panic. Immediately close the tab. Run a malware scan using your device's built-in security tools. Change the password of any account you may have entered credentials into. Contact your bank if any financial information was involved.

**Can a scammer fake a legitimate email address completely?**

Yes — a technique called email spoofing lets scammers make an email appear to come from a real domain. The best defence is to check the email headers (available via "Show original" in Gmail or "View source" in Outlook) and look for SPF/DKIM/DMARC pass or fail results.

**Q: How do I check if an email address is valid or fake?**

Hover over the sender's name to reveal the full address. Then Google the exact address, check it against a breach database, and look up the sending domain's age using a WHOIS lookup tool. Newly registered domains (less than six months old) used by "banks" or "delivery companies" are almost always fraudulent.

**Q: What is the safest way to verify a suspicious email from my bank?**

Close the email and call the number on the back of your bank card or navigate to your bank's official website manually. Do not use any contact details provided inside the suspicious email itself.

**Q: Can my email account be used to send scam emails without my knowledge?**

Yes — if your account credentials appear in a data breach, scammers can use them to send phishing emails from your address. Check your account's sent folder for any emails you did not send, change your password immediately, and enable two-factor authentication.
