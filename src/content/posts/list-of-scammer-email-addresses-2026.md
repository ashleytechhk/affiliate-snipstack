## Step 1 — Open the Suspicious Email (But Do Not Click Any Links)

Open the email you are unsure about. Do not click any links inside it, do not download any attachments, and do not reply yet.

Your only goal at this stage is to find the sender's email address.

**Screenshot placeholder:** *[Image showing a sample suspicious email with the sender address highlighted in the "From" field]*

### Step 1a — Find the Full Sender Address

Look at the "From" field at the top of the email. Many email apps show only a display name like "PayPal Support" — you need to see the actual address behind the name.

- **Gmail:** Click or tap the sender's name. A small popup shows the full address underneath.
- **Apple Mail:** Hover your mouse over the sender's name. The real address appears.
- **Outlook:** Click the sender's name. Select "Show Details" or look for the email in angle brackets.

Write down or copy the full address — for example `support@paypa1-security.com` (note the "1" instead of "l" — a classic trick).

## Step 2 — Search Known Scammer Email Lists

There are several publicly maintained databases and community-reported lists of scam email addresses. Start here:

**Step 2a — Check government fraud databases**

- The US Federal Trade Commission keeps a scam reporting database at **reportfraud.ftc.gov**. While it does not publish a searchable list of addresses, you can search by company name to find recent scam patterns.
- Action Fraud (UK) at **actionfraud.police.uk** maintains reports by category.

Sites like **EmailAbuse.org**, **ScamSearch.io**, and Reddit's r/Scams community publish user-reported scammer addresses. ## Step 4 — Look for These Red Flags Yourself

Even if an address is not yet in a database (new scams get registered daily), you can spot scammers by examining the address carefully.

**Common scammer address tricks in 2026:**

| Red Flag | Example |
|---|---|
| Letter swaps (l vs 1, 0 vs o) | `paypa1@secure.com` |
| Extra words added | `amazon-help-desk@amazon-support.net` |
| Domain does not match brand | `apple@verification-account.org` |
| Random numbers after name | `support48271@gmail.com` |
| Misspelled brand names | `netfliix@billing-center.com` |

A legitimate company email will almost always use its own domain (e.g. `support@amazon.com`, not `amazon-help@outlook.com`).

## Step 5 — Report the Scammer Address

Once you have confirmed or strongly suspect the address is a scam, report it. This helps protect everyone else.

**Step 5a — Mark it as spam in your email app**

Every major email service has a "Report Spam" or "Report Phishing" option. In Gmail, click the three-dot menu next to the email. In Outlook, right-click the message. Reporting helps the email provider train its filters.

**Step 5b — Report to official agencies**

- **US residents:** Forward the email to `spam@uce.gov` and file a report at **reportfraud.ftc.gov**
- **UK residents:** Forward to `report@phishing.gov.uk`
- **Australian residents:** Forward to `reportascam@accc.gov.au`

**Step 5c — Warn others**

Post the address to community sites like r/Scams or ScamSearch.io. Your report may be the one that stops the next person from being fooled.

## Troubleshooting

**"I cannot find the full sender address in my email app."**
Try viewing the email on a computer rather than a phone. On mobile apps the full address is sometimes hidden. Alternatively, look for a "View Source" or "Show Original" option which reveals all email headers.

**"The checker says the address is not found, but the email still feels like a scam."**
Trust your instincts. New scam addresses are created every day and may not be in databases yet. Look for the red flags in Step 4. When in doubt, do not click, do not reply, and call the company directly using a [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/) from their official website.

**"I already clicked a link. What do I do?"**
Close the browser tab immediately. Run a virus scan on your device. Change any passwords you may have entered. If you entered payment information, contact your bank right away and explain what happened.

**"The email came from a real person I know."**
Scammers frequently hijack or spoof real contacts' addresses. Call or text your contact through a separate channel to confirm they actually sent the message.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is there an official government list of scammer email addresses I can download?**
No single government publishes a downloadable master list, because scam addresses are created and discarded constantly. The most useful approach is a combination of real-time checkers (like the Surfshark tool), community-reported databases, and your own knowledge of the red flags described in Step 4. [Start with a free check here.](/blog/surfshark?sub=list-of-scammer-email-addresses-2026__faq)

**How do scammers get my email address in the first place?**
Your address can be harvested from data breaches (when a website you used is hacked), scraped from social media, purchased from marketing list brokers, or guessed by bots that try common name patterns. Checking whether your address has appeared in a breach gives you useful context.

**Can a scam email come from a Gmail or Outlook address?**
Yes. Scammers routinely create free accounts on major providers because they are fast to set up and carry some trust. A Gmail address alone does not make an email safe. Look at the full context: does the message make an unexpected request, create urgency, or ask for personal information?

**What is email spoofing and how does it differ from a scam address?**
Spoofing is when a scammer forges the "From" display name or even the visible address to look like a trusted sender, while the actual message is delivered from a different address entirely. Checking the email headers (via "Show Original" in Gmail) reveals the true sending server. Most scam checkers focus on the address in the headers, not just the display name.

**Will reporting a scam email actually do anything?**
Yes, in two ways. First, reporting to your email provider trains their spam filters, which helps everyone using that service. Second, reporting to agencies like the FTC or Action Fraud contributes to enforcement investigations. Large-scale scam operations have been shut down partly because of aggregated user reports.
