## What a No-Logs Policy Actually Means

Start with the term itself. "Logs" in the VPN context refers to records a provider creates as traffic passes through their servers. Not all logs are equal — and almost no provider is truly log-free in the absolute sense.

### The Three Types of VPN Logs

**Activity logs** are the sensitive kind. These record which websites you visited, which IP addresses you connected to, DNS queries you made, and the content of your browsing session. A genuine no-logs policy means none of this is captured.

**Connection logs** are a middle ground. These record when you connected, which server you used, and how long the session lasted — but not what you did during it. Some providers keep connection logs for troubleshooting while claiming a no-activity-logs policy. Whether this is acceptable depends on your threat model.

**Operational logs** are the minimum any functioning service needs. Aggregate bandwidth data across servers, crash reports, account authentication records — these keep the infrastructure running without tying back to individual user activity. Reputable providers are upfront about retaining these.

When a provider says "no logs," verify which category they mean. The strongest policies cover activity and connection logs. Operational data is generally unavoidable and does not represent the same risk.

## Why the Policy Alone Is Not Enough

A privacy policy is a document. Writing it costs nothing. A VPN company can publish a detailed no-logs commitment, have it reviewed by lawyers, and still log everything on the backend — users would have no way to know unless they examined the server directly.

This is not a hypothetical concern. Several [VPN providers](https://snipstack.io/vpn-providers-best-2026/) have been caught producing user records after being served legal demands — records that their privacy policies claimed did not exist. The exposure happens when a provider's word is the only evidence of their policy.

![How VPN no-logs audits work versus self-reported privacy policies](/og/vpn-no-logs-policy-explained-2026-inline-1.png)

### What Third-Party Auditing Actually Checks

An independent audit brings in a firm with no commercial relationship with the VPN provider to examine the actual infrastructure. This is not a review of the privacy policy document. The auditors look at:

- Whether logging software is installed on the servers
- Whether the network configuration writes any user-linked records at any layer of the stack
- Whether internal data retention policies and employee access controls match what the privacy policy claims
- Whether DNS queries are being stored anywhere in the flow

A passed audit is evidence. It is point-in-time evidence — infrastructure changes, and a 2022 audit says little about 2026 operations — but it is the difference between a claim and a verified claim.

Providers worth trusting repeat audits regularly, not just once for the press release.

## How to Evaluate a No-Logs Claim

Not every provider that says "no logs" has done the work to back it. When assessing a VPN for this standard, four questions narrow the field quickly.

**Has the policy been audited by a named independent firm?**
Look for a specific auditor — a recognizable accounting or cybersecurity firm — and a described scope. "Self-certified" or "internally reviewed" does not meet this bar. Surfshark's audit was conducted by Deloitte; that level of attribution is what credibility looks like in practice.

**What exactly did the audit examine?**
A privacy policy review and a server infrastructure inspection are entirely different things. The more meaningful audit examines actual server configurations, network traffic paths, and data retention mechanisms — not legal text.

**How recent is the audit, and is it repeated?**
An audit from several years ago is not evidence of current practice. Providers that take this seriously publish recurring audit results. Annual cadence is a reasonable expectation.

**Has the provider ever been served legal demands?**
A provider that received a court order for user records and had nothing to produce is more credible than one that has never been tested. Search for coverage of legal demands the provider has faced. A genuine no-logs provider's response to such demands is: here is the audit, and here is what we cannot produce.

## Common Misconceptions About No-Logs VPNs

**"No logs means I am completely private online."**
No. A no-logs policy removes the VPN provider from the picture. Your device, browser, logged-in accounts, and payment method for the VPN itself can all still carry identifying information. It is one layer of protection, not a complete solution.

**"[Free VPNs](https://snipstack.io/vs-free-vpns-2026/) also claim no logs."**
Free VPN business models require monetization. The most common form is selling user data. A no-logs claim from a free provider is contradicted by the economic structure of the service. Audited no-logs infrastructure costs real money to maintain — pricing that reflects those costs is itself a signal.

**"If a VPN has a no-logs policy, it will never get blocked by Netflix."**
These are unrelated. Streaming platforms detect VPNs based on IP reputation — whether the IP address looks like a datacenter range or a residential connection. The logging policy has no effect on detection. What matters for streaming is IP quality and rotation.
