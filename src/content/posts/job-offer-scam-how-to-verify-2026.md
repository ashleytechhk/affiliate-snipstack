## Red Flag 1: The Sender's Email Domain Does Not Match the Company

This is the single most reliable signal. Real recruiters from real companies send email from their company's domain. A message claiming to be from Google HR should come from `@google.com` — not `@google-careers-hr.com`, not `@gmail.com`, not `@google.recruitment-portal.net`.

Open the suspicious email and look at the full sending address — not just the display name. On Gmail, click the sender's name to expand the full address. On Outlook or Apple Mail, hover over or tap the name.

**What to check:**
- The domain after the `@` should exactly match the company's main website
- Watch for extra words inserted around the real brand name: `amazon-jobs-apply.com` is not Amazon
- Watch for lookalike characters: `rn` together can look like `m` at a glance
- A recruiter claiming to represent a Fortune 500 company but writing from a Gmail address is a hard stop

If anything looks wrong here, do not click any link in the email. Verify independently first.

## Red Flag 2: You Never Applied — and They Found You Anyway

Unsolicited job offers are not automatically scams. Real headhunters do cold-contact candidates. But a legitimate recruiter will be specific: they will mention where they found your profile, reference your actual experience, and represent a real company you can verify.

Scam job emails tend to be vague about how they found you. They use flattery ("We were very impressed by your background") without citing any specifics. They offer roles that are unusually well-paid for the described responsibilities. They are urgent — they need your information quickly or the opportunity disappears.

**Ask yourself:**
- Does the recruiter name a specific team, location, or hiring manager?
- Can you find the open role on the company's official careers page?
- Does the salary range make sense for the type of work described?

If the email is vague on all three points, treat it with significant skepticism.

## Red Flag 3: They Ask You to Pay — for Anything

No legitimate employer charges candidates money at any stage of the hiring process. Scammers disguise these charges in several ways:

- A "background check fee" paid directly to them (not to a third-party service)
- A "training materials" purchase before you start
- Equipment costs that you pay upfront and will "be reimbursed later"
- A fee to access the job application portal

These requests are universal scam signals. If any payment is requested before you have signed a legitimate employment contract and received your first paycheck from a real payroll system, stop completely.

## Red Flag 4: The Interview Is Over Chat Only — With No Video

Real hiring processes involve at minimum a video call at some stage. Scammers avoid video because they cannot easily fake a face. Watch for:

- Interviews conducted entirely over WhatsApp, Telegram, or Google Chat — with no email from a company domain to back it up
- Recruiters who claim their camera is broken for every call
- An offer extended after a text-only conversation with no verification of your identity

A job offer with no video interview from a "major company" is not normal in 2026. Push for a video call on a platform the company officially uses. A scammer will usually stall, make excuses, or disappear entirely.

## Red Flag 5: The Link in the Email Does Not Go Where It Claims

Before clicking any link in a job offer email, hover over it on desktop to preview the actual URL in your browser's status bar. On mobile, long-press the link to see the destination before tapping.

**What to look for:**
- The URL should take you to the company's real domain — `careers.amazon.com`, not `amazon-careers-apply.net`
- Shortened URLs (bit.ly, tinyurl) in job offer emails are a warning sign — the destination is hidden
- Links that go to Google Forms or Dropbox asking for personal details are unusual for large companies

If the link destination looks wrong, do not click it. Instead, open a new browser tab and navigate manually to the company's careers page.

## What to Do If You've Already Responded

If you replied to a job scam email, clicked a link, or shared personal information, take these steps immediately:

1. **Do not send any money.** If a payment was requested and not yet sent, stop. If money was sent, contact your bank immediately to report fraud and request a reversal.
2. **Change passwords** on any account you may have accessed on the same device after clicking any link from the email.
3. **Monitor your email address** for signs of breach exposure — scammers who collect your details through fake job applications often sell them or use them in follow-up attacks.
4. **Report the scam** to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov and to the job board where the listing appeared, if applicable.

The faster you act, the more damage you can limit.

## FAQ

### How do I verify if a job offer email is real or a scam?

Start by checking the sender's full email address — it should come from the company's real domain, not a lookalike or free email service. Then search the company name and the recruiter's name online. Look for the open role on the company's official careers page. For a faster check, [run the sender's address through Surfshark Scam Checker](/blog/scamcheck?sub=job-offer-scam-how-to-verify-2026__faq) to see if it matches known phishing infrastructure.

### Is it normal to get a job offer without applying?

Unsolicited recruiter outreach does happen legitimately, particularly on LinkedIn or for senior roles. The difference is specificity — real recruiters reference your actual experience and represent verifiable companies. Vague flattery, unusual salary promises, and urgency are the signals that separate genuine headhunting from a scam attempt.

### What information should I never give to a recruiter before a job offer is confirmed?

Never provide your Social Security number, bank account details, a copy of your passport or driver's license, or any payment before you have a signed contract and have verified the employer independently. Legitimate hiring processes collect sensitive documents during onboarding after a formal offer — not during initial contact.

### Can scammers fake a real company's email address?

Yes. A technique called email spoofing allows scammers to display a real company's name in the "From" field while the actual sending address is different. Always click or hover to reveal the full sending address. You can also check email headers (via "Show original" in Gmail) for SPF and DKIM authentication results — failed authentication on an email claiming to be from a major company is a strong indicator of spoofing.

### What should I do if the job posting looks real but the email still feels wrong?

Trust the instinct. Go directly to the company's official website, navigate to their careers page, and look for the specific role mentioned. Call the company's main switchboard number (found on their official site — not in the email) and ask to be connected with their HR or recruiting team to confirm the outreach. If they have no record of contacting you, you have your answer.
