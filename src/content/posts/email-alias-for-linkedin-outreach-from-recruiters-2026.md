### SimpleLogin

SimpleLogin is the most respected standalone alias service in 2026. It is open-source, audited, and owned by Proton (the makers of ProtonMail). You get unlimited aliases on the free tier with some restrictions, and the paid plan at around $4/month removes those limits entirely.

Each alias can be toggled on or off independently. You can set custom display names. Replies from your real inbox go out through the alias so the recipient never sees your actual address.

The downside is setup friction. SimpleLogin requires you to configure a custom domain to get clean-looking aliases, otherwise your address will contain the SimpleLogin domain — which flags it to savvy recruiters as an alias.

**Best for:** Privacy-conscious users who want open-source infrastructure and do not mind a slightly longer setup.

---

### AnonAddy

AnonAddy (now rebranded as addy.io) is the budget pick. The free plan is generous — you get unlimited standard aliases and unlimited forwarding bandwidth. The paid tier starts at $1/month and unlocks custom domains and additional features.

The interface is clean and the alias management is solid. The main limitation is that AnonAddy lacks the identity-masking layer that Alt ID provides. You get a forwarding pipe, not a new professional identity.

**Best for:** Frugal users who need basic alias forwarding without paying a monthly fee.

---

### Apple Hide My Email

If you are deep in the Apple ecosystem, Hide My Email is the lowest-friction option. It is built into Safari and iOS/macOS, so you can generate a new alias in seconds without leaving your browser. Each alias forwards to your iCloud address and can be disabled per-alias.

The limitation is Apple lock-in. Your aliases only work through iCloud, and managing them outside Apple devices is clunky. There is also no way to make the alias look professional — the random Apple-generated strings are immediately recognizable.

**Best for:** iPhone/Mac users who want quick throwaway addresses for low-stakes signups.

---

### Gmail + Tag

The classic `yourname+linkedin@gmail.com` trick is not an alias — it is a tag. Your real address is fully visible in the raw email header, and any competent recruiter (or their CRM) strips the tag automatically. It provides zero privacy. It is listed here only to be ruled out.

**Best for:** Nothing, if privacy is your goal.

## What to Look For in an [Email Alias for](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-dentist-appointment-confirmation-emails-2026/) Professional Use

Not all alias tools are equal when the use case is professional networking. Here is what matters:

**Appearance.** A recruiter receiving a reply from `xk7f92@anondomain.io` knows immediately it is an alias. That changes the dynamic. A clean-looking custom-domain alias looks professional and does not signal evasion.

**Reply support.** Some tools only receive mail — they do not let you reply through the alias. For recruiter conversations you actually want to have, reply support is essential.

**Per-alias kill switch.** The whole point is to be able to retire a noisy alias without disrupting others. Make sure your tool supports per-alias toggling, not just domain-level blocking.

**Speed of setup.** If creating a new alias takes ten minutes, you will not do it consistently. The best tools let you spin up a new alias in under 30 seconds.

## How to Use an [Email Alias for](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-booking-cheap-flights-and-travel-deals-2026/) LinkedIn Outreach

The workflow is simple once it is set up:

1. Create a dedicated alias for LinkedIn-adjacent use (job searching, networking, or just keeping your profile active).
2. Set that alias as your contact email in your LinkedIn settings, or use it only when responding to inbound recruiter messages.
3. All recruiter outreach goes to the alias and forwards to your real inbox. You can read and reply normally.
4. When the alias becomes too noisy — or when you land the job and want to go quiet — toggle it off. The alias stops forwarding. Recruiters get no bounce, you get no more mail.
5. If you start a new search cycle, create a fresh alias. Your old recruiter contacts cannot follow you.

## FAQ

### Does using an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-veteran-benefit-portal-registration-2026/) on LinkedIn violate their terms of service?

No. LinkedIn's terms require that you use your real identity for your profile, but they do not specify what email address you use as your contact method. Using a forwarding alias is entirely consistent with their policies — you are still reachable, which is all the platform requires.

### Can recruiters tell I am using an alias?

It depends on the tool. Generic free-tier aliases from services with recognizable domain names are easy to spot. A custom-domain alias from Alt ID or SimpleLogin looks identical to a regular professional email. [Alt ID specifically](/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-for-linkedin-outreach-from-recruiters-2026__faq) generates masked identities designed to look like ordinary addresses.

### Will I miss important messages if I turn off an alias?

Yes, intentionally — that is the point. Before you disable an alias, move any active conversations to a new alias or your real address. Disabling is for when you want the channel closed, not just quieter.

### Can I reply to recruiter emails through my alias?

Yes, with tools that support reply routing (Alt ID, SimpleLogin, AnonAddy). Your reply goes out from the alias address, so the recruiter never sees your real address at any point in the conversation.

### What is the difference between an email alias and a temporary email?

A temporary email (like Mailinator or Guerrilla Mail) is purely throwaway — no forwarding, usually public, and expires. An alias is permanent until you delete it, forwards to your real inbox, and lets you reply. For LinkedIn outreach where you want real two-way communication, you need an alias, not a temp address.

### Is Alt ID worth it if I already have SimpleLogin?

If privacy is your main concern, both are solid. The differentiator is the identity layer. SimpleLogin gives you a forwarding alias; Alt ID gives you a masked identity with a fresh email address that has no visible connection to your real one. For professional networking where appearance matters, Alt ID's cleaner output is worth the switch.
