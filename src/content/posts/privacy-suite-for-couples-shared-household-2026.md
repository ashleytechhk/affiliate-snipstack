## Norton 360 Deluxe — Familiar, Heavy, Expensive to Keep

Norton is the most recognised name in consumer security for a reason. The antivirus engine is solid, the dark web monitoring is thorough, and the interface has improved in recent years. If you grew up with Norton, it feels familiar.

The problems are structural, not just cosmetic.

The VPN that comes with standard Norton 360 plans caps data usage. To get unlimited VPN, you need the Plus tier or higher — at meaningfully more cost. For a household where both partners travel or work from cafés, that limitation matters.

The bloatware criticism is earned. Norton installs multiple background services, adds a [browser extension](https://snipstack.io/browser-extension-to-check-scam-sites-2026/), and pops up recommendations for additional products regularly. On older hardware, the performance impact is noticeable. On newer machines it is less severe, but the constant surface area for upsell prompts remains.

**Year-two pricing is the dealbreaker for many.** A plan that cost $50 in year one routinely renews at over $100 unless you manually cancel and re-subscribe at a new introductory rate. That is a chore that should not have to be a chore.

**Best for:** Users deeply embedded in the Norton ecosystem who have a family plan and are happy to negotiate the renewal price every year.

## McAfee Total Protection — Improving, But the Habits Die Hard

McAfee went through a rough patch — performance bloat, complicated interfaces, aggressive upsells. The current version is better. The security engine is competitive, the identity monitoring is solid, and the interface is cleaner than it used to be.

The unlimited VPN is available on higher-tier McAfee plans — a genuine advantage if you need both antivirus and a reliable VPN. Device limits at 5 are adequate for most couples but will strain a household with more gadgets.

The renewal trap is identical to Norton's. First-year rates are competitive; second-year rates are not. And McAfee's cross-sell ecosystem is extensive: once you are in, you will be offered add-ons, identity products, and plan upgrades on a regular basis.

**Best for:** Households that need unlimited VPN at the major-brand tier and are disciplined about checking renewal terms before they auto-charge.

## Side-by-Side: What Matters Most for Two People

If you strip away the brand names and just look at the household use case, three factors drive the decision:

**1. Devices per person, not per plan.** Two people with modern tech habits easily hit 5–6 devices. A 5-device cap is not a household plan — it is a single-person plan with one slot of wiggle room.

**2. VPN without a cap.** One partner traveling for work, the other using café Wi-Fi to work remotely — both need an unrestricted VPN. A 200 MB daily cap runs out in a short video call.

**3. Breach monitoring for both people's email addresses.** Your partner's email being in a breach is your problem too, especially if they use the same password on shared services. A plan that covers multiple email addresses under one alert account is materially more useful for couples.

[Surfshark One](https://snipstack.io/surfshark-one-vs-avast-one-review-2026/) covers all three cleanly. Norton and McAfee cover all three only if you pay for their higher tiers, which brings the annual cost up toward or past what Surfshark charges for an unlimited plan.
