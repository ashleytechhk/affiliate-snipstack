## The Core Problem: One Address Does Too Many Jobs

Most people have a single personal email address that they've used for everything since they first got online. Job applications, streaming subscriptions, online shopping, loyalty cards, forum signups — it all goes to the same place. When that address becomes your work contact too, you've officially lost the ability to triage anything.

The underlying issue is that **email addresses are not revocable once shared.** You gave your address to a retailer three years ago, they sold it, it got resold twice, and now it's baked into a dozen marketing databases. Unsubscribing removes you from one sender's list, not the ecosystem they've seeded your address into.

Separating shopping from work mail requires a structural fix, not a behavioral one.

## How [Email Alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-healthcare-appointment-reminders-2026/)es Solve This

An alias is a forwarding address that accepts incoming mail and routes it to your real inbox (or a separate inbox you designate). You never give out your actual address to shops or subscriptions — you give out the alias instead.

When that alias starts filling up with junk, you have two moves: pause it temporarily or delete it permanently. Either way, your real address stays clean. Create a fresh alias for any legitimate retailers you still want to hear from, and the spam line is cut.

The key comparison criteria across alias tools:

- Number of aliases allowed
- Whether you can use custom domain or branded addresses
- Forwarding flexibility (which inboxes can receive the forwarded mail)
- Ability to delete or disable individual aliases
- Price and platform lock-in

## Option 2: Apple Hide My Email — Best if You're All-In on Apple

Apple Hide My Email generates randomised aliases on the fly and is integrated directly into Safari autofill and the iOS keyboard. When a checkout form asks for your email, Safari offers to generate a Hide My Email address instead of filling in your real one.

**Where it works well:** If your personal device is an iPhone or Mac and you're comfortable with the iCloud ecosystem, the friction to adopt this is nearly zero. The aliases are managed in iCloud settings, and you can disable any one of them instantly.

**Where it breaks down:** The Apple ecosystem lock-in is real. You can't easily use Hide My Email on Android, Windows Chrome, or non-Apple browsers. If your work machine runs Windows and your personal phone is an iPhone, you'll end up with an alias tool that only works half the time. There's also no cross-device alias dashboard — management happens inside Apple's settings, not a purpose-built alias manager.

For pure Apple households, it's a strong free-tier option. For mixed environments, it creates more friction than it removes.

## Option 3: Gmail "+" Trick — Free but Easily Defeated

Gmail allows you to append a `+tag` to your address — so `yourname+shopping@gmail.com` delivers to your regular Gmail inbox. You can filter on the `+shopping` tag to sort incoming mail.

**The problem:** This is the worst-kept secret in email, and most major retailers' signup forms strip the `+tag` before storing your address. You end up handing over your real address anyway, just with a label that gets discarded. A quick test: sign up for a major retailer's newsletter using your `+shopping` alias and check whether promotional mail actually arrives at the tagged address or bypasses it. In many cases, the tag is gone.

Even when it works, you can't "kill" the alias. There's no delete. If your real address (`yourname@gmail.com`) gets sold, the `+shopping` tag doesn't protect you at all — the base address is what's in the database.

Use this as a filter helper for trusted services, not as a real separation strategy.

## Option 4: SimpleLogin — Best for Power Users Who Want Control

SimpleLogin is an open-source alias service with a clean dashboard, custom domain support, and integration with most major email providers. The free tier offers a limited number of aliases; paid tiers remove the cap.

**Best for:** developers, privacy-focused users, or anyone who wants to run their alias service on their own server. Not the fastest setup for someone who just wants to stop shopping spam from hitting their work inbox by lunchtime.

## Option 5: Firefox Relay — Decent Free Option with a Low Cap

Firefox Relay offers five free aliases under a Firefox account. Like Alt ID and SimpleLogin, each alias forwards to your real inbox and can be deleted individually.

Five aliases is enough to cover the core separation — one for shopping, one for newsletters, one for one-off signups — but leaves no headroom for the per-retailer granularity that makes spam identification possible. The paid tier removes the cap.

If you're a Firefox user who wants zero cost and a quick start, Relay gets you functional. If you expect to create more than five aliases in the next few months, you'll hit the ceiling and need to upgrade or switch anyway.

5. Never give your work address to anything non-work-related again

The moment a spam wave hits your shopping alias, you delete that alias, cut the spam line, and create a fresh one for the retailers you still want to hear from.

**What is the fastest way to stop shopping spam from hitting my work inbox?**

Create a dedicated [email alias for](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-privacy-without-creating-new-account-2026/) shopping and update your major retail accounts to use it this week. **Can I use an alias to reply to a shop's email without revealing my real address?**

This depends on the alias tool and your email client configuration. Some services support reply-from-alias so the retailer sees only the alias address. Alt ID, SimpleLogin, and Firefox Relay all handle inbound forwarding; reply-from-alias support varies by plan and setup. Check the documentation of whichever service you choose before relying on it for outbound replies.

**Do I need a separate email account for shopping, or will an alias do the job?**

An alias is almost always better than a second account. With a second account you have to log in and out constantly, you'll miss emails, and the inbox becomes orphaned. An alias forwards to your existing personal inbox, so you see everything in one place but shopping mail is clearly separated from your work traffic. No inbox management overhead.

**Will retailers reject alias email addresses?**

Most major retailers accept aliases without issue. Occasionally a service will block known alias domains — this is more common with temporary disposable address providers than with persistent alias services like Alt ID or SimpleLogin. If a signup is rejected, try an alias on a different domain if your service offers multiple, or use a browser extension that can generate a new address format.

**How many aliases do I actually need to separate shopping from work?**

At minimum: one alias for retail/e-commerce, one for subscriptions and newsletters, one for loyalty programmes. That covers 90% of the noise. If you want forensic-level tracking — knowing exactly which retailer leaked your data — use one alias per major store. Alt ID's unlimited alias allowance makes this practical without extra cost per alias.

**Is it safe to have all shopping mail forwarding to my personal inbox?**

Yes. The forwarding itself is handled by the alias service's servers — your real address stays hidden from the sender. The risk model here is about keeping your real address out of retail databases, which aliases accomplish. The forwarding is just a routing step; it doesn't expose your address.
