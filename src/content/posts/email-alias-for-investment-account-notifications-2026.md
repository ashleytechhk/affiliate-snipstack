## Why Investment Platforms Generate So Much Spam

Before getting into the tools, it helps to understand why the inbox problem is worse for investment accounts than most other use cases.

**Financial leads are high-value.** Advertisers pay more for someone with a brokerage account than for someone who bought shoes online. That makes your email address more commercially valuable the moment it appears on a financial platform. Brokerages, robo-advisors, and stock-screener apps operate in an ecosystem where data sharing with "trusted partners" is standard language in terms of service.

**You sign up for more platforms than you think.** Tracking dividends, screening stocks, reading earnings summaries, following IPO calendars — each of these typically requires an account. Each account is another point where your email enters a new lead pipeline. An active investor using five or six tools has five or six potential spam sources.

**The emails you need look like the ones you do not.** A trade confirmation and a promotional email from a financial newsletter can arrive in the same inbox the same minute. If your investment inbox is noisy enough, the signal gets buried. That is not just annoying — it is a risk.

Using a separate alias per platform solves the signal-to-noise problem at the source. The alias that receives your Fidelity trade confirmations is different from the alias that receives your stock screener's weekly digest. If the screener goes quiet one month and spammy the next, you know exactly which alias to delete — and your Fidelity confirmations keep arriving unaffected.

### Gmail's `+tag` Trick

Gmail allows you to add `+anything` to your address — `youremail+fidelity@gmail.com` still delivers to your inbox. You can then filter by tag and bulk-archive.

The problem specific to financial platforms: brokerage and financial services companies are particularly likely to strip the `+tag` suffix before storing your address. Many CRM and email marketing systems normalise email addresses by removing the `+` section. Your tag disappears, the base address is stored, and you have no way to revoke it. You have given your permanent address to the platform's entire marketing stack.

For notifications you genuinely want — trade alerts, account security emails — the tag-based filter approach also requires ongoing manual maintenance. Gmail filters can drift, labels pile up, and the filtering logic breaks when platforms change their sending domain (which financial newsletters do regularly).

### Apple Hide My Email

Apple's alias service (included with iCloud+ from $0.99/month) generates random `@privaterelay.appleid.com` addresses that forward to your iCloud email. It integrates seamlessly into Safari and iOS autofill.

For investment accounts, the main limitation is platform lock-in. If you do your research on a Windows desktop or use Chrome on any platform, the autofill integration is unavailable and setup becomes manual. Brokerages often require ongoing communication across platforms — desktop for research, mobile for quick trades, browser for account settings. A system that works smoothly only on Apple hardware creates friction at inconvenient moments.

There is also no generated identity alongside the alias, meaning your real name enters financial forms even when a fake name would suffice (screener accounts, newsletter sign-ups).

For investors who work entirely within the Apple ecosystem and already pay for iCloud+, Hide My Email is a reasonable secondary tool. For cross-platform use, the friction is real.

### Firefox Relay

Firefox Relay gives five free aliases, then charges $1.99/month for unlimited. It works across browsers (not just Firefox) and integrates with the Firefox browser extension for quick alias insertion.

The five-alias free limit is a real constraint for investors managing accounts on multiple platforms — five platforms fills the quota immediately. The paid tier at $1.99/month is affordable and unlocks what you need, but at that price SimpleLogin and Alt-ID both offer more.

For casual investors managing one or two accounts, Firefox Relay's free tier is a workable starting point. For anyone with a broader stack of financial tools, the limit becomes a problem quickly.

### Free Throwaway Services

Guerrilla Mail, 10 Minute Mail, and similar throwaway services provide a temporary address with a separate inbox you check in a browser tab. They cost nothing and require no account.

For investment notifications, they fail on two fundamental counts:

**They do not forward.** Trade confirmations, security alerts, and settlement notices arrive in the throwaway inbox, not your real email. You would need to keep the browser tab open indefinitely — which defeats the purpose of having a primary inbox at all.

**They expire.** Most throwaway services reset or delete the inbox within minutes to hours. Any time-sensitive notification sent after expiry is gone.

Throwaway addresses have legitimate uses for one-shot form fills with immediate confirmation. Investment account notifications are the opposite of that use case: ongoing, time-sensitive, and often security-critical.
