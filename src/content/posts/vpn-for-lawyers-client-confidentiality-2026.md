## What Does "Client Confidentiality" Actually Mean for Lawyers?

Client confidentiality is the legal and ethical obligation lawyers carry to protect information shared by clients in the course of representation. In most jurisdictions, this is not a best-practice recommendation — it is a professional duty enforced by bar associations. Breaching it can result in disciplinary action, malpractice liability, and damage to the client relationship that built your practice.

Traditionally, confidentiality meant locking file cabinets and choosing conversations carefully. In 2026, it means securing digital communications with the same rigor. Emails, document transfers, video calls, and cloud-based case management platforms are all potential exposure points — and most of the networks lawyers use while working are not inherently secure.

A VPN does not replace attorney-client privilege or substitute for proper data governance. What it does is close the specific gap that open and semi-trusted networks create: the window during which traffic in transit can be intercepted and read.

## The Network Problem: Where Confidential Data Gets Exposed

Most confidentiality breaches in legal settings do not happen because someone cracked a law firm's servers. They happen because a lawyer connected to a risky network without protection.

The threat landscape for attorneys working outside a secured office includes:

**Hotel and conference wifi.** Bar conferences, depositions, and client meetings routinely take place in hotels. These networks are shared environments. Without encryption, traffic passing over them — including email attachments, document transfers, and login credentials — is potentially visible to other network users.

**Courthouse and government building networks.** Public networks in these environments may be poorly secured. The fact that a network is in a professional or official building does not mean it is protected.

**Opposing counsel's office or shared spaces.** Co-working spaces and client offices often run networks accessible to parties with unclear intentions. Traffic on these networks faces the same risks as any public wifi.

**Coffee shops and airport lounges.** Lawyers who bill hours while traveling — and most do — regularly use these networks. Unencrypted traffic on open wifi can be captured by standard tools freely available online.

## How a VPN Works: The Non-Technical Explanation

A VPN creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a server operated by the VPN provider. Every piece of data your device sends travels through this tunnel before reaching the internet.

Here is what that looks like in practice for a lawyer:

1. **You open the VPN app and connect.** Takes five seconds. The app establishes an encrypted connection to a VPN server in your chosen location.
2. **All your device's traffic routes through the tunnel.** Your email client, document management platform, video conferencing app, and browser all send data through the encrypted channel simultaneously. There is no manual selection of "which apps to protect."
3. **The VPN server forwards your requests to the internet.** The destination service — your email provider, your firm's document platform, the court's filing system — sees the VPN server's IP address, not yours.
4. **Responses return through the same encrypted tunnel.** The documents, emails, and data you receive are encrypted in transit back to your device.
5. **Anyone watching the network sees nothing useful.** On a hotel wifi or airport lounge, the only visible information is that your device has an encrypted connection to a VPN server. The content is computationally inaccessible.

The encryption standard that matters is AES-256, the same standard used by financial institutions and government agencies for sensitive data. It is not marketing language — it is the actual specification that makes intercepted traffic useless without the encryption keys.

## Why Client Confidentiality Obligations Point Toward a VPN

Bar associations in multiple jurisdictions have issued guidance specifically addressing lawyers' technology obligations. The American Bar Association's Model Rules of Professional Conduct Rule 1.6(c) requires lawyers to make "reasonable efforts" to prevent unauthorized disclosure of client information.

What counts as "reasonable efforts" evolves with the technology landscape. In an era when packet-sniffing tools are freely downloadable and hotel networks are known attack surfaces, a strong argument exists that practicing without network-level encryption on public wifi falls below a reasonable standard of care.

This is not a claim about legal conclusions — the applicable standard varies by jurisdiction and context. It is an observation about where professional responsibility and practical security align: using a VPN on any network you do not fully control is a straightforward step that substantially reduces exposure.

## FAQ

### Does a VPN actually protect attorney-client privileged communications?

A VPN encrypts traffic in transit between your device and the VPN server, preventing interception on shared networks. This closes a specific and real exposure vector. It does not create legal privilege — that is determined by substantive law and the nature of the communication — but it prevents privileged communications from being readable if intercepted on a shared network. The two concepts are distinct: privilege is a legal rule; encryption is a technical protection. Both are relevant to confidentiality.

### Is using a VPN enough to meet bar association technology obligations?

A VPN is one component of a reasonable technology security posture, not a complete solution on its own. Bar association guidance in most jurisdictions addresses the overall handling of client data, including secure email practices, encrypted storage, and access controls. A VPN specifically addresses network-level transmission security. Consult your jurisdiction's current ethics guidance for a complete picture. ### Can my firm's IT department see my VPN traffic?

If your device is managed by your firm and has monitoring software installed at the device level, a VPN does not prevent that monitoring — it encrypts traffic on the network, not at the device layer. For firm-issued devices with MDM profiles, check with IT about what is monitored and where. Surfshark protects traffic on the network between your device and the VPN server; it does not affect what software on the device itself can log.
