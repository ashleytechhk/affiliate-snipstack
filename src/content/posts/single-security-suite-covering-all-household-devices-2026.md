## The Real Cost of Counting Devices in a Household Plan

Norton and McAfee weren't built with the modern household in mind — they were built around "your computer," singular, then patched over the years to cover "a few devices." That legacy shows up as a hard cap: five devices on most mid-tier plans, ten on the priciest one.

A household doesn't fit that shape. Two parents, two kids, a couple of laptops, a tablet for homework, and a smart TV puts you at seven or eight connected devices before anyone mentions a console or a guest's phone. Every device past the cap goes unprotected or forces an upgrade.

That's the quiet tax of legacy antivirus pricing — you pay per device, and the family that needs the most coverage pays the most. It's also where the bloatware complaint comes from: plans built around upselling nag you toward the next tier. A single suite with no device ceiling removes the calculation. Install it once, and the next device you buy doesn't trigger a new decision.

## VPN: The Feature That Actually Scales With Household Size

This is where the household math gets interesting. Norton's VPN is included, but on base and mid tiers it's capped — often around 50GB per month — fine for one person's light browsing, hopeless for a family streaming and video-calling across multiple devices at once. Unlocking unlimited data means a pricier tier.

McAfee's VPN allocation varies by plan and has previously been criticized for weaker privacy practices on lower tiers.

Surfshark One's VPN has no data cap and no device cap, runs on the WireGuard protocol for speed, and includes a no-logs policy plus split tunneling — useful if one family member needs a specific connection while everyone else browses normally. For a household, where "the VPN" is something the whole family leans on for public Wi-Fi, hotel stays, or restrictive school networks, unlimited access on every device turns it from a nice-to-have into something everyone actually uses.

## Beyond Antivirus: What a Household Actually Needs Covered

### Data Breach Monitoring (Alert)

Surfshark Alert checks your email addresses, credit cards, and ID details against known breach databases and flags you in real time if something appears. In a household juggling several email addresses and accounts for school portals, streaming trials, and online shopping, that's more useful than it sounds — breaches happen quietly, and most people find out months later.

Norton and McAfee both offer comparable dark-web monitoring, though McAfee's alert cadence has historically lagged.

### Ad and Tracker Blocking (CleanWeb)

CleanWeb blocks ads, trackers, and malicious URLs at the network level, before they load — which matters double in a household where kids click around unsupervised. It's not a parental control tool, but fewer ads and malicious links means fewer accidental clicks into something bad.

### Private Search (Search)

A private search tool that doesn't build a profile on your queries is a small but real privacy layer — useful for the household member researching something sensitive, like a health question or a gift they don't want autocompleted on a shared device later.
