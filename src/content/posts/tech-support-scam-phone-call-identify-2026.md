## How Tech Support Scams Work: The Setup

Before comparing identification tools, it helps to understand the full picture. Tech support scams almost never arrive by phone alone.

The typical sequence looks like this:

1. **A fake alert appears on your screen** — a pop-up or browser warning claiming your computer is infected, with a [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/) to call
2. **A call arrives claiming to be from a tech company** — triggered either by you calling the number in the pop-up, or by the scammer cold-calling from a purchased list
3. **A follow-up email arrives** — containing a fake invoice, a remote-access link, or a "confirmation" message for a service you never signed up for
4. **The real damage happens** when you grant remote access, make a payment, or click a link in that email

Understanding this means you need to identify the scam at multiple points — not just during the phone call itself. The comparison table below covers all of them.

## The 4 Warning Signs: How to Identify a Tech Support Scam Call in Real Time

These four patterns cover the vast majority of tech support scam calls. You do not need any app to use them — just recognise the pattern and hang up.

### Warning Sign 1: They Called You First

Microsoft, Apple, Google, and your internet provider do not call you out of the blue to tell you about a problem on your device. No legitimate tech company monitors your computer remotely and then phones you when something goes wrong. If someone calls you claiming to have detected an issue on your device, that claim is false — full stop.

The scam works because it sounds plausible. It isn't. Hang up.

### Warning Sign 2: They Ask You to Install Remote Access Software

Common tools scammers request: AnyDesk, TeamViewer, UltraViewer, LogMeIn. Once you grant remote access, the caller can see everything on your screen, access your banking apps, steal saved passwords, and install malware that persists after the call ends.

**No legitimate support call from an unsolicited caller should ever begin with a request to install software.** Genuine tech support — when you contact the company yourself — may use remote access tools, but only after you have verified the contact and initiated the interaction.

### Warning Sign 3: They Ask for Payment by Gift Card, Wire, or Crypto

Legitimate companies do not accept gift cards as payment. Not Apple. Not Microsoft. Not the IRS. Not any utility company. If the caller asks you to buy iTunes cards, Google Play cards, or Amazon gift cards and read the numbers over the phone, that is a scam. This payment method is used specifically because it is irreversible — once the codes are read out, the money is gone.

### Warning Sign 4: The Pop-Up Gave You the Number

If you called them because a browser pop-up told you to, the number was put there by the scammer. That pop-up is not a real security warning from Microsoft or Apple — it is a webpage designed to look like one. Close the browser (force-quit if necessary), run your device's built-in security scan, and do not call the number.

## Method 2: Reverse Phone Lookup for Caller ID Spoofing

Tech support scammers frequently spoof their caller ID to display a local number, a familiar area code, or even the real phone number of a company like Apple Support. This makes the call appear legitimate when it is not.

A reverse phone lookup — searching the phone number in a service like Truecaller, Hiya, or a simple Google search — can reveal whether the number has been reported by other people as a scam.

**How to use it:**

1. Write down the number that called you (or check your recent calls list)
2. Paste the full number into Google with the word "scam" (e.g., `+1 800 275 2273 scam`)
3. Or paste it into a reverse lookup directory like truecaller.com

Community-reported databases often flag known scam numbers within days of a campaign starting. If hundreds of people have reported the same number, you will see forum posts, ScamNumbers.info entries, or Reddit threads confirming it.

**Limitation:** Scammers rotate numbers constantly. A number with no reports is not necessarily safe — it may just be new.

## Why Scammers Use Email After the Phone Call

You might wonder why a scammer who failed to get what they wanted on the phone would bother sending an email. The answer is persistence.

Many people feel more comfortable acting on written communication than on a verbal request. A fake invoice "confirming" a $399 annual subscription to a service you supposedly agreed to often generates callbacks — people who want to cancel something they didn't sign up for. The scammer answers that callback and gets a second shot at the original scam.

Others feel reassured by a written record. The email makes the interaction feel more official, which overcomes the hesitation that ended the call.

**What to do:** If you receive a suspicious email after a suspicious call, treat them as connected. Do not call the phone number in the email — it routes back to the same operation. Check the email with Surfshark's Email Scam Checker, then delete both the email and the call log.

## FAQ

### Can I tell from a phone call alone that it is a tech support scam?

Yes — in most cases. The combination of an unsolicited call, a claim about your device, and a request to install software or make a payment is the signature pattern of tech support fraud. If all three are present, it is a scam. Hang up immediately. You do not need to be polite or explain yourself — scammers are trained to keep you talking.

### What if the caller ID shows a real company number like Apple or Microsoft?

Caller ID is easy to fake. Scammers can display any number they choose, including the real support lines of major tech companies. The number on your screen proves nothing about who is actually calling. Apply the four warning signs regardless of what the caller ID shows — if the caller is asking you to act on a problem you didn't know you had, it is a scam.

### I already gave them remote access. What should I do now?

Act immediately. Disconnect your device from the internet (turn off Wi-Fi, unplug the ethernet cable). Then: change the passwords for your email, banking, and any other accounts you were logged into during the session — from a different device. Contact your bank if any financial accounts were visible. Run a full security scan using your device's built-in tools. If you installed any software at their request, have a technician examine the device before reconnecting it to the internet.

### Why do tech support scams so often target older adults?

Scammers target people they assess as less likely to recognise the warning signs and more likely to comply with urgent requests. Seniors who did not grow up with computers are often unfamiliar with how Microsoft and Apple actually communicate, which makes the "we detected a problem" script more credible. Sharing the four warning signs with family members — particularly the rule that no legitimate company calls you unsolicited about your device — is one of the most practical things you can do to help protect them.
