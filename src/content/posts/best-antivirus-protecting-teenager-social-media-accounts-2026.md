## The Risk, Explained Simply

![How a teenager's social media account gets hacked and used against family](/og/best-antivirus-protecting-teenager-social-media-accounts-2026-inline-1.png)

Here is how these attacks actually happen, step by step:

**1. A phishing link arrives disguised as something normal.** A message from a "friend," a fake login page copying the real site exactly — teenagers, like adults, tap links from people they know without a second thought.

**2. The link steals the login details.** The fake page asks for a username and password "to verify." The moment those details are typed in, the attacker has full access.

**3. The attacker studies the account quietly.** They read old messages to learn how the teenager writes, and which family contacts to target for money.

**4. The scam message goes out to family, especially grandparents.** A request for money or a gift card is sent from what looks like a completely genuine account, timed to create urgency and prevent anyone from stopping to check.

**5. By the time anyone realizes, the money is often already gone.** Gift cards and instant transfers cannot usually be reversed — exactly why criminals ask for them specifically.

None of this requires the teenager to have done anything reckless. It requires one unprotected tap on one convincing link — something that happens to careful, sensible people every day.
