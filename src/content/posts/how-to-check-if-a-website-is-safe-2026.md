## What Does "Safe Website" Actually Mean?

A safe website is one that does three things:

1. **Encrypts the connection** between your browser and the site so no one can intercept what you type
2. **Belongs to who it claims to belong to** — the domain name matches the company you expect, with no tricks
3. **Does not contain malware** — scripts, downloads, or redirects designed to harm your device or steal your data

Most scam websites fail on at least one of these three counts. The checks below address each one.

## Check 1 — Look at the Padlock and the URL First

This takes five seconds and catches a large share of fake sites immediately.

**What to do:** Look at the address bar in your browser before you do anything else.

**What you are looking for:**

- The address should start with `https://` — the "s" stands for secure. If it starts with `http://` (no S), your connection is not encrypted and the site should be treated with caution.
- A padlock icon should appear to the left of the address. In most browsers — Chrome, Safari, Firefox — this appears automatically for secure connections.
- The domain name itself should match exactly what you expect. `amazon.com` is real. `amazon-secure-login.com` is not. `arnazon.com` (with a lowercase "r" and "n" that look like an "m") is a trick.

**One important note:** HTTPS alone does not mean a site is legitimate. Scammers can and do obtain HTTPS certificates for fake websites. It just means the connection is encrypted — not that the site itself is honest. You still need to check the domain carefully.

**Red flags to stop you immediately:**

- Extra words or hyphens in the domain (e.g. `paypal-account-verify.com`)
- A country-code extension where you do not expect one (e.g. `yourbank.com.ru`)
- Numbers replacing letters (e.g. `g00gle.com` instead of `google.com`)

## Check 2 — Run the URL Through Google Safe Browsing

Google Safe Browsing is a free tool that checks a URL against Google's constantly updated list of phishing sites, malware distributors, and deceptive pages.

**How to use it:**

1. Copy the full URL of the site you want to check
2. Go to `transparencyreport.google.com/safe-browsing/search`
3. Paste the URL into the search field and press Enter
4. Google will show you whether the site has been flagged as dangerous in the past 90 days

This check takes about 30 seconds. It catches sites that have already been reported and identified. It will not catch a brand-new scam site that went live this morning — but it reliably identifies the large category of known phishing pages.

## Check 3 — Use a URL Scanner for a Deeper Look

URL scanner tools do more than Google Safe Browsing. They check the site against multiple [antivirus](https://snipstack.io/vs-antivirus-2026/) and reputation databases simultaneously and can detect suspicious page behaviour that a simple blocklist would miss.

**The two most useful free tools:**

**VirusTotal (virustotal.com)**
Paste any URL into VirusTotal and it checks it against 70+ security vendors at once. The results show you which vendors flagged the URL and why. A result of 0/70 (nothing flagged) is reassuring. Even 2-3 flags on an obscure engine can be worth noting.

**URLVoid (urlvoid.com)**
URLVoid checks a domain against blacklists and shows you the domain's age, location of the hosting server, and blacklist status. A domain registered two weeks ago claiming to be your electricity provider is an immediate warning sign.

**How to use either tool:**
1. Copy the URL from your browser address bar or the email you received
2. Paste it into the search field on the tool's homepage
3. Review the results — focus on whether the URL is flagged by any trusted engine, and note how long ago the domain was first registered

Do not worry if the results look technical. You are just looking for red or orange warning signals and a recent domain registration date.

## Check 4 — Do a WHOIS Lookup to Find Out Who Owns the Site

WHOIS is a public database that stores registration information for every domain on the internet. It tells you when the domain was registered and sometimes who registered it.

**Why this matters:** Legitimate businesses have websites with years of history. Scam sites are usually brand new — registered days or weeks before you received the suspicious link.

**[How to check](https://snipstack.io/how-to-check-if-your-pc-is-hacked-2026/):**

1. Go to `whois.domaintools.com` or `lookup.icann.org`
2. Enter the domain name (just the domain, e.g. `suspicious-site.com`)
3. Look at the **Creation Date** — how old is this domain?
4. Look at the **Registrar** and **Registrant Country** — does it match what you would expect from this company?

A site claiming to be a well-known bank or retailer but with a domain registered last month is almost certainly fraudulent.

**What WHOIS does not show:** Some domain owners use privacy protection services that hide their personal details. Legitimate businesses and scammers both use these, so hidden owner information alone is not a red flag — but a brand-new domain with hidden information used by an alleged financial institution is.

## Check 5 — Look for Trust Signals on the Page Itself

Once you have done the technical checks above, a quick visual inspection of the page adds another layer of confidence.

**Signs a site is likely legitimate:**

- Contact information is visible — a [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/), physical address, or support email that matches the company's known details
- The privacy policy and terms of service links work and contain real text (not placeholder Lorem Ipsum)
- The site loads without redirecting you through multiple different URLs
- Product images are high quality and consistent with what you would find on the company's official site

**Signs a site may be fraudulent:**

- Spelling mistakes in the main content or the URL
- Prices that are dramatically lower than anywhere else (common in shopping scam sites)
- Urgent pop-ups pressuring you to act before a countdown expires
- A checkout process that asks for payment by wire transfer, gift cards, or cryptocurrency — legitimate retailers never do this

## Common Phishing Red Flags to Memorise

Phishing sites follow predictable patterns. Once you know these, you will spot them faster.

**The "account suspension" page:** You are told your account has been suspended and you must log in immediately to restore access. The login form sends your credentials directly to the scammer.

**The fake delivery notification:** A parcel could not be delivered and you need to pay a small fee to release it. The payment page captures your card details.

**The "you have won" page:** A prize notification asking for your address and payment details to cover shipping. There is no prize.

**The lookalike banking site:** A near-perfect copy of your bank's login page, hosted on a slightly different domain. Everything looks right except the URL.

**The tech support page:** A pop-up warning that your device is infected and you must call a [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-privacy-for-social-media-2026/) immediately. Legitimate operating systems do not issue alerts this way.

In every case, the goal is the same: get you to enter personal information, payment details, or login credentials. The domain check, HTTPS inspection, and URL scanner steps above catch most of these before you reach that point.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does HTTPS mean a website is safe?**

No. HTTPS means the connection between your browser and the site is encrypted — which prevents others from intercepting what you type. It does not mean the site itself is trustworthy. Scammers routinely obtain HTTPS certificates for fake websites. Always check the domain name carefully in addition to the padlock icon.

**Q: I already typed my password into a suspicious site. What do I do?**

Change the password for that account immediately from a known-safe device. If you use the same password elsewhere, change those too. Contact your bank if any financial credentials were involved. Run a URL check on the site after the fact to confirm whether it was a phishing page — the scan results can be useful if you need to report the incident.

**Q: Are free URL scanners reliable?**

Yes, for known threats. Tools like Google Safe Browsing and VirusTotal are genuinely effective against phishing pages and malware sites that have already been identified and reported. They are less effective against brand-new scam sites that have not yet been flagged. For the freshest threats, combining a WHOIS check (domain age) with a URL scan covers most cases. For ongoing automatic protection, [Surfshark Alert](/blog/scamcheck?sub=how-to-check-if-a-website-is-safe-2026__faq) adds real-time monitoring without any manual effort.

**Q: Can a website steal my information just by visiting it, without me typing anything?**

Yes, in some cases. So-called "drive-by download" attacks can run malicious code when a page loads, particularly on outdated browsers. Keeping your browser and operating system updated closes most of these vulnerabilities. If a URL scanner warns you about a site, do not visit it at all.

**Q: How do I check a website on my phone?**

The same methods apply on mobile. Tap and hold a link before opening it to see the full URL. Open a browser tab and use Google Safe Browsing or VirusTotal to scan the URL manually. The Surfshark Alert app on iOS and Android provides automatic link scanning so you do not have to check every link individually.

**Q: What if the website looks exactly like the real one?**

Scammers use tools that copy the entire visual design of legitimate websites in minutes. The URL is almost always the giveaway — check it character by character. A genuine bank or retailer owns its own domain. If the domain is even slightly different from what you expect, close the tab.
