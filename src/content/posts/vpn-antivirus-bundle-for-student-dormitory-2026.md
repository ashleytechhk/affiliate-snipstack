## The Dormitory Problem Nobody Talks About

### Shared WiFi Is the Real Threat

Dormitory networks are not private. Dozens or hundreds of students share the same network infrastructure, and depending on how your university manages its residential WiFi, traffic on that subnet can be intercepted, throttled, or logged. This is not paranoia — it is how shared network architecture works.

Without a VPN, every device on dorm WiFi broadcasts unencrypted traffic that can be sniffed by anyone with the right tools and the same network access. That includes login credentials on sites that do not enforce HTTPS, DNS lookups that reveal which services you use, and application-layer data from apps that do not encrypt by default.

A VPN encrypts traffic at the device level before it leaves your laptop or phone. Whatever the dormitory network logs, it sees encrypted packets — nothing more.

### Your Laptop Cannot Afford Norton's Resource Appetite

The average student laptop is a mid-range machine: 8–16 GB of RAM, a mid-tier CPU, and a storage drive already filling up with coursework, apps, and media. Norton and McAfee were engineered for the desktop era, when computers sat idle most of the day. On modern student hardware running Zoom, browser tabs, IDE tools, and a music app simultaneously, a bloated antivirus eats the headroom you cannot spare.

Norton's background processes are a known complaint. During a scheduled scan, expect meaningful CPU spikes. McAfee is not significantly better. The result is a laptop that stutters during your 9am lecture when the antivirus decides to update its definitions.

### The Multi-Device Reality

A typical dorm setup involves at least three devices: a laptop for coursework, a phone for everything else, and a tablet or gaming device for downtime. [Norton 360](https://snipstack.io/norton-360-antivirus-2026/) Standard covers a single device on its base plan. Getting your phone protected means upgrading to a pricier tier or buying a separate subscription. McAfee is more flexible but still imposes device limits that do not match how students actually live.
