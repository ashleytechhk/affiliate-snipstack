## What is Coworking Space WiFi — and Why Is It Risky?

Coworking spaces sell convenience: fast internet, good coffee, and a community of other self-employed workers. The WiFi is typically a large, shared network where dozens or hundreds of people connect each day using the same router and, often, the same password that's printed on a wall somewhere.

That convenience creates a real privacy problem. On a shared network, other connected devices can potentially intercept unencrypted data traveling over the connection. This includes:

- Login credentials for client portals, freelance platforms, or your bank
- Email attachments and contract documents
- API keys and admin passwords you type into browser-based dashboards
- Payment details if you invoice or pay suppliers while connected

This is not a hypothetical threat. Packet-sniffing attacks on open and shared WiFi are well-documented and require no special skill level to execute with widely available tools.

### What Is a VPN and How Does It Fix This?

A VPN (Virtual Private Network) creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a server operated by the VPN provider. All your internet traffic flows through that tunnel before reaching the public internet.

From the perspective of anyone on the coworking space network — including the router itself — your traffic looks like a stream of unreadable encrypted data going to a single IP address. They cannot see which sites you visit, what you type, or what files you transfer.

![How a VPN tunnel works on shared WiFi — step-by-step diagram](/og/vpn-for-freelancers-on-coworking-space-wifi-2026-inline-1.png)

The encrypted tunnel has a second benefit that freelancers often overlook: it also masks your real geographic location and replaces it with the location of the VPN server you connect to. Choose a server in Germany, and websites see a German IP address. Choose one in Brazil, and you look like a Brazilian user. This location-switching is the engine behind subscription arbitrage.

## The Subscription Arbitrage Angle: Saving Real Money

Streaming platforms, software tools, and even cloud storage services use regional pricing. The same Netflix plan that costs $15.99/month in the US can cost the equivalent of $6–8 in certain other regions. The same logic applies to Spotify, Adobe Creative Cloud, Canva Pro, productivity apps, and various SaaS tools that freelancers buy every year.

Subscription arbitrage means purchasing a plan in a lower-priced region by making the service think you are located there. A VPN is the tool that makes this possible.

For a freelancer who subscribes to three or four services, the annual savings can easily exceed $200–400 — which is several times the cost of the VPN itself.

More server locations mean more regional pricing options. A VPN with servers in 80+ countries gives you meaningful flexibility when hunting for lower subscription tiers.

Freelancers often work across a laptop, a phone, and sometimes a tablet. Some VPNs cap you at 5 or 6 simultaneous connections. Surfshark offers unlimited simultaneous connections on a single account — a meaningful advantage if you use multiple devices or want to cover a household.

### No-Logs Policy

For a privacy tool to be credible, the provider should not keep records of which sites you visited or when. Look for providers with audited no-logs policies, meaning an independent security firm has reviewed and confirmed the claim.

### Kill Switch

A kill switch automatically cuts your internet connection if the VPN drops unexpectedly, preventing your real IP address from leaking to the network in the gap before the VPN reconnects. This is especially important on coworking WiFi where network conditions can be inconsistent.

## FAQ: VPN for Freelancers on Coworking Space WiFi

### Is using a VPN on coworking WiFi legal?

In the vast majority of countries, using a VPN is entirely legal. It is a widely used privacy tool by businesses, remote workers, and individuals alike. Check local regulations if you operate in a jurisdiction with specific internet laws, but for most freelancers in North America, Europe, Australia, and similar markets, there is no legal concern.

### Will a VPN slow down my internet at a coworking space?

A small overhead is inevitable because your traffic is being encrypted and routed through an additional server. With a quality provider using modern protocols like WireGuard, the speed difference is typically 10–20% — barely noticeable for most tasks. Video calls and large uploads may see slightly more impact on slower coworking connections. ### Can I use a VPN on all my devices at the same coworking space?

### How does subscription arbitrage work with a VPN?

You connect to a VPN server in a region where your target subscription is priced lower, then visit the subscription page and sign up as if you were a local user. The service's pricing system sees the IP address of the VPN server and presents regional pricing. Note that this works differently across services, and not all platforms permit it in their terms of service.

### Is a VPN enough to protect me on public WiFi?

A VPN encrypts your traffic and hides it from other network users, which is the primary risk on shared WiFi. It does not replace other good security practices: keep your operating system and apps updated, use strong unique passwords with a password manager, enable two-factor authentication on important accounts, and be cautious about which sites you enter sensitive data on.
