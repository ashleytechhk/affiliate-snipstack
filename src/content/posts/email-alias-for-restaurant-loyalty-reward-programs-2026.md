## Why Restaurant Rewards Apps Spam Harder Than Most Loyalty Programs

Grocery stores send a weekly circular. Airlines send a monthly points statement. Restaurant chains send daily, sometimes twice-daily, promotional pushes — and the mechanics of why are specific to the category.

**Visit-frequency economics.** A restaurant's marketing math rewards getting you in the door as often as possible. A grocery chain does not need you to visit today specifically. A quick-service restaurant chain absolutely does, because a missed visit this week is often a missed visit entirely — you ate lunch somewhere else. That pressure translates directly into email frequency.

**The multi-channel stack.** Sign up for one restaurant's app and you are often opted into three separate streams at once: the loyalty program itself, the "national marketing" list, and a location-specific list tied to whichever store you first visited. Unsubscribing from one leaves the other two running.

**Third-party delivery cross-promotion.** Many restaurant loyalty programs share data with delivery partners as part of in-app ordering integrations. That means your loyalty sign-up can quietly generate a second promotional stream from a delivery platform you never directly signed up with.

**The reactivation email.** If you go quiet on a rewards app for a few weeks, most systems automatically fire a "we miss you" campaign with a bonus offer attached. This resets any goodwill you built by ignoring earlier emails — the app interprets your silence as a re-engagement opportunity, not a signal to back off.

## Option-by-Option Breakdown

### Real Email + Unsubscribe

The default. You sign up with your actual address and try to manage the volume reactively.

This mostly fails for restaurant apps specifically because of the multi-stream structure described above. Even a disciplined unsubscriber ends up re-subscribed by the next reactivation campaign or location-specific list they didn't know existed.

**Verdict:** Not sustainable if you use more than one or two restaurant apps regularly.

### Gmail `+tag` Alias

You register as `yourname+tacoapp@gmail.com`, hoping to filter by tag later.

Restaurant loyalty apps run on fast, cheap sign-up flows, and a fair number of them validate the address by stripping anything after the `+` before it ever reaches their database. When that happens, your tag vanishes and the promotional stream lands on your bare address — same as if you'd never tagged it.

**Verdict:** Occasionally works for organizing your own inbox. Does nothing to actually block or kill the sender.

### Throwaway Address

Services like 10MinuteMail or Guerrilla Mail generate an inbox that exists just long enough to click a verification link.

Restaurant rewards accounts need a live email address for the life of the account — points confirmations, birthday reward triggers, occasional account recovery. A ten-minute inbox breaks the account the moment it expires.

**Verdict:** Wrong tool for anything you plan to actually use past sign-up.

### iCloud Hide My Email / SimpleLogin / Firefox Relay

These generate durable forwarding aliases that stay live, forward reliably, and can be deleted whenever you choose. They handle restaurant loyalty sign-ups reasonably well.

The catches: iCloud Hide My Email only works cleanly inside the Apple ecosystem. SimpleLogin's free tier caps out at ten aliases, which is fine for two or three restaurant apps but tight if you are chasing rewards at half a dozen chains. Firefox Relay's free tier caps at five and does not support replying from the alias, which rarely matters for restaurant apps but can if a location ever emails you directly about an order issue.

**Verdict:** Solid, no-cost options if you are only tracking a small handful of rewards programs.
