## Why Your Real Email Leaks From Nextdoor

Nextdoor's revenue model depends on connecting local businesses with residents. That means your email address has value beyond your neighborhood chats. According to Nextdoor's own privacy policy, they share data with advertising partners, enable business owners to contact you through the platform, and aggregate usage data that can be linked back to your account.

Even without third-party sharing, Nextdoor itself sends a lot of email: weekly neighborhood digests, urgent alerts, neighbor replies, real estate promotions, and local business announcements. By 2026, many users report 20 to 40 Nextdoor-origin emails per week on an active account. If your real address is attached, all of that lands in your primary inbox alongside your important messages.

![Nextdoor email types and alias setup flow](/og/email-alias-for-nextdoor-neighborhood-account-2026-inline-1.png)

An alias breaks this link cleanly. You still get the neighborhood value (forwarded to your real inbox), but the alias address is the one Nextdoor sees, stores, and potentially shares. When spam hits the alias, you delete it. Your real email stays untouched.

## Apple Hide My Email

If you own an iPhone, iPad, or Mac and pay for any iCloud+ tier (starting at $0.99/month for 50GB), Hide My Email is already available to you. It generates addresses in the format `randomstring@privaterelay.appleid.com` and forwards mail to your real iCloud inbox.

The integration is tight: Safari can autofill a Hide My Email address when you tap an email field, making it a one-tap setup on iPhone. Nextdoor's iOS app supports this natively. For Mac users signing up through Safari on desktop, the same autofill works.

The gap versus Alt ID is the missing identity layer. Hide My Email gives you a masked address but not a fake name — you still need to decide what to put in Nextdoor's name fields. It also offers no way to generate the alias outside Apple devices, so Windows or Android users are excluded entirely.

## SimpleLogin: Open-Source Control

SimpleLogin (now owned by Proton) is the go-to choice for people who want full transparency over how their alias service works. The free tier allows five aliases; the paid plan at roughly $30 per year gives unlimited aliases with custom domain support.

For Nextdoor, SimpleLogin works reliably. You create an alias from the dashboard or browser extension, use it on Nextdoor, and all incoming mail forwards to your primary address. You can reply from the alias address too, which matters if Nextdoor ever needs you to reply to a platform email for account changes.

SimpleLogin's edge is control: you can see every email that passes through any alias, set per-alias forwarding rules, and host your own instance if you want zero third-party involvement. The trade-off is that setup requires more steps than Alt ID or Hide My Email, and there is no identity profile feature.

## Firefox Relay

Mozilla's Relay service offers five free aliases and upgrades to unlimited for $1.99/month. Aliases end in `mozmail.com` and forward to the Firefox account email you registered with.

It works fine for Nextdoor verification. The free tier is generous enough for light users who only need aliases on a handful of sites. The browser extension makes alias creation fast during signup flows.

Relay's weakness is feature depth: no identity profile, no reply-from-alias on the free tier, and no custom domain support even on paid plans. For a single-use case like Nextdoor, it is adequate. For people who want one tool that covers all their privacy needs, Alt ID or SimpleLogin scale better.

The pattern is clear: for platforms that require real engagement — neighborhood apps, government services, healthcare portals — persistent forwarding aliases win every time.

## FAQ

### Can I use a Gmail alias (the + trick) on Nextdoor instead?

You can — Gmail lets you add `+tag` to your address, for example `yourname+nextdoor@gmail.com`. Nextdoor accepts this format for signup and verification. The problem is that your real Gmail address is trivially visible: anyone who receives your email or sees it in a data breach can strip the `+tag` part and expose your real address. It offers naming/filtering convenience, not real privacy.

### Will Nextdoor block alias addresses?

Nextdoor blocks known disposable domains but does not block persistent forwarding aliases from services like Alt ID, Hide My Email, or SimpleLogin. These addresses look like real email accounts because they function as real email accounts — they just forward to a hidden destination.

### [What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) if I delete my alias and Nextdoor tries to email me?

Emails sent to a deleted alias bounce back to Nextdoor's server. Your account remains active on Nextdoor's side, but you simply stop receiving their emails. If you need to reactivate contact, you can update your Nextdoor email address in Settings to a different alias or your real address.

### Does Alt ID let me reply to Nextdoor emails?

Yes. Emails forwarded through Alt ID can be replied to from within your real inbox — the reply routes back through the alias so Nextdoor sees a reply from your alias address, not your real one. This is useful if Nextdoor sends a message you need to respond to for account security reasons.

### Is Alt ID useful beyond Nextdoor?

Definitely. The same alias-plus-identity workflow applies to any site where you want to avoid inbox spam or protect your personal details: local business loyalty programs, event ticket sites, HOA portals, fitness apps, and anywhere else that demands an email but has loose privacy standards.
