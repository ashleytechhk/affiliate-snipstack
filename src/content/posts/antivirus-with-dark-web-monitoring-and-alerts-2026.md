## What Is the Dark Web and Why Should You Care?

Most people use the internet every day without ever seeing the dark web. That is because it is intentionally hidden. It is a part of the internet that regular browsers like Chrome or Safari cannot access. Criminal groups use it to buy and sell stolen information: email addresses, passwords, passport numbers, credit card details, and more.

Here is the alarming part. You do not have to do anything wrong to end up on the dark web. A company you trusted — a hospital, a retailer, an insurance provider — can suffer a data breach. Your information gets stolen from their servers. Days or months later, it shows up for sale in dark web forums. And you never get a warning.

According to reports from cybersecurity firms, billions of email-and-password combinations are circulating on the dark web right now. The majority of victims find out only after their bank account is drained, their identity is used to open credit cards, or their email is locked out.

Seniors are disproportionately targeted because scammers know that older adults are more likely to have retirement savings, less likely to check their credit reports frequently, and may not recognize early warning signs.

## Why Seniors Face Higher Risk in 2026

The threat landscape has changed. Scammers are no longer sending obviously fake emails full of spelling mistakes. They are using stolen personal information to craft messages that sound completely legitimate.

Imagine receiving an email that reads: "Dear [your full name], we noticed unusual activity on your account ending in [the last four digits of your actual credit card]." That information did not come from guessing. It came from the dark web, where your data was purchased for pennies.

This kind of targeted attack, called spear phishing, has become far more common in 2026. Without monitoring, you have no way of knowing which of your details are already out there.

Additionally, many seniors have older devices that have not been updated in years. Outdated software contains security holes that viruses, ransomware, and spyware can slip through. Basic antivirus catches known threats. But only software with active dark web monitoring alerts you when your information is being traded — before a criminal acts on it.

## What to Do When You Receive a Dark Web Alert

Receiving an alert can feel alarming. Here is a calm, step-by-step response that works for most situations.

1. **Do not panic.** An alert means the monitoring worked. You now have time to act before the damage happens.
2. **Change the password for the affected account immediately.** Use a strong password you have not used anywhere else. A mix of letters, numbers, and symbols works well.
3. **Check whether you use that same password anywhere else.** If you do, change it on those accounts too.
4. **Consider placing a fraud alert with the major credit bureaus.** This makes it harder for someone to open new accounts in your name. It is free and takes about ten minutes by phone.
5. **Contact your bank if financial details were involved.** They can monitor for unusual transactions or issue new card numbers.

The key advantage of having alerts is time. The faster you know, the more options you have.
