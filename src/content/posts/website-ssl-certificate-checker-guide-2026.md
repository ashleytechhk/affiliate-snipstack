## What an SSL Certificate Actually Tells You

SSL (Secure Sockets Layer) — now technically called TLS — is the technology behind the padlock icon and the `https://` prefix in your browser's address bar. When a website has a valid SSL certificate, it means two things:

1. The data travelling between your browser and the website is encrypted — a third party intercepting the traffic cannot read it.
2. The certificate was issued to that specific domain by a recognised certificate authority.

What it does **not** tell you is whether the website itself is honest, safe, or operated by who it claims to be. Getting an SSL certificate costs nothing — free certificates from services like Let's Encrypt are available to anyone within minutes. A scammer running a fake banking page can have a perfectly valid padlock just as easily as the real bank.

![What an SSL certificate tells you — and what it does not](/og/website-ssl-certificate-checker-guide-2026-inline-1.png)

This is the gap that trips up many people, especially with scam emails. The link in a phishing email might lead to an `https://` page that looks exactly like your bank's login screen — padlock and all. The SSL certificate is real. The bank is not.

## Method 1: The Browser Padlock (What Everyone Uses)

Clicking the padlock icon in your browser's address bar opens a small panel showing basic certificate information. In most browsers you can drill further to see:

- Who the certificate was issued to
- Which certificate authority issued it
- The expiry date

**What to check:**

- The domain on the certificate should match the address you typed or the link you were sent. A certificate issued to `secure-bank-verify.net` is not a certificate for your actual bank, even if the page looks identical.
- Check the expiry date. Expired certificates are a red flag — legitimate businesses renew them promptly.
- Look for "Extended Validation" (EV) certificates. These require more rigorous identity checks from the certificate authority, though they are less common than standard certificates.

**Limitation:** This method requires you to know what a legitimate certificate for this site should look like. For a site you use regularly and trust, that is manageable. For an unfamiliar site you arrived at via an email link, it is harder to evaluate.

## Method 2: Free Online SSL Checkers (Manual Tools)

Several free websites let you enter a domain and receive a detailed SSL certificate report. These tools typically show:

- Certificate validity and expiry
- Who issued the certificate (the certificate authority)
- Whether the domain name matches the certificate
- Whether the certificate chain is correctly configured

These tools are useful for IT professionals troubleshooting a business website. For an ordinary user trying to decide if an email link is safe, they return more technical detail than is practical to interpret — and they only check the SSL certificate, not whether the site is associated with phishing or fraud.

**When this is useful:** If you manage a website yourself and want to verify your SSL setup is correct. Not ideal for quick scam detection on an unfamiliar link.

## Method 3: Browser Developer Tools (For Technical Users)

In any modern browser, pressing F12 (or right-clicking and choosing "Inspect") opens developer tools. The Security tab shows the full certificate chain — every issuing authority from root to end certificate — along with connection details.

This is the most thorough manual method. It will catch mismatched certificates, weak encryption, and improperly configured certificate chains that simpler tools miss.

**The catch:** This level of inspection requires knowing what a correctly configured certificate chain looks like, what encryption standards are considered current, and [what to do](https://snipstack.io/what-to-do-after-malware-infection-2026/) with the information once you have it. Most people who receive a suspicious email are not in a position to make that assessment quickly.

## FAQ

### Does a padlock icon mean a website is safe?

No. A padlock means your connection to the website is encrypted, which prevents third parties from intercepting your data in transit. It does not mean the website itself is legitimate. Scammers obtain valid SSL certificates for [fake website](https://snipstack.io/fake-website-detector-tool-2026/)s routinely. Always check the full domain name and run it through a [scam checker](/blog/scamcheck?sub=website-ssl-certificate-checker-guide-2026__faq) if something feels off.

### Can a fake website have a real SSL certificate?

Yes. Free SSL certificates are available to anyone in minutes. A scammer building a fake banking page, fake delivery notification site, or fake government portal can have a fully valid SSL certificate that looks identical to the real thing in your browser. The padlock tells you the connection is encrypted — it says nothing about who is on the other end.

### What is the fastest way to check if an email link is safe?

Copy the link without clicking it (right-click on desktop, press and hold on mobile), then paste it into Surfshark's Email Scam Checker. The result takes seconds and tells you in plain language whether the destination is suspicious. This is faster and more reliable than manual SSL inspection for everyday use.

### What information does an SSL certificate actually contain?

An SSL certificate contains the domain name it was issued for, the name of the certificate authority that issued it, the validity period (start and expiry dates), and the public encryption key. Higher-level "Extended Validation" certificates also include the legal name and location of the organisation. You can view this by clicking the padlock in your browser and looking for certificate details.

### What should I do if I already clicked a suspicious link?

Do not enter any information on the page that opened. Close the tab immediately. If you entered a password on that page, change that password right away from a trusted device. If you entered banking or payment details, contact your bank directly using the number on the back of your card — not any number shown on the suspicious site. Report the incident to your country's relevant consumer or cybersecurity agency.

Standard SSL checkers report only on the certificate itself — validity, issuer, expiry, configuration.
