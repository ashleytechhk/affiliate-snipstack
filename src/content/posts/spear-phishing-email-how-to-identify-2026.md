## What Is Spear Phishing, and Why Is It Different?

Most phishing is a numbers game. Criminals send millions of identical emails — "Your account is suspended," "Confirm your PayPal details" — hoping a small percentage of recipients will click. Because these messages are generic, most people have learned to be cautious about them. Your email app catches many of them in the spam folder.

Spear phishing is the opposite approach. Instead of blasting millions of strangers, the attacker picks a specific target — and researches them first.

Before the email arrives in your inbox, the attacker may already know:

- Your full name
- Where you bank or which services you use
- The name of your employer, accountant, or doctor
- Your general location or home suburb
- A recent event in your life — a purchase, a delivery, a medical appointment

That information comes from data breaches (when websites you use are hacked and their user databases leaked), social media profiles, public records, and other sources that are widely available online. Scammers buy this data in bulk.

The result is an email that reads nothing like generic spam. It says your name. It references something plausible. It may even appear to come from someone you know.

## How to Identify a Spear Phishing Email: The Key Warning Signs

### The email uses your real name and personal details

A standard scam email says "Dear Customer" or "Dear User." A spear phishing email says "Dear Margaret" — and may also reference your bank, your suburb, or a recent transaction.

This personalisation is the single biggest reason people fall for these attacks. It feels like proof that the email is legitimate. It is not. Your name and basic details are available to anyone with a few dollars and a willingness to buy from a data broker.

If an email uses your real name, that is not a sign of safety. It is a sign that more research was done before the attack was sent.

### The sender address does not quite match the organisation

Spear phishing emails are designed to look as if they come from a company or person you trust. But the actual sending address — the technical address that carries the email, not just the display name — often gives the game away.

Look carefully at the full sender address, not just the name shown at the top of the email:

- A bank email that should come from `@barclays.co.uk` but actually comes from `@barclays-secure-alert.com`
- A message from "Your Accountant — James Wilson" but the sending address is `jwilson48237@protonmail.com`
- A courier notification from `parcels@royal-mail-delivery.net` instead of a genuine Royal Mail domain

The gap between the display name and the real sending address is where spear phishing most often exposes itself. The question is whether you look carefully enough to catch it.

### It creates pressure to act before you have time to think

Urgency is the engine of every email scam, but spear phishing makes that urgency feel personal and credible.

Rather than a generic "Your account will be closed," you might receive:

- "Margaret, we noticed an unusual transaction of £340 on your account this morning. Please verify within 4 hours or the charge will be processed."
- "Your delivery from [a retailer you actually use] requires payment of a customs fee before 6 PM today."
- "James asked me to send you the updated invoice — please approve it by end of business."

The detail makes the deadline feel real. That feeling of urgency is manufactured. It is designed to stop you pausing long enough to question what you are reading.

### It asks you to do something your bank or service provider would not normally request by email

Legitimate organisations have specific policies about what they will and will not ask you to do over email. Understanding these defaults makes spear phishing emails easier to catch.

Your bank will not ask you to:

- Confirm your full card number or PIN via email
- Click a link and re-enter your login details to "unlock" your account
- Transfer money to a different account for "security reasons"
- Install software to give someone remote access to your computer

Government offices and utility companies follow similar rules. If an email is asking you to take an action that feels unusual — even if every other detail seems correct — treat that as a warning.

### The email contains no obvious errors

This is counterintuitive, but important. Many people have learned to spot scams by looking for spelling mistakes, broken English, or strange formatting. Spear phishing emails often have none of these flaws.

Because the attacker has invested time in researching you, they typically also invest time in writing a convincing message. The email may be written in perfect English, use your bank's correct logo, and match the formatting of real messages you have received before.

Do not use the absence of errors as a reason to trust an email. Spear phishing emails are designed to pass that test.

## What Attackers Want When They Target You

Understanding the goal helps you recognise which actions in an email are red flags.

**Login credentials.** The most common goal is to get you to enter your username and password on a fake website that looks real. Once they have your credentials, they access your account, change the password to lock you out, and either drain it or sell the access.

**Financial transfers.** Business email compromise — where an attacker impersonates an executive or supplier — tricks victims into sending money to a fraudulent account. This is how most large spear phishing losses occur.

**Personal information.** Some attacks are not about immediate money but about gathering information — your date of birth, your mother's maiden name, your national insurance or tax number — that can be used later for identity fraud.

**Installing malware.** Some spear phishing emails do not ask you to click a link at all. They contain an attachment — a "document" or "invoice" — that, when opened, installs software that monitors your device, captures what you type, or gives the attacker remote access.

## Three Steps to Take When a Suspicious Email Arrives

### Step 1: Do not click or reply before you investigate

The manufactured urgency in a spear phishing email is designed to make you act immediately. Resist it. Close the email. You can come back to it.

One minute of investigation is worth more than acting on an email that turns out to be fraudulent.

### Step 2: Check the sender address and any links

Open the email again. Look at the full sender address — not the display name, but the actual address in angle brackets. On most email apps you can see this by tapping or clicking on the sender's name. Does it match the organisation the email claims to be from?

If there is a link in the email, copy it without clicking it. On a computer, right-click and choose "Copy link address." On a phone, press and hold the link until a menu appears, then choose the copy option.

### Step 3: If in doubt, contact the organisation directly

If the email claims to be from your bank, call your bank using the number on the back of your card — not any number shown in the email. If it appears to be from a colleague, call that person on a number you already have saved.

Do not reply to the email, do not use contact details given in the email, and do not assume the sender can be trusted because the email looks convincing.

## Why Spear Phishing Targets Older Adults Disproportionately

Older adults are targeted at higher rates than any other group. Scammers know that retirement savings, property equity, and investment accounts make the extra research worthwhile. Many people who did not grow up online are also less familiar with how criminals use breached data to craft personalised messages.

This is not about intelligence or naivety. Spear phishing fools security-trained employees at large corporations, lawyers, and financial professionals. The personalisation is genuinely convincing. The solution is not more suspicion of yourself — it is a checking habit and a tool that catches what the eye misses.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between phishing and spear phishing?

Regular phishing casts a wide net — millions of identical emails sent to anyone, hoping a small percentage click. Spear phishing is targeted: the attacker researches a specific person and sends one highly personalised email designed to look legitimate to that individual. Spear phishing emails typically use your real name, reference services you actually use, and are written in polished, error-free language. They are harder to spot but the same checking habits apply. [You can verify any suspicious email here.](/blog/scamcheck?sub=spear-phishing-email-how-to-identify-2026__faq)

### How did the scammer know so much about me?

Your personal details are available through several channels: data breaches (when websites you use are hacked), social media profiles where you have posted personal information, public records, and data brokers who compile and sell contact lists. Scammers buy this data cheaply and use it to personalise attacks. The fact that an email contains accurate information about you does not mean the sender is who they say they are.

### Can spear phishing emails pass through spam filters?

Yes. Because spear phishing emails are sent in low volumes and crafted to look legitimate, they frequently bypass spam and junk filters that catch generic mass-phishing messages. Your email app is more likely to deliver a spear phishing email straight to your inbox than a standard mass scam. This is why manual checking matters.

### What should I do if I already clicked a link in a spear phishing email?

If you clicked but did not enter any information: close the browser tab. Run a malware scan on your device if possible. Monitor your accounts over the following days for any unusual activity. If you clicked and entered a password, change that password immediately on the genuine company's website — accessed by typing the address yourself, not from the email. If you entered banking or payment details, call your bank directly using the number on the back of your card and explain what happened.

### Is it safe to paste a suspicious link into a checker?

Yes. Submitting a link address or email address to a checking tool sends the text of that address for analysis — your device does not visit the suspicious destination. There is no risk in pasting a link into a checker. The risk comes only from clicking the link itself.

### Can my email address be used against me even if I have never been hacked?

Yes. Your address can appear in breaches from services you signed up for years ago, even if your own accounts were never compromised. Scammers cross-reference multiple leaked databases to build profiles, so the more services you have used over the years, the more likely your details exist somewhere.

Spear phishing emails are convincing because they are built to be. They use your name, your bank, your relationships. They create pressure and offer no obvious red flags. The absence of bad grammar is not evidence of safety. The presence of your real name is not evidence of legitimacy.
