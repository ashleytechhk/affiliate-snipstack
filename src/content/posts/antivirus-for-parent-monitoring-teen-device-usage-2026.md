## The Threat You Cannot See

Here is something most grandparents and parents do not realize: when a teenager visits a sketchy website on their phone, the danger does not stay on that phone.

Home Wi-Fi networks connect all your devices together. The moment malware lands on your teen's device, it can spread. It can quietly reach your laptop, the smart TV, even the printer. Criminals design it this way on purpose.

And teens are prime targets. They spend hours on social media, gaming sites, and video streaming platforms — many of which run pop-up ads disguised as prize notifications or free download buttons. Clicking one of these takes less than a second. The damage can take months to discover.

### What Scammers Are Actually After

When scammers infect a teen's device inside your home, they are rarely after the teenager's information. They are after yours.

- **Online banking credentials** stored in browsers or password managers
- **Credit card numbers** saved for streaming subscriptions
- **Government ID and Medicare numbers** often stored in email threads
- **Personal photos and videos** that can be used for blackmail

A 2025 cybersecurity report found that adults over 60 lose an average of $1,500 more per fraud incident than younger adults, partly because seniors are more likely to have savings and are less likely to report fraud quickly.

## Why Standard Device Protection Is Not Enough

Many people assume the built-in security on iPhones or Windows computers is sufficient. It handles some threats, but it has major blind spots.

Built-in protection does not block:

- Phishing websites that look exactly like real banks or government sites
- Spyware that records keystrokes and sends passwords to criminals
- Adware bundled inside free game downloads your teenager installs
- Fake antivirus alerts that trick you into calling a scam phone number

Your teen is not doing anything malicious. They are just doing what teenagers do — watching videos, downloading games, chatting with friends. The problem is that not every site they visit is safe, and they often cannot tell the difference.

## Talking to Your Teenager About Device Safety

Installing antivirus software is the practical step. But a short conversation with your teenager goes a long way too.

You do not need to deliver a lecture. A few simple reminders are enough:

- "If a website offers something for free that normally costs money, do not download it."

Teenagers are not trying to cause problems. Most of them simply do not know what the warning signs look like. Once they understand the risks in plain terms, they are usually willing to be more careful.
