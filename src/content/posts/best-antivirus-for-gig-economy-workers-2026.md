## Why Gig Workers Are a Favourite Target for Scammers

Most people picture hackers attacking big corporations. The truth is different. Criminals prefer easy targets — and a senior gig worker checking their delivery app on a coffee shop Wi-Fi network is far easier to attack than a company with an IT department.

Here is why gig work creates specific risks:

**You connect to unfamiliar networks constantly.** Coffee shops, restaurant parking lots, library hotspots — every network you join is a potential trap. A criminal can set up a fake Wi-Fi network called "Free Customer WiFi" and capture everything you type: passwords, bank details, app logins.

**Your income depends on your phone.** If malware gets onto your phone, it can lock your delivery app account, steal your earnings payout details, or quietly drain your saved payment methods. Losing access to your phone means losing your income — that is leverage criminals use.

**Phishing messages look official.** Scammers send fake messages pretending to be Uber, DoorDash, TaskRabbit, or your bank. The message says there is a problem with your account and asks you to click a link. That link takes you to a fake site designed to steal your login. Many seniors find these messages convincing because they look identical to real ones.

**Apps and downloads carry hidden threats.** Gig workers sometimes download utility apps to track mileage, manage invoices, or handle scheduling. Some of these apps from unofficial sources carry malware that runs silently, harvesting your data.

## What Happens When You Get Infected — Real Scenarios

Understanding the risk in concrete terms matters more than abstract warnings.

**Scenario 1 — The stolen payout.** A delivery driver connects to public Wi-Fi and logs into his earnings app to check his balance. An attacker on the same network intercepts his session. Within hours, his payout destination is changed to an account he does not recognise. By the time he notices, the money is gone.

**Scenario 2 — The ransomware lockout.** A freelance bookkeeper downloads what looks like a free invoice template. It contains ransomware. Every file on her laptop — years of client records — gets encrypted. A message demands payment to unlock them. She pays. The files are never returned.

**Scenario 3 — The fake app update.** A senior rideshare driver gets a text saying his driver app needs an urgent security update. He clicks the link and installs what he thinks is the update. It is spyware. It reads his messages, tracks his location, and eventually captures his bank login when he checks his account.

None of these scenarios require sophisticated victims. They happen to careful, intelligent people every day.

## How to Stay Even Safer: Five Quick Habits

Antivirus software does the heavy lifting, but a few simple habits strengthen your protection further.

**Use strong, unique passwords.** Each app and account should have a different password. A password manager app makes this easy — you only need to remember one master password.

**Never click links in unexpected messages.** If your delivery app, bank, or any service sends you an unexpected message asking you to log in, go directly to the app or website yourself rather than clicking the link.

**Keep your apps and operating system updated.** Updates fix security holes that criminals exploit. Set your phone to update automatically overnight.

**Avoid public Wi-Fi for financial tasks.** When you need to check your earnings or move money, wait until you are on your home network — or use a VPN if you must use public Wi-Fi.

**Trust your instincts.** If something feels wrong — a message is too urgent, a deal seems too good, a website looks slightly off — stop and verify before you proceed. Criminals rely on rushing people into mistakes.
