## Why Food Delivery Drivers Are Prime Targets for Hackers

Most people think hackers only go after big companies or wealthy individuals. That is a dangerous myth. Hackers target whoever is easiest to attack — and food delivery drivers tick almost every box.

Here is why drivers are especially vulnerable:

**They connect to public Wi-Fi constantly.** Waiting for an order at a restaurant? Most drivers hop on the restaurant's Wi-Fi. Public Wi-Fi networks are often unencrypted, meaning anyone nearby can potentially see what you are doing on your phone — including what apps you are logged into and what passwords you type.

**They use many different apps at once.** A typical delivery driver has the delivery platform app, Google Maps, a payment app like Venmo or PayPal, and possibly a banking app all running at the same time. More apps mean more ways for something to go wrong.

**They download apps and click links frequently.** Fraudulent "new delivery platform" apps, fake bonus offers, and phishing text messages disguised as restaurant notifications are common traps. A single tap on the wrong link can install malware on the phone within seconds.

**Their phones contain valuable financial data.** Delivery drivers link their bank accounts to receive pay. If a hacker gains access to the phone, they gain access to that money. The Federal Trade Commission logged over 2.4 million fraud reports in 2023, with mobile devices accounting for the largest share of entry points.

The reality is stark: a delivery driver's smartphone is a moving target that carries real money and real personal data through dozens of risky environments every single day.

## What Can Happen If a Delivery Driver's Phone Is Not Protected

You might wonder: what is the worst that could actually happen? The answer is worse than most people expect.

**Account takeover.** Malware can silently record passwords and usernames as you type them. A thief then logs into your delivery app account, changes the bank deposit information, and collects your next several weeks of earnings before you even notice.

**Bank fraud.** If spyware captures your banking app login, criminals can drain accounts, apply for loans in your name, or make purchases before you receive a single alert.

**Identity theft.** Your phone likely contains your home address, Social Security number fragments in old emails, and photos of important documents. Criminals use this information to open credit cards or file fraudulent tax returns.

**Fake app scams.** Criminals create copycat apps that look identical to DoorDash or Uber Eats. You log in, hand over your credentials, and they own your account within minutes.

**Ransomware.** In some cases, malicious software locks your phone entirely and demands payment to unlock it. For a driver who depends on the phone to earn a living, even a few hours without a working phone means lost income.

None of these scenarios are rare. They happen to ordinary people — including seniors who drive for delivery platforms to supplement retirement income — every week.

Every time you download an app, open a file, or click a link, the antivirus scans it instantly. If it detects something dangerous, it blocks it before it can do any harm. Think of it like a security guard checking everyone who tries to enter your phone.

### Phishing and Fake Website Detection

Phishing is when criminals send you a text or email with a link that looks like it comes from a real company — your bank, your delivery platform, even a government agency. The link takes you to a fake website designed to steal your login. Antivirus software recognizes these fake sites and stops you from loading them, even if the link looks completely legitimate.

### Wi-Fi Threat Detection

When you connect to a public Wi-Fi network, good antivirus software warns you if that network is known to be unsafe or if someone appears to be monitoring traffic on it. This is especially important for delivery drivers who connect to dozens of different networks each week.

### App Permission Monitoring

Some apps quietly request access to your contacts, camera, or location even when they do not need it. Antivirus tools flag apps that are behaving suspiciously, so you can remove them before they cause damage.

You do not have to remember to run a scan. The software checks your phone automatically on a schedule and alerts you only when it finds something worth knowing about. It runs quietly, without slowing your phone down during a busy shift.
