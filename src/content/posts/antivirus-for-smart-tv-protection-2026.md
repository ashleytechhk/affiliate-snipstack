## Why Criminals Are Targeting Your Smart TV in 2026

Think about what your Smart TV can access: your home Wi-Fi network, your streaming passwords, your payment details saved in apps like Netflix or Amazon, and sometimes even your camera or microphone if you have a Smart TV with those built in.

That is a lot of valuable information sitting inside one device — and unlike your laptop, your TV probably never gets a security check.

Hackers use several methods to break into Smart TVs:

**Fake apps** — Some appear in app stores pretending to be legitimate streaming services. Once installed, they log your keystrokes or steal account credentials.

**Phishing messages** — A pop-up on your screen tells you your TV needs an urgent update and asks you to call a number or visit a website. That number connects to a scammer.

**Drive-by malware** — Visiting certain websites on your TV browser can silently install malicious software without you clicking anything.

**Unsecured Wi-Fi** — If your home network is not properly protected, an attacker nearby can intercept data flowing between your TV and the internet.

The average senior loses over $1,600 per fraud incident, according to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center. Smart TV scams are a growing part of that picture.

## What Happens If Your Smart TV Gets Infected

This section is important because many people assume that if their TV still works, nothing bad has happened. That is not how modern malware behaves.

Once malicious software is on your TV, it typically does one or more of the following:

- **Steals your passwords** silently in the background while you type them into streaming apps
- **Mines cryptocurrency** using your TV's processing power — you might notice the TV running hot or slower than usual
- **Spies through your camera or microphone** if your TV has those features
- **Recruits your TV into a botnet** — a network of infected devices criminals use to launch attacks on other targets
- **Serves you fake alerts** designed to frighten you into calling a scam helpline

Many victims do not find out until their bank account is drained, their streaming subscriptions are cancelled by strangers who changed the passwords, or their TV stops working entirely.

## Are All Smart TVs at Risk?

Not equally, but more than most people expect.

**Android TV and Google TV** (used by Sony, Hisense, TCL, Philips, and many others) run on the same operating system as Android phones. This means they can install apps from the Google Play Store — and unfortunately, they can also be targeted by the same malware that hits Android devices.

**Roku** has a more locked-down system with fewer reported incidents, but it is not immune. Phishing scams targeting Roku users have been documented.

**Samsung Tizen and LG webOS** are proprietary systems. They carry a lower risk from traditional app-based malware, but browser-based threats and phishing pop-ups still apply.

**Amazon Fire TV** runs a version of Android. It is more open than Roku and carries moderate risk.

If your TV is an Android TV or Google TV model — which covers a large portion of sets sold in the past five years — then antivirus protection is genuinely worth considering.
