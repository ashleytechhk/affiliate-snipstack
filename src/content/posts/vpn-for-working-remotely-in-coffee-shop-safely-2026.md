## What Is a VPN, and Why Remote Workers Reach for One First

A VPN (Virtual Private Network) creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a server run by the VPN provider. Instead of your laptop talking directly to the coffee shop's router — and everything on that router being visible to whoever else is on it — your traffic gets wrapped in encryption before it ever touches the local network.

Two things happen as a result:

1. **Your data is unreadable in transit.** Even on a network with no password, anyone else on it sees encrypted noise instead of your emails, logins, or file uploads.
2. **Your IP address is masked.** The websites and tools you use see the VPN server's location, not your table at the coffee shop.

This is distinct from your employer's corporate VPN, if you have one — that connects you into your company's internal network and is usually mandatory for accessing internal tools. A personal VPN like Surfshark protects the connection itself, on any network, for any app, including the personal browsing and streaming you do on breaks. Many remote workers run both: company VPN for internal systems, personal VPN as the default for everything else.

## Why Coffee Shop Wi-Fi Is Riskier When You're Actually Working

Casual browsing on public Wi-Fi carries some risk. Working from it raises the stakes, because a remote work session usually involves more sensitive traffic than a typical browsing session:

**Client files and shared drives.** Uploading a contract, a design file, or a spreadsheet with client data means that data leaves your device over whatever network you're connected to. On an open network, that transfer is more exposed than it would be at home.

**Logged-in sessions to work tools.** Your project management dashboard, your email, your invoicing software — all of these stay logged in through your session cookies. On an unsecured network, poorly secured session tokens can, in principle, be intercepted and reused.

**Video calls and screen shares.** A client call where you're screen-sharing a proposal is exactly the kind of traffic you don't want visible to anyone else on the network, even in metadata form (who you're calling, for how long, how often).

**Evil twin networks.** A fake hotspot named something like "CafeGuest_Free" can sit right next to the legitimate one, collecting traffic from anyone who connects without checking. It's not the most common attack, but coffee shops are a favorite target because people connect on autopilot.

## How a VPN Actually Protects Your Work Session

When you connect a VPN before opening your laptop's usual apps, here's what changes:

**Everything routes through an encrypted tunnel.** Email, file uploads, video calls, browser tabs — all of it passes through the VPN's encryption before touching the coffee shop's router.

**DNS queries are protected too.** Every time your device looks up a website or a work tool's address, that query would normally be visible to anyone watching the local network. A VPN routes DNS through its own servers instead.

**A kill switch prevents silent exposure.** If the VPN connection briefly drops — which happens on flaky public Wi-Fi — a kill switch cuts your internet access rather than quietly falling back to an unprotected connection mid-upload. For remote work specifically, this is the feature that matters most: you'd rather your call freeze for a second than send a file unencrypted without noticing.

Worth being clear about what a VPN doesn't cover: it won't stop you from clicking a phishing link, and it won't protect a weak password. It secures the network layer, not every layer of a work session.
