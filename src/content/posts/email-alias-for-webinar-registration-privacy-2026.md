## Why Webinar Sign-Ups Are a Spam Trap

Webinar registrations are high-value lead events for organizers. Here is what typically happens to your email address after you hit "Register":

**Sponsor data sharing.** Most free webinars are funded by sponsors who receive registrant data as part of the deal. The registration page fine print — if you read it — usually discloses "your information may be shared with our partners." Those partners may include dozens of companies.

**Post-event nurture sequences.** Even without sponsor sharing, every webinar platform (Zoom Webinar, Demio, Hopin, GoTo Webinar) puts your address into the host's email marketing system. You will receive replays, upsell pitches, follow-up surveys, and invitations to future events — indefinitely.

**List sales and acquisitions.** When a company that ran a free webinar series gets acquired or shuts down, its email list is often sold as an asset. Your email address from a 2024 registration can start generating spam in 2026 from a completely different company.

**Aggregated profiles.** Data brokers collect webinar registration data from multiple sources. Each sign-up with your real email adds a data point to your broker profile, increasing the precision and volume of targeted spam you receive.

Using a dedicated [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-example-2026/) per webinar — or one alias for all webinar activity — puts you back in control.

### 2. Apple Hide My Email

Apple's iCloud+ feature creates `@privaterelay.appleid.com` forwarding addresses. If you pay for any iCloud+ storage tier (50 GB or above), you have access at no additional cost.

**Strengths for webinar use:** Native integration on iPhone and Mac. If you sign up for a webinar through Safari on any Apple device, Safari prompts "Use Hide My Email" directly in the registration form. Aliases are permanent, deletable, and deliver to your iCloud inbox (which forwards on).

**Limitations:** The `@privaterelay.appleid.com` domain is recognizable. Some enterprise webinar platforms — particularly those with strict email validation or corporate IT policies — occasionally reject it. On non-Apple devices (Windows, Android, Chrome), the native autofill is unavailable, and managing aliases requires the Settings app or iCloud.com. No fake identity generation.

**Verdict:** Excellent if you attend webinars primarily from Apple devices. Less convenient for cross-platform users or enterprise-hosted events with strict validation.

### 3. Firefox Relay

Mozilla's alias service provides five permanent forwarding aliases on the free tier and unlimited aliases on the Premium plan ($1.99/month). A Firefox browser extension auto-suggests an alias when it detects an email input field.

**Strengths:** The free tier is usable for light webinar attendance — five aliases covers five separate organizers. Firefox Relay also strips tracking pixels from forwarded emails before they reach your inbox, which matters because webinar follow-up emails are pixel-loaded by default.

**Limitations:** The five-alias free cap runs out fast if you attend multiple webinars per month. The extension works only in Firefox, so Chrome or Safari users get no autofill assistance. Once you delete an alias, it cannot be reactivated; you must generate a new one. The `@mozmail.com` domain is recognizable as an alias service.

**Verdict:** A good free starting point for infrequent webinar attendees. Power users and frequent registrants will hit the cap or outgrow the free tier quickly.

### 4. SimpleLogin

An open-source alias service with a free tier of 15 aliases and an unlimited paid plan at approximately $4/month. Acquired by Proton in 2022 and trusted in the privacy community.

**Strengths:** Works with any email provider. Browser extensions available for Chrome and Firefox. Two-way alias support — you can reply from your alias address, keeping your real address hidden even in email exchanges with webinar hosts. Useful if a webinar platform sends a reply-required confirmation.

**Limitations:** Dashboard and setup are more technical than Alt-ID or Apple's offering. No fake identity generator. The 15-alias free tier is more generous than Firefox Relay but still finite for regular webinar attendees.

**Verdict:** Strong for technically-minded users who want open-source tools and two-way email capability. The setup friction is higher than the other options here.

### 5. DuckDuckGo Email Protection

DuckDuckGo's free forwarding service assigns you a `@duck.com` address and generates unique aliases per site. Tracking pixels are stripped from every forwarded email before delivery.

**Strengths:** Completely free. Strong tracker removal. No account creation required beyond signing up for the DuckDuckGo email address. Unlimited aliases.

**Limitations:** You cannot permanently delete a specific alias — you can only pause it. A paused alias bounces incoming mail, but the address remains reachable if unpaused. For webinar spam, this is less decisive than full deletion. The `@duck.com` domain is also recognized as a privacy tool by some email validation systems.

**Verdict:** Best for tracker blocking and cost. Weaker on the core webinar-spam use case because pausing is not as final as deleting.

### 6. Gmail `+` Tags

Gmail allows `youraddress+tag@gmail.com` variants — for example, `yourname+webinars@gmail.com` — to route into your real inbox.

**Why this fails for webinar privacy:** Webinar registration forms frequently strip the `+tag` suffix before storing the address in their CRM. When this happens, the organizer records your plain `yourname@gmail.com`. You get no spam isolation and no deletion capability. Even when the tag is preserved, anyone with the variant address can trivially derive your real Gmail address. There is no privacy layer and no kill switch.

**Verdict:** No protection, no deletion. Do not use for webinar registrations if privacy is the goal.

## FAQ

**What is an [email alias for](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-ecommerce-returns-tracking-2026/) webinar registration privacy?**

An [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-outlook-2026/) is a forwarding address that routes emails to your real inbox without revealing your actual email address. You give the alias to the webinar platform during registration. You receive the joining link and event materials normally, but the organizer and their sponsors only ever see the alias — not your real address. When you want to [stop receiving](https://snipstack.io/stop-receiving-spam-from-unsubscribe-forms-2026/) their emails, you delete the alias. No trace remains.

**Will a masked alias address work with Zoom Webinars, Demio, Hopin, and other platforms?**

In most cases, yes. Alt-ID's alias domains are designed to look like standard email addresses rather than obvious disposable-address services. They pass validation on major webinar platforms. If a platform rejects an alias, it is usually one that explicitly blocks non-corporate domains — uncommon but not impossible. Apple Hide My Email occasionally triggers enterprise email validators. Alt-ID addresses have a lower rejection rate in practice.

**Can I still receive the replay recording and follow-up slides after deleting the alias?**

No — once you delete the alias, all emails to that address bounce, including any you might have wanted. Best practice: wait until you have downloaded the replay or confirmed you do not need further materials from that organizer before deleting the alias. Some users keep a "webinar" alias active for 30 days and then delete it as a routine. [Alt-ID's dashboard](/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-for-webinar-registration-privacy-2026__faq) makes it easy to manage multiple aliases and delete them in batches.

**Do I need a different alias for every webinar, or can I use one alias for all webinars?**

Both approaches work. One shared "webinars" alias is simpler to manage but means you cannot selectively cut off one organizer without cutting off all of them. One alias per organizer — or one per webinar — gives you surgical control. With Alt-ID's unlimited aliases, there is no cost to being granular. A practical middle ground: one alias per recurring organizer, one shared alias for one-off events.

**Is it safe to use an alias on a paid or premium webinar?**

Yes. Payment is processed through Stripe or the platform's payment system and is not linked to your email address in a way that breaks if you later change or delete the alias. You receive purchase receipts and event access via the alias forwarding to your real inbox. If you ever need to log back in to retrieve a recording, use the alias address as your login identifier. The alias still exists in your dashboard unless you have deleted it. Alt-ID keeps a list of all active aliases for reference.

**What if the webinar organizer sends a survey or certificate to my alias after I delete it?**

The email bounces. If you want survey results or a certificate of attendance, keep the alias active until after those materials are sent, then delete it. Alternatively, contact the organizer directly with your real address for specific materials. Deletion is permanent and immediate — only do it when you are done with that email channel entirely.
