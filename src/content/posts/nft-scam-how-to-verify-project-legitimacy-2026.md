## Why NFT Scams So Often Arrive by Email

NFT scams are not new, but the delivery method has shifted. In the early years of NFT trading, most scams happened on Discord or through fake minting websites. Scammers have moved toward email for a practical reason: it is harder to moderate at scale, and people are more likely to trust something in their inbox than a message from a stranger on a platform they rarely check.

The most common formats in 2026:

- **Fake whitelist invitations** — you have supposedly been selected for early access to a new NFT project. The link leads to a wallet-draining site that mimics a real minting page.
- **Airdrop claim notices** — free NFTs are waiting for you. Claiming them requires connecting your wallet to a malicious contract.
- **Account verification requests** — a marketplace like OpenSea or Magic Eden supposedly needs you to re-verify your account. The sender is not them.
- **Project update emails from cloned domains** — the message looks exactly like a newsletter from a project you follow, because scammers have copied the design. The domain in the sender address is one character different from the real one.

In every case, the email arrives first. That is where the check has to happen — before you click anything.

## Manual Verification: Five Steps That Work

When an email passes an automated check but still feels suspicious, or when you want to go beyond a single tool, these five steps give you a reliable picture.

### Step 1: Check the Sender Domain Character by Character

Do not read the sender name — read the email address. The name can say anything. The address is harder to fake convincingly.

Look specifically for: one-character domain swaps (`opensea-nft.com` instead of `opensea.io`), hyphens inserted into a real brand name (`magic-eden-support.com`), or extra words appended to the real domain (`opensea-verify-account.com`). If the domain does not exactly match the organisation's official site — not similar, exactly the same — treat it as suspicious.

### Step 2: Look Up the Domain Age

Scam domains are disposable. A fraudulent site is typically set up in a hurry, used for a few weeks to collect victims, then abandoned before complaints accumulate.

Go to `lookup.icann.org` or `whois.domaintools.com`, type in the domain from the sender's email or the link destination, and look at the creation date. An email claiming to be from a well-established NFT marketplace, sent from a domain registered three weeks ago, is almost certainly fraudulent. Legitimate projects have domain history that matches their stated founding date.

### Step 3: Cross-Check the Project's Official Channels

Every legitimate NFT project has at least one verifiable public presence — usually a Twitter/X account and a website. Before engaging with any email about an NFT project, go directly to that project's official channels (found through a search engine, not through any link in the email) and verify that:

- The same announcement appears on their official feed
- The whitelist or airdrop being promoted matches what the project has publicly announced
- The email address being used matches the contact information on their official website

If the email announces something that the project's own accounts know nothing about, you have your answer.

### Step 4: Do Not Connect Your Wallet Without Verifying the Contract

If an email leads to a minting page or a "claim your free NFT" page, the single most important thing to verify before connecting your wallet is the smart contract address. Legitimate projects publish their contract address publicly before launch — it appears in their official announcements, on Etherscan or the equivalent for their chain, and sometimes in their Discord pinned messages.

Copy the contract address from the page and search for it on the relevant blockchain explorer. If the contract is unverified, was deployed yesterday, or has already interacted with dozens of wallets in suspicious patterns, do not proceed.

### Step 5: Check the Email's Headers

For technical users, email headers reveal the actual path a message took to reach you. In Gmail, click the three-dot menu on a message and select "Show original." In Outlook, open the message and look at "View message source" under the File menu.

Look for the `Return-Path` field and compare it to the From address. If they do not match, the message is almost certainly spoofed. Also check `Received-SPF`, `DKIM-Signature`, and `DMARC` — a fail or softfail on any of these is a strong indicator that the message did not actually come from the domain it claims to represent.

## Red Flags That Appear in Almost Every NFT Scam Email

Across the verification methods above, certain signals appear consistently in fraudulent NFT communications. If you see two or more of these in a single email, treat the message as fraudulent regardless of what any tool tells you.

**Urgency language.** "Claim before midnight." "Limited spots remaining." "Your window closes in 2 hours." Legitimate NFT projects announce timelines in advance. Manufactured urgency is designed to prevent you from running any of the checks in this guide.

**Unsolicited contact.** You did not sign up for a whitelist. You did not enter a competition. You have not interacted with this project before. Receiving an unexpected opportunity from an unknown sender is not how legitimate projects reach new holders.

**Requests to connect a wallet through email.** Legitimate minting events are conducted through verified, publicly announced contract addresses on well-known platforms. An email that contains a direct link to a wallet connection page — especially one that asks for seed phrases or private keys — is fraudulent. No legitimate project will ever ask for your seed phrase.

**Generic greeting.** "Dear NFT Enthusiast" rather than your actual name or the wallet address associated with your account suggests a mass-sent scam rather than a genuine communication.

**Mismatched visual branding.** Logos that look slightly wrong, colours that are close but not quite right, or a footer that references a company name different from the project name are all [signs of a](https://snipstack.io/signs-of-a-fake-website-2026/) quickly assembled fake.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if an NFT project email is a scam?

The fastest check is to paste the link or sender address into Surfshark's Email Scam Checker. Beyond that, look at the sender domain character by character — not the display name, the actual email address. If the domain is even one character different from the project's official site, it is almost certainly a scam. Also look for urgency language, requests to connect your wallet through a link you were not expecting, and any request for a seed phrase or private key.

### Can I verify an NFT project's legitimacy from an email alone?

Not fully — email verification tells you whether the message is safe to engage with, not whether the underlying project is legitimate as an investment. For the email itself, check the sender domain, run the link through a scam checker, and verify any announcements against the project's official public channels. For project-level due diligence (team background, contract audits, trading history), that requires separate research on blockchain explorers and community platforms.

### What should I do if I already clicked a link in a suspicious NFT email?

If you clicked the link but did not connect your wallet or enter any personal information, close the browser tab and run a malware scan on your device. If you connected your wallet, act immediately: go to your wallet settings and revoke the contract's approval permissions using a revocation tool like Revoke.cash. If you entered payment information or personal details on the page, contact your bank or card provider immediately.

### Why do NFT scam emails sometimes pass through my spam filter?

Spam filters look at volume signals and known blacklists. A well-constructed scam email from a freshly registered domain, sent in low quantities to specific targets, can pass through automated filters that catch obvious mass-spam. Dedicated checking tools use additional signals — domain age, authentication records, threat intelligence feeds — that go beyond what email provider spam filters examine by default.

### Do NFT scams only arrive by email?

No. They also arrive through Discord DMs, Twitter/X direct messages, Telegram, and text messages. The same verification principles apply regardless of the channel: do not click links from unsolicited messages, verify any announcement against a project's official public accounts, and never share a seed phrase with any tool or person regardless of what they claim. Email is highlighted in this guide because it is the channel where people are most likely to lower their guard.
