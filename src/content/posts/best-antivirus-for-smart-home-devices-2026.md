## Why Smart Home Devices Are a Target for Criminals

Most people think of home security as locks on doors and cameras watching the yard. But your home now has a second type of door — and it is digital.

Every device that connects to your Wi-Fi is part of your home network. Your smart doorbell, your voice assistant, your connected TV, your Wi-Fi thermostat — they all share the same network as your phone, your computer, and anything else you use for banking or email.

When a criminal gets access to one weak point, they can often reach everything else.

Here is how these attacks typically happen:

**1. Weak or default passwords.** Many smart devices come with a default password like "admin" or "1234." Millions of people never change it. Attackers use automated tools that try these default passwords at scale — scanning thousands of homes every hour.

**2. Outdated software on the controlling device.** The app on your phone that controls your smart thermostat or doorbell is a target too. If your phone has not been updated in a while, it may have security holes that a skilled attacker can exploit.

**3. Phishing links sent to seniors.** A text message arrives saying "Your smart home account has been flagged — click here to verify." It looks official. It is not. Clicking it can hand over your login details to a criminal in seconds.

**4. Unsecured Wi-Fi.** If your home network does not use a strong password, or if you connect your devices to public Wi-Fi while traveling, your smart home data can be intercepted.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I install antivirus directly on a smart home device like a camera or doorbell?

No, and you do not need to. Smart cameras, doorbells, and thermostats are simple devices that do not run traditional apps. You cannot install security software on them the same way you would on a phone. The right approach is to protect the device you use to control them — your phone, tablet, or computer — and to make sure your Wi-Fi router is secured with a strong, unique password.

The risk is real, though the likelihood varies based on how you use your devices. The most common attack affecting seniors is not a targeted hack of your specific camera — it is a phishing message designed to steal your login credentials, which then gives a criminal access to everything connected to that account. ### Do I need a separate product for my router, or does antivirus cover that?

Antivirus software on your phone and computer does not directly protect your router. Your router is best protected by: (1) using a strong, unique password for your Wi-Fi network, (2) updating your router's firmware when updates are available (check your router brand's website for instructions), and (3) turning off remote access if you do not use it. Antivirus handles the device-level protection; these router steps handle the network-level protection. Together, they cover the main attack surfaces in your home.

### My grandchildren set up my smart home devices. Should they be doing anything differently?

A few things worth asking them to check: confirm that every smart device has its own unique password (not the factory default), make sure each device's app is updated to the latest version, and consider whether the guest Wi-Fi network option on your router is switched on — it lets smart devices connect separately from the network you use for banking and email, which limits how far an attacker can move if one device is compromised.
