## What Is an Ad Blocker to Filter Adult Ads on a Shared Computer?

Let's define the pieces first. A **shared computer or shared device** is any phone, tablet, or laptop used by more than one person — a family iPad, a household Android tablet, a kid's hand-me-down phone, or a computer at home that everyone borrows.

An **ad blocker** is software that intercepts advertising requests before they reach the screen. When an app or webpage tries to load a banner, a popup, or a video ad, the blocker checks the request against known ad-network domains and drops it if it matches — before any image, video, or script ever renders.

Put those together, and "**ad blocker to filter adult ads on shared computer**" describes using that interception layer specifically to stop the kind of unexpected, embarrassing, explicit ad content that low-quality ad networks sometimes serve — on a device where more than one person, often including kids or guests, might see whatever pops up next.

### Why This Happens on Ordinary, Safe Sites

This is the part that confuses people most: the site or app itself did nothing wrong. Most ad-supported apps and websites don't hand-pick every ad — they plug into ad exchanges that auction remaining ad space in real time to the highest bidder. The publisher sets broad content categories, but enforcement across thousands of ad networks is inconsistent, and a handful of low-quality networks slip explicit banner or popup creatives past the filters, especially on mobile-web pages and free game apps.

The result is that a completely appropriate news site, recipe blog, or casual game can occasionally serve an inappropriate ad — not because anyone browsed toward adult content, but because the ad slot on that page got auctioned to a network with weak content standards.

## How Ad-Network Filtering Actually Works on Mobile

On a desktop browser, an extension like uBlock Origin can intercept ad requests inside that one browser. On mobile, that approach breaks down — apps run in separate sandboxes, and a browser extension has no visibility into what a game or social app is loading.

The fix that works across an entire shared device is filtering at the **network level**, before any app receives a response:

**DNS-level filtering** intercepts the lookup for known ad-server domains and returns nothing, so the app never gets a valid address to fetch the ad from. No address, no ad, no matter which app made the request.

**VPN-based filtering** routes all device traffic through a filtering layer that inspects every outbound request, catching ad delivery methods that skip DNS lookups entirely, such as hardcoded server addresses.

Both methods apply to every app and every browser on the device at once — the browser used for homework research, the game app used for entertainment, the video player, all of it, without configuring each one separately.

Most ad-supported apps and sites plug into real-time ad auctions run by third-party exchanges, rather than hand-picking every ad shown. A small number of low-quality networks in that auction chain serve explicit banner or popup creatives that slip past content screening, especially on mobile-web pages. **Does an ad blocker filter ads inside apps too, or just in the browser?**

A network-level ad blocker like CleanWeb filters at the DNS and network-request layer, which sits underneath every app on the device — not just the browser. That includes games, social apps, and video players, all of which typically pull ads from the same third-party networks a browser extension would never reach.

**Will this stop every single inappropriate ad?**

It stops requests to known ad-serving and malicious domains, and those blocklists are updated regularly. No filter list is exhaustive, so a brand-new ad network not yet catalogued could occasionally slip through. In practice, filtering removes the overwhelming majority of this content because most explicit-ad traffic flows through a relatively small set of recurring networks.

**Does turning on CleanWeb slow down a shared tablet or phone?**

DNS-level filtering adds a negligible lookup delay, generally a few milliseconds. Because ad content is often the heaviest part of a page or app screen to load, removing it tends to make browsing feel faster overall, not slower.

Yes. CleanWeb filters at the network layer rather than per-account or per-browser, so once it's enabled and set to auto-connect, it applies the same way regardless of who is holding the device or which app they open.
