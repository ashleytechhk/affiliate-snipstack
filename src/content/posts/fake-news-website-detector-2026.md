## Google Fact Check Tools — The Search Giant's Database

Google's Fact Check Tools (accessible at toolbox.google.com/factcheck) aggregates fact-check reviews from accredited news organisations and fact-checking publishers worldwide. You can search for a claim, a URL, or a news story title and see whether it has been reviewed and what the verdict was.

**What it does well:** The database is large and sourced from credible organisations like PolitiFact, AFP Fact Check, and Reuters Fact Check. If a viral story has been reviewed anywhere by a reputable fact-checker, it is likely to appear here.

**Where it falls short:** Google Fact Check Tools only returns results for claims that someone has already investigated. A brand-new story, a freshly launched website, or a scam email link will return nothing — not because it is safe, but because no one has reviewed it yet. The tool is also designed for checking news claims, not website safety. It does not tell you whether the site behind a link is trying to steal your personal information.

**Best for:** Verifying whether a specific news story that is circulating on social media or email has already been fact-checked by a credible source.

## Snopes and FactCheck.org — Established Myth-Busting Resources

Snopes has been verifying viral claims since 1994, and FactCheck.org has focused on political accuracy since 2003. Both are free, independent, and widely cited. You search for a claim or paste a headline, and the sites return a detailed analysis with a verdict — true, false, mixture, unproven, satire, and so on.

**What they do well:** Depth and credibility. When Snopes labels something false, the page explains exactly why — showing the original claim, the evidence against it, and often the origin of how it spread. For someone trying to understand whether a forwarded email contains accurate information, this context is genuinely valuable.

**Where they fall short:** Like Google Fact Check Tools, both services are reactive. They review claims after they have spread widely enough to attract attention — which means newer or more targeted misinformation may not yet have a published verdict. Neither site provides any form of automatic protection: you must actively search for each claim. And crucially, neither tells you whether the website itself is a scam or phishing site. They assess the accuracy of content, not the safety of the domain.

**Best for:** Researching a claim you have seen repeated across multiple emails or social media posts — and want a detailed, sourced explanation of whether it is accurate.

## NewsGuard — Browser Extension Ratings for News Sites

NewsGuard assigns reliability scores to news and information websites, rating them against nine criteria: whether the site is transparent about ownership, whether it corrects errors, whether it clearly labels advertising, and whether it has a history of publishing false content. A browser extension displays a coloured shield next to links in search results and on social media — green for generally reliable, red for sites that have repeatedly failed the criteria.

**What it does well:** Contextual, persistent guidance. Rather than requiring you to look anything up, the extension tells you about a source as you encounter it. This is useful for people who read news across many different websites and want a quick signal about whether a source is considered trustworthy.

**Where it falls short:** NewsGuard rates established news publishers, not random websites or newly created scam domains. A phishing site disguised as a bank, a one-day-old scam page, or a fraudulent email link will not appear in NewsGuard's database — it only covers publishers it has actively reviewed, which skews toward well-known media outlets. The full extension functionality requires a paid subscription; the free tier is limited. Coverage outside English-language media is also narrower.

**Best for:** Ongoing monitoring of which news sources you can trust, particularly for people who read widely across unfamiliar publications.

## Media Bias/Fact Check — Source-Level Credibility Ratings

Media Bias/Fact Check (mediabiasfactcheck.com) rates news sources on a left-to-right political bias scale and a separate factual accuracy scale. Each entry explains the rating with examples, covers the site's ownership and history, and links to external assessments. It has reviewed more than 4,000 sources.

**What it does well:** Long-term track record. For any major news outlet or information site that has been active for more than a year, there is likely a detailed rating. The dual-axis approach — bias separately from factual accuracy — is more nuanced than a single credibility score.

**Where it falls short:** Media Bias/Fact Check covers established sources, not new domains or suspicious links in individual emails. If you receive an email linking to a domain registered last week, it will not appear in the database. There is no browser extension, no real-time protection, and no ability to check whether a link is a phishing attempt. The tool is designed for media literacy research, not personal security.

**Best for:** Evaluating the credibility of a news organisation over time — useful background research, but not a substitute for checking whether a specific link in your inbox is safe.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a fake news website detector?

A fake news website detector is a tool that helps you assess whether a website or the content on it is reliable, fabricated, or designed to mislead. The term covers two overlapping categories: fact-checking services (which verify specific claims against evidence) and website safety tools (which check whether a domain is associated with scams, phishing, or malware). For email security, the website safety category is usually more directly relevant.

### Can any tool automatically detect fake news in real time?

Not comprehensively. Fact-checking requires human judgment to assess evidence, which means databases are always slightly behind emerging stories. Tools like NewsGuard provide ratings for established publishers, but new sites can operate for months before receiving a review. Real-time tools like Surfshark Scamcheck focus on the security dimension — whether a site is dangerous — rather than whether its content is accurate.

### Are free fake news checkers reliable enough?

Free tools like Snopes, FactCheck.org, and Google Fact Check Tools are credible and useful for checking widely circulated claims. Their limitation is coverage: newer or more targeted misinformation may not yet be in the database. For link safety specifically, free manual lookup tools require you to remember to check before clicking — which is harder to do reliably under pressure.

### What is the difference between a phishing site and a fake news site?

A fake news site publishes fabricated or misleading stories, usually to attract advertising revenue, spread political narratives, or cause confusion. A phishing site impersonates a legitimate organisation — a bank, a government agency, a retailer — to trick you into entering your login details or financial information. Scam emails often lead to phishing sites, not fake news sites. Surfshark Scamcheck addresses phishing; Snopes and similar tools address fake news content.

### How do scammers make phishing emails look like real news?

They use several techniques: copying the visual design of legitimate news organisations, creating domain names that closely resemble real ones (like `bbc-news-alert.com` instead of `bbc.co.uk`), and writing in a professional tone without obvious spelling errors. In 2026, many phishing emails are written with automated tools that produce fluent, plausible-sounding content. The best defence is checking the link itself — not just reading the email — before clicking.
