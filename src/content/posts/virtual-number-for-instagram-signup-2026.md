## Why Your Real Number Does Not Belong on Instagram

### Instagram Treats Your Number as an Identifier, Not Just a Safety Tool

When you enter your phone number during Instagram signup, you are not just confirming you are a real person. You are handing Meta a unique identifier that links your account to your real-world identity. That number can be used to suggest your account to people in their contacts, to tie your Instagram presence to your Facebook profile, and — until Meta was fined billions for it — was searchable via their own API.

In 2019, researchers discovered that phone numbers provided to Facebook and Instagram for two-factor authentication were being used for ad targeting. The number you submitted for security was being used for advertising. Meta has since changed that policy, but the pattern it reveals is instructive: your number was never just a verification tool. It is data.

### Data Breaches Do Not Discriminate

The 2021 Facebook breach exposed the phone numbers of over 500 million users. Instagram accounts linked to Facebook profiles were included. Those numbers — tied to names, locations, and account details — ended up on hacker forums and have been circulating ever since.

If your real number is in that dataset, any stalker, scammer, or harasser who finds your Instagram profile can run your username through a breach database and potentially match it to your phone number. From there, reverse lookup services can surface your address, relatives, and workplace.

This is not hypothetical. It is the documented attack chain that researchers and journalists have used to illustrate exactly how connected these data points become once they leave a platform's control.

### The "It Won't Happen to Me" Gap

Most people who use their real number for Instagram signup are not thinking about breach databases and stalker toolkits. They are thinking about getting past the verification screen. That is understandable — the friction of signup is designed to feel trivial. But the decision made in those thirty seconds persists for as long as your account exists and well beyond if the number ever surfaces in a breach.
