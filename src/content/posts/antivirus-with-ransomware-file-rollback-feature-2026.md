## What Is Ransomware, and Why Do Seniors Get Hit the Hardest?

Ransomware is a type of malicious software (malware) that sneaks onto your computer — usually through a suspicious email attachment, a fake software update pop-up, or a link that looks legitimate — and then quietly locks all your files. Within minutes, every photo, every document, every saved email is encrypted. You cannot open anything. Instead, you see a message demanding payment (typically hundreds to thousands of dollars) to get your files back.

Criminals deliberately target older adults for several reasons:

- **Less familiarity with phishing tricks.** Deceptive emails and fake pop-ups are designed to look exactly like messages from trusted banks, delivery companies, or government agencies. They are engineered to fool people who did not grow up spotting these tactics.
- **More valuable files.** Retirees often have decades of irreplaceable documents, family photos, and financial records stored locally on one machine — making the ransom demand feel unavoidable.
- **Slower response time.** Ransomware often goes unnoticed for hours. The longer it runs, the more files it encrypts.
- **Willingness to pay.** Studies have found older victims are more likely to pay the ransom, which only funds more attacks.

Paying the ransom, by the way, is rarely the solution criminals promise. Many victims pay and still never recover their files.

## The Problem With "Regular" Antivirus

You might already have some form of antivirus software on your computer. Many people do. But standard antivirus works primarily by blocking known threats before they get in. It compares files and programs against a database of identified malware.

The problem: ransomware evolves constantly. Criminal groups release new variants specifically designed to slip past detection databases. A "zero-day" ransomware attack — one using a brand-new method that has never been seen before — can defeat ordinary antivirus entirely.

Even good antivirus software sometimes catches ransomware only after it has already encrypted a handful of files. A few seconds of ransomware activity can destroy years of your memories.

Yes, and increasingly so. Ransomware used to be aimed mainly at businesses. In recent years, attackers have shifted significant effort toward home users, particularly older adults, because home computers are often less well protected. Any computer connected to the internet can be a target.

### Can ransomware attack a Mac, or is it only a Windows problem?
