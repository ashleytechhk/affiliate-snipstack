## What Is a VPN for Coffee Shop Wifi, and Why Does It Matter?

A VPN (Virtual Private Network) creates an encrypted tunnel between your laptop and the internet. Instead of your data traveling in a form that anyone on the local network could potentially read, it's scrambled before it ever leaves your device. When you connect through a VPN server, a few things happen at once:

1. **Your traffic gets encrypted** — the coffee shop's router, its wifi access point, and anyone else logged into the same network cannot read what you're sending or receiving.
2. **Your IP address is masked** — websites and services see the VPN server's address instead of your laptop's actual location.
3. **You can appear to browse from a different region** — useful for comparing prices, checking availability, or accessing content that varies by country.

For a coffee shop worker, freelancer, or remote employee, this matters every time you connect to a network you don't control. Sending an invoice, logging into a work account, checking a banking app, or simply browsing while your laptop auto-syncs files in the background — all of it travels over the same open network as everyone else in the room.

### The Specific Risks of Working on Public Wifi

Most coffee shop networks use minimal or no encryption between your device and the router itself, which is different from the encryption a website provides. While HTTPS protects the content of most modern web traffic, it doesn't hide which sites you're visiting, and older apps or background services on your laptop may not enforce HTTPS consistently.

There's also the router itself. Public wifi networks are rarely monitored closely, and it's not always clear who set up the access point you're joining — an open network named after the shop doesn't guarantee it's run by the shop. A VPN removes this uncertainty by encrypting everything before it reaches the local network, so it doesn't matter who's watching the router.
