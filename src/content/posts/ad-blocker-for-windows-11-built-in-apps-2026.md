## What Are "Windows 11 Built-In Apps," Exactly?

When Microsoft ships Windows 11, it pre-installs a collection of apps that come with the operating system — sometimes called "inbox" or "built-in" apps. These include:

- **Microsoft News** (also branded as Microsoft Start)
- **Weather** and **Maps**
- **Xbox** app
- **Microsoft Edge** (the default browser)
- **Start Menu widgets** (the panel that slides out from the taskbar)

The key detail: several of these apps display sponsored content, promoted listings, or outright banner and video advertisements. The Widgets panel in particular has drawn user frustration for blending paid promotions alongside actual news content, making it difficult to distinguish editorial from advertising.

This is distinct from the third-party apps you install yourself from the Microsoft Store, which may have their own ad models. We are talking about Microsoft's own software, bundled with the OS at no extra charge to you — but monetized through advertising.

## Can Windows 11 Block Its Own Built-In Ads?

Short answer: not natively, not fully.

Microsoft provides some limited controls:

- **Personalized ads toggle** — Settings > Privacy & Security > General. Turning this off reduces targeted advertising but does not eliminate ads from built-in apps.
- **Widgets disable** — You can right-click the taskbar and hide the Widgets button entirely, which stops the news/ad feed from auto-loading.
- **Edge's built-in tracking prevention** — Edge has a "Balanced" and "Strict" tracking prevention mode under Settings > Privacy. This helps inside the Edge browser but has no effect on standalone built-in apps like News or Xbox.

None of these amount to a true [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-impact-on-website-revenue-explained-2026/). They reduce personalization or hide the surface area, but sponsored content persists.

## How Third-Party [Ad Blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-that-removes-youtube-pre-roll-ads-2026/)s Approach the Problem

Third-party solutions take one of three technical approaches:

### 1. Browser Extension [Ad Blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-that-hides-promoted-amazon-listings-2026/)s

Tools like [uBlock Origin](https://snipstack.io/ublock-origin-not-working-chrome-alternatives-2026/) work inside a browser tab. They are extremely effective at [blocking ads on](https://snipstack.io/blocking-ads-on-spotify-mobile-2026/) websites you visit in Edge, Firefox, or Chrome. The limitation: they cannot see anything outside the browser. The News app, Xbox app, and Widgets panel operate outside any browser context, so extensions cannot reach them.

### 2. Hosts-File / DNS-Based Blocking

A hosts file edit (or a local DNS resolver like Pi-hole, if you run one on your home network) blocks connections to known ad-serving domains before they can load — system-wide. This approach can catch ad requests from built-in apps because it operates at the network level, beneath the application layer.

Drawbacks: hosts file editing requires some technical comfort, breaks certain Microsoft update mechanisms if done carelessly, and needs ongoing maintenance as ad server domains change. Pi-hole requires dedicated hardware or a server and only works on your home network — not when you are out with your phone.

### 3. VPN-Level Ad Blocking (CleanWeb Style)

A VPN with an integrated ad-blocking feature filters DNS requests before they leave your device, regardless of which app is making them. This covers built-in apps, third-party apps, browser tabs, and mirrored phone apps — all at once. Because the VPN client runs as a system-level service rather than a browser plugin, it can intercept ad requests from any application.
