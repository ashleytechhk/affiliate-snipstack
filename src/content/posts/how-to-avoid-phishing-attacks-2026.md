## What You Need Before You Start (Prerequisites)

You do not need to be technical. You need:

- A working email account you can access
- A smartphone or computer
- About 15 minutes to set up the protections below
- A healthy skepticism toward any message that asks you to act fast

## Step 1 — Learn to Identify a Phishing Email

Phishing emails are designed to trigger a gut reaction: fear, urgency, or curiosity. Before you click anything, scan for these warning signs.

**Red flags to look for:**

- **Sender address does not match the company.** A message claiming to be from your bank will come from something like `noreply@secure-banking-alert.net` rather than `noreply@yourbank.com`. Look at the full address, not just the display name.
- **Generic greeting.** "Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your name is a sign the sender does not actually know who you are.
- **Urgency and threats.** "Your account will be closed in 24 hours." "Unusual activity detected — act now." Legitimate companies do not communicate this way.
- **Grammar and formatting errors.** Odd spacing, mismatched fonts, and broken English remain common giveaways, though AI-written scams have improved noticeably.
- **Unexpected attachment.** An invoice you did not request, a "delivery confirmation" you did not expect, or a "security report" out of nowhere.

## Step 2 — Hover Over Every Link Before You Click

Your mouse is your first line of defence. Hovering over a link — without clicking — reveals the actual destination URL in the bottom bar of most email clients and browsers.

**How to do it on desktop:**

1. Move your cursor over the link.
2. Look at the bottom-left of your screen for the real URL.
3. Ask: does this domain match the company name exactly?

A link claiming to go to `www.paypal.com` might actually lead to `www.paypal-securelogin.com` or `www.paypa1.com` (note the number 1 replacing the letter l). These are not the same.

**On mobile:**

1. Press and hold the link without releasing.
2. A preview panel will appear showing the full URL.
3. Read it carefully before tapping "Open."

If the domain looks wrong or unfamiliar, do not proceed.

## Step 3 — Never Enter Your Password Through an Email Link

This is the most important rule in the entire guide.

Even if a link looks legitimate, even if it takes you to a page that looks exactly like your bank's login screen, do not type your password there.

**What to do instead:**

1. Close the email or tab.
2. Open a new browser tab.
3. Type the company's address directly into the address bar (e.g., `www.yourbank.com`).
4. Log in from there.

If there is a genuine security issue with your account, you will see it after you log in normally. If nothing is wrong, the email was a scam.

Phishing pages are built to capture your credentials the moment you type them. There is no safe version of "just checking." Do not do it.

## Step 4 — Use a Password Manager (It Catches Fakes Automatically)

![Password manager auto-fill blocking a phishing site](/og/how-to-avoid-phishing-attacks-2026-inline-1.png)

A password manager does something no human can do reliably: it checks the exact domain before it offers to fill your password.

If you visit `www.paypal.com`, your password manager fills in your PayPal credentials automatically. If you land on `www.paypa1-login.com` — even if the page looks identical — the password manager stays silent. No fill. No suggestion. That silence is the alert.

**[How to set](https://snipstack.io/how-to-set-up-vpn-on-iphone-2026/) this up:**

1. Choose a reputable password manager (most devices have a built-in option; third-party options offer more features).
2. Save your login credentials for your important accounts through the official websites, not through phishing pages.
3. Enable auto-fill in your browser settings.
4. From now on, if auto-fill does not trigger on a login page, treat that as a warning.

This one habit catches phishing sites that fool even experienced users.

## Step 5 — Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Two-factor authentication adds a second layer. Even if a phishing page captures your password, the attacker still cannot get into your account without the second factor — a one-time code from your phone, a fingerprint, or a hardware key.

**How to enable 2FA:**

1. Go to the security settings of each important account (email, bank, social media).
2. Look for "Two-Factor Authentication," "Two-Step Verification," or "Login Verification."
3. Follow the setup instructions — usually involves scanning a QR code with an authenticator app or receiving SMS codes.
4. Store your backup codes somewhere safe offline.

Authenticator apps (which generate rotating codes) are more secure than SMS codes, but either is far better than no 2FA at all.

## Step 7 — Report and Delete

Once you have confirmed something is a phishing attempt, two more actions matter.

**Report it:**

- In Gmail: click the three-dot menu → "Report phishing."
- In Outlook: click "Report" → "Report phishing."
- In Apple Mail: move to Junk, then forward to `reportphishing@apple.com` if it impersonated Apple.
- Report to the impersonated company directly — most large banks and services have a dedicated fraud email address.

**Delete it:**

Do not leave it sitting in your inbox. Delete it and empty your trash. There is no benefit to keeping it.

## SMS Phishing (Smishing) — The Same Rules Apply

Phishing does not only arrive by email. Text message phishing — called smishing — follows the same playbook:

- A fake delivery notification with a tracking link.
- A "bank alert" asking you to verify your account.
- A government impersonation ("Your tax refund is ready — claim it here").

Apply the same steps: hover (or long-press), never tap through to enter credentials, and run the link through a scam checker if you are unsure.

One extra tip for SMS: legitimate couriers and banks will never ask you to pay a fee or re-enter your card details via text message.

## Voice Phishing (Vishing) — When the Scam Calls You

Voice phishing, or vishing, involves a caller pretending to be from your bank, a government agency, or a [tech support](https://snipstack.io/tech-support-scam-phone-call-identify-2026/) team. AI voice cloning has made this more convincing in recent years.

**How to handle suspicious calls:**

1. Do not give out personal information to an inbound caller, no matter how official they sound.
2. Hang up.
3. Look up the organisation's official [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/) independently and call them back yourself.
4. Never follow instructions from a caller to install remote access software on your device.

If the caller claims to be from your bank and says the matter is urgent, that urgency is designed to stop you from thinking clearly. Hang up and call the number on the back of your card.

## Troubleshooting: "I Already Clicked the Link"

If you clicked a suspicious link but did not enter any information:

- Close the tab immediately.
- Run a malware scan on your device.
- Change your password for any account the email mentioned, using the official website directly.

If you entered your password:

- Change it immediately on the official site.
- Check whether any other accounts share that password and change those too.
- Contact your bank if financial details were involved.
- Enable 2FA on the affected account right away.

Acting fast limits the damage significantly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I know if an email is really from my bank?**
Look at the full sender address — not just the name. Your bank's emails will always come from its official domain (e.g., `@yourbank.com`). If in doubt, log in to your account directly through the bank's website rather than through the email.

**Is it safe to open a phishing email if I do not click anything?**
Opening an email is generally low-risk in modern email clients, but clicking any links or downloading attachments is not. Some older email clients can load remote images that confirm your address is active — worth disabling remote image loading in your settings if you receive a lot of suspicious email.

**What is the difference between phishing and smishing?**
Phishing arrives by email. Smishing (SMS phishing) arrives by text message. Vishing arrives by phone call. The psychological tactics are identical — urgency, impersonation, and a request for credentials or payment.

**Does two-factor authentication stop phishing completely?**
It makes phishing much harder to exploit. An attacker who steals your password still cannot access your account without the second factor. However, some sophisticated attacks intercept 2FA codes in real time — which is why never entering credentials on a page you reached from an email link remains the most important rule.

**What should I do if I think I have been phished?**
Act immediately: change your passwords, contact your bank if financial details were involved, and enable 2FA on affected accounts. Report the phishing message to your email provider and to the impersonated organisation. The faster you act, the less damage occurs.
