## Method 2: Unsubscribe Campaigns

The instinct most people follow: scroll to the bottom of every political email, click unsubscribe, and repeat until the flood stops.

**Why it seems reasonable:** It's built into every email and requires no new accounts or tools.

**Why it fails against political email:**

Commercial email senders are required under CAN-SPAM to honor opt-out requests within 10 business days. Political campaigns and their affiliated PACs operate under different rules — they are largely exempt from CAN-SPAM's opt-out requirements. The campaign you unsubscribed from may legally continue sending. More importantly, unsubscribing does nothing about the copies of your address already shared with affiliated organizations. You opt out of one list and remain on twenty others you don't even know about.

Unsubscribing also confirms to the sender that your address is active and monitored — a signal that can make your address *more* valuable to re-sell, not less.

## Method 3: Gmail `+tag` Plus-Addressing

Gmail allows you to append a custom tag: `yourname+politics@gmail.com`. All mail to that address lands in your main Gmail. You can set automatic filters to archive or label tagged political emails.

**Why it partially works:** Zero setup, free, and compatible with Gmail's existing filter system.

**Why it breaks down:**

Most large email sending platforms — including those widely used by political campaigns and fundraising vendors — automatically strip everything after the `+`. The email effectively arrives at `yourname@gmail.com`. Your real address is fully visible. When that database gets shared with affiliated campaigns, they receive your untagged, real address.

Even when the `+` tag survives, you cannot revoke it. You can filter mail to that tag, but you cannot prevent the sender from holding `yourname+politics@gmail.com` indefinitely — or from distributing it further.

## Method 4: Secondary "Junk" Gmail Account

The classic workaround: create a second Gmail account and use it exclusively for political signups, donations, and petitions.

**Why it works initially:** Your real inbox stays clean. Political emails pile up in the junk account instead.

**Why it degrades:**

- You have to actively check the junk inbox. Confirmation emails, receipts, and anything time-sensitive (like a voter registration confirmation) land there too. Most people stop checking within a few weeks.
- The junk address is still a permanent email address. It accumulates its own spam, and it can be linked back to your real identity if you signed up using your real name, [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-for-facebook-verification-2026/), or physical address during any of those political form fills.
- You cannot selectively kill one campaign's access — you'd have to abandon the entire account and start fresh.
- Managing two inboxes indefinitely is a maintenance tax that most people find unsustainable.

## Method 5: Inbox Filters and Rules

Gmail, Outlook, and Apple Mail all let you create rules: if sender contains "campaign" or subject contains "URGENT DEADLINE," archive or delete automatically.

**Why it helps manage volume:** You stop seeing the worst offenders in your primary inbox view.

**Why it doesn't hide your email from political campaigns:**

Filters are reactive. They catch senders you've already identified and built rules around. Every new campaign that acquires your address gets through until you add another rule. There is no finish line — political data sharing means new senders appear continuously through each election cycle.

Filters also do nothing about your real address already sitting in campaign databases, fundraising vendor lists, and data broker records. Your address is still exposed. The filters just rearrange how it reaches you.

**Why can't I just unsubscribe from political campaign emails?**

Political campaigns and their affiliated PACs are largely exempt from the opt-out requirements that govern commercial senders under CAN-SPAM. Even campaigns that voluntarily honor opt-out requests typically share donor and supporter data with affiliated organizations before processing the removal — so unsubscribing from one often has no effect on related organizations that already hold your address.

**Will deleting an Alt-ID alias affect confirmation emails or voter registration records I need?**

Before deleting, check whether you need to retain access to any important communications tied to that alias — such as a voter registration confirmation or donation receipt. No. An Alt-ID alias is a functional email address that receives mail like any other. From the campaign's perspective, they have a valid email address on file. The alias structure is not flagged or identifiable as different from a regular address during signup.
