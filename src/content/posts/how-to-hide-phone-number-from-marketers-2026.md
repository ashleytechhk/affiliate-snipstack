## How Marketers Get Your Number From Dating Apps

It doesn't happen the way most people picture it. It's rarely a breach in the dramatic sense. The pipeline is quieter and more systematic.

### Step 1: App Verification

Nearly every dating app requires phone number verification at signup. This is standard practice — it reduces fake accounts and bots. But it also means the app now holds your number in its database.

### Step 2: Third-Party SDKs and Ad Networks

Dating apps, like most free apps, monetise through advertising. They embed third-party analytics and ad SDKs that can collect device identifiers and, in some cases, hashed or partial contact data. When an ad network aggregates this data across thousands of apps, your number becomes part of a larger profile even if the dating app itself never "sold" your data explicitly.

### Step 3: Data Brokers

Data brokers purchase, license, or scrape data from multiple sources — public records, app data, website forms, loyalty programs, and breached datasets. They cross-reference everything to build profiles. Your phone number from a dating app verification gets matched to your name from a public record, your email from a retail signup, and your home zip code from a loyalty card. The combined profile is worth significantly more than any single data point.

### Step 4: You Start Receiving Calls and Texts

Marketers buy lists from data brokers. Automated diallers and SMS platforms push campaigns at scale. You receive calls about loan refinancing, car warranties, and insurance quotes — none of which you asked for. The connection back to a dating app signup from months ago is invisible.

## Why Your Real Number Is More Dangerous Than You Think

**It's persistent.** You can change your email. Changing your phone number means updating every bank, employer, and contact you have. Most people keep their number for years or decades, which means a single exposure has a very long tail.

**It's a 2FA anchor.** Most people use their phone number for two-factor authentication on email, banking, and social accounts. A number in the wrong hands is therefore not just a spam vector — it's a potential account-takeover entry point.

**It's reverse-searchable.** Paid people-finder services can return a full name, current address, relatives, and associated email addresses from a phone number in under a minute. Your dating app profile may be anonymous. Your phone number is not.

**It creates unwanted connections between platforms.** When a dating match has your real number, they can find you on WhatsApp, Telegram, iMessage, and any other platform tied to that number — even if you've blocked them on the dating app itself. Blocking someone on Tinder does not block them from texting your real number.

## What Doesn't Work (And Why People Keep Trying It)

### The Do Not Call Registry

The FTC's Do Not Call Registry applies to compliant telemarketing companies. Actual spam operations — robocallers, data-broker-driven campaigns, offshore SMS blasters — do not check the registry. Registering your number is not useless, but it addresses a narrower slice of the problem than most people expect.

### Blocking Individual Numbers

Blocking a number after it calls you is entirely reactive. The number already has your contact information, and the operation behind it typically has thousands of numbers in rotation. Blocking one makes no meaningful dent. You're playing whack-a-mole with a caller that has infinite hammers.

### Spam Call Filters

Carrier spam filters (AT&T ActiveArmor, Verizon Call Filter, T-Mobile Scam Shield) and third-party apps like Hiya and RoboKiller are genuinely useful — but they operate downstream of the core problem. They intercept calls after your number is already in the marketing database. The noise may reduce, but the underlying exposure remains.

### Giving Out a Google Voice Number

Google Voice is a workable free option for US-based users. You get a real-looking local number to hand out instead of your primary number, and calls forward to your actual phone. The limitation is that it's US-only, requires an active Google account, and the number can be suspended if it goes unused. It's also not designed specifically for mobile — call quality and app reliability have historically been inconsistent.
