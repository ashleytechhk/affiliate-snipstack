## Why "I Have Backups" Is Not Enough Anymore

Most business owners who lose data to ransomware had some form of backup. The problem is the kind of backup they had.

An external hard drive plugged into your computer is not a backup — it is a second target. Ransomware scanners map every connected drive and encrypts all of them. The same applies to a NAS device on your home network: if your laptop is on the same Wi-Fi, ransomware can reach it.

Even cloud sync tools like Dropbox or Google Drive can be weaponized. If ransomware encrypts your local files and your sync client dutifully uploads the encrypted versions, your cloud copy gets overwritten. Some services keep version history — but only for a limited window, and only if you notice quickly enough.

## How Ransomware Actually Gets Into a Small Business

Understanding the attack path helps you see where protection matters most.

### Phishing Emails Are Still the Front Door

Over 90% of ransomware infections start with a phishing email. For remote workers and sole traders, these emails are convincingly personalized — fake invoice attachments from "clients," fake contract PDFs, fake shipping notifications for orders you actually placed. You open the file. A macro runs. Ransomware deploys before your screen has time to react.

### Credential Theft Opens the Back Door

Remote work means RDP (Remote Desktop Protocol), VPNs, and cloud dashboards. If a credential from any of these services appears in a data breach — and millions do every year — attackers can log in as you, disable your antivirus manually, and deploy ransomware directly. This is called a "hands-on-keyboard" attack and it bypasses most file-scanning defenses because a human is operating the machine.

### Outdated Software Creates the Side Door

Unpatched software is a skeleton key for ransomware gangs. Remote workers often skip updates because they cannot afford downtime. But that unpatched VPN client or PDF reader becomes an entry point that requires no click from you at all.

**What is the difference between cloud sync and cloud backup for [ransomware protection](https://snipstack.io/ransomware-protection-for-small-business-2026/)?**

Cloud sync (like standard Dropbox or Google Drive) mirrors your local files in real time. If ransomware encrypts those files, the sync client uploads the encrypted versions and overwrites your clean copies. Cloud backup with version history is different — it retains previous versions of files for days or weeks, so you can restore to a point before the infection. For [ransomware protection](https://snipstack.io/ransomware-protection-software-2026/), version history is not optional.

**If I have antivirus running, can ransomware still get through?**

No antivirus catches 100% of threats. Sophisticated ransomware designed to evade detection, or attacks that begin with stolen credentials rather than a malicious file, can sometimes reach a system even with antivirus running. This is why the full stack — antivirus, breach monitoring, and isolated cloud backup — exists. Each layer catches what the others miss.
