## Epic Games Launcher: Where the Spam Actually Comes From

Epic's biggest spam driver isn't a data breach — it's the weekly free-game giveaway. Every week, Epic emails its entire user base about the newest free title, and marketing consent is often bundled into account creation by default. Millions of accounts exist purely to claim free games, keeping Epic's mailing list enormous and active.

Epic accounts are also frequently linked to Fortnite, which draws a disproportionate amount of third-party phishing — fake "free skins" and "free V-Bucks" emails that spoof Epic's branding closely enough to fool a tired inbox scan. None of that requires your account to be compromised; it just requires your email address to be known and associated with an active Epic account.

The upside: Epic's ecosystem is relatively contained. Outside of Fortnite-adjacent phishing, most of the volume is first-party marketing rather than third-party data sharing. An alias with a simple label — something like `epic-yourname@` — absorbs nearly all of it without any ongoing maintenance.

## Steam: Where the Spam Actually Comes From

Steam's email footprint is different in shape, not just size. Beyond account verification, Steam sends Steam Guard security codes, trade offer confirmations, community market notifications, and wishlist sale alerts — a wider mix of transactional and marketing mail than Epic's.

The bigger factor is Steam's trading economy. Skin trading, key marketplaces, and third-party price-tracking sites often ask users to link an account or provide an email for notifications. Each of those is a separate data handler outside Valve's own systems, and each is a potential leak point. This is also where most Steam-branded phishing originates — fake trade offer emails and spoofed login pages designed to catch traders who move fast and don't check the sender address closely.

There's one Steam-specific detail worth knowing before you set up an alias: changing the email on file can trigger a temporary hold on trading and market activity, typically lasting several days up to a few weeks, as an anti-theft measure. That's not a reason to avoid an alias — it's a reason to set it up correctly the first time.

## FAQ

**Should I use the same [email alias for](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-avoiding-re-marketing-after-cart-abandonment-2026/) both Epic and Steam?**

No — use a separate alias for each. If spam starts arriving, a single shared alias makes it impossible to tell which platform leaked it. Two labeled aliases mean you can delete one without disrupting the other account's recovery access.

**Will an [email alias](https://snipstack.io/email-alias-for-receiving-government-benefit-update-notices-2026/) break Steam Guard or two-factor authentication?**

No, as long as the alias is active and forwarding normally. Steam Guard codes sent to your alias arrive in your real inbox the same as any other forwarded mail. The only caution is around changing the email address itself, which can trigger Steam's trading and market hold — not an issue with 2FA delivery.

**Does Epic Games or Steam block alias or forwarding email domains?**

Neither platform currently blocks established alias providers like [Alt ID](/blog/altid?sub=email-alias-for-game-launcher-account-epic-vs-steam-2026__faq). Some services do block known disposable-mail domains that auto-expire after minutes, but persistent alias domains generally register without issue on both launchers.

**[What happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) if my Epic or Steam alias starts getting spam?**

Delete it and create a replacement. On Epic, this is low-friction — update the account email and move on. On Steam, plan the swap for a moment when you're not mid-trade, since changing the address on file can pause market and trading activity for a period.

**Is switching to an alias worth it if I already have both accounts set up with my real email?**

Yes, though the payoff is gradual. Updating the email on each account stops future leaks going forward, even though it won't undo mail already sent to your real address from past exposure.

**Do I need a different alias for third-party trading sites and marketplaces?**

For heavy Steam traders, yes — a separate alias per major trading site or marketplace pinpoints exactly which one leaks if spam shows up. Unlimited alias plans make this practical at no extra cost.
