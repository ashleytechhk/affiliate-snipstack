## What Is a Pop-Under? (And Why Sports Betting Sites Use Them)

A **pop-under** is an advertisement that opens in a new browser window or tab *behind* your current page. Unlike a pop-up — which interrupts you immediately — a pop-under hides until you close or minimise your main window. Then it is sitting there, waiting.

Sports betting and gambling websites are heavy users of this technique for a straightforward reason: pop-unders are harder to block than standard banner ads, and they generate impressions even when the user never consciously sees them. On desktop browsers, extensions like uBlock Origin have been suppressing pop-unders for years. On mobile — where browser extensions are either unavailable or poorly supported — the landscape is much weaker.

**Why mobile is worse:**

- Safari on iOS does not support browser extensions in the same way desktop Chrome or Firefox do
- Android Chrome's extension support is limited and requires extra steps
- Many users read sports content inside social media in-app browsers, which have zero ad-blocking capability
- Mobile screens make it harder to notice a new tab has opened behind the current one

The result is that sports and betting content sites have quietly migrated their most intrusive ad formats to mobile, knowing most users are unprotected.

## The Real Risks Beyond Annoyance

Pop-unders from sports betting sites are not only irritating — they carry a few risks worth understanding.

### Malvertising and Fake Promotions

Some pop-under networks used by lower-tier betting sites serve ads from third-party ad exchanges with minimal vetting. This creates a pathway for **malvertising** — malicious code embedded in ad creatives. A user who lands on a malvertising pop-under page could be exposed to drive-by download attempts or phishing forms dressed up as "sign up for a bonus" prompts.

### Privacy Tracking

Every pop-under that loads fires tracking pixels, sets cookies, and logs your device fingerprint. Even if you close it immediately, the advertiser now knows your approximate location, device model, and browsing context. Across dozens of site visits, this builds a detailed behavioural profile.

### Gambling Exposure to Minors

If children use shared devices or family phones to watch sports highlights, pop-unders from betting sites are an unwanted delivery mechanism for gambling content. Blocking them is a practical parental control measure, not just a convenience.

## How [Ad Blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-that-stops-notification-permission-popups-2026/)s Handle Pop-Unders (And Why Most Fall Short on Mobile)

A standard [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-that-blocks-sponsored-pins-on-pinterest-2026/) works by comparing every network request a page makes against a list of known ad-serving domains. If a request matches a blocked domain, the blocker cancels it before anything loads.

Pop-unders are trickier because they use JavaScript events — specifically, they hook into clicks, taps, and scroll actions to trigger the window.open() command at a moment when the browser is most likely to permit it (right after a user interaction, to bypass pop-up blockers). A good [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-that-removes-youtube-pre-roll-ads-2026/) needs to:

1. Block the ad network domains that deliver the pop-under scripts
2. Prevent the JavaScript from executing those window.open() calls
3. Do this *before* the browser renders the page — not after

### Browser Extension [Ad Blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-that-removes-sponsored-search-results-2026/)s (Desktop-Focused)

Extensions like uBlock Origin and AdGuard for desktop are highly effective at all three steps on a desktop Chrome or Firefox. On mobile, however, iOS completely prohibits browser extensions in third-party browsers (only Safari Content Blockers are allowed, with limited capabilities), and Android Chrome does not support extensions at all in its standard installation.

### DNS-Level Blockers

DNS-level solutions (like Pi-hole or certain VPN-based filters) block entire domains at the network layer. This is powerful — it works across every app and browser on your device — but it requires technical setup and can cause false positives that break legitimate sites.
