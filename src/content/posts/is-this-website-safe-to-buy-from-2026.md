## Why Shopping Safety Checks Are Different

General website safety advice ("look for HTTPS," "check the padlock") is necessary but not enough for e-commerce. A site can have a valid SSL certificate and still take your money and disappear. The risks that matter when you are *buying* are:

- **Your card details being harvested** by a fake checkout form
- **Your money leaving** your account for a product that will never arrive
- **Your personal information** (name, address, phone) being sold or used for identity fraud

These outcomes require shopping-specific signals to detect. The six checks below are built around that buyer-risk frame — not general site legitimacy.

## Check 1 — Find a Real Business Address

Scroll to the bottom of the site. Look for a footer with a physical address. Copy it and paste it into Google Maps.

What you want to see: a real street address that resolves to a commercial premises, not a residential street or — worse — a virtual mailbox service with no street-level reality. Some legitimate small businesses do operate from home, but a brand that sells hundreds of products should have a real registered address you can verify.

Red flags:
- No address listed anywhere
- Address resolves to a mailbox rental storefront
- Address is in a country that does not match the currency or language of the site

---

## Check 2 — Read the Returns and Refund Policy

Every legitimate retailer has a returns policy. It does not have to be generous — but it has to exist and be specific.

Look for: exact timeframes ("30 days from delivery"), who pays return shipping, and what qualifies as a returnable item. Vague language like "we reserve the right to refuse returns at our discretion" with no further detail is a warning sign. Absent policies are worse.

Scam sites often copy-paste returns policies from other retailers and leave placeholder text or contradictory rules. Read a paragraph carefully. Does it actually describe a process, or does it just use the right words with no substance?

---

## Check 3 — Check the Payment Processor

This is one of the most reliable single signals available. Look at your checkout page carefully.

**Safe signals:** A checkout that redirects you to Stripe, PayPal, Apple Pay, or a major bank's payment gateway. These processors have fraud and dispute systems that protect buyers. If something goes wrong, you have recourse.

**Risk signals:** A checkout that asks you to enter your card number directly into a form on the retailer's own page, with no recognisable payment brand in sight. This does not always mean fraud — smaller merchants sometimes process cards directly through their own gateway — but combined with other red flags, it is a significant concern.

Never pay by bank transfer, cryptocurrency, or gift card voucher for a purchase from a site you have not bought from before. These payment methods have no consumer protection and are irreversible.

## Check 4 — Test the Customer Service Contact

Before you buy, send a test message to the contact email or support chat. Ask a basic question about shipping times or product sizing. You do not need a reply before checkout, but you should verify that:

- An email address exists and is on the same domain as the store (not a Gmail or Yahoo address)
- A phone number, if listed, is a real geographic number — not a VOIP line with no business registration
- A support chat, if available, responds to a basic query with a real answer rather than a bot loop that goes nowhere

Scam sites often list `support@storename.com` as a contact address but never respond to pre-sales enquiries. If the site has a "live chat" that only offers FAQ links and cannot handle a direct question, that tells you something about what post-purchase support will look like.

---

## Check 5 — Look Up Social Media and Reviews

A store that has been operating for more than a few months leaves traces. Search the store name plus "reviews" on Google. Check Trustpilot. Look at the brand's social media pages.

What credible stores look like:
- Reviews that span different dates, with varied language and specific product mentions
- A social media presence with posts going back at least several months
- Trustpilot responses to both positive and negative reviews

What fake stores look like:
- A flood of five-star reviews posted within the same week, all with generic language ("Great product! Fast shipping!")
- Social media accounts created in the past 30 days
- No Trustpilot page, or a Trustpilot page with zero reviews despite claiming thousands of customers

## Putting It Together: A Pre-Checkout Routine

Before you hit Buy on an unfamiliar site, run this sequence:

1. **Footer check** — does a real business address exist? Google it.
2. **Policy check** — is there a clear, specific returns policy?
3. **Payment check** — is a recognisable processor handling the transaction?
4. **Contact check** — is a real email address on the domain available?
5. **Review check** — do Trustpilot and Google reviews look organic?
6. **Site scan** — paste the URL into Scamcheck for a database-backed verdict.

The whole routine takes under two minutes. Steps 1–5 are manual. Step 6 is one paste and a few seconds.

Most legitimate sites pass all six without issue. Most scam sites fail at least two. If a site fails three or more, close the tab.

## FAQ

**How do I know if a website is safe to buy from?**
Look for a verifiable physical address, a specific returns policy, a recognisable payment processor like Stripe or PayPal, a real customer service contact on the store's domain, and a healthy spread of dated reviews on Trustpilot or Google. Then paste the URL into a site scanner like Scamcheck to check it against fraud databases. If a site passes all of those, the risk level is low.

**What payment method is safest for online shopping?**
Credit cards and PayPal offer the strongest buyer protection because both support dispute resolution and chargebacks. Debit cards offer some protection depending on your bank but are slower to resolve disputes. Avoid bank transfers, cryptocurrency, or gift card payments for any purchase from an unfamiliar site — these have no recourse if the seller disappears.

**Is HTTPS enough to tell me a site is safe?**
No. HTTPS means the connection between your browser and the site is encrypted, but it says nothing about whether the site itself is legitimate. Scam sites routinely use HTTPS. It is a necessary but not sufficient signal.

**Can I check a website's age before buying?**
Yes. Domain registration date is publicly available through WHOIS lookup tools, and a Scamcheck scan surfaces domain age automatically. A store claiming to have "thousands of satisfied customers" but registered two months ago is a significant mismatch worth noting. **[Check a site's age and fraud history in one step](/blog/scamcheck?sub=is-this-website-safe-to-buy-from-2026__faq)**

**What should I do if I already paid a suspicious site?**
Contact your card provider or PayPal immediately and report the transaction as potentially fraudulent. The earlier you report, the better your chances of a chargeback. Also report the site URL to your national consumer protection authority — in the US that is the FTC, in the UK it is Action Fraud.

**Does Scamcheck work on mobile?**
Yes. You can paste any URL into Scamcheck from a phone browser before completing a mobile purchase. This matters because mobile shopping makes it harder to inspect site details visually — the pre-checkout scan is even more useful on a small screen.
