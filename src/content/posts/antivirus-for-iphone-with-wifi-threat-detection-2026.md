## "I Was Just Checking My Email"

That is the most common thing people say after they discover their bank account was drained or their identity was stolen. They were just checking email. Just looking at a restaurant menu. Just paying a bill quickly at the airport.

The problem is not what you were doing. It is where you were doing it.

When you join a public WiFi network — at a hotel, a pharmacy, a shopping mall — you are sharing that network with every other person connected to it. On a properly secured home network, that is not a problem. On an open public network, it means a criminal with inexpensive software can sit nearby and intercept the information traveling between your iPhone and the internet.

This type of attack is called a **man-in-the-middle attack**. The criminal does not need to physically touch your phone. They do not need your password. They simply read the data passing through the air around them, the same way you might overhear a conversation in a crowded room.

Passwords, bank login sessions, email content, and credit card numbers have all been stolen this way. It happens quietly, with no warning on your screen, and victims often do not discover what happened until days or weeks later when unfamiliar charges appear or accounts stop working.

## Why Your iPhone Is Not Automatically Safe

There is a popular belief that iPhones are secure by default and do not need extra protection. This is partly true and importantly wrong at the same time.

Apple builds strong security into iOS. Your iPhone resists many types of attacks that affect other devices. But there is one protection Apple cannot build in: it cannot protect the WiFi network you connect to. Once your data leaves your iPhone and travels across a public WiFi network, it is out of Apple's control.

Think of it this way. Apple gives you a very secure house with strong locks on every door. But if you walk outside into a neighbourhood full of pickpockets, the locks on your front door do not help you. The threat is on the network, not on the device.

## The Fake Hotspot Trick That Catches Everyone

There is one particular WiFi scam that security experts warn about repeatedly because it is so effective and so difficult to spot without protection.

A criminal sets up a WiFi hotspot with a name that sounds completely normal. "Airport WiFi," "Hotel Guest Network," or "Starbucks Free WiFi." You search for available networks, see it listed, and connect — because the name looks right. But you have just connected directly to the criminal's device.

Every website you visit, every account you log into, every piece of information you send goes through their machine first. They record all of it.

This attack is called an **evil twin hotspot**, and it works on every iPhone and Android phone that has ever been made. The phone cannot tell the difference between a real hotel network and a fake one with the same name. Neither can you, just by looking.

The only defense is encrypted protection that travels with you. When a VPN is active, it does not matter what network you are on — legitimate or fake. The data your iPhone sends is encrypted before it goes anywhere, so the criminal reading it sees nothing useful.
