## Why Pharmacy Accounts Generate So Much Spam

Chain pharmacies operate loyalty and wellness programs that depend on repeat engagement. When you hand over your email for prescription alerts, that address is enrolled in a system that tracks purchase history, refill cadence, and product categories. That data feeds a marketing operation designed to cross-sell.

**Promotional upsell.** Pharmacies stock thousands of over-the-counter products — vitamins, supplements, health devices, cosmetics. Your email is a direct channel for pushing promotions on all of them, regardless of whether you asked.

**Third-party health brand partnerships.** Pharmacy chains enter co-marketing agreements with consumer health brands. Your inbox becomes a delivery vehicle for those partners too. By the time a campaign runs its course, emails are arriving from brands you have never directly interacted with.

**Wellness program nudges.** Many pharmacy loyalty programs include health coaching features, vaccination reminders, and wellness challenges. These generate their own email streams, often separate from the transactional ones you signed up for.

**The catch.** All of this comes packaged with the one thing you legitimately need: the refill-ready notification. Unsubscribing from promotional emails through the pharmacy's preference center sometimes works, but often these settings reset, re-enroll after a new prescription is filled, or simply don't suppress everything. An alias gives you an independent off-switch that the pharmacy cannot override.

### Apple Hide My Email

Apple's alias service (available with iCloud+ from $0.99/month) creates addresses ending in `@privaterelay.appleid.com` and forwards them to your iCloud inbox. It is tightly integrated into Safari and iOS, so generating an alias on a pharmacy's sign-up form is as seamless as tapping a suggestion.

The `@privaterelay.appleid.com` domain is well-established and widely accepted, which means pharmacy forms rarely block it. Alias management happens inside iOS Settings or iCloud on the web — straightforward enough.

The limitation is ecosystem dependency. If your primary inbox is Gmail, Outlook, or a work address, you need to add iCloud as a forwarding intermediary, which adds complexity. It also does not generate alternative identity details alongside the alias. For pharmacy accounts specifically — where your real name, date of birth, and insurance details are required anyway — this isn't a practical problem. But for users who aren't already paying for iCloud+, the subscription overhead adds friction when Alt-ID is free with a plan many people already have.

### SimpleLogin and Firefox Relay

SimpleLogin (part of the Proton ecosystem) and Firefox Relay are both purpose-built alias services with clean interfaces and reliable forwarding. ### DuckDuckGo Email Protection

DuckDuckGo's service generates `@duck.com` aliases and includes a tracker removal step before forwarding. The privacy angle is strong, and the service is genuinely free.

The `@duck.com` domain is recognizable enough that some pharmacy systems flag it during form validation. This is inconsistent — some platforms accept it without issue — but it introduces friction that the other tools avoid. If you already use DuckDuckGo as your browser and want a zero-cost option, test it on your specific pharmacy's sign-up form before committing. If it gets blocked, Alt-ID or Apple's service are reliable fallbacks.

### Gmail's `+tag` Trick

Adding `+pharmacy` or `+cvs` to your Gmail address is a common workaround that appears to solve the problem. It does not.

Most pharmacy marketing systems strip the `+tag` suffix before storing your address in their database. The base address — the permanent one — is what they retain and what gets passed to marketing partners. You have no ability to revoke it. The tag exists in your inbox filter but not in the pharmacy's records.

This approach creates the appearance of email hygiene without the reality. Skip it for pharmacy accounts.

### Free Throwaway Services

10 Minute Mail, Guerrilla Mail, and similar services provide addresses that expire quickly and require checking a separate inbox. Both of these properties make them unsuitable for pharmacy use.

Prescription refill alerts arrive on a schedule that aligns with your medication cycle — days or weeks after initial registration. A 10-minute address will be gone by then. Even services with longer lifespans require you to log back into a web interface to retrieve emails, meaning you will miss time-sensitive notifications unless you check manually.

For a one-time form submission with immediate confirmation, throwaway services are convenient. For pharmacy accounts that you need to receive notifications from for months, they are the wrong tool.
