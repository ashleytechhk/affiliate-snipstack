## Why Shoppers Are the Number One Target for Email Fraud

Scammers follow the money — and for email fraud, that means following the inbox of anyone who shops online.

Think about the volume of legitimate email a regular online shopper receives: order confirmations, shipping notifications, delivery updates, return authorisations, tracking numbers, promotional offers, account alerts. Every one of those email types has a fraudulent equivalent. Scammers build templates around them because shoppers are conditioned to open, read, and act on them quickly.

The seasonal spikes make it worse. Around Black Friday, Cyber Monday, holiday shipping periods, and Amazon Prime events, scam email volume surges because shoppers are expecting more legitimate email and are more likely to click without scrutinising.

The psychology is also exploitable in both directions. Urgency — "your package cannot be delivered, act now" — overrides careful thinking. So does excitement — "your refund of $89.99 has been approved, click to process." Both emotional states reduce the pause that would otherwise make someone stop and check whether the email is real.

In 2026, AI tools have made this significantly worse. Scam emails used to be easy to spot from poor spelling, awkward phrasing, or obviously fake logos. Now they are grammatically perfect, use real brand logos pulled from official websites, and are personalised with data scraped from breaches. The email that got the Florida woman was indistinguishable from a genuine Amazon notification.

## The Four Scams Targeting Shoppers Right Now

Understanding the specific patterns scammers use makes them much easier to spot.

### 1. Fake Shipping and Delivery Alerts

These are the most common. You receive a message appearing to come from UPS, FedEx, USPS, DHL, or a major retailer's own shipping service. The email claims there is a problem with your delivery — an address issue, a customs hold, a failed delivery attempt — and asks you to click a link to resolve it.

The link leads to a fake page that either harvests your personal and payment details under the guise of "confirming your delivery address," or installs tracking software on your device when loaded.

The tell: you are not expecting a package, or the tracking number does not match anything in your actual orders. But if you are a regular shopper, there is often a real package in transit somewhere, which makes this scam particularly effective.

### 2. Fake Order Confirmation Emails

You receive an order confirmation for a purchase you did not make — often for a high-value item like electronics or gift cards. The email looks exactly like a real order confirmation and includes a customer service number or link to "cancel the order if this was not you."

If you call the number, you reach a scammer who will attempt to extract your account credentials or payment details under the pretence of processing the cancellation. If you click the link, you land on a phishing page.

The correct response is to ignore the link and the number in the email entirely, and log in directly to the retailer's website to check whether the order actually exists.

### 3. Refund and Overpayment Scams

These emails claim a refund is waiting for you — from a retailer, a bank, or a payment processor. To receive the refund, you are asked to click a link and enter your bank or card details to "receive the transfer."

No legitimate refund process works this way. Real refunds are processed back to the original payment method automatically. Any email asking you to enter financial information to receive a refund is a fraud attempt.

### 4. Account Suspension Notices

These emails appear to come from Amazon, eBay, PayPal, or another major platform and claim your account has been flagged, suspended, or locked due to suspicious activity. You are urged to verify your identity immediately to restore access.

The link leads to a convincing fake login page that captures your username and password. Once scammers have those, they can access your real account, change the password to lock you out, and drain any stored payment methods or loyalty points.

## [What Happens](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) If You Click the Wrong Link

Acting fast limits the damage significantly.

**If you clicked a link but did not enter any information:** Close the tab immediately. Run a malware scan using your device's built-in security tools ([Windows Defender](https://snipstack.io/windows-defender-download-2026/) on Windows, the Security section in System Preferences on Mac). Monitor the email account associated with the link for any unusual login activity over the next 48 hours.

**If you entered your email or password:** Change your password immediately, starting with the account the email appeared to come from. Then change it anywhere else you use the same password. Enable two-factor authentication if you have not already — this means a stolen password alone is not enough to access your account.

**If you entered payment information:** Contact your bank or card provider immediately and report potential fraud. Ask them to monitor the account for unusual transactions and, if necessary, issue a new card. File a report with the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov.

**If money has been transferred:** Call your bank immediately. Wire transfers and some payment platforms have short reversal windows — the faster you call, the better the chance of recovery.

## Building Ongoing Fraud Protection — Not Just One-Off Checks

Reacting to a suspicious email is one thing. Building habits that reduce your exposure over time is more effective.

**Use a dedicated email address for shopping.** Create a separate email account to use exclusively for online shopping sign-ups. This keeps your main inbox cleaner and means that if a retailer's database is breached and your address is sold to scammers, it does not expose the primary email address tied to your banking or social accounts.

**Monitor your email for breach exposure.** Data breaches are the primary way scammers obtain real email addresses tied to real shopping accounts. When a retailer's database is hacked, the email addresses — along with partial or full order histories — get sold on fraud marketplaces. Surfshark's Email Scam Checker includes monitoring that alerts you when your address appears in newly discovered breaches, so you know to be on heightened alert before scam emails start arriving.

**Enable login alerts on your shopping accounts.** Amazon, eBay, PayPal, and most major retailers allow you to turn on email or push notifications for any new login. This gives you immediate warning if someone accesses your account from a new device.

**Use two-factor authentication everywhere you shop.** A scammer who has your password still cannot access your account if they also need a code from your phone. Set this up on every account that holds payment information.

**Check orders directly, not through email links.** Build the habit of opening your retailer apps or bookmarked sites directly when you want to check an order status, rather than clicking through from emails. This eliminates the risk entirely — even a sophisticated [fake email](https://snipstack.io/fake-email-sender-with-attachment-2026/) cannot do anything if you never click its links.

### What is the most common online shopping scam in 2026?

Fake shipping and delivery notification emails remain the most widespread. Scammers send messages impersonating UPS, FedEx, DHL, USPS, and major retailers, claiming a package cannot be delivered and asking you to click a link to resolve an issue. The link leads to a phishing page or triggers a malware download. If you receive an unexpected delivery alert, always verify by navigating directly to the carrier's official website using the tracking number — not the link in the email.

### Is it safe to click links in emails from retailers I actually shop with?

The safest habit is to avoid clicking links in emails at all, even from retailers you trust. Instead, open the retailer's official app or navigate to their website directly in your browser and check your account from there. This means a [fake email](https://snipstack.io/fake-email-checker-2026/) — even one that looks perfectly legitimate — cannot do anything. If you do click links in emails, check the sender address and hover over links before clicking to confirm they point to the real domain.

### What information should I never give out in response to a shopping email?

Never provide passwords, full credit or debit card numbers, bank account numbers, PIN codes, one-time verification codes, or government ID numbers in response to an email, regardless of how official it appears. Legitimate retailers and payment platforms will never request this information by email. If an email asks for any of these, it is a fraud attempt.
