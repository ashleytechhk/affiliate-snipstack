## What AdGuard Free Actually Does (And What It Misses)

### AdGuard Browser Extension (Free)

The free AdGuard browser extension is genuinely capable. After Manifest V3 effectively neutered [uBlock Origin](https://snipstack.io/ublock-origin-vs-adguard-which-is-better-2026/) on Chrome, AdGuard rebuilt from the ground up for the new framework and shipped the most functional MV3-native blocker available.

**What the free extension blocks:**
- Display ads on most websites — banner ads, sidebar ads, pop-ups
- YouTube pre-roll and mid-roll ads (with AdGuard's YouTube-specific filter list)
- Tracking scripts and third-party analytics pixels
- Social media tracking buttons embedded on pages
- Cosmetic filtering — it hides the empty containers left when ads are removed

**What the free extension misses:**
- Ads in mobile apps (extension only works in desktop browsers)
- Ads in non-browser applications
- Smart TV ads
- System-level trackers that operate outside the browser
- Network-level threats (malware domains being contacted by apps, not pages)

The free extension works well within its domain — desktop browser, while you are actively browsing. The moment you pick up your phone or open a non-browser app, it is not there.

### AdGuard for Windows / Mac (Free Trial, Then Paid)

This is a different product entirely. AdGuard for Windows is a system-wide application that installs at the OS level and intercepts traffic from all applications — browsers, desktop apps, email clients, and system processes. The free trial is 14 days; after that it is a paid product.

**What this adds over the browser extension:**
- Blocks ads in all desktop applications, not just browsers
- HTTPS filtering — can inspect and block encrypted traffic to ad servers
- Phishing and malware site protection at the system level
- Parental controls (content filtering by category)
- App-level blocking rules

**What it still misses:**
- Mobile devices (this is the Windows/Mac application; there are separate iOS and Android apps)
- Smart TV and streaming device coverage

### [AdGuard DNS](https://snipstack.io/adguard-dns-hostname-2026/) (Free Tier, Premium Tier)

AdGuard DNS is a public DNS service. You point your router or device's DNS settings at AdGuard's servers, and DNS queries to known ad and tracking domains get blocked at the DNS level. This is the same fundamental approach as Surfshark CleanWeb.

**Free DNS tier:** Blocks ads and trackers using AdGuard's default filter lists. No account required — just change your DNS settings.

**AdGuard DNS Premium:** Adds a personal dashboard, custom blocklists, per-device rules, statistics tracking, and higher query limits.

**Limitation of free DNS:** No setup interface, no statistics, no custom rules, no support. You are trusting AdGuard's default lists with no visibility into what is being blocked.

## The AdGuard Pricing Confusion

Here is where most users get lost. AdGuard has at least five distinct products that can be purchased:

1. **AdGuard for Windows** — annual license, single device or family
2. **AdGuard for Mac** — same pricing model
3. **AdGuard for Android** — one-time purchase or subscription
4. **AdGuard for iOS** — App Store purchase
5. **AdGuard DNS Premium** — subscription per number of devices
6. **AdGuard VPN** — separate subscription (add-on)

There is no single "AdGuard Everything" subscription at a transparent price. If you want ad blocking across your Windows laptop, iPhone, and smart TV, you are buying multiple separate products.

This is the core problem. A user who starts with the free browser extension and then wants cross-device coverage discovers they are looking at multiple separate purchases — not an upgrade path.

## AdGuard vs. AdBlock: Is There a Difference?

This is one of the most searched questions in this category, so worth addressing directly.

**AdGuard** (the Russian-founded, Cyprus-based company) and **AdBlock** (the US-based browser extension) are completely different products from different companies. The naming similarity is coincidental.

Key differences:
- AdGuard's MV3 extension outperforms AdBlock on most test benchmarks — particularly [YouTube ad](https://snipstack.io/youtube-ad-blocker-2026/) blocking and tracker blocking
- AdBlock's "Acceptable Ads" feature (enabled by default) whitelists some ads in exchange for revenue from advertisers. This is opt-out, not opt-in. Turn it off if you install AdBlock.
- AdGuard has no equivalent revenue-sharing whitelist — blocks ads regardless of whether advertisers pay
- AdGuard offers system-level products (Windows, Mac, iOS, Android apps); AdBlock is extension-only
- AdGuard's filter lists are maintained in-house with more granular YouTube-specific rules

For Chrome users on the free tier: **AdGuard beats AdBlock**. The Acceptable Ads issue alone makes AdBlock a weaker default choice.

## FAQ

### Is AdGuard better than AdBlock?

Yes, in most benchmarks. AdGuard's Manifest V3 extension outperforms AdBlock on YouTube ad blocking, tracker blocking, and filter list depth. More importantly: AdBlock enables "Acceptable Ads" by default — whitelisting ads from advertisers who pay AdBlock. AdGuard does not do this. For users who want complete ad blocking, AdGuard is the better choice.

### Does AdGuard free block YouTube ads on Chrome?

Yes — better than most alternatives. AdGuard maintains a dedicated YouTube filter list that is updated regularly to counter YouTube's detection and ad-serving changes. It is not perfect (YouTube actively works against browser extensions), but it performs better than uBlock Origin Lite on Chrome.

### Is AdGuard safe to use?

AdGuard is a legitimate, established security company. The browser extension is open source and auditable. The Windows/Mac app uses HTTPS certificate interception for some filtering features — which some security professionals view as a concern since it operates as a man-in-the-middle on encrypted traffic. For users uncomfortable with this, DNS-level solutions (AdGuard DNS or Surfshark CleanWeb) do not require certificate interception.

### What is AdGuard DNS and is it free?

AdGuard DNS is a public DNS resolver that blocks ads and trackers at the DNS lookup level. The free tier works without an account — you just change your device or router's DNS settings to AdGuard's addresses. The free tier uses default blocklists with no customization. AdGuard DNS Premium ($3-5/month) adds personal blocklists, per-device configuration, and statistics.

### Does AdGuard work on iOS?

Yes — AdGuard has a dedicated iOS app available on the App Store. On iOS, the app uses Safari Content Blockers and DNS filtering (since iOS does not allow the same type of system-level blocking as Windows/Mac). AdGuard for iOS is a separate purchase from the desktop apps.

### Can AdGuard block ads in all apps, or just the browser?

The browser extension blocks ads only in the browser. AdGuard for Windows and AdGuard for Mac block ads system-wide — across all applications. For mobile (iOS/Android), the apps use DNS-level filtering to cover all apps. Coverage varies by platform and product tier.

### When does it make sense to pay for AdGuard instead of using Surfshark?

Pay for AdGuard when you primarily want granular browser-level control: custom filter lists, per-site rules, cosmetic filtering, and extension-style customization. CleanWeb is the better choice when you want cross-device coverage, do not care about per-site granularity, and want the VPN included.
