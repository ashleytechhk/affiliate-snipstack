## The Risk, Explained Simply

### What actually happens during an attack

Most small business file losses do not start with a dramatic hacking scene. They start small and quiet:

- **An email that looks like it's from a client or a bank**, asking you to open an attachment or click a link.
- **A fake invoice or shipping notice** that installs software the moment you open it.
- **A pop-up warning** claiming your computer has a virus, urging you to call a number or download a "fix."

Once malware is on your computer, it can quietly copy itself into every folder, including the ones holding your business files. Some malware, called **ransomware**, locks every file it can find and demands payment to unlock them. Paying does not mean your files come back — many victims pay and never receive a working key.

![Diagram showing how ransomware spreads from one email click to locked business files](/og/antivirus-with-cloud-backup-for-small-business-files-2026-inline-1.png)

### Why "I have a backup drive" is not enough

Many small business owners believe they are covered because they occasionally copy files to a USB drive or an external hard drive. Here is the problem: if that drive is plugged into your computer when malware strikes, it can be locked or damaged right alongside everything else. A backup sitting next to your computer is not a real safety net — it is simply a second target in the same room.

A proper cloud backup stores your files somewhere completely separate from your computer, so an attack on your laptop cannot reach it. That separation is the entire point.

Yes, arguably more than most. Small, one-person businesses are frequent targets precisely because they usually have no dedicated protection. Every invoice you email, every client attachment you open, and every online payment you process is a potential entry point for malware.

### Isn't a USB drive backup good enough for my business files?

Not on its own. If that drive is connected to your computer during an attack, it can be locked or damaged along with everything else. A cloud backup, stored in a separate location, protects your files even if your computer is compromised.

Antivirus is the guard at the door — it works to stop malware and ransomware from getting onto your computer in the first place.
