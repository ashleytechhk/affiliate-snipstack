## Why "Anonymous Email Sign-Up" Is Not One Thing

Before comparing tools, it helps to be clear about what "anonymous email sign-up" can mean, because the options serve different needs.

**Hiding your real email from a service:** You want to sign up for something but not give them your actual address. A forwarding alias — Alt-ID, Hide My Email, Firefox Relay — is the right tool. You still receive all emails in your real inbox; the service just never learns what that inbox is.

**Hiding your identity entirely from the email provider:** You want an email account where the provider itself has no link to your real identity. ProtonMail with a VPN connection and no recovery email is the classic answer here. This is a different, more involved setup.

**One-time verification with zero trace:** You need to pass a registration check and never return. Self-destructing inboxes handle this.

Most people dealing with spam have the first problem. The service signed them up, shared their data, and now the spam flows. The fix is aliases — not a new email account. The rest of this comparison focuses on that scenario.

## Apple Hide My Email

Apple's built-in alias service is excellent — if you are fully inside the Apple ecosystem. It generates forwarding aliases that look like legitimate iCloud addresses, supports replies, and is managed directly from iPhone or Mac settings with no additional app required.

The core limitation is hard: Hide My Email only forwards to an iCloud Mail address. If your primary inbox is Gmail, Outlook, or anything outside Apple's ecosystem, the setup becomes friction-heavy and the replies get complicated. For users on Windows or Android, it's effectively unavailable.

For iPhone-native, iCloud-primary users who want zero-effort anonymous sign-up: Hide My Email is excellent. For anyone else, the ecosystem lock-in makes it impractical as a primary solution.

## Firefox Relay

Firefox Relay offers five free aliases that forward to any email address — no ecosystem lock-in. The browser extension makes alias creation seamless: when a sign-up form appears, Relay offers to generate an alias in one click without leaving the page.

The free tier's five-alias limit is the binding constraint. If you're serious about using a different alias for every service — which is the strategy that actually works — you'll exhaust five aliases quickly. The paid tier removes the limit at a reasonable price, but you're now paying for a standalone alias tool rather than getting it bundled with broader privacy software.

Firefox Relay is a solid choice for a user who lives in Firefox and wants frictionless in-browser alias creation. It does not solve the broader personal-info problem the way Alt-ID's identity generation does.

## Gmail Plus-Addressing: Doesn't Actually Hide Anything

Gmail's `yourname+tag@gmail.com` format routes tagged mail to your inbox and is widely misunderstood as providing privacy. It does not.

Anyone who receives `yourname+shopping@gmail.com` can strip the `+tag` and immediately see your real address: `yourname@gmail.com`. Marketers know this. Most spam list processors strip the tag automatically. You're not hiding anything from anyone who knows what to look for — and they do.

Plus-addressing is useful for inbox filtering. It is not useful for anonymous sign-up where "no personal info" is the actual goal.

## Self-Destructing Inboxes: One Job Only

Services like Temp Mail, Guerrilla Mail, and 10-Minute Mail generate a temporary address that anyone can access if they know it (or guess it). They are public inboxes, not private ones. There is no account, no password, and no way to reply.

For a one-time site registration you'll never return to, they're fine. For anything you need to access again — account settings, password resets, purchase confirmations — they fail. The inbox expires or disappears, and you're locked out.

The "no personal info" part is technically true: you don't give these services anything. But they also give you no security, no persistence, and no ability to manage what happens with the alias over time.

## FAQ

**Does anonymous email sign-up actually prevent spam?**

It prevents future spam tied to that particular address. The alias becomes the spam target — not your real inbox. When the alias goes noisy, you delete it and the spam stops. It does not affect spam already arriving on your real address from previous exposures. For that, you'd need inbox filters or — if the volume is severe — migrating to a fresh primary address.

**Will services block or reject an Alt-ID alias address?**

Most services accept Alt-ID aliases — they look like regular email addresses, not obviously temporary or disposable domains. A small minority of services run aggressive alias detection. If a service rejects the alias, you'll need to decide whether to use your real address for that account or skip the service entirely.

**Does the sender know I'm using an alias?**

No. From the sender's perspective, the alias is just an email address. There is no indication it's forwarded or that a different real address exists behind it. When you reply through the alias, the reply also comes from the alias address — not your real one.

**Is there a free way to sign up anonymously with no personal info?**

Firefox Relay's free tier offers five aliases at no cost. Guerrilla Mail and Temp Mail offer zero-persistence inboxes for free. For a persistent, reply-capable alias system with no limit and no ecosystem lock-in, a paid option is required. Alt-ID's effective cost is zero if you're already using Surfshark for VPN protection.

**Should I use a different alias for every service?**

Yes, if you want to trace which service leaked your data when spam arrives. One alias per service gives you forensic-level identification: spam on `amazon-alias` means Amazon's data was compromised; spam on `newsletter-alias` means a newsletter shared your address.
