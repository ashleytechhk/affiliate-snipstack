## Quick Verdict

Browser extensions like [uBlock Origin](https://snipstack.io/ublock-origin-mobile-2026/) do not work inside the YouTube app — they only run in desktop browsers. For mobile users, you have four realistic options: pay for YouTube Premium, switch to Brave browser for browser-based watching, use AdGuard with a system-wide filter, or run a DNS-level blocker like [Surfshark CleanWeb](https://snipstack.io/surfshark-cleanweb-review-2026/) to cover ads and trackers across your entire phone. ## Why Browser Extensions Won't Save You on Mobile

On desktop, the equation is simple: install a browser extension, it intercepts every page request, ads disappear. Chrome, Firefox, and Safari on desktop all support extensions that can inspect and block network requests before they render.

On mobile, browser extensions as a category barely exist. Safari on iOS has Content Blockers (a limited subset), Chrome on Android has no extension support at all. More importantly, even if your mobile browser supported extensions, they would only affect content loaded *in that browser*. The YouTube app is a native app — it runs its own network stack, completely separate from any browser on your phone. An extension cannot touch it.

This is the core problem. Nearly every "[how to block](https://snipstack.io/how-to-block-ads-on-android-phone-2026/) ads on YouTube mobile" article written before 2023 recommends a browser extension. Those articles are wrong for mobile.

## Method 1: YouTube Premium

**Who it's for:** People who watch YouTube daily and want a complete, zero-friction solution.

YouTube Premium ($13.99/month in the US) removes ads across all YouTube surfaces — the mobile app, the web, YouTube Music, and background play. It's the only method that fully blocks ads inside the native YouTube app with no workarounds, no configuration, and no risk of breaking when YouTube updates its ad detection.

The downsides are obvious: it costs money every month, and that money goes directly to Google. If you watch YouTube occasionally or you're already paying for other subscriptions, the math may not add up.

**Verdict:** Best coverage, highest cost.

## Method 2: Brave Browser

**Who it's for:** People who watch YouTube in a browser rather than the app, and want a free solution.

Brave is a Chromium-based [browser with](https://snipstack.io/browser-with-built-in-ad-blocker-2026/) a built-in [ad blocker](https://snipstack.io/ad-blocker-for-iphone-browser-2026/) called Brave Shields. Because the ad blocking happens at the browser level — not as an extension — it works on both Android and iOS without any additional setup.

![Brave browser vs YouTube app comparison on mobile](/og/how-to-block-ads-on-youtube-mobile-2026-inline-1.png)

Open YouTube in Brave instead of the YouTube app and most pre-roll ads are blocked. Brave also supports background audio playback on iOS (a feature YouTube locks behind Premium), which makes it a genuinely useful alternative for podcast-style watching.

**The limitation:** Brave only covers what you watch through Brave. The moment you tap a YouTube link and it opens in the YouTube app, Brave Shields can't help. You'll also lose some YouTube app-specific features — notifications, Shorts, your watch history syncing may behave differently.

**Verdict:** Free, effective in-browser, zero help for the native app.

## Method 3: AdGuard

**Who it's for:** Power users willing to configure a system-level filter on their device.

AdGuard is an app available on both Android and iOS that sets up a local VPN or DNS configuration to intercept ad requests across the whole device — not just one browser. Unlike a browser extension, it processes network traffic at the system level, so it can catch ads in apps, browsers, and other services simultaneously.

AdGuard maintains extensive filter lists and has a dedicated YouTube-specific list. On Android, it can block a meaningful portion of YouTube pre-roll ads through HTTP filtering. On iOS, results vary — Apple's app review rules limit how aggressively AdGuard can intercept encrypted traffic from other apps.

**The limitation:** YouTube serves its video content and its ad content from overlapping domains (primarily `googlevideo.com`). DNS-level blockers — any of them, including AdGuard's DNS filter mode — cannot reliably separate "this request is an ad" from "this request is the video you want to watch." A strict DNS block often breaks video playback entirely. AdGuard's HTTP-level filtering on Android handles this better, but setup is more involved.

**Verdict:** Strong on Android with HTTP filtering; more limited on iOS; requires configuration.

## FAQ

### Does [uBlock Origin](https://snipstack.io/ublock-origin-vs-adguard-which-is-better-2026/) work on YouTube mobile?

No. uBlock Origin is a browser extension that only runs in desktop browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Edge on desktop). It has no effect on the YouTube mobile app, and it isn't available for Chrome on Android or Safari on iOS. For mobile ad blocking, you need one of the methods described above.

### Can I block [YouTube ad](https://snipstack.io/youtube-ad-blocker-2026/)s on iPhone without paying?

The closest free option is watching YouTube in the Brave browser instead of the YouTube app. Brave's built-in Shields block most pre-roll ads in the browser. There is no free method that reliably blocks ads inside the native YouTube app on iPhone.

### Does a VPN block YouTube ads?

A standard VPN does not block ads — it only routes your traffic through a different server. However, some VPNs include DNS-level ad blocking as an add-on feature. Surfshark CleanWeb is one example. Even then, DNS-level blocking does not fully remove ads from the YouTube native app due to how YouTube serves ads on shared infrastructure with video content.

### Is AdGuard safe to use?

AdGuard is a well-established privacy tool with a long track record. On iOS, it requires you to install a configuration profile or trust a root certificate, which some users find uncomfortable. On Android, its local VPN mode is less invasive. Regardless of which tool you use, download only from official sources (the App Store or Google Play).

### Will these methods still work in 2026?

YouTube actively updates its ad delivery to counter blocking attempts. DNS-based methods are the most vulnerable to YouTube's countermeasures.
