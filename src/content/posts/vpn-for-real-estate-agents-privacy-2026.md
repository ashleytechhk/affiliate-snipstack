## What Is a VPN and Why Do Real Estate Agents Need One?

VPN stands for Virtual Private Network. The mechanism is straightforward: instead of your traffic going directly from your device to the internet, it first travels through an encrypted tunnel to a server operated by the VPN provider. From that server, it proceeds to its destination.

Three things happen as a result:

- **Your real IP address is hidden.** The websites, apps, and services you connect to see the VPN server's IP, not yours.
- **Your traffic is encrypted in transit.** Anyone on the same network — a coffee shop router, a hotel switch, even your ISP — sees scrambled data instead of readable content.
- **Your apparent location changes.** If the VPN server is in a different country, services see traffic coming from that country.

For real estate agents specifically, the first two effects are the core value proposition. You handle sensitive client information on a daily basis — financial documents, identity verification, contract signing. Much of that happens away from a secured office network, on wifi connections you don't control and wouldn't know how to audit.

## The Data You're Actually Sending on Public Networks

It's worth being specific about what travels across those connections, because abstract privacy advice rarely changes behavior.

On a typical day in the field, a real estate agent might send or receive:

- Pre-approval letters and financial summaries from lenders
- Signed offers and counteroffers via DocuSign or similar platforms
- Client identity documents for KYC compliance
- MLS login sessions with access to non-public listing data
- CRM credentials covering contact and lead databases
- Payment details for earnest money wiring instructions

Wiring instruction fraud — where intercepted email leads a buyer to wire their down payment to a fraudulent account — is one of the most financially damaging forms of real estate cybercrime. It typically starts with a compromised email account, not the wire transfer itself. The habit of encrypting your sessions on untrusted networks is one layer of defence against session hijacking that makes that initial compromise possible.

None of this requires an adversary who is specifically targeting you. Passive network monitoring on public wifi is trivial to set up and requires little skill to run. Encrypting your traffic removes you from the pool of easy targets.

## FAQ: VPN for Real Estate Agents Privacy

**Do I need a VPN if I already use HTTPS sites and apps?**
HTTPS encrypts the content of individual connections, but your IP address and the metadata of your browsing are still visible to your ISP and anyone monitoring the network. A VPN adds a layer that protects that metadata and prevents network-level observers from seeing which services you're connecting to, not just the content of those connections.

**Will a VPN protect against wiring fraud and phishing?**
A VPN reduces the risk of session hijacking on public networks, which is one attack vector that can lead to email compromise. It does not protect against social engineering, phishing links, or password reuse. Think of it as one layer in a broader security posture, not a complete solution. Always verify wire instructions by phone using a number you already have on file.

**Is using a VPN for streaming pricing allowed?**
VPNs are legal tools in most countries. Using them to access regional streaming pricing may conflict with a platform's terms of service — most platforms' ToS restrict use to the country of registration.
