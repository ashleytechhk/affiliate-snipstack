## The Problem With Short-Lived Temporary Emails

Most people searching for a temporary email that lasts longer hit the same sequence of frustrations:

1. They use a throwaway address and the confirmation email doesn't arrive in time.
2. They extend the timer but the site won't resend the confirmation.
3. They try a different service and get blocked — many websites actively detect and reject known temporary email domains.
4. They give up and use their real address, which then receives spam for years.

The root problem is that throwaway services are designed for a single transaction lasting a few minutes. They were never built for an inbox you can return to. If you need a temporary email that lasts longer, you don't actually need a temporary email at all — you need a permanent alias.

## Option 1: 10 Minute Mail

**Lifespan: 10 minutes (extendable in 10-minute increments)**

10 Minute Mail does exactly what the name says. You land on the page, an address is waiting for you, and you have 10 minutes. Each extension adds another 10 minutes.

**What it's good for:** Downloading a gated PDF. Redeeming a single promo code on a site you'll never return to. Any transaction where the confirmation arrives within minutes and you never need the address again.

**Where it breaks down:** The moment you close the tab, your session ends. There's no account, no stored inbox, no way to retrieve emails from a previous session. If the confirmation email from a retailer takes 12 minutes to arrive — not uncommon during peak times — it lands in an expired inbox you can no longer access. For anyone who needs a temporary email that lasts longer than a short sprint, 10 Minute Mail is not the answer.

Blocking is also a consistent issue. Sites that care about email quality — software trial registrations, SaaS platforms, financial services — maintain blocklists of known temporary email domains. 10 Minute Mail's domain appears on most of them.

## Option 2: Guerrilla Mail

**Lifespan: ~1 hour of inactivity before expiry**

Guerrilla Mail is the most feature-complete of the free throwaway options. You get a random address instantly, you can customise the username prefix, and — uniquely among the free services — you can send email from the temporary inbox as well as receive it. Messages survive for roughly an hour if the tab stays open and you remain active.

**What it's good for:** One-time sign-ups, verifying accounts on services you distrust, anything requiring a quick back-and-forth that can be completed in a single sitting.

**Where it breaks down:** Like all throwaway services, Guerrilla Mail stores your inbox in your browser session. Clear your cookies, switch devices, or close the browser and the inbox is gone. The one-hour window also resets on inactivity — if you're waiting for a delayed confirmation email and step away, you may return to a blank inbox.

Guerrilla Mail's domain is widely recognised by anti-spam filters. High-security services — banks, government portals, many SaaS platforms — will reject it at registration.

## Option 3: Temp-Mail

**Lifespan: ~2 hours (session-tied)**

Temp-Mail edges out Guerrilla Mail on lifespan and has a noticeably cleaner mobile interface, which matters if you're signing up for something on your phone. The address is tied to your browser session and persists for roughly two hours, giving you a wider window than the other free options.

**What it's good for:** Short-term sign-ups where you need a slightly longer window, mobile signups where the interface of Guerrilla Mail feels cramped.

**Where it breaks down:** Two hours is still two hours. Shipping confirmation emails, account verification follow-ups, and password reset links that arrive later than expected will all land in an expired inbox. And because the address resets with each new browser session, you cannot retrieve the inbox from a different device or after clearing your browser.

The domain detection issue is consistent across all three free services. Temp-Mail, Guerrilla Mail, and 10 Minute Mail all use domains that appear on public blocklists. If a site rejects your address, the common workaround — trying a different throwaway service — usually produces the same result.

## FAQ

**Why do free temporary email addresses expire so fast?**

Free throwaway services store your inbox in a temporary session. The short lifespan is a deliberate design choice — it keeps storage costs low and the service fast. Some services extend the session while you actively use the tab, but there's no persistent account, so the inbox disappears when the session ends. If you need a temporary email that lasts longer, you need a service with persistent storage or a forwarding alias like Alt-ID.

**Will a temporary email address work for bank or government sign-ups?**

Almost certainly not. High-security services routinely check submitted addresses against known temporary email domains and reject them. For banks, government portals, and healthcare providers, use your real address — those are accounts where a direct relationship with your actual inbox is appropriate. Reserve aliases for everything else.

**Can I retrieve emails from a Guerrilla Mail or Temp-Mail inbox after I close the tab?**

No. Free throwaway services tie the inbox to your browser session. Once the tab is closed, the session ends and the inbox is inaccessible. If you received an email you needed, it's gone. This is one of the clearest use-case limits of short-lived temporary addresses.

**Does Surfshark Alt-ID work if a website blocks temporary email domains?**

Alt-ID uses the `@surfshark.id` domain, which is not on the public blocklists that free throwaway services appear on. Most services that block Guerrilla Mail and Temp-Mail will accept an Alt-ID alias. There are exceptions — a small number of services block all non-major-provider domains — but Alt-ID passes the majority of registration filters that the free services fail. **What happens to my Alt-ID aliases if I cancel Surfshark?**

If your Surfshark subscription lapses, your aliases stop forwarding. Emails sent to the alias will bounce rather than reach your real inbox. Before cancelling, note any important accounts tied to aliases and update those accounts with a new email address. This is a meaningful consideration — unlike standalone alias services, Alt-ID is dependent on an active Surfshark subscription.

**How many aliases can I create with Surfshark Alt-ID?**

Surfshark allows multiple aliases per account. The practical approach most users find effective is one alias per service category — one for shopping, one for newsletters, one for SaaS trials — so you can kill a category if it turns bad without affecting the others.

**Is a masked email alias actually private?**

A forwarding alias hides your real email address from the sender. The website, company, or mailing list has your alias, not your personal address. If they share or sell the alias, you can delete it and the exposure ends there. Your real address is never disclosed. For most privacy purposes — spam prevention, reducing data broker exposure, reducing breach risk — this level of separation is effective.
