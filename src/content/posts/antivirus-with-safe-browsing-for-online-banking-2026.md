## Why Seniors Are the Primary Target

Cybercriminals do not pick victims at random. They profile their targets carefully, and older adults consistently rank as the most profitable demographic to attack. Here is why:

**Larger savings balances.** People who have spent decades working and saving tend to have more money in their accounts. That makes a successful attack much more rewarding for a criminal.

**Less familiarity with scam tactics.** Techniques like phishing emails, spoofed bank websites, and fake "security alert" pop-ups are well-known among younger internet users — but they are newer, less familiar territory for many people who came to the internet later in life.

**Delayed reporting.** Studies show older adults are more likely to feel embarrassed about being scammed and wait longer before reporting it. That delay gives criminals more time to move stolen funds offshore, making recovery nearly impossible.

**Frequent banking activity.** Retirement often brings more time for personal finance management — checking investment accounts, paying Medicare premiums, transferring money to grandchildren. More online banking sessions mean more opportunities for an attack.

None of this is your fault. The scams are sophisticated. Even experienced tech professionals get tricked. But knowing the risk exists is the first step to dealing with it.

## [What Happens When](https://snipstack.io/what-happens-when-email-is-in-data-breach-2026/) You Bank Without Protection

Picture this: You receive an email that looks exactly like one from your bank. The logo is right. The colors match. The message says your account has been temporarily suspended due to suspicious activity, and you need to click a link and log in to confirm your identity.

You click. You land on a page that looks completely identical to your bank's real website. You type in your username and password. Within minutes, a criminal in a different country has your credentials and is already logged into your real account.

This is called **phishing**, and it is the most common form of online banking fraud targeting seniors today.

![How a phishing attack targets online banking users — step-by-step diagram](/og/antivirus-with-safe-browsing-for-online-banking-2026-inline-1.png)

There are other threats too:

- **Keyloggers** are tiny programs that install silently on your device and record every keystroke — including your bank password as you type it.
- **Fake banking apps** mimic legitimate apps but funnel your login details directly to criminals.
- **Man-in-the-browser attacks** intercept your banking session in real time, changing payment details after you have already confirmed a transfer.
- **Malicious pop-ups** warn you that your computer is infected (it is not) and prompt you to call a fake "[tech support](https://snipstack.io/tech-support-scam-phone-call-identify-2026/)" number that charges hundreds of dollars to "fix" a non-existent problem.

These are not theoretical dangers. They happen to real people every single day. The Federal Trade Commission receives hundreds of thousands of fraud reports from seniors annually, and online banking fraud represents one of the fastest-growing categories.

## Good Habits That Multiply Your Protection

[Antivirus software with](https://snipstack.io/antivirus-software-with-parental-controls-2026/) safe browsing is your most important line of defense, but a few simple habits make it even more effective:

**Type your bank's address directly.** Never click a link in an email to reach your bank. Open your browser and type the address yourself — for example, type `www.yourbank.com` directly into the address bar. Your bank will never penalize you for doing this instead of clicking their email link.

**Check for the padlock.** Look for a small padlock icon in the browser address bar when you are on your bank's site. If it is missing, close the browser immediately.

**Use unique passwords.** If you use the same password for your bank that you use for a shopping site, a breach of that shopping site gives criminals your banking password too. A password manager can help you maintain unique passwords without having to memorize them.

**Never bank on public Wi-Fi.** Coffee shops, airport lounges, and hotel lobbies offer free Wi-Fi, but those networks are not secure. If you must access your bank while away from home, use your phone's cellular data instead.

**Enable account alerts.** Most banks allow you to set up text or email alerts for every transaction over a certain amount. This catches unauthorized activity fast.

### How do I know if a banking website is fake?

Look for three things: the padlock icon in the address bar, the correct address spelled exactly right (scammers often use addresses like `www.bankofamerica-secure.com` instead of `www.bankofamerica.com`), and no spelling errors on the page. When in doubt, close the tab, open a fresh browser window, and type the address yourself. Antivirus software with safe browsing will also warn you automatically if the site is known to be fraudulent.

Yes, even if someone set up your device for you. Unless they specifically installed and configured antivirus software with web protection at that time, your device may not be covered. ### What if I get a pop-up saying my computer is already infected?

Close it immediately without clicking anything inside the pop-up window. Do not call any [phone number](https://snipstack.io/phone-number-privacy-for-online-selling-2026/) displayed.
