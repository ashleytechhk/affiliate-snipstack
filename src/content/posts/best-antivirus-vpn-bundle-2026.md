## Why Antivirus Alone Is No Longer Enough

A good antivirus catches viruses, malware, and spyware on your device. That protection is essential. But it does nothing to protect your data while it is traveling across a network — and that gap is exactly what criminals exploit.

When you use Wi-Fi at a hotel, a library, a doctor's waiting room, or a coffee shop, your connection passes through equipment you do not control and cannot inspect. Anyone on the same network with basic tools can intercept what you send and receive. Banking details. Email passwords. Credit card numbers entered on a checkout page.

A VPN — a Virtual Private Network — closes that gap. It encrypts everything you send before it leaves your device, turning your data into unreadable code that cannot be intercepted in transit. Even on an unsecured public Wi-Fi network, a VPN means your connection is private.

## What to Do Right Now — Even Before You Install Anything

Installing a security bundle is the most effective step you can take. But while you are getting that set up, these four habits add immediate protection:

**Never click links in emails or texts asking you to log in.** If your bank says there is a problem with your account, open a new browser tab and type your bank's address yourself. Real banks do not ask you to click email links to log in.

**Check the address bar before entering any password.** A fake page for "Barclays" might use an address like "barclays-secure-login.com" instead of "barclays.co.uk." Look carefully — one wrong letter means you are on a fraud site.

**Use a different password for your bank than for any other site.** If your email password is leaked in a breach, criminals will try it on every banking site they can find. A unique password for your bank stops that cold.

**Turn on two-step verification at your bank.** Most banks offer this now. When you log in, the bank sends a one-time code to your phone by text. Even if someone has your password, they cannot log in without that code.
