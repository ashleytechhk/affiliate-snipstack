## What Is a "Local Install" Antivirus?

A local install antivirus is a program you download and install directly onto your computer. The protection database — the list of known viruses and threats it uses to scan your files — lives on your hard drive.

You have almost certainly used one. Norton, McAfee, older versions of AVG, and the disc-based security software sold in electronics stores in the 2000s and 2010s are all examples of local install antivirus programs.

**The critical weakness:** That threat database only knows about viruses that existed when it was last updated. If your program has not updated recently, it is working from an outdated list. Cybercriminals create thousands of new threats every single day. A local database that has not been refreshed in weeks — or months — has blind spots you cannot see.

### Why Updates Matter More Than You Think

A new piece of malware called a "zero-day" threat is one that security researchers have not encountered before. Local antivirus programs cannot catch what they do not know about. In the time it takes your local software to receive an update, download it, and apply it, you could have already clicked the wrong link.

## What Is Cloud-Based Antivirus?

Cloud-based antivirus works differently. Instead of keeping the threat database on your computer, it connects to a constantly updated database stored on remote servers. Every time you open a file, visit a website, or download an attachment, your device checks it against that live database.

**The key advantage:** That database is updated in real time — sometimes within minutes of a new threat being discovered. You do not have to download anything. You do not have to remember to update. The protection is always current.

![How cloud antivirus protection works vs local install — diagram](/og/cloud-based-antivirus-vs-local-install-2026-inline-1.png)

Think of it this way. A local antivirus is like a printed phone book from three years ago. It is useful, but it is missing everyone who moved or got a new number since it was printed. Cloud-based antivirus is like a live online directory that updates every few minutes.

The only reliable way to catch these in real time is with cloud-connected threat detection — because the fake pages are often created and taken down within hours, long before a local database would ever update to include them.

### 2. Criminals Are Specifically Targeting Older Adults

Fraud targeting seniors is not random. It is coordinated. Criminal networks know that older adults are more likely to:

- Have savings accounts, retirement funds, or real estate equity worth stealing
- Be less familiar with social engineering tactics
- Trust authority figures like banks, the IRS, or Medicare representatives
- Be slower to spot the warning signs that something is wrong

A real-time, cloud-based security layer that flags suspicious sites and malicious files immediately is one of the few defenses that works without requiring you to spot anything yourself.

### 3. Older Local Software Often Has Support That Has Ended

Many seniors are running antivirus programs they installed years ago. Some of those programs are no longer supported by the companies that made them. When support ends, updates stop. When updates stop, the protection freezes in time — and attackers know exactly what those frozen programs can and cannot catch.

If you are not sure whether your current antivirus is still receiving updates, that uncertainty is itself a warning sign.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is cloud-based antivirus safe? My information goes to a server somewhere?**

The threat scanning process does not send your personal files or private documents to any server. Cloud-based antivirus sends small pieces of information about the structure and characteristics of a file — not the file itself — to check against the threat database. Your photos, bank statements, and emails stay on your device. **I already have [Windows Defender](https://snipstack.io/windows-defender-download-2026/). Is that enough?**

Windows Defender (now called [Microsoft Defender](https://snipstack.io/microsoft-defender-antivirus-for-windows-11-2026/)) is a genuine improvement over doing nothing, and Microsoft has invested heavily in it over the past several years. It offers real-time protection and updates through Windows Update. For seniors who want an additional layer with features like phishing-site blocking, webcam protection, and scam-call alerts, a dedicated cloud-based antivirus adds meaningful coverage that Defender alone does not include.

**What if I do not have a fast internet connection?**

Cloud-based antivirus uses very little data for its real-time checks — far less than loading a single web page. Even on a slower broadband connection, it runs smoothly in the background without causing noticeable slowdowns. The connection only becomes an issue in areas with very poor or intermittent signal.

**How is cloud antivirus different from just using a VPN?**

A VPN (virtual private network) encrypts your internet traffic so that others on the network cannot intercept it. It does not scan files, [block malicious](https://snipstack.io/block-malicious-ads-before-they-load-2026/) downloads, or detect viruses. Antivirus and VPN do different jobs. Many [security suite](https://snipstack.io/security-suite-for-unlimited-devices-2026/)s, including Surfshark's, bundle both together — which is convenient and often cheaper than buying them separately.

Possibly — but worth checking. Ask them to confirm: Is the subscription still active? Is the program receiving updates? What was the last date it scanned? If they are not nearby, the quickest way to check is to open the program and look for a green status screen and a recent "last updated" date. If you see anything in red or yellow, or if the program will not open, it is time for a fresh install.

**Does cloud-based antivirus work on iPhones and tablets, not just computers?**
