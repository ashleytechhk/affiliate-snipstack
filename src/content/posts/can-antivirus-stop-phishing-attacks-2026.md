## What Is Phishing, Exactly?

Phishing is a scam where a criminal pretends to be someone you trust — your bank, the post office, Amazon, Medicare, even a family member — to trick you into handing over your password, credit card number, or personal details.

It usually arrives as:

- An **email** that looks almost identical to a real message from a real company
- A **text message** warning about an undelivered package or a problem with your account
- A **phone call** from someone claiming to be from your bank's fraud department
- A **[fake website](https://snipstack.io/fake-website-detector-tool-2026/)** that looks like your bank's login page but is controlled by criminals

The goal is always the same: get you to click a link, fill in a form, or call a number — and in doing so, hand over something valuable.

### Why seniors are targeted most

Criminals deliberately target people over 60 because older adults tend to be more trusting, have more savings to steal, and may be less familiar with the tricks criminals use. Research from AARP finds that older adults are **34% less likely** to report online fraud when it happens — which means criminals face fewer consequences.

Phishing attacks targeting seniors are also becoming more personal. Scammers now use information from social media and previous data breaches to personalise their messages — mentioning your name, your bank, even recent purchases you made. These personalised attacks are far harder to spot than the clumsy "URGENT: verify your account" emails of years past.

## What Antivirus Can Stop

Modern antivirus software is more capable than most people realise. Here is what it genuinely protects you from:

### Malware downloaded through [phishing link](https://snipstack.io/phishing-link-checker-free-2026/)s

If you click a link in a phishing email and it tries to install malicious software on your computer — a keylogger that records your passwords, or ransomware that locks your files — antivirus software can stop that software from running. This is the core job antivirus was built to do, and it does it well.

### Known phishing websites

Most [reputable antivirus](https://snipstack.io/reputable-antivirus-software-2026/) programs maintain a database of known phishing and scam websites. If you click a link that leads to one of these sites, your antivirus will block the page from loading and show you a warning instead. This works reliably for scam sites that have already been identified and added to the database.

### Dangerous email attachments

Phishing emails often include attachments — a fake invoice, a fake shipping notice, a supposed document from your doctor. Antivirus software scans these files before they open and blocks the ones that contain harmful code.

### Suspicious downloads

If a website tries to automatically download a file to your computer without your permission — a tactic called a "drive-by download" — antivirus software can flag and block it before it runs.

## What Antivirus Cannot Stop

This is the part that matters most, and the part most people do not know until it is too late.

### Brand-new phishing websites

Criminals create new phishing websites every single day — sometimes hundreds of them. When a scam site is brand new, it has not yet been added to any antivirus database. Your software cannot warn you about a threat it has never seen. Studies suggest there can be a gap of hours to days between when a new phishing site goes live and when security databases catch up.

### Phishing that does not involve software

If a phishing scam tricks you into typing your bank details into a realistic-looking fake website — and no malware is involved — many antivirus programs have no way to intervene. You did not download a virus. You just typed your password into the wrong website. That is enough for the criminal to empty your account.

### Phone and text phishing (vishing and smishing)

Phishing does not only arrive by email. Criminals call by phone pretending to be from the bank's fraud team and talk you through giving them access to your account. Antivirus software cannot listen to a phone call or read a text message. These attacks are completely outside its range.

### Targeted, personalised attacks

When a criminal uses information from a data breach to craft a scam email that includes your name, your bank, and a recent transaction — antivirus software may not flag it as suspicious, because it looks so much like a legitimate email. The danger is in the deception, not in any code the software could detect.

## The Gap That Criminals Exploit

Think of antivirus software like a deadbolt on your front door. It is essential protection, and you absolutely should have it. But a skilled criminal does not need to break down the door — they can simply knock and pretend to be the postman.

The equivalent in the digital world is a phishing email so convincing that you willingly open it, click the link, and type in your details. The deadbolt never needed to be bypassed, because you opened the door yourself.

This is not a reason to panic. It is a reason to understand what your current protection covers — and where it ends.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does [Windows Defender](https://snipstack.io/windows-defender-download-2026/) protect against phishing?

Windows Defender — the built-in antivirus on [Windows 10](https://snipstack.io/windows-10-antivirus-free-download-2026/) and 11 — does include some phishing protection through Microsoft SmartScreen, which checks websites and downloads against a list of known threats. It catches many phishing attempts, especially ones involving malware. But it has the same limitation as other antivirus tools: brand-new phishing sites that have not yet been identified can slip through. SmartScreen also works mainly in Microsoft Edge — if you use Chrome or Firefox, the protection level may differ.

### Can phishing attacks steal money directly from my bank account?

Yes — though usually not instantly. A successful phishing attack typically results in the criminal stealing your login credentials (your username and password). Once they have these, they log into your bank account themselves. From there they can transfer money, change your contact details to lock you out, or use your account details for fraud. The speed with which this can happen is why early warnings — like breach alerts — matter so much.

### I already clicked a suspicious link. What should I do?

First, do not enter any information on the page that opened. Close the tab immediately. Then run a full scan using your antivirus software. If the site asked for your password and you typed it in, go to the real website (type the address yourself in a new tab) and change your password right away. If any financial information was entered, contact your bank as soon as possible and let them know. Change any other accounts where you use the same password.

### Are phishing texts and phone calls as dangerous as email phishing?

Yes — and in some ways more so, because people tend to be more alert to suspicious emails than to phone calls from a convincing-sounding voice. Phone phishing (called "vishing") works by creating a sense of urgency and authority. A caller says they are from your bank's fraud team, there is suspicious activity on your account, and they need to verify your identity right now. The key defence: hang up and call your bank directly on the number printed on the back of your card. Never give information to someone who called you.

### Will antivirus software slow down my computer?

Modern antivirus software — including [Surfshark Antivirus](https://snipstack.io/surfshark-antivirus-vs-windows-defender-2026/) — is designed to run quietly without affecting everyday performance. Scans happen in the background. Most users notice no slowdown at all during regular activities like browsing, reading email, or watching videos. If you are running a very old computer, there may be a minor impact during a full scan, but this typically lasts only a few minutes.

### How do I know if an email is real or phishing?

Look at the sender's actual email address (not just the display name). A scam email might display "Your Bank" as the name but the actual address will be something like `support@bank-security-verify.com`. Real emails from your bank come from the bank's own domain, like `@yourbank.com`. Also check whether the link in the email, when you hover your mouse over it without clicking, shows a web address that matches the real company. Mismatches between what a link says and where it actually leads are a strong sign of phishing.
